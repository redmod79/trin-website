# Web Research Findings

## Study Question
What does "firstborn of all creation" (prototokos pases ktiseos) mean in Colossians 1:15-20, and does it teach that Jesus was the first created being? Also examine "the beginning of the creation of God" (arche tes ktiseos tou theou) in Revelation 3:14.

**Author credentials:** PhD, University of Aberdeen; lecturer in New Testament at Edinburgh Theological Seminary
**Key claims:**
- "Firstborn" functions figuratively rather than literally in Colossians 1:15, denoting exaltation and supremacy rather than birth order
- Psalm 89:27 is decisive evidence: David was "the youngest of his brothers" yet God declared he would make him "the firstborn, the highest of the kings of the earth"

## Summary of Scholarly Landscape

The scholarly debate over Colossians 1:15 centers on the genitive construction "prototokos pases ktiseos" and whether it should be read as a partitive genitive (Christ is the first *within* creation) or a genitive of subordination/relation (Christ is preeminent *over* creation). The overwhelming majority of published commentary — from the patristic era through modern scholarship — favors the preeminence reading. The key arguments for this position are: (1) the causal gar/hoti ("for") in v.16, which explains that Christ is called "firstborn" *because* all things were created by/through/for him; (2) the OT usage of "firstborn" (bekor) as a title of rank rather than birth order, especially Psalm 89:27 where David (the youngest son) is called "firstborn"; (3) Paul's deliberate choice of prototokos over the available alternative protoktistos ("first-created"); (4) the logical impossibility of Christ being both a created being and the creator of "all things" (Col 1:16; John 1:3); and (5) Chrysostom's early observation that Paul said "firstborn," not "first-created."

However, a minority scholarly position (represented in this research by Foster and the OnlyTrueGod analysis) argues that the partitive genitive cannot be ruled out on purely grammatical grounds. Foster cites Wallace's own criterion that context, not grammar alone, determines whether a genitive is partitive. The non-Trinitarian position emphasizes that BDAG (3rd ed.) considers "first created" to be "linguistically probable" for arche in Rev 3:14, that Simcox and Moule acknowledged the "first creature" reading is grammatically possible for Col 1:15, and that the standard NT term for "ruler" is archon, not arche. The strongest non-Trinitarian argument on Rev 3:14 is the statistical claim that arche + genitive consistently means "beginning of" (temporal) rather than "originator of" in every other NT occurrence.

The resolution depends heavily on contextual and theological factors beyond grammar alone. The key contextual arguments are: (1) whether v.16's "for" clause makes the partitive reading incoherent (orthodox position) or simply describes the firstborn's subsequent role (non-Trinitarian position); (2) whether John's broader Christology (John 1:1-3, "all things were made through him") prevents reading Rev 3:14 as "first creature"; and (3) whether the "creation" in Rev 3:14 refers to original creation or new creation (the redeemed). These contextual questions must be resolved by examining the biblical text directly.

## Research Directives for Downstream Agents

1. **Verify the genitive construction claim:** Wallace argues the genitive in Col 1:15 is "genitive of subordination" — verify by examining how prototokos + genitive functions in other biblical occurrences (e.g., Col 1:18 "firstborn from the dead"; Rom 8:29 "firstborn among many brethren"). Does the genitive consistently indicate the group Christ is preeminent *over* or the group he belongs *to*?

2. **Verify the Psalm 89:27 parallel:** Multiple scholars cite Ps 89:27 (LXX Ps 88:28) as decisive for proving "firstborn" = preeminence. Verify: (a) Does the LXX use prototokos here? (b) Is the Davidic context genuinely parallel to the cosmic context of Col 1:15? (c) Check also Exodus 4:22 (Israel as firstborn) and Jeremiah 31:9 (Ephraim as firstborn).

3. **Verify the gar/hoti connection (v.15-16):** Beetham and Wilson argue v.16 begins with "for" (hoti), explaining *why* Christ is firstborn. Verify: (a) Does the Greek text actually begin v.16 with hoti? (b) Parse the verse to confirm the causal relationship. (c) Does "all things were created by him" logically exclude Christ from the category "all things"?

4. **Verify the protoktistos distinction:** Multiple sources claim Paul could have used protoktistos but chose prototokos. Verify: (a) Does protoktistos actually exist as a Greek word in the biblical period? (b) Where and when does it first appear in Greek literature? (c) Is this argument from silence valid?

5. **Verify the BDAG claim on Rev 3:14:** The OnlyTrueGod source claims BDAG 3rd ed. considers "first created" to be "linguistically probable" for arche in Rev 3:14. Verify: (a) How does arche function elsewhere in Revelation (Rev 1:8, 21:6, 22:13)? (b) How does John use arche in his Gospel and epistles (John 1:1-2; 1 John 1:1, 2:13-14)? (c) Does John's broader Christology (John 1:3 "all things were made through him") constrain the meaning?

6. **Verify the archon vs. arche distinction:** The non-Trinitarian source argues that if John meant "ruler," he would have used archon (as in Rev 1:5). Verify: (a) Does John use arche and archon with distinct meanings? (b) Are there biblical passages where arche clearly means "ruler" rather than "beginning"?

7. **Verify the "new creation" interpretation of Rev 3:14:** Neal argues "the creation of God" in Rev 3:14 refers to the new creation (redeemed people), not original creation — making Christ "the firstborn from the dead." Verify: (a) Does ktisis/ktiseos elsewhere in Revelation or John refer to new creation? (b) Does the Laodicean context support this reading? (c) Compare with 2 Cor 5:17 and Gal 6:15 where "new creation" language appears.

8. **Verify the creation paradox:** Multiple sources argue John 1:3 ("all things were made through him; without him was not anything made that was made") creates a logical impossibility if Christ is himself created. Verify: (a) Does John 1:3 use panta (all things) in the same comprehensive sense as Col 1:16? (b) How do non-Trinitarian interpreters handle this text? (c) Does Hebrews 1:2 ("through whom also he made the worlds") reinforce or qualify this argument?

9. **Verify the Col 1:15-20 hymn structure:** Multiple scholars identify two parallel stanzas — creation (vv.15-18a) and redemption (vv.18b-20) — with "firstborn of creation" paralleling "firstborn from the dead." Verify: (a) Parse the Greek text to confirm the structural parallels. (b) Does the parallel suggest both uses of "firstborn" carry the same semantic force (preeminence)? (c) Does the hymn structure illuminate whether "firstborn" implies inclusion in the group or authority over it?

10. **Examine the Colossian heresy context:** Several sources note Paul may be combating a proto-Gnostic or Jewish-mystic heresy at Colossae that diminished Christ's role. Verify: (a) What does the letter itself reveal about the false teaching (Col 2:8, 2:16-23)? (b) Does Paul's emphasis on Christ's supremacy over "thrones, dominions, rulers, powers" (1:16) address a specific theological threat? (c) Does this polemical context favor the "preeminence" reading of prototokos?

11. **Verify the Lightfoot claim about Rabbi Bechai:** Lightfoot reportedly cites Rabbi Bechai calling God "firstborn of the world" (prototokos tou kosmou) — meaning supremacy, not origin. Verify: (a) Is this Jewish usage attested elsewhere? (b) Does it establish that "firstborn" could be used of an uncreated being in the ancient world?
