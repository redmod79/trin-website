# Web Research Findings

## Study Question
If Jesus is not God but a created being or merely a perfect human, what logical contradictions arise within the Bible's own framework? Trace the consequences across six domains: (1) worship, (2) forgiveness, (3) creation, (4) savior exclusivity, (5) prayer/omnipresence, (6) atonement sufficiency.

### Precept Austin — Hebrews 9:13-14 Commentary (compilation of commentators)
**URL:** https://www.preceptaustin.org/hebrews_913-14
**Key claims:**
- **F.F. Bruce:** The phrase "eternal Spirit" (aioniou pneumatos) draws on Isaianic Servant imagery; "It is in the power of the Divine Spirit, accordingly, that the Servant accomplishes every phase of his ministry"
- **Spurgeon:** The phrase indicates Christ's "eternal Godhead" gave His offering "extreme value that otherwise could not have been attached to it"
- **Leon Morris:** "Christ's own spirit was indeed involved in his voluntary sacrifice, but so is the divine Spirit"
- **Kenneth Wuest:** Greek amomos (without blemish) carries legal-cultic weight; Christ's unblemished offering superior because He "was a man without blemish in all that He did"
- **Steven Cole:** "The difference between the Levitical offerings and Christ's self-offering was infinite rather than relative"
- **Andrew Murray:** "It was the Word that became flesh... That life gave His blood... an infinite value"

- **Paul Ellingworth:** Christ uniquely qualified as both "high priest and offering" simultaneously
- The passage employs rabbinic qal wahomer (a fortiori) reasoning: if animal blood sanctified externally, Christ's blood infinitely purifies internally
**Greek analysis:** amomos = without blemish (legal-cultic term); aioniou pneumatos = eternal Spirit (debated: Holy Spirit vs. Christ's eternal divine nature)
**Relevance:** Domain 6 (atonement sufficiency) — multiple scholars argue the atonement's efficacy depends on Christ's divine nature; a finite creature's sacrifice cannot produce infinite/eternal redemption
**Status:** Lead to verify against Scripture

### Granville Sharp's Rule and Titus 2:13 — Multiple Sources
**URL (primary):** https://christianpublishinghouse.co/2019/08/04/titus-213-and-2-peter-11-what-is-the-long-debated-controversial-granville-sharp-rule/
**URL (secondary):** https://theopedia.com/granville-sharps-rule
**Key claims:**
- Granville Sharp (1798) formulated the rule: when two singular, personal nouns appear under one article linked by "and" (article-noun-kai-noun), they designate the same individual
- Applied to Titus 2:13 Greek: "tou megalou theou kai soteros" (the great God and Savior) — under the rule, both "God" and "Savior" refer to Jesus Christ
- Applied to 2 Peter 1:1 Greek: "tou theou kai soteros Iesou Christou" (the God and Savior Jesus Christ)
- The primary source notes Sharp worked with "a limited sample size" and later papyri discoveries showed "Koine authors often omitted the second article even if they were speaking of distinct subjects"
- The primary source also notes Paul's typical pattern in Titus maintains distinction between God (Father) and Christ/Savior (Son), citing Titus 1:4 and 3:4-6
- The secondary source (Theopedia) notes modern research has confirmed Sharp's observation in "some eighty New-Testament instances with no exceptions"
**Greek analysis:** The rule concerns the article-noun-kai-noun construction; debate centers on whether Titus 2:13 follows the standard pattern or represents an exception
**Relevance:** Domain 4 (savior exclusivity) — if the rule holds, Paul explicitly calls Jesus "God and Savior" in the same breath; downstream agents should verify the Greek construction and examine Daniel Wallace's research
**Status:** Lead to verify against Scripture

## Summary of Scholarly Landscape

The scholarly landscape on this question divides into two primary camps with distinct methodological approaches.

**The pro-deity position** argues that the Bible's own internal logic requires Jesus' deity across all six domains. Scholars like F.F. Bruce, Spurgeon, Andrew Murray, Leon Morris, and Kenneth Wuest emphasize that the atonement's infinite efficacy depends on the divine nature of the one offering it. The worship acceptance/refusal pattern (angels refuse, apostles refuse, Jesus alone accepts) creates an inescapable dilemma: either Jesus is God and rightly receives worship, or he is the Bible's greatest moral contradiction — a righteous man who accepts idolatry. The Granville Sharp rule in Greek grammar provides a linguistic argument that Paul directly identifies Jesus as "God and Savior" in Titus 2:13. The transfer of YHWH-exclusive language (Isa 45:23 to Phil 2:10-11, Isa 43:11 to Acts 4:12) is treated as identification, not mere delegation.

**The anti-deity position** (represented by Kermit Zarley, Biblical Unitarian sources, and some NT scholars) offers counterarguments focused on delegation: forgiveness authority was delegated (Matt 9:8, John 20:23), the title "savior" was applied to human judges (Neh 9:27, Obad 21), worship (proskuneo) could mean "homage" rather than divine worship, and Phil 2:9-11 represents exaltation to shared authority rather than identification with YHWH. E.P. Sanders is cited arguing that pronouncing forgiveness would not have been blasphemy under any known Jewish law.

The critical scholarly battleground is whether the "delegation model" can sustain itself across all six domains simultaneously. Individual counterarguments exist for each domain, but the cumulative force of all six — Jesus accepts worship, forgives sins, creates all things, is the exclusive savior, receives prayer while claiming omnipresence, and provides eternal redemption through indestructible life — creates a weight that the delegation model must bear across every domain without exception. The downstream research should test whether a single coherent non-deity Christology can account for all six without internal contradiction.

## Research Directives for Downstream Agents

1. **Worship pattern verification:** Verify all instances where Jesus accepted proskuneo (Matt 8:2, 9:18, 14:33, 28:9, 28:17, John 9:38, Luke 24:52) and all refusal instances (Rev 19:10, 22:8-9, Acts 10:25-26, 14:11-15). Confirm the Greek term is identical in accept and refuse cases. Check whether Jesus ever redirected worship to the Father.

2. **John 20:28 Greek construction:** Verify that "eipen auto" (said to him) proves Thomas addressed Jesus directly, refuting the "exclamation" theory. Check whether Jesus' response (John 20:29) constitutes acceptance of the title "my God."

3. **Mark 2:5-7 delegation rebuttal:** Zarley claims Matt 9:8 ("given such authority to men") proves delegation. Verify the Greek of Matt 9:8 — does "men" (anthropois, plural) refer to Jesus specifically or to humanity generally? Test whether the passive voice in Mark 2:5 actually dissolves the divine prerogative claim. Verify whether Jesus forgives sins committed against God (not just interpersonal offenses), which would exceed any delegation model.

4. **Isaiah 43:11 / savior exclusivity:** Verify the Hebrew of Isa 43:11 (moshia). Compare with the "saviors" in Neh 9:27, Judg 3:9, 3:15, Obad 21. Test whether there is a qualitative difference between temporal national deliverance and eternal salvation. Check whether Isa 43:11 context (Isa 43:10-13) makes broader claims than just Red Sea deliverance.

5. **Isaiah 44:24 creation paradox:** Verify YHWH's claim to have created "alone" and "by myself" (Isa 44:24). Cross-reference with John 1:3 ("all things were made through him; without him was not anything made") and Col 1:16. If Jesus is a created being, he is part of "all things" that were made through him — a logical impossibility.

6. **Philippians 2:9-11 / Isaiah 45:21-23:** Verify the full Isaiah context — does YHWH say "there is no God else beside me" in the same breath as "every knee shall bow"? If so, can the "exaltation/delegation" reading survive the exclusive monotheistic claim in the source text?

7. **Titus 2:13 Granville Sharp:** Verify the Greek construction. Check Daniel Wallace's research (if available in tools) on how many NT instances follow the article-noun-kai-noun pattern. Note whether exceptions exist for singular, personal, non-proper nouns.

8. **Hebrews 7:16 akatalutos:** Verify the Greek akatalutos (indestructible). Check how this word is used elsewhere in the NT/LXX. Test whether "indestructible life" is compatible with a created nature (all created things are by definition destructible by their Creator).

9. **Hebrews 9:12-14 eternal redemption:** Verify the argument chain: eternal redemption (aionion lutrosin) requires more than a finite sacrifice. Check whether "eternal Spirit" (aioniou pneumatos) refers to the Holy Spirit or Christ's own eternal nature. Verify Spurgeon's claim that "the blood that He shed was the blood of God" against Acts 20:28.

10. **Self-defeating moral argument:** If Jesus is not God, trace the consequences for his moral character: he accepted worship (idolatry by OT standards), claimed to forgive sins against God (blasphemy by the scribes' correct reasoning), accepted the title "my God" (deception), and claimed omnipresence (falsehood). The Bible's greatest moral hero becomes simultaneously its greatest violator of the first two commandments.

11. **Prayer to Jesus:** Verify Acts 7:59 (Stephen prays to Jesus while dying), 2 Cor 12:8 (Paul prays to "the Lord" three times — verify whether "Lord" here is Christ from context), and 1 Cor 1:2 (Christians defined as those who "call upon the name of our Lord Jesus Christ"). If Jesus is not God, prayer to him violates the first commandment.

12. **Matt 18:20 and 28:20 omnipresence:** Verify Jesus' claims to be present "where two or three are gathered" and "with you always, to the end of the age." If believers are scattered across the globe, simultaneous presence requires omnipresence — an attribute of God alone. Test whether any created being in Scripture claims omnipresence.
