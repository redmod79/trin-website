# Verses

## Question
How do the New Testament writers reconcile strict Old Testament monotheism ('YHWH alone is God') with attributing divine titles, worship, creation, and judgment to Jesus Christ? What does 'one God' mean in light of the NT's Christological claims? (Capstone synthesis of trin-01 through trin-07.)

### The Shema — Israel's foundational monotheistic confession

> Hear, O Israel: The LORD our God [is] one LORD:

**Context:** Moses addresses Israel before entering the Promised Land. The Shema is the central confession of Israelite faith, recited daily. The word 'one' (echad, H259) permits composite unity (cf. Gen 2:24 'one flesh') — Moses chose echad over yachid (H3173, solitary/only).

- אֱלֹהֵ֖ינוּ () — Noun.mp.Abs.+1up — god(s)
- יְהוָ֥ה () — PropN.ms.Abs.+NAN — YHWH

**Notes:** Hebrew parser confirms: YHWH (PropN.ms) + elohim (Noun.mp — plural form) + YHWH (PropN.ms) + echad (Noun.s). The plural elohim with singular verb patterns throughout the OT. The Shema uses echad, not yachid — significant because yachid denotes absolute solitary oneness and is never applied to YHWH in Scripture.

### YHWH's exclusive 'First and Last' self-designation

> Thus saith the LORD the King of Israel, and his redeemer the LORD of hosts; I [am] the first, and I [am] the last; and beside me [there is] no God.

**Context:** Isaiah 44 — YHWH's polemic against idolatry. The 'First and Last' title is an exclusive monotheistic claim meaning no deity existed before or will exist after YHWH. When Rev 1:17 applies this title to Jesus, the monotheistic framework demands Christ shares YHWH's identity.

**Notes:** The LXX renders 'first and last' with protos and eschatos — the exact words used of Christ in Rev 1:17, 2:8, 22:13. The clause 'beside me there is no God' makes this an absolute exclusivity claim — if Jesus bears this title, he cannot be 'beside' YHWH but must be within YHWH.

### YHWH created ALONE — the lebaddi exclusion

> Thus saith the LORD, thy redeemer, and he that formed thee from the womb, I [am] the LORD that maketh all things; that stretcheth forth the heavens alone; that spreadeth abroad the earth by myself;

**Context:** Isaiah 44 — continuation of YHWH's self-identification. The Hebrew lebaddi ('alone, by myself') excludes any co-creator who is not within YHWH. If Christ is the agent of creation (John 1:3, Col 1:16, Heb 1:2), and YHWH created alone, Christ must be within YHWH's identity.

**Notes:** Four independent NT witnesses attribute creation to Christ: John 1:3 (panta di autou egeneto), Col 1:16 (en auto ektisthe ta panta), 1 Cor 8:6 (di hou ta panta), Heb 1:2 (di hou epoiesen tous aionas). YHWH's lebaddi ('by myself alone') would exclude even an angelic co-worker — only divine identity resolves this.

### YHWH exclusivity: 'there is no God beside me'

> I [am] the LORD, and [there is] none else, [there is] no God beside me: I girded thee, though thou hast not known me: That they may know from the rising of the sun, and from the west, that [there is] none beside me. I [am] the LORD, and [there is] none else.

**Context:** Isaiah 45 — addressed to Cyrus but declaring universal monotheism. The threefold denial ('none else,' 'no God beside me,' 'none beside me') establishes the OT framework that the NT must work within when attributing divine prerogatives to Christ.

**Notes:** This is the strongest OT monotheistic declaration. The NT does not contradict this — it includes Christ within the identity that makes this claim. Paul's reformulation of the Shema in 1 Cor 8:6 distributes 'one God' and 'one Lord' within this single YHWH-identity.

### YHWH as sole savior — every knee shall bow (source for Phil 2:10-11)

> Tell ye, and bring [them] near; yea, let them take counsel together: who hath declared this from ancient time? [who] hath told it from that time? [have] not I the LORD? and [there is] no God else beside me; a just God and a Saviour; [there is] none beside me. Look unto me, and be ye saved, all the ends of the earth: for I [am] God, and [there is] none else. I have sworn by myself, the word is gone out of my mouth [in] righteousness, and shall not return, That unto me every knee shall bow, every tongue shall swear.

**Context:** Isaiah 45 — YHWH's oath that every knee will bow to him. Phil 2:10-11 directly quotes this passage and applies it to Jesus Christ. YHWH swears 'by myself' — this oath is made to YHWH's own person, and Paul transfers it to Jesus.

**Notes:** Phil 2:10-11 uses kamptō (bow) and exomologeō (confess) to render this Isaiah text. The application to Jesus is the most explicit YHWH-title transfer in the NT, made by Paul — a trained Pharisee who would have known the original context. YHWH's exclusive savior claim ('none beside me') yet the NT calls Christ 'Savior' repeatedly (Tit 2:13, 2 Pet 1:1).

### YHWH will not share glory with 'another'

> I [am] the LORD: that [is] my name: and my glory will I not give to another, neither my praise to graven images.

**Context:** Isaiah 42 — YHWH declares his glory is non-transferable to 'another.' If Christ is ontologically 'another' (a creature), then the glory-sharing between Father and Son (John 17:5, Rev 5:13) would contradict God's own declaration. The resolution: Christ is not 'another' but shares the divine identity.

**Notes:** John 17:5 records Jesus requesting the glory he had with the Father 'before the world was.' If Christ is a creature — i.e., 'another' — this violates Isa 42:8. The text forces a binary: either Christ shares YHWH's identity (and glory-sharing is not giving to 'another'), or the NT contradicts the OT.

### Three-agent sending formula: First and Last speaks, sent by Lord YHWH and his Spirit

> Hearken unto me, O Jacob and Israel, my called; I [am] he; I [am] the first, I also [am] the last. Mine hand also hath laid the foundation of the earth, and my right hand hath spanned the heavens: [when] I call unto them, they stand up together. All ye, assemble yourselves, and hear; which among them hath declared these [things]? The LORD hath loved him: he will do his pleasure on Babylon, and his arm [shall be on] the Chaldeans. I, [even] I, have spoken; yea, I have called him: I have brought him, and he shall make his way prosperous. Come ye near unto me, hear ye this; I have not spoken in secret from the beginning; from the time that it was, there [am] I: and now the Lord GOD, and his Spirit, hath sent me.

**Context:** Isaiah 48 — The speaker identifies as 'the First and the Last' (v.12) and the creator of heavens and earth (v.13), yet says 'the Lord GOD and his Spirit hath sent me' (v.16). Three distinct agents are visible: the speaker (First and Last, creator), the Lord GOD (sender), and the Spirit (co-sender).

**Notes:** This is the strongest OT three-agent passage. The speaker claims creator and First-and-Last titles (which Isa 44:6 reserves for YHWH exclusively), yet is sent by another person identified as 'Lord GOD' together with 'his Spirit.' This maps directly onto the NT trinitarian pattern: the Son who is YHWH (John 1:1-3, creator) is sent by the Father and the Spirit.

### The Word was God — Logos Christology and the creation boundary

> In the beginning was the Word, and the Word was with God, and the Word was God. The same was in the beginning with God. All things were made by him; and without him was not any thing made that was made.

**Context:** John's prologue — the most concentrated Christological statement in the NT. Three clauses in 1:1 establish: (a) eternal pre-existence ('In the beginning was'), (b) relational distinction ('was with God'), (c) essential deity ('was God'). Verse 3 then attributes universal creation to the Word, crossing the creator-creature boundary.

### The Word became flesh — incarnation bridge

> And the Word was made flesh, and dwelt among us, (and we beheld his glory, the glory as of the only begotten of the Father,) full of grace and truth.

**Context:** The incarnation event. The Logos who 'was God' (1:1c) now 'became' (egeneto) flesh. The verb shift from en (continuous existence) to egeneto (becoming) marks a transition — the eternal Word entered a new mode of existence without ceasing to be what he was.

**Notes:** Greek parser: egeneto (ginomai, G1096, Aor Mid Ind) — the Word 'became' flesh, contrasting with the 'was' (en) of 1:1. Monogenous (monogenes, G3439, Gen Sg M) — unique-of-its-kind. Eskenosen (skenoō, G4637) — 'tabernacled,' echoing OT tabernacle/shekinah imagery.

### The only-begotten God who declares the Father

> No man hath seen God at any time; the only begotten Son, which is in the bosom of the Father, he hath declared [him].

**Context:** The prologue's climax. The N1904 reads monogenēs theos ('only-begotten God') rather than the TR's monogenēs huios ('only-begotten Son'). Either reading affirms unique divine status — the one who is 'in the bosom of the Father' uniquely reveals God.

**Notes:** Greek parser on N1904: monogenēs (G3439, Nom Sg M) + theos (G2316, Nom Sg M) — 'only-begotten God.' The present participle ōn (eimi, G1510, Pres Act Ptcp) = 'the one being in the bosom' — continuous state. Exēgēsato (exēgeomai, G1834, Aor Mid Ind) — 'exegeted/declared' — the Son is the exegesis of the Father.

### Equal honor for the Son AS for the Father — judgment transferred

> For the Father judgeth no man, but hath committed all judgment unto the Son: That all [men] should honour the Son, even as they honour the Father. He that honoureth not the Son honoureth not the Father which hath sent him.

**Context:** John 5 — Jesus' response to the Sabbath controversy. After claiming God as 'my own Father' (making himself equal with God, 5:18), Jesus explains the divine prerogative of judgment. The 'even as' (kathōs) demands equal, not lesser, honor.

**Notes:** The logic is bidirectional: honoring the Son is honoring the Father, and NOT honoring the Son is NOT honoring the Father. This establishes functional identity in the reception of honor — the very honor due to YHWH alone is now extended to the Son. This would be idolatry if the Son were merely a creature.

### Seeing the Son = seeing the Father; mutual indwelling

> Jesus saith unto him, Have I been so long time with you, and yet hast thou not known me, Philip? he that hath seen me hath seen the Father; and how sayest thou [then], Shew us the Father? Believest thou not that I am in the Father, and the Father in me? the words that I speak unto you I speak not of myself: but the Father that dwelleth in me, he doeth the works. Believe me that I [am] in the Father, and the Father in me: or else believe me for the very works' sake.

**Context:** Upper Room discourse — Philip asks to see the Father. Jesus' reply claims that seeing him IS seeing the Father, and asserts mutual indwelling ('I in the Father and the Father in me'). This goes beyond representational agency to ontological unity.

**Notes:** The mutual indwelling claim (perichoresis) — 'I am in the Father and the Father in me' — exceeds what any agent or angel could claim. No prophet ever said 'if you have seen me you have seen God.' Moses hid in the rock to survive God's passing glory (Exod 33:20-22); Jesus claims to BE the visible manifestation of the Father's nature.

### Monos alethinos theos — the 'only true God' question

> And this is life eternal, that they might know thee the only true God, and Jesus Christ, whom thou hast sent.

**Context:** High Priestly Prayer — Jesus addresses the Father. The phrase 'the only true God' (ton monon alēthinon theon) is often cited as excluding Jesus from deity. But the reductio argument from trin-03 is decisive.

**Notes:** Greek parser: monon (monos, G3441, Acc Sg M) + alēthinon (alēthinos, G228, Acc Sg M) + theon (theos, G2316, Acc Sg M). If monos absolutely excludes Christ, then 1 Tim 6:15-16's 'only Sovereign' with 'immortality' excludes Christ from immortality — contradicted by Rev 1:18 'alive forevermore.' And 1 Tim 1:17's 'only wise God' excludes Christ from wisdom — contradicted by Col 2:3 'in whom are hid all treasures of wisdom.' The monos+theos pattern affirms the Father's unique deity without excluding the Son who shares that deity.

### Pre-incarnate glory — 'before the world was'

> And now, O Father, glorify thou me with thine own self with the glory which I had with thee before the world was.

**Context:** High Priestly Prayer — Jesus requests the restoration of pre-incarnate glory shared with the Father. 'Before the world was' (pro tou ton kosmon einai) asserts pre-cosmic existence. This refutes Dunn's Adam Christology, which denies preexistence.

**Notes:** This verse directly contradicts the Adam Christology reading of Phil 2. Adam never had glory 'before the world was.' The parallel with John 1:1 ('in the beginning was the Word') and Col 1:17 ('he is before all things') establishes pre-existence as a multi-author NT witness, not a Johannine idiosyncrasy. Combined with Isa 42:8 ('my glory I will not give to another'), the glory-sharing implies Christ is not 'another' but shares the divine identity.

### The kenosis hymn: morphe theou, self-emptying, and the Isa 45:23 climax

**Context:** Philippians 2 — Paul's ethical appeal grounded in Christology. ### Firstborn of creation — sovereignty title with causal hoti clause

> Who is the image of the invisible God, the firstborn of every creature: For by him were all things created, that are in heaven, and that are in earth, visible and invisible, whether [they be] thrones, or dominions, or principalities, or powers: all things were created by him, and for him: And he is before all things, and by him all things consist.

**Context:** Colossians 1 — Christ hymn. 'Firstborn' (prōtotokos, G4416) functions as a sovereignty title (cf. Psa 89:27 — David, youngest son, made 'firstborn' = highest king). The causal hoti clause in v.16 explains: he is firstborn BECAUSE by him all things were created. The repeated 'all things' (ta panta, 5x in vv.16-17) encompasses the entire created order.

**Notes:** Prior study trin-01 established that prōtotokos functions as preeminence, not chronological order. Col 1:17 'he IS before all things' (estin pro pantōn) uses present tense for pre-existence (paralleling John 1:1's en). The word study for G4416 confirms: all 8 NT occurrences carry sovereignty/preeminence sense, never mere chronological birth order.

### Son as radiance of glory and exact imprint of God's substance

> God, who at sundry times and in divers manners spake in time past unto the fathers by the prophets, Hath in these last days spoken unto us by [his] Son, whom he hath appointed heir of all things, by whom also he made the worlds; Who being the brightness of [his] glory, and the express image of his person, and upholding all things by the word of his power, when he had by himself purged our sins, sat down on the right hand of the Majesty on high; Being made so much better than the angels, as he hath by inheritance obtained a more excellent name than they.

**Context:** Hebrews 1 opening — the most systematic NT argument for Christ's deity. Seven claims in rapid succession: (1) heir of all things, (2) maker of the worlds, (3) radiance of God's glory, (4) exact imprint of God's substance, (5) upholder of all things, (6) purifier of sins, (7) enthroned at God's right hand. **Context:** The burning bush theophany. God reveals his name as 'I AM' (ehyeh). The LXX renders this ego eimi ho ōn ('I am the one who is'). Jesus' absolute ego eimi sayings in John deliberately echo this self-revelation.

**Notes:** The grammar reference on absolute ego eimi traces the LXX background: Exod 3:14 LXX uses egō eimi ho ōn. The Isaianic ani hu cluster (Isa 41:4, 43:10, 43:25, 46:4, 48:12) further develops this into a YHWH-identification formula that John's Jesus adopts.

### Child called 'Mighty God, Everlasting Father' — divine titles for Messiah

> For unto us a child is born, unto us a son is given: and the government shall be upon his shoulder: and his name shall be called Wonderful, Counseller, The mighty God, The everlasting Father, The Prince of Peace.

**Context:** Isaiah 9 — messianic prophecy. The 'child born' receives divine titles: El Gibbor (Mighty God, same title applied to YHWH in Isa 10:21) and Avi-Ad (Everlasting Father/Father of Eternity). These are not merely honorific — El Gibbor is used of YHWH himself.

**Notes:** El Gibbor (H410 + H1368) — the exact combination used of YHWH in Isa 10:21 and Deut 10:17. If the messianic child bears this title, which is YHWH's own, the monotheistic framework demands he shares YHWH's identity.

### 'Let US make man' — plurality in creation language

> And God said, Let us make man in our image, after our likeness: and let them have dominion over the fish of the sea, and over the fowl of the air, and over the cattle, and over all the earth, and over every creeping thing that creepeth upon the earth.

**Context:** The creation account. God speaks in the plural ('let us make man in our image'). The prior study on Elohim grammar established that this forecloses a purely royal-we reading (especially Gen 3:22 where pronominal suffixes on prepositions cannot be plural-of-majesty).

**Notes:** The 'let us' language is one of four OT plural self-references by God: Gen 1:26, 3:22, 11:7, Isa 6:8. While this alone does not prove trinitarianism, it is consistent with the intra-divine plurality that the NT makes explicit.

### Two-YHWH passage: 'YHWH rained from YHWH out of heaven'

> Then the LORD rained upon Sodom and upon Gomorrah brimstone and fire from the LORD out of heaven;

**Context:** Destruction of Sodom. The text distinguishes between YHWH on earth (who has been speaking with Abraham) and YHWH in heaven (from whom fire comes). Two distinct agents, both identified as YHWH.

**Notes:** This is the strongest OT two-YHWH passage. One YHWH acts on earth, another YHWH sends fire from heaven. Both are called by the divine name. This OT plurality within YHWH's identity is the foundation for the NT's Christological claims.

### 'This is the true God and eternal life' — referent debate

> And we know that the Son of God is come, and hath given us an understanding, that we may know him that is true, and we are in him that is true, [even] in his Son Jesus Christ. This is the true God, and eternal life.

**Context:** 1 John 5 — the closing affirmation. The demonstrative 'this' (houtos) most naturally refers to the nearest antecedent — 'his Son Jesus Christ.' If so, John identifies Jesus as 'the true God and eternal life.'

**Notes:** The grammatical antecedent debate: houtos ('this one') could refer to (a) the Father ('him that is true') or (b) Jesus Christ (the nearest antecedent). The predicate 'eternal life' (zōē aiōnios) favors the Christ reading because 1 John 1:2 calls Jesus 'the eternal life which was with the Father.' Either way, 'the true God' (ho alēthinos theos) echoes John 17:3.

### Granville Sharp: 'our God and Savior Jesus Christ'

> Simon Peter, a servant and an apostle of Jesus Christ, to them that have obtained like precious faith with us through the righteousness of God and our Saviour Jesus Christ:

**Context:** 2 Peter opening. The Granville Sharp construction (tou theou hēmōn kai sōtēros Iēsou Christou) identifies one person as both 'our God' and 'Savior.' Same grammatical pattern as Titus 2:13.

**Notes:** Grammar reference on Granville Sharp confirms: single article tou governing theou and sōtēros with proper name — one referent. Peter calls Jesus 'our God and Savior.' This is a second Granville Sharp identification of Christ as God alongside Titus 2:13.

### 'The church of God which he purchased with his own blood'

> Take heed therefore unto yourselves, and to all the flock, over the which the Holy Ghost hath made you overseers, to feed the church of God, which he hath purchased with his own blood.

**Context:** Paul's farewell to the Ephesian elders. 'The church of God' — God's own blood purchased the church. Since God the Father does not have blood, this implies the blood is God's through Christ's divine identity.

**Notes:** The textual variant debate: some MSS read 'church of the Lord.' But the harder reading 'church of God...with his own blood' is preferred precisely because it is theologically challenging — a scribe would more likely smooth it to 'Lord' than introduce the difficulty. Prior study trin-04 analyzed this verse: God's own blood through the incarnation mechanism.

### Isaiah saw Christ's glory — Isa 6:1-3 vision attributed to pre-incarnate Son

> These things said Esaias, when he saw his glory, and spake of him.

**Context:** John 12 — after quoting Isa 53:1 and Isa 6:10, John explains: Isaiah said these things 'when he saw his [Christ's] glory.' This identifies the Lord on the throne in Isa 6:1-3 (before whom the seraphim cry 'Holy, holy, holy, LORD of hosts') as the pre-incarnate Christ.

**Notes:** John directly identifies the YHWH of hosts whom Isaiah saw on the throne (Isa 6:1-3) as Christ. The trisagion ('Holy, holy, holy') addressed to YHWH in Isa 6:3 was addressed to the pre-incarnate Son. This is a Johannine YHWH-identification, independent of Paul's transfers.

### Believers defined as those who 'call upon the name of our Lord Jesus Christ'

> Unto the church of God which is at Corinth, to them that are sanctified in Christ Jesus, called [to be] saints, with all that in every place call upon the name of Jesus Christ our Lord, both theirs and ours:

**Context:** 1 Corinthians opening — Christians are defined by their practice of 'calling upon the name of the Lord Jesus Christ.' This phrase echoes Joel 2:32's 'call upon the name of YHWH' — the defining devotional act of YHWH-worship now directed to Jesus.

**Notes:** Hurtado's key evidence: this is not a theological statement but a description of actual practice. Earliest Christians defined themselves as people who invoke Jesus' name — the same act that defined YHWH-worship in the OT (Joel 2:32, Gen 4:26, Gen 12:8).

### Maranatha — Aramaic prayer invocation to the exalted Jesus

> If any man love not the Lord Jesus Christ, let him be Anathema Maranatha.

**Context:** 1 Corinthians closing — Paul preserves an Aramaic liturgical formula: Maranatha ('Our Lord, come!' or 'The Lord has come'). An Aramaic invocation prayer directed to Jesus preserved in a Greek letter — evidence of earliest-stratum Christ-devotion among Aramaic-speaking Jewish Christians.

**Notes:** Hurtado's evidence: the preservation of the Aramaic form in a Greek-language letter indicates this was a well-known, fixed liturgical formula predating Paul's letter. Aramaic-speaking Jewish Christians were praying to Jesus as 'our Lord' — the earliest datable evidence of Christ-worship as a regular devotional practice.

### 'Who can forgive sins but God alone?' — divine prerogative exercised by Jesus

> Why doth this [man] thus speak blasphemies? who can forgive sins but God only?

**Context:** Mark 2 — the paralytic healing. The scribes' reaction reveals their understanding: forgiveness of sins is God's exclusive prerogative. Jesus exercises this prerogative and confirms it with a physical miracle.

**Notes:** Prior study trin-05 identified the identical phrase 'ei mē heis ho Theos' in Mark 2:7 (forgiveness prerogative) and Mark 10:18 (goodness claim) as a Markan literary device. Both are implicit deity claims: Jesus exercises what only God can do.

### Glorified Christ uses 'my God' while holding YHWH titles

> Him that overcometh will I make a pillar in the temple of my God, and he shall go no more out: and I will write upon him the name of my God, and the name of the city of my God, [which is] new Jerusalem, which cometh down out of heaven from my God: and [I will write upon him] my new name.

**Context:** Revelation 3 — message to Philadelphia. The glorified Christ (who has already been identified as 'the First and the Last' in 1:17 and 'the Alpha and Omega') uses 'my God' four times. This creates an exegetical tension: the one who bears YHWH's exclusive titles also addresses the Father as 'my God.'

**Notes:** This is a genuine exegetical challenge and must be addressed honestly. The resolution: the 'my God' language reflects the incarnational and mediatorial relationship. Just as the eternal Son who 'was God' (John 1:1) also said 'my God' in John 20:17 during his earthly ministry, the glorified Christ in Revelation maintains the relational language of the Father-Son dynamic. The Psa 45:6-7 two-elohim pattern (addressed as God while having 'God, your God') provides the OT template. Prior study trin-05 established: 'my God' language is compatible with deity, not a denial of it.

### Worship of the One on the throne alone — baseline for co-worship

> And the four beasts had each of them six wings about [him]; and [they were] full of eyes within: and they rest not day and night, saying, Holy, holy, holy, Lord God Almighty, which was, and is, and is to come. And when those beasts give glory and honour and thanks to him that sat on the throne, who liveth for ever and ever, The four and twenty elders fall down before him that sat on the throne, and worship him that liveth for ever and ever, and cast their crowns before the throne, saying, Thou art worthy, O Lord, to receive glory and honour and power: for thou hast created all things, and for thy pleasure they are and were created.

**Context:** Revelation 4 — the heavenly throne scene. Before the Lamb enters in chapter 5, worship is directed solely to the One on the throne. This establishes the baseline: proper heavenly worship goes to God alone. The progression in chapter 5 — where the Lamb joins and receives identical worship — is therefore structurally significant.

**Notes:** The four living creatures echo Isa 6:3's trisagion. The twenty-four elders worship the creator. This is monotheistic worship in its purest form — directed to the One on the throne. When Rev 5:8 then shows the Lamb receiving prostration and 5:13 shows co-worship, the reader knows the Lamb has crossed onto the divine side of the worship boundary.

### 'The Lord IS that Spirit' — Spirit identified with YHWH

> Now the Lord is that Spirit: and where the Spirit of the Lord [is], there [is] liberty.

**Context:** 2 Corinthians 3 — Paul discusses the veil over Moses' face. 'The Lord (kyrios) is the Spirit (to pneuma)' — a direct identification. Prior study trin-06 established this as one of three independent Spirit-equals-YHWH identifications.

**Notes:** The Spirit's identification with YHWH broadens the monotheism question beyond binitarian to trinitarian. Three independent Spirit-YHWH identifications: Acts 28:25 (Spirit speaks what Isaiah attributed to YHWH), Heb 3:7 (Spirit speaks what Psa 95 attributed to YHWH), 2 Cor 3:17 (the Lord IS the Spirit).

### Spirit speaks what Isaiah attributed to YHWH

> And when they agreed not among themselves, they departed, after that Paul had spoken one word, Well spake the Holy Ghost by Esaias the prophet unto our fathers,

**Context:** Paul's final address in Acts. Paul attributes Isaiah 6:9-10 (spoken by YHWH in the OT) to the Holy Spirit. This Spirit-YHWH identification is independent of 2 Cor 3:17 and Heb 3:7.

**Notes:** In Isa 6:8-10, YHWH himself speaks: 'Go, and tell this people...' Paul says the Holy Spirit spoke these words through Isaiah. The identification is implicit but clear: what YHWH said = what the Holy Spirit said.

### Spirit speaks what Psalm 95 attributed to YHWH

> Wherefore (as the Holy Ghost saith, To day if ye will hear his voice, Harden not your hearts, as in the provocation, in the day of temptation in the wilderness: When your fathers tempted me, proved me, and saw my works forty years. Wherefore I was grieved with that generation, and said, They do alway err in [their] heart; and they have not known my ways. So I sware in my wrath, They shall not enter into my rest.)

**Context:** Hebrews 3 — the author attributes Psalm 95:7-11 (where the 'I' who swore in wrath is YHWH) to the Holy Spirit. A third independent Spirit-YHWH identification.

**Notes:** Psa 95:7-11 presents YHWH speaking in first person ('I was grieved,' 'I sware in my wrath'). The Hebrews author says the Holy Spirit is the one who says these words. Combined with Acts 28:25 and 2 Cor 3:17, this constitutes a three-author witness to Spirit-YHWH identification.

### Monos+doxology: 'the only wise God' — exclusion reductio

> Now unto the King eternal, immortal, invisible, the only wise God, [be] honour and glory for ever and ever. Amen.

**Context:** Pauline doxology. 'The only wise God' — if monos absolutely excludes Christ, Christ lacks wisdom. But Col 2:3 says 'in whom are hid ALL the treasures of wisdom and knowledge.'

**Notes:** Part of the monos+theos reductio pattern. Each monos doxology would exclude Christ from an attribute the NT explicitly ascribes to him: wisdom (Col 2:3), immortality (Rev 1:18), sovereignty (Matt 28:18, Rev 19:16). The pattern demonstrates that monos affirms the Father's unique deity without functioning as absolute Christological exclusion.

### Ek/dia/eis applied to the Father alone — collapses hierarchy argument

> For of him, and through him, and to him, [are] all things: to whom [be] glory for ever. Amen.

**Context:** Romans 11 — Paul's doxology after discussing Israel's destiny. Ek autou (from him) + di autou (through him) + eis auton (to him) = all three prepositions applied to ONE person (the Father).

**Notes:** This is decisive against Perriman's argument that ek/dia in 1 Cor 8:6 marks an ontological hierarchy (ek = source = superior, dia = agency = inferior). Paul uses both ek and dia of the Father in this verse, demonstrating they do not encode an ontological distinction. The prepositions describe functional roles, not hierarchical ranking.

### 'God was manifest in the flesh' — incarnation confession

> And without controversy great is the mystery of godliness: God was manifest in the flesh, justified in the Spirit, seen of angels, preached unto the Gentiles, believed on in the world, received up into glory.

**Context:** An early Christian hymn/confession. The TR reads 'God' (theos); some MSS read 'who' (hos). Either way, the subject who was 'manifest in the flesh' and 'received up into glory' is a divine figure who entered human existence.

**Notes:** The textual variant (theos vs. hos) does not substantially affect the theology — the six-line confession describes a being who pre-existed in a non-fleshly state, was manifested in flesh, and was received back into glory. This is an incarnation narrative regardless of whether 'God' or 'who' is the original reading.

### Akatalytos — power of indestructible life

> Who is made, not after the law of a carnal commandment, but after the power of an endless life.

**Context:** Hebrews 7 — Christ's priesthood after the order of Melchizedek. The word akatalytos (hapax, 'indestructible/endless') describes a quality of life that cannot be destroyed — beyond what any creature possesses.

**Notes:** Prior study trin-07 analyzed this: akatalytos (G179, hapax) = 'indestructible.' This predicate goes beyond creaturely existence. Combined with aparabatos (G531, hapax in Heb 7:24 = 'untransferable/permanent') — Christ's priesthood and life possess inherent qualities that no finite being can claim.

### Divine triad in atonement: Christ + eternal Spirit + God

> How much more shall the blood of Christ, who through the eternal Spirit offered himself without spot to God, purge your conscience from dead works to serve the living God?

**Context:** Hebrews 9 — the superiority of Christ's sacrifice. The triad: Christ offers himself, through the eternal Spirit, to God. All three persons of the Godhead are active in the atonement.

**Notes:** The triadic structure is not formulaic (like Matt 28:19) but organic — three divine persons cooperating in the central act of redemption. The Spirit is called 'eternal' (aiōniou), an attribute of deity.

### Triadic benediction: grace of Christ + love of God + fellowship of Spirit

> The grace of the Lord Jesus Christ, and the love of God, and the communion of the Holy Ghost, [be] with you all. Amen.

**Context:** Paul's closing benediction — the most explicit triadic formula in the Pauline letters. Three persons are coordinated as co-sources of distinct divine blessings.

**Notes:** The coordination of three persons as co-sources of blessing implies shared divine identity. No creature could be coordinated with God as a co-source of grace/love/fellowship in a liturgical formula.

### Another Comforter — Spirit as distinct divine person

> And I will pray the Father, and he shall give you another Comforter, that he may abide with you for ever; [Even] the Spirit of truth; whom the world cannot receive, because it seeth him not, neither knoweth him: but ye know him; for he dwelleth with you, and shall be in you.

**Context:** Upper Room — Jesus promises the Spirit. 'Another' (allon) Comforter — another of the same kind (not heteron/different kind), distinct from Jesus yet performing the same role. The Spirit is a 'he' (ekeinos, masculine, despite pneuma being neuter).

**Notes:** The masculine pronoun ekeinos overriding the neuter pneuma is significant — it indicates personal agency, not impersonal force. The Spirit is 'another Comforter' of the same kind as Jesus — another divine person performing the same paraklēsis ministry.

### Same Spirit, same Lord, same God — triadic operational unity

> Now there are diversities of gifts, but the same Spirit. And there are differences of administrations, but the same Lord. And there are diversities of operations, but it is the same God which worketh all in all.

**Context:** 1 Corinthians 12 — spiritual gifts. Paul coordinates three persons as the source of gifts/service/working: Spirit, Lord (Jesus), God (Father). The same pattern as 2 Cor 13:14 — triadic coordination of divine persons.

**Notes:** The 'same...same...same' (to auto) repetition emphasizes unity of source across three distinct persons. The Spirit gives gifts, the Lord assigns service, God produces workings — three persons, one divine operation. No creature could be coordinated with God in this way.

---

### Monos+doxology: 'the only wise God our Savior'

> To the only wise God our Saviour, [be] glory and majesty, dominion and power, both now and ever. Amen.

**Context:** Jude's closing doxology. 'The only wise God our Savior' — if monos absolutely excludes Christ, Christ is excluded from being Savior. But the NT repeatedly calls Christ 'Savior' (Tit 2:13, 2 Pet 1:1, Phil 3:20).

**Notes:** This is the third monos+theos doxology (alongside 1 Tim 1:17 and 1 Tim 6:15-16). The reductio is complete: monos excludes Christ from wisdom (1 Tim 1:17), immortality/sovereignty (1 Tim 6:15-16), and being Savior (Jude 1:25) — all of which the NT explicitly attributes to Christ. Therefore monos does not function as absolute Christological exclusion.

### All fullness pleased to dwell in Christ — divine fullness language

> For it pleased [the Father] that in him should all fulness dwell;

**Context:** Colossians 1 — the Christ hymn. Pleroma (fullness) dwells in Christ — this anticipates the fuller statement in Col 2:9 where the fullness is identified as the fullness of theotētos (essential deity).

**Notes:** Word study G4138-pleroma: 17 occurrences. The 'divine fullness dwelling in Christ' category includes Col 1:19, Col 2:9, Eph 1:23, Eph 3:19. This is not merely an attribute Christ possesses but the sum total of deity itself inhabiting him permanently.

### Wisdom personified at creation — background for Logos/Wisdom Christology

> The LORD possessed me in the beginning of his way, before his works of old. I was set up from everlasting, from the beginning, or ever the earth was. When [there were] no depths, I was brought forth; when [there were] no fountains abounding with water. Before the mountains were settled, before the hills was I brought forth: While as yet he had not made the earth, nor the fields, nor the highest part of the dust of the world. When he prepared the heavens, I [was] there: when he set a compass upon the face of the depth: When he established the clouds above: when he strengthened the fountains of the deep: When he gave to the sea his decree, that the waters should not pass his commandment: when he appointed the foundations of the earth: Then I was by him, [as] one brought up [with him]: and I was daily [his] delight, rejoicing always before him; Rejoicing in the habitable part of his earth; and my delights [were] with the sons of men.

**Context:** Proverbs 8 — Wisdom personified as present at creation. The NT applies this Wisdom-at-creation imagery to Christ: John 1:1-3 (Logos with God, creating all things), 1 Cor 1:24 (Christ the wisdom of God), Col 1:15-17 (firstborn, creator), Heb 1:1-4 (creator, radiance).

**Notes:** Davidson (JATS 2006) argues this goes beyond personification to hypostasis — an actual divine being. The Hebrew qanah (8:22) can mean 'possessed' or 'created' — the NT's identification of Christ with Wisdom favors 'possessed' (eternal attribute of God) over 'created.' The verb 'brought forth' (chul, 8:24-25) may describe eternal generation rather than creation.

### Isaiah's throne vision — YHWH of hosts identified with Christ in John 12:41

> In the year that king Uzziah died I saw also the Lord sitting upon a throne, high and lifted up, and his train filled the temple. Above it stood the seraphims: each one had six wings; with twain he covered his face, and with twain he covered his feet, and with twain he did fly. And one cried unto another, and said, Holy, holy, holy, [is] the LORD of hosts: the whole earth [is] full of his glory.

**Context:** Isaiah 6 — the throne room theophany. The seraphim's trisagion addresses YHWH of hosts. John 12:41 identifies the one Isaiah saw as Christ: 'These things said Isaiah when he saw HIS [Christ's] glory.'

**Notes:** This is a critical Johannine YHWH-identification independent of Paul's transfers. The one enthroned in Isaiah's vision — YHWH of hosts, worshipped with the trisagion — is identified as Christ by John. Rev 4:8 then echoes this same trisagion in the heavenly worship scene.

### 'One God and one mediator' — mediatorial role within shared identity

> For [there is] one God, and one mediator between God and men, the man Christ Jesus;

**Context:** 1 Timothy 2 — Paul affirms monotheism and Christ's mediatorial role. The phrase 'one God...one mediator' echoes the 'one God...one Lord' pattern of 1 Cor 8:6. The mediator is described as 'the man Christ Jesus' — emphasizing his humanity, not denying his deity.

**Notes:** The mediatorial role requires both divine and human nature: a mediator 'between God and men' must be able to represent both parties. 'The man Christ Jesus' describes the incarnate mediator's qualification to represent humanity. This does not deny his deity any more than Phil 2:7's 'form of a servant' denies his prior 'form of God.'

### Peter refuses worship — creature refusal pattern

> And as Peter was coming in, Cornelius met him, and fell down at his feet, and worshipped [him]. But Peter took him up, saying, Stand up; I myself also am a man.

**Context:** Cornelius meets Peter. Peter immediately refuses prostration with the explanation 'I myself also am a man' — establishing the principle that human servants of God refuse worship.

**Notes:** Peter's refusal ('I myself also am a man') parallels the angels' refusal in Rev 19:10, 22:8-9 ('I am thy fellowservant'). Both human and angelic creatures refuse proskyneo. Jesus never once refuses it.

### Paul and Barnabas refuse worship — creature refusal pattern

> And when the people saw what Paul had done, they lifted up their voices, saying in the speech of Lycaonia, The gods are come down to us in the likeness of men. And they called Barnabas, Jupiter; and Paul, Mercurius, because he was the chief speaker. Then the priest of Jupiter, which was before their city, brought oxen and garlands unto the gates, and would have done sacrifice with the people. [Which] when the apostles, Barnabas and Paul, heard [of], they rent their clothes, and ran in among the people, crying out, And saying, Sirs, why do ye these things? We also are men of like passions with you, and preach unto you that ye should turn from these vanities unto the living God, which made heaven, and earth, and the sea, and all things that are therein:

**Context:** Lystra — after healing a lame man, the crowd tries to worship Paul and Barnabas as gods. They tear their clothes in horror and redirect worship to 'the living God.'

**Notes:** The most dramatic creature refusal: Paul and Barnabas tear their clothes (a sign of extreme distress) when worship is attempted. They redirect: worship belongs to God alone. The contrast with Jesus, who accepts worship without any such protest, is stark.

### Disciples worship Jesus after walking on water

> Then they that were in the ship came and worshipped him, saying, Of a truth thou art the Son of God.

**Context:** After Jesus walks on water and Peter's failed attempt. The disciples worship Jesus with the confession 'thou art the Son of God.' No correction or rebuke.

**Notes:** Worship acceptance scene #1 in the compilation. No rebuke, no 'stand up, I am a man,' no 'worship God!' Instead, the worship is accepted and the confession affirmed.

### Women worship the risen Jesus

> And as they went to tell his disciples, behold, Jesus met them, saying, All hail. And they came and held him by the feet, and worshipped him.

**Context:** Post-resurrection — women at the empty tomb encounter the risen Jesus and worship him. Again, no rebuke.

**Notes:** Worship acceptance scene in a post-resurrection context. The risen Christ accepts worship — consistent with Thomas's confession in John 20:28.

### Disciples worship the risen Jesus on the mountain

> And when they saw him, they worshipped him: but some doubted.

**Context:** Great Commission context — the disciples worship the risen Jesus. This is immediately followed by the Trinitarian baptismal formula (28:19) and the all-authority claim (28:18).

**Notes:** Worship acceptance scene followed immediately by 'All authority in heaven and earth has been given to me' (v.18) — claiming universal sovereignty — and 'baptizing them in the name of the Father, and of the Son, and of the Holy Spirit' (v.19) — placing himself within the divine name.

### Healed blind man worships Jesus

> And he said, Lord, I believe. And he worshipped him.

**Context:** John 9 — the man born blind, after Jesus reveals himself as the Son of Man, responds with belief and worship. Jesus accepts without correction.

**Notes:** Worship acceptance scene in John's Gospel — the healed man's proskyneo is accepted. This is particularly significant because John also records the angel refusal scenes in Revelation — the same author understands the distinction between proper and improper worship.
