# When Jesus says 'the only true God' (John 17:3) and Paul says 'one God, the Father' (1 Corinthians 8:6), do these passages exclude Jesus from deity? Examine the full chapter context of John 17 and the Shema background of 1 Corinthians 8:6.

## Question
When Jesus says 'the only true God' (John 17:3) and Paul says 'one God, the Father' (1 Corinthians 8:6), do these passages exclude Jesus from deity? Examine the full chapter context of John 17 and the Shema background of 1 Corinthians 8:6.

## Summary Answer
Neither John 17:3 nor 1 Corinthians 8:6 excludes Jesus from deity. John 17:3's 'only true God' (monos alethinos theos) contrasts the genuine God with false gods — the same contrastive pattern found in 1 Thessalonians 1:9, 1 Timothy 1:17, 1 Timothy 6:15-16, and 1 John 5:20, where the same author applies 'the true God' language to Jesus. The full chapter context of John 17 contains claims only deity could make: pre-existent glory (v.5), mutual ownership with the Father (v.10), and pre-creation love (v.24). Paul's 1 Corinthians 8:6 distributes the Shema's heis between theos (Father) and kyrios (Jesus Christ), placing Jesus within — not outside — Israel's foundational monotheistic confession, while the ek/dia preposition distinction collapses as an ontological hierarchy argument because Paul uses both prepositions of the Father alone in Rom 11:36.

> **Isolated crux text** John 17:3	And this is life eternal, that they might know thee the only true God, and Jesus Christ, whom thou hast sent.

> **The Johannine prologue** John 1:1	In the beginning was the Word, and the Word was with God, and the Word was God.

> **Paul calling Christ theos** Romans 9:5	Whose [are] the fathers, and of whom as concerning the flesh Christ [came], who is over all, God blessed for ever. Amen.

> **Paul's Christological reformulation of the Shema** 1 Corinthians 8:4	As concerning therefore the eating of those things that are offered in sacrifice unto idols, we know that an idol [is] nothing in the world, and that [there is] none other God but one.

> **Granville Sharp rule** Titus 2:13	Looking for that blessed hope, and the glorious appearing of the great God and our Saviour Jesus Christ;

> **Psalm 102:25-27** Hebrews 1:10	And, Thou, Lord, in the beginning hast laid the foundation of the earth; and the heavens are the works of thine hands:

> **Thomas's confession** John 20:28	And Thomas answered and said unto him, My Lord and my God.

## Analysis

### Main Thesis
Neither John 17:3 nor 1 Corinthians 8:6 excludes Jesus from deity. John 17:3's 'only true God' contrasts the genuine God with false gods (the same contrastive function found across every NT monos+theos doxology), while the same prayer's pre-existent glory claim, mutual ownership language, and pre-creation love make an exclusionary reading self-defeating. Paul's 1 Corinthians 8:6 distributes the Shema's vocabulary between heis theos (Father) and heis kyrios (Jesus Christ), placing Jesus within Israel's monotheistic confession. The ek/dia preposition distinction collapses as an ontological hierarchy argument because Paul uses both prepositions of the Father in Rom 11:36, and the kyrios derives from YHWH (confirmed by the application of YHWH-exclusive OT texts to Jesus), not from Psalm 110:1's adon.

### Evidence

- Every NT monos+theos doxology (1 Tim 1:17, 6:15-16, Jude 1:25, Rom 16:27) would exclude Christ from immortality, sovereignty, and wisdom if monos functioned as an absolute exclusion — the NT's own testimony about Christ contradicts this reading
- The alethinos+theos pairing in 1 Thessalonians 1:9 explicitly contrasts the true God with idols ('turned from idols to serve the living and true God'), establishing the semantic template for the same pairing in John 17:3
- John 17:5's imperfect eichon (continuous past possession of glory) and para soi (personal proximity with the Father) establish that the speaker of 'the only true God' personally possessed shared divine glory before creation
- The dual accusative structure of John 17:3 — ginoskosi governing both se ton monon alethinon theon and hon apesteilas Iesoun Christon as coordinate objects — makes the Father and Jesus co-constitutive of eternal life, a pairing that would be idolatrous if Jesus were merely a creature
- Paul's 1 Corinthians 8:6 maps every key word of the Shema (Deut 6:4 LXX: kyrios, theos, heis) into a confession that distributes the 'one' between Father (theos) and Christ (kyrios)
- Rom 11:36 (ex autou kai di autou kai eis auton ta panta) applies both ek and dia to the Father, collapsing the ek/dia ontological hierarchy argument for 1 Corinthians 8:6
- Three NT passages apply YHWH-exclusive OT texts to Jesus — Phil 2:10-11 (Isa 45:23), Rom 10:13 (Joel 2:32), Heb 1:10 (Ps 102:25) — demonstrating that the kyrios of 1 Corinthians 8:6 derives from YHWH, not from Psalm 110:1's adon
- Isaiah 44:24 declares YHWH created alone (lebaddi), yet four independent NT witnesses (John 1:3, 1 Cor 8:6, Col 1:16, Heb 1:2) assign creation to Christ — if YHWH created alone and Christ is creation's agent, Christ must be within YHWH
- Thomas's confession 'my Lord and my God' (John 20:28, articular ho theos mou addressed directly to Jesus) is accepted by Jesus and presented by the same author as the climactic statement of genuine faith — the same author who recorded 'the only true God' in 17:3

### Word Studies
The vocabulary network provides precision that the proof-text approach cannot. Monos (G3441, 47 occurrences) functions doxologically across its NT uses with theos — praising divine uniqueness against false gods rather than cataloguing who is excluded from deity. Alethinos (G228, 27 occurrences, 80% Johannine) consistently contrasts the genuine with the counterfeit, and the alethinos+theos pairing in 1 Thessalonians 1:9 explicitly identifies the contrast as God vs. idols. Together, monos alethinos theos means 'the only genuine God as opposed to counterfeits' — the same function as 'the living and true God' in 1 Thessalonians 1:9. Theos (G2316, 1343 occurrences) primarily designates the Father but is directly applied to Jesus in at least five independent passages (John 1:1, 20:28, Rom 9:5, Titus 2:13, 2 Pet 1:1, Heb 1:8), demonstrating that 'one God, the Father' never precluded calling Jesus theos. Kyrios (G2962, 748 occurrences) renders YHWH in the LXX over 5,107 times, and the NT applies YHWH-kyrios texts to Jesus (Isa 45:23 to Phil 2:10-11, Joel 2:32 to Rom 10:13, Ps 102:25 to Heb 1:10), confirming that the kyrios of 1 Corinthians 8:6 derives from the Shema's YHWH, not from Psalm 110:1's adon. Heis (G1520, 353 occurrences) is distributed twice in 1 Corinthians 8:6 — once for theos (Father) and once for kyrios (Christ) — distributing the Shema's oneness across two persons within a single divine identity. Echad (H259, 980 occurrences) in the Shema is referent-neutral, permitting composite unity (Gen 2:24 'one flesh'), while yachid (H3173) is never applied to YHWH in the entire Hebrew Bible. Dia (G1223, 662 occurrences) marks Christ's creative agency in 1 Corinthians 8:6, but Paul uses dia of the Father in Romans eleven, verse thirty-six. Ek (G1537, 901 occurrences) marks the Father as source — yet the prepositions are interchangeable between Father and Son, collapsing any ontological hierarchy. Soter (G4990, 24 occurrences) is applied to both God and Jesus, and since YHWH declares 'beside me there is no saviour' (Isa 43:11), the dual application constitutes an implicit deity claim for Christ. Doxa (G1391, 168 occurrences) in John 17:5 describes the glory the Son continuously possessed (imperfect eichon) alongside the Father before creation — a claim no creature could make. The eimi/ginomai distinction (G1510/G1096) places the Logos on the uncreated side: the Word 'was' (en, continuous existence) while creation 'came into being' (egeneto, point-in-time creation).

## Difficult Passages

### 1 Corinthians 15:24-28 — Eschatological submission

**Difficulty:** Paul says the Son will be 'subjected' (hupotagesetai, G5293, future passive indicative) to the Father when all enemies are destroyed and the kingdom is delivered up. Perriman cites this as incompatible with full deity — if the Son must be subjected to the Father in the eschaton, how can the Son be fully included within the divine identity? A being truly equal with God would not need to be subjected.

**Resolution:** The future tense of hupotagesetai is decisive: it marks a CHANGE of state — the Son is not currently in this subjected condition but will transition into it after the mediatorial mission is complete. The hotan...tote construction (hotan de hupotagē auto ta panta, tote kai autos ho huios hupotagesetai — 'when all things are subjected to him, then the Son himself will be subjected') is temporal and sequential. This parallels the Philippians 2:5-11 kenosis pattern in reverse: the kenosis describes equality with God (morphe theou, isa theo) followed by voluntary self-emptying and incarnational subordination, followed by hyper-exaltation. First Corinthians 15:24-28 describes the final phase of the mediatorial mission: after the Son destroys all enemies including death (v.26), he hands the kingdom to the Father (v.24), and the eschatological purpose clause (hina e ho theos panta en pasin, 'that God may be all in all') describes the restoration of direct divine rule without the mediatorial structure. John 17:5's request for restoration of pre-incarnation glory ('glorify me with the glory which I had with thee before the world was') demonstrates the same movement: from incarnational mission back toward the pre-incarnation state. Functional submission within an economic role does not negate ontological equality — a king who completes a military campaign and returns authority to the governing council does not cease to be who he was before the campaign began.

### John 17:3 — 'The only true God' as potential exclusion of the Son

**Difficulty:** The surface grammar of John 17:3 appears to identify the Father alone as 'the only true God' (se ton monon alethinon theon) and to distinguish Jesus Christ from that identification by the kai conjunction and the subordinate clause 'whom thou hast sent' (hon apesteilas). If the Father is 'the only true God,' how can the Son also be true God? The sending language (apesteilas) further implies subordination — the sent one is under the authority of the sender.

**Resolution:** Three converging lines of evidence resolve this. First, the monos+theos doxological pattern across the NT (1 Tim 1:17, 1 Tim 6:15-16, Jude 1:25, Rom 16:27) demonstrates that monos in these contexts praises God's unique supremacy in contrast with false gods without excluding the Son — if it did, Christ could not be immortal (Rev 1:18), could not be King of kings (Rev 19:16), and could not receive equal honor (John 5:23). Second, the alethinos (G228) semantic field consistently contrasts the genuine with the counterfeit (John 6:32 genuine bread vs. manna, John 1:9 true light vs. darkness, 1 Thess 1:9 true God vs. idols) — 'the only true God' means 'the only genuine God as opposed to counterfeit deities,' not 'the only divine being.' Third, the immediate chapter context makes an exclusionary reading self-defeating: the same prayer contains the pre-existent glory claim (v.5, imperfect eichon — continuous past possession), mutual ownership claim (v.10, 'thine are mine'), divine unity as the model for believer unity (vv.21-22), and pre-creation love (v.24, pro kataboles kosmou). The grammatical structure also supports this: ginoskosi (present active subjunctive) governs TWO accusative objects connected by kai — both the Father and Jesus Christ are coordinate objects of the knowing that constitutes eternal life. Pairing knowledge of a creature with knowledge of God as co-constitutive of eternal life would be idolatrous in any Jewish framework.

### 1 Corinthians 8:6 — Does the Shema distribution divide God from Lord?

**Difficulty:** Paul's confession — 'one God, the Father... one Lord, Jesus Christ' — assigns different titles to different persons. If the Father is 'God' and Jesus is 'Lord,' does this create two categories of being? Perriman argues the kyrios title for Jesus derives from Psalm 110:1's adon (exalted human king) rather than from YHWH, and that the ek/dia preposition distinction (source vs. agent) establishes ontological hierarchy. Paul's failure to quote the Shema verbatim in 8:4 (dropping kyrios) is cited as evidence that the Shema is not the background for 8:6.

**Resolution:** The Shema (Deut 6:4 LXX: kyrios ho theos hemon kyrios heis estin) contains BOTH heis theos AND heis kyrios — every key word of Paul's confession appears in the Shema. Paul distributes the Shema's vocabulary between the Father (heis theos) and Christ (heis kyrios), placing Jesus within the monotheistic framework rather than alongside it as an additional figure. The ek/dia ontological hierarchy argument collapses because Paul himself uses BOTH ek AND dia of the Father in Rom 11:36 (ex autou kai di autou kai eis auton ta panta). Hebrews 2:10 likewise uses dia of God. If dia marks ontological inferiority, then the Father is ontologically inferior to himself when Paul writes di autou in Rom 11:36 — which is absurd. The Psalm 110:1 derivation fails because the NT applies to Jesus OT texts where kyrios unambiguously means YHWH: Philippians 2:10-11 applies Isaiah 45:23 (YHWH's exclusive worship-oath), Romans 10:13 applies Joel 2:32 ('whosoever calls on the name of YHWH'), and Hebrews 1:10 applies Psalm 102:25 (a prayer to YHWH as creator). Paul would not apply a YHWH-exclusive worship-oath to a merely human adon. Malachi 2:10 as an alternative background fails because it lacks the kyrios element entirely, addresses covenant faithfulness rather than cosmic monotheism, and does not match the anti-polytheistic context of 1 Corinthians 8:4-5.

### Perriman's Malachi 2:10 alternative and the kyrios derivation question

**Difficulty:** Perriman argues that 1 Corinthians 8:6 derives from Malachi 2:10 ('Have we not all one father? Hath not one God created us?') rather than from the Shema, because Malachi 2:10 has both 'one father' and 'one God' language connected to creation — matching Paul's 'one God, the Father, of whom are all things.' If Malachi 2:10 is the background, the Christological-Shema thesis collapses and kyrios for Jesus derives from Psalm 110:1's adon (an exalted human king), not from YHWH.

**Resolution:** The comparison between Malachi 2:10 and 1 Corinthians 8:6 reveals that the Malachi background is substantially weaker. First, Malachi 2:10 lacks the kyrios element entirely — it has av (father) and el echad (one God) but not 'Lord.' Paul's confession has BOTH heis theos AND heis kyrios, matching the Shema's dual terminology (kyrios ho theos hemon kyrios heis). Second, Malachi's context is covenant faithfulness ('why do we deal treacherously?'), not cosmic monotheism — Paul's context is anti-polytheistic ('an idol is nothing in the world,' 'gods many and lords many' in 8:4-5). Third, Malachi addresses Israel's communal identity ('have we not all one father?'), while Paul addresses the fundamental ontological structure of reality ('all things' from one God and 'through' one Lord). Fourth, the lexical match is decisive: the Shema (Deut 6:4 LXX) contains kyrios + theos + heis — every key word reappearing in 1 Corinthians 8:6. Malachi contains only av and el echad. The Shema-derivation provides a far more comprehensive lexical and contextual match. Furthermore, the kyrios derivation from Psalm 110:1 fails the YHWH-text application test: Philippians 2:10-11 applies Isaiah 45:23 (YHWH's exclusive oath) to Jesus, Romans 10:13 applies Joel 2:32 (calling on YHWH's name) to Christ, and Hebrews 1:10 applies Psalm 102:25 (YHWH as creator) to the Son. These are unambiguous YHWH texts, not Psalm 110:1.

### Romans 9:5 — Punctuation debate: Christ as 'God blessed forever' or independent doxology?

**Difficulty:** Romans 9:5 is one of the most debated punctuation questions in NT textual criticism. The Greek ho on epi panton theos eulogetos eis tous aionas ('the one being over all, God blessed unto the ages') can be punctuated either as a continuation of the Christ-description ('Christ, who is over all, God blessed forever') or as an independent doxology after a full stop ('Christ came. God, who is over all, be blessed forever'). If the latter, Paul does not call Christ theos here.

**Resolution:** The Christ-referent reading has stronger grammatical support for multiple reasons. First, the present participle ho on ('the one being') naturally continues the description of its antecedent, Christ. Greek participles connect to their grammatical subject unless a clear break intervenes — and no conjunction, relative pronoun, or discourse marker signals a break here. Second, Paul never writes asyndetic independent doxologies. Every Pauline doxology (Romans 1:25, 11:36, 16:27, Galatians 1:5, Ephesians 3:21, Philippians 4:20, 1 Timothy 1:17, 2 Timothy 4:18) connects to its context with a relative pronoun (hos) or conjunction. If Romans 9:5b were an independent doxology, it would be the sole Pauline exception. Third, in independent doxologies, eulogetos ('blessed') normally precedes theos (as in 2 Corinthians 1:3, Ephesians 1:3: eulogetos ho theos), but here it follows — suggesting this is not a doxological formula but a descriptive clause. Fourth, the kata sarka ('according to the flesh') in 9:5a implies a contrasting non-flesh description — Christ came according to the flesh AND is something more, which the participial clause then identifies: he is theos eulogetos, God blessed forever. Fifth, the context of Romans 9:1-5 lists Israel's accumulated privileges, building to a climax — the climax is not 'Christ came from Israel, period' but 'Christ came from Israel and he is God blessed forever.'

### 1 John 5:20 — Does houtos ('this one') refer to Jesus Christ or to the Father?

**Difficulty:** First John 5:20 reads: 'we are in him that is true, even in his Son Jesus Christ. This is the true God, and eternal life' (houtos estin ho alethinos theos kai zoe aionios). The demonstrative pronoun houtos ('this one') could refer either to 'his Son Jesus Christ' (the nearest grammatical antecedent) or to 'him that is true' (the Father, the broader contextual subject). If houtos refers to the Father, this verse does not call Jesus 'the true God' and loses its force as a parallel to John 17:3.

**Resolution:** The Jesus-Christ referent has stronger grammatical and contextual support. Grammatically, houtos ('this one,' nominative singular masculine) typically refers to the nearest antecedent in Johannine usage. The nearest antecedent is 'his Son Jesus Christ' (en to huio autou Iesou Christo). Contextually, the phrase 'the true God and eternal life' (ho alethinos theos kai zoe aionios) echoes John's characteristic description of Jesus: John 14:6 ('I am the way, the truth, and the life'), John 11:25 ('I am the resurrection, and the life'), and John 1:4 ('In him was life'). The connection between 'the true God' and 'eternal life' in a single predicate mirrors John 17:3, where eternal life consists in knowing the Father AND Jesus Christ. If houtos refers to Jesus, then the same author who wrote 'the only true God' (17:3) applies 'the true God' language to Jesus — using the identical vocabulary (alethinos + theos + zoe aionios). Even if houtos refers to the Father, the shared vocabulary between 17:3 and 1 John 5:20 demonstrates that John uses alethinos theos language within a theological framework where the Son is not excluded from deity. The verse immediately following (5:21, 'Little children, keep yourselves from idols') confirms the contrastive function: 'the true God' is contrasted with idols, not with the Son.

## Conclusion

This study demonstrates that neither John 17:3 nor 1 Corinthians 8:6 excludes Jesus from deity when examined in their full literary, canonical, and linguistic contexts. John 17:3's 'only true God' operates within the same prayer where Jesus claims continuously possessed pre-existent glory (v.5), mutual ownership of everything the Father possesses (v.10), status as the model for all unity (vv.21-22), and a love relationship with the Father 'before the foundation of the world' (v.24). The same author who records this verse opens his Gospel with 'the Word was God' (1:1), records Thomas confessing 'my Lord and my God' (20:28), and writes 'this is the true God and eternal life' (1 John 5:20) with identical vocabulary and Jesus as the nearest antecedent. Paul's 1 Corinthians 8:6 does not separate God from Lord into two ontological categories but distributes the Shema's own vocabulary — heis theos and heis kyrios — between the Father and Jesus Christ, expanding Israel's monotheistic confession to include the Son within it. The ek/dia preposition argument for ontological hierarchy collapses because Paul applies both prepositions to the Father in Rom 11:36, and Colossians 1:16 applies the Father's entire preposition set (en, dia, eis) to Christ. The kyrios title for Jesus derives from YHWH, as demonstrated by three passages that apply YHWH-exclusive OT texts to Jesus: Isaiah 45:23 to Philippians 2:10-11, Joel 2:32 to Romans 10:13, and Psalm 102:25-27 to Hebrews 1:10-12. The creation paradox seals the argument: YHWH declares he created alone (Isaiah 44:24, lebaddi), yet four independent NT witnesses assign creation to Christ — meaning Christ must be within YHWH, not alongside him. The strongest counter-arguments — Perriman's Malachi 2:10 background (which lacks the kyrios element), the ek/dia hierarchy (which Paul himself collapses), and the 1 Corinthians 15:24-28 eschatological submission (which the Philippians 2 kenosis framework explains as the final phase of a voluntary mission, not evidence of eternal subordination) — have been examined and found insufficient to overturn the convergent biblical evidence. What remains genuinely unresolved is the precise mechanics of how two persons share a single divine identity without either collapsing into modalism or splitting into ditheism — the NT preserves the paradox of unity-in-distinction without explaining it, using language that holds both truths simultaneously: 'I and my Father are one' (John 10:30, neuter hen — unity of essence, not numerical identity) and 'the Father is greater than I' (John 14:28, meizon — positional rank during the incarnation, not ontological superiority). The text refuses to resolve this tension by eliminating either the unity or the distinction, and responsible exegesis must follow the text's own refusal.
