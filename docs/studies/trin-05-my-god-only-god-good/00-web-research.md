# Web Research Findings

## Study Question
When Jesus says "Why do you call me good? No one is good except God alone" (Mark 10:18) and "I ascend to my Father and your Father, to my God and your God" (John 20:17), is he denying his own deity?

### Jordan Hampton — "Did Jesus Deny His Divinity?" (The Analytic Christian)
**URL:** https://www.theanalyticchristian.com/blog/did-jesus-deny-his-divinity
**Key claims:**
- The phrase "except God alone" (ei me heis ho theos) appears only twice in Mark's Gospel: Mark 10:18 and Mark 2:7. Both instances begin with tis ("why"), creating an intentional verbal connection between the two passages.
- This lexical parallel links the "good teacher" episode back to Mark 2:7, where scribes ask "Who can forgive sins but God alone?" — in that context, Jesus proceeds to demonstrate he possesses that divine prerogative by forgiving sins.
- Cites Robert Gundry: "Jesus proceeds to answer the present question for proof of his divine possession of goodness" and borrowed the scribes' phraseology from Mark 2.
- Cites Simon Gathercole: If God alone determines requirements for eternal life, and Jesus gives his own command ("sell all...follow me"), then Jesus possesses this divine prerogative. The transition from v.18 to v.19 demonstrates Jesus acting with God-like authority.
- Jesus cites only commandments 5-10 (neighbor obligations), omitting commandments 1-4 (God obligations), then replaces the first tablet with his command to "follow me" — implying Jesus takes God's position in the man's priorities.
**Greek/Hebrew analysis:** Notes the lexical link between ei me heis ho theos in Mark 2:7 and 10:18 as a deliberate Markan literary device.
**Relevance:** Provides a strong scholarly case that Mark 10:18 is an implicit deity claim, not a denial, based on intra-Gospel literary parallels and the logic of divine prerogatives.
**Status:** Lead to verify against Scripture — check Mark 2:7 exact Greek, verify the commandment selection pattern in Mark 10:19.

### John Calvin — Commentary on Matthew, Mark, Luke (Volume 2)
**URL:** https://ccel.org/ccel/calvin/calcom32/calcom32.ii.lxx.html
**Key claims:**
- Calvin explicitly rejects the "implicit deity claim" reading: "I do not understand this correction in so refined a sense as is given by a good part of interpreters, as if Christ intended to suggest his Divinity."
- Calvin argues Jesus' point concerns doctrinal authority, not divine identity: Christ says "Thou falsely callest me a good Master, unless thou acknowledgest that I have come from God."
- The correction aims to direct the young man toward God as the source of true doctrine, maintaining "the authority of his doctrine" rather than "the essence of his Godhead."
**Relevance:** A major Reformation commentator who dissents from the majority interpretation. Calvin sees the statement as about doctrinal authority (Jesus teaches from God) rather than an implicit deity claim. This is significant because it shows disagreement among Trinitarian scholars themselves about what the verse proves — even those who affirm Jesus' deity may not use this verse to prove it.
**Status:** Lead — Calvin's minority position among Trinitarian commentators should be noted. The research agent should examine whether the text better supports the "doctrinal authority" reading or the "implicit deity claim" reading.

### Multiple Commentators on Mark 10:18 (BibleHub Commentaries)
**URL:** https://biblehub.com/commentaries/mark/10-18.htm
**Key claims (by commentator):**
- **Matthew Poole:** "not as denying that he was good... but checked him who did not believe him such, yet called him God" — the phrase proves Christ's divinity rather than denying it.
- **John Gill:** "nor do these words at all militate against the deity of Christ... he is good in the same sense in which God is good" — Christ tacitly suggests his godhood by claiming the same goodness as God.
- **Bengel's Gnomon:** "God is good: there is no goodness without Godhead... Why dost thou call Me good?" — Jesus refuses the title of "goodness" without recognition of his godhood, vindicating the Father.
- **Cambridge Bible:** "Our Lord does not decline the appellation 'good.' He repels it only in the superficial sense" — the question challenges shallow understanding rather than rejecting the designation.
- **Pulpit Commentary:** "If you call me good, believe that I am God; for no one is good" — forces recognition of Christ's divine nature to validate using "good."
**Relevance:** Shows broad commentarial consensus (Poole, Gill, Bengel, Cambridge, Pulpit) that Jesus does not deny deity in Mark 10:18 but rather challenges the ruler to think through the implications.
**Status:** Lead — consensus among major commentators supports the "implicit deity claim" reading, with only Calvin dissenting.

### Multiple Sources — The Shaliach (Agency) Concept
**URL:** https://thehumanjesus.org/2023/05/23/the-agent-in-the-gospel-of-john/ (and related)
**Key claims:**
- In Jewish law, the agent (sheluach/sheliach) principle states: "the one sent by a man is as the man himself" (Mishnah Berakhot 5:5).
- Jesus is depicted as God's authorized envoy against the background of Jewish notions of agency. As the shaliach endowed with full authority of the sender, Jesus functions as the unique emissary with all things placed in his hands.
- John's gospel is defined by Father-Son language denoting subordination of the Son, yet also by the principle of agency. The Son acts in dependence upon the one who sent him.

**Relevance:** The shaliach/agency concept is critical background for John 20:17. If "my God" language reflects the agent-sender relationship, it does not necessarily exclude deity. But this must be tested against what kind of agent Jesus claims to be — does he fit within normal agency bounds or exceed them?

## Summary of Scholarly Landscape

The scholarly consensus on Mark 10:18 overwhelmingly reads the passage as an implicit Christological claim rather than a denial of deity. The dominant interpretation, supported by commentators from Poole and Gill through Bengel, the Pulpit Commentary, and modern scholars like Gundry and Gathercole, argues that Jesus' question functions as a Socratic challenge: "If only God is good, and you call me good, what are you saying about me?" The lexical parallel with Mark 2:7 (where the same "except God alone" formula appears in the context of Jesus forgiving sins) strengthens this reading. The notable dissent comes from Calvin, who reads the statement as about doctrinal authority rather than divine identity, though Calvin himself affirmed Jesus' deity on other grounds.

On John 20:17, the scholarly conversation centers on the incarnational framework. The "my God and your God" language is interpreted through the lens of Jesus' human nature relating to the Father — even the glorified Christ continues to use this language (Revelation 3:12), suggesting it reflects the permanent reality of the incarnation rather than ontological inferiority. The critical contextual counterweight is John 20:28, just eleven verses later, where Thomas declares "my Lord and my God" and Jesus accepts it. The grammatical debate about nominative vs. vocative case in John 20:28 is largely resolved by the eipen auto ("said to him") indicating direct address, supported by Murray Harris's appeal to LXX precedent. The Hebrews 1:8-9 / Psalm 45:6-7 passage provides the strongest structural parallel: the Father addresses the Son as "God" (elohim/theos) while simultaneously referring to "God, your God" — demonstrating that "my God" language is compatible with deity within the biblical framework itself.

The shaliach (agency) concept from Jewish legal tradition provides important background. Some scholars use it to explain away deity claims (Jesus acts as God's agent, not as God himself), while others argue Jesus' authority exceeds any normal agent — he receives worship, forgives sins, claims pre-existence, and accepts the title "my God." The question is not whether Jesus was sent (he was), but whether the "sent one" framework accounts for everything the texts attribute to him.

3. **Synoptic divergence — Matthew 19:16-17 vs. Mark 10:17-18:** Parse the Greek of both passages. Matthew's version reportedly reads "why askest thou me concerning that which is good?" rather than "why callest thou me good?" — verify this and assess the Christological implications of the difference. Is Matthew softening a hard saying, or preserving a different tradition?

4. **John 20:17 vs. 20:28 tension:** Parse the Greek of both passages. Verify that eipen auto in John 20:28 constitutes direct address. Check whether the nominative ho Theos mou can function as vocative (Harris's claim re: Psalm 35:23 LXX). Assess whether Jesus' acceptance of Thomas's confession contrasts with worship-refusal in Acts 10:25-26 and Revelation 22:8-9.

5. **Hebrews 1:8-9 / Psalm 45:6-7 two-elohim pattern:** Parse the Hebrew of Psalm 45:6-7 to verify that elohim is used for both the one addressed and the one anointing. Parse the Greek of Hebrews 1:8-9 to confirm the author applies this to the Father-Son relationship. Check whether the vocative reading of elohim in Psalm 45:6 is grammatically supported.

8. **Psalm 22:1, 10 — "my God" in a Messianic psalm:** Verify the content of Psalm 22 and its combination of "my God" language with divine attributes. Check whether the psalm contains deity claims alongside the "my God" cry.

10. **agathos usage scope:** Verify whether agathos is used of humans elsewhere in the NT (e.g., Acts 11:24, Matthew 12:35) and whether Jesus' usage in Mark 10:18 specifies a unique, absolute sense applicable only to God.

11. **Shaliach/agency concept limits:** Examine whether Jesus' claims exceed the shaliach framework. Check John 8:58 (pre-existence claim), John 5:23 (equal honor), John 10:30 ("I and the Father are one") — do these fit within normal agent-sender bounds or transcend them?

12. **Calvin's "doctrinal authority" reading:** Test Calvin's interpretation that Jesus means "you falsely call me a good Master unless you acknowledge I have come from God" — does the subsequent exchange about commandments and "follow me" support this reading, or does it better support the deity-claim reading?
