# What do John 14:28 ('the Father is greater than I'), John 5:19 ('the Son can do nothing by himself'), Mark 13:32 (the Son does not know the day or hour), and 1 Corinthians 15:28 ('the Son himself will be made subject') teach about the relationship between the Father and the Son? Do these passages describe Jesus' eternal nature or his voluntary mission?

## Question
What do John 14:28 ('the Father is greater than I'), John 5:19 ('the Son can do nothing by himself'), Mark 13:32 (the Son does not know the day or hour), and 1 Corinthians 15:28 ('the Son himself will be made subject') teach about the relationship between the Father and the Son? Do these passages describe Jesus' eternal nature or his voluntary mission?

## Summary Answer
The four subordination passages (John 14:28, John 5:19, Mark 13:32, 1 Corinthians 15:28) describe the Son's voluntary incarnational mission, not his eternal nature. Each statement of apparent limitation is embedded in a context that simultaneously asserts the highest deity claims: John 5:19's dependence escalates to equal honor and universal judgment (vv.22-23), John 14:28's meizon (positional rank) contrasts with the same discourse's 'he that hath seen me hath seen the Father' (v.9), Mark 13:32's ignorance is contradicted by the same Jesus who 'knew all men' (John 2:24-25) and post-resurrection claims 'all power in heaven and earth' (Matt 28:18), and 1 Corinthians 15:28's future passive subjection (hupotagēsetai) marks a temporal transition completing the mediatorial mission within the Adam-Christ typology. The Philippians 2:5-11 kenosis hymn provides the controlling framework: the Son existed in the form of God, voluntarily emptied himself, and was exalted — the subordination belongs to the middle phase of this sequence, bracketed by pre-incarnation equality and post-exaltation glory.

## Analysis

### Main Thesis
The four subordination passages (John 14:28, John 5:19, Mark 13:32, 1 Corinthians 15:28) describe the Son's voluntary incarnational mission, not his eternal nature. Each statement of apparent limitation is embedded in a context that simultaneously asserts the highest deity claims, and the linguistic, grammatical, and structural evidence consistently points to a bounded, purposive, self-directed subordination within the incarnational economy rather than a permanent ontological hierarchy within the Godhead.

### Evidence

- The meizon (G3187) / kreitton (G2909) distinction establishes two distinct vocabulary registers for comparison: meizon for positional rank among entities of the same nature (30 occurrences, zero ontological uses), kreitton for qualitative/nature-level superiority (17 occurrences, concentrated in Hebrews). John 14:28 uses meizon, indicating positional comparison, not kreitton, which would indicate nature-level inferiority.
- The John 5:19-30 pericope's concessive-climactic structure moves from dependence (v.19) through divine prerogatives (life-giving v.21, all judgment v.22, equal honor v.23, self-existent life v.26) and back to dependence (v.30), demonstrating that the subordination language FRAMES the equality claims rather than contradicting them.
- The word study for oida (G1492, 334 occurrences) found zero instances of a causative/declarative sense, confirming that Mark 13:32's ignorance is genuine — but Luke 2:52, John 2:24-25, and Matt 28:18 demonstrate this ignorance is temporary and incarnational, not a permanent attribute.
- The hotan...tote temporal construction in 1 Cor 15:28 with future passive indicative (hupotagēsetai) indicates a CHANGE of state, not an ongoing arrangement — the Son is not currently in this subjection but will enter it at a specific future point when the mediatorial mission is complete.
- The Philippians 2:5-11 kenosis hymn provides the controlling framework: pre-incarnation equality (morphe theou, isa theo, v.6) → voluntary self-emptying (heauton ekenosen, v.7) → incarnational subordination (obedient unto death, v.8) → hyper-exaltation and universal Lordship (vv.9-11). The subordination is bounded on both sides by equality/glory.
- No pre-incarnation text in the NT describes the Son as subordinate to the Father. John 1:1 ('the Word was God'), John 17:5 (shared glory before the world), John 17:24 (Father's love before the foundation of the world), Phil 2:6 (isa theo), John 8:58 (ego eimi) — all describe equality, shared glory, and timeless being without subordination language.
- Post-resurrection and eschatological evidence consistently removes incarnational limitations: Matt 28:18 (all authority), Phil 2:9-11 (hyper-exaltation), John 20:28 (full deity confession accepted), Rev 1:8 (Alpha and Omega/Almighty), Rev 22:1,3 (shared throne), Rev 5:12-13 (identical worship). Permanent subordination is contradicted by this post-resurrection portrait.
- The reflexive constructions (heauton ekenosen Phil 2:7, ap emautou John 10:18, aph heautou John 5:19) and the voluntary participation verb (meteschen aorist vs. kekoinoneken perfect in Heb 2:14) consistently present the Son's subordination as self-initiated and voluntary, not externally imposed or ontologically necessary.

### Word Studies
The vocabulary network provides the precision that the subordination/equality binary lacks. Meizon (G3187, 30 occurrences). Kreitton (G2909, 17 occurrences) constitute two distinct semantic registers: meizon for positional rank among same-nature entities, kreitton for qualitative superiority. The deliberate use of meizon (not kreitton) in John 14:28 is the single strongest lexical argument for the incarnational reading. Hupotasso (G5293, 49 occurrences) in 1 Cor 15:28 appears in the future passive indicative — a tense that marks a change of state, not an ongoing condition, and the word itself is semantically neutral regarding purpose (the salvific sense comes from the Adam-Christ typological context, not from the word itself). Kenoo (G2758, 7 occurrences) in Phil 2:7 describes a voluntary, self-directed act (aorist active with reflexive heauton), and the participial phrase morphen doulou labon (aorist of addition) confirms the kenosis was not subtraction of deity but addition of humanity. Morphe (G3444, 3 occurrences) denotes the outward expression corresponding to inner essence — not mere appearance (schema) — making 'form of God' a genuine divine-nature claim and 'form of servant' a genuine servant-nature addition. Eídō/oida (G1492, 334 occurrences) as a stative perfect with present force means 'to possess settled knowledge,' and the comprehensive word study found zero instances of the Macknight causative reading, confirming Mark 13:32's ignorance is genuine. Isos (G2470, 7 occurrences) denotes full equivalence, not mere similarity — both John 5:18 and Phil 2:6 use it for Christ's equality with God, establishing the pre-incarnation baseline. Harpagmos (G725, 1 occurrence) as a hapax legomenon strongly favors the res rapta reading — Christ already possessed equality and chose not to exploit it, making the kenosis a voluntary surrender from a position of equality. Thelema (G2307, 64 occurrences) in the 'Christ's submission' texts (John 5:30, 6:38, Luke 22:42) consistently distinguishes 'my will' from 'the Father's will,' and the Gethsemane texts make clear that 'my will' is the HUMAN will, not a second divine will. Eimi (G1510) denotes continuous being. Ginomai (G1096, 702 occurrences) denotes coming-into-being. Together they constitute the foundational ontological divide in Johannine Christology: the Word IS (continuous being) while creation BECAME (coming into existence) — placing the Son permanently on the uncreated side. Doxa (G1391, 168 occurrences) traces a pre-incarnation-possession → incarnational-veiling → post-exaltation-restoration arc that mirrors and confirms the temporary nature of the subordination. Apostello (G649, 144 occurrences). Pempo (G3992, 82 occurrences) constitute John's sending vocabulary, and the shaliach principle (the sent one carries the sender's full authority) is confirmed by John 5:22-23 (all judgment, equal honor), John 12:44-45 (believing on the sent = believing on the sender), and John 13:20 (receiving the sent = receiving the sender). Collectively, the vocabulary network demonstrates that the NT authors chose their words with theological precision: they had words for ontological inferiority (kreitton), permanent ongoing states (perfect tense), and mere appearance (schema), but consistently used words for positional comparison (meizon), future transitions (future passive), genuine essence (morphe), and voluntary self-action (reflexive constructions).

## Difficult Passages

### Mark 13:32 — The Son genuinely does not know

**Difficulty:** Mark 13:32's graduated exclusion chain (no man < angels < the Son < the Father) is the most difficult subordination text for a high Christology because it attributes genuine ignorance to the Son — not merely a choice not to reveal, but an explicit statement that the Son does not 'know' (oiden, G1492). The word study for oida (334 occurrences) found ZERO instances of a causative sense, refuting the Macknight reading. The Son genuinely lacked this knowledge. How can the one who 'knew all men' (John 2:24-25) and claimed timeless existence (John 8:58) be genuinely ignorant of a specific datum?

**Resolution:** The resolution lies in the kenotic framework of Phil 2:5-11 and the distinction between divine attributes possessed and divine attributes exercised. Colossians 2:9 states that 'all the fulness of the Godhead dwelleth in him bodily' (present tense katoikei — ongoing during the incarnation). The divine omniscience was present in Christ's person but not always accessed in his incarnate consciousness. Luke 2:52 ('Jesus increased in wisdom and stature') confirms that the incarnate Son experienced genuinely developmental knowledge — real cognitive growth within the human experience. The selective nature of the ignorance is significant: the same incarnate Jesus knew human hearts (John 2:24-25, ginosko — experiential knowledge), foreknew events (John 13:1, 'knew that his hour was come'), and the disciples confessed 'thou knowest all things' (John 16:30) — yet he did not possess settled knowledge (oida) of the eschatological date. This selectivity points to a bounded, voluntary self-limitation within the incarnation rather than wholesale cognitive deficiency. The post-resurrection claim of 'all power in heaven and in earth' (Matt 28:18) removes the limitation entirely, confirming it belonged to the incarnate state and was reversed at the exaltation. The ignorance was real during the incarnation, but it was an incarnational feature, not a permanent attribute of the Son's eternal nature.

### 1 Corinthians 15:28 — The Son 'will be made subject' (future tense)

**Difficulty:** The future passive indicative hupotagēsetai in 1 Cor 15:28 describes a future event: 'then shall the Son also himself be subject unto him that put all things under him.' The Eternal Functional Subordination camp (Frame, Grudem, Ware) argues that even after the mediatorial mission, the Son remains subordinate — his permanent humanity grounds permanent subordination. Since the Son retains human nature forever (Heb 13:8, Rev 1:13-16), does the human nature entail permanent subjection? And if the subjection is only the delivery of the mediatorial kingdom, what does the ongoing relationship look like after that delivery?

**Resolution:** The resolution turns on three converging lines of evidence. First, the temporal construction: hotan...tote with future passive indicative describes a CHANGE of state — the Son is not currently in this subjection but will enter it at a specific future point when all things are subdued. This is a temporal transition, not the continuation of an ongoing arrangement. Second, the Adam-Christ typological context: the entire chapter frames Christ's work through the second Adam paradigm (vv.20-23, 45-49). The subjection in v.28 is the second Adam's mission completion — delivering the completed mediatorial kingdom because the redemptive work is finished. The paradidomi verb ('delivers,' v.24) indicates voluntary transfer, not imposed subjection. Third, the Revelation portrait of the glorified Christ contradicts permanent subordination: Rev 1:8 applies 'Alpha and Omega... the Almighty' to Christ, Rev 22:1,3 describes 'the throne of God and of the Lamb' (singular throne — shared, not subordinate), and Rev 5:12-13 ascribes identical worship, honor, blessing, and glory to both 'him that sitteth upon the throne' and the Lamb. If the glorified Christ shares the throne, receives identical worship, and bears the title 'Almighty,' permanent post-mission subordination is not the eschatological reality. The hina clause ('that God may be all in all') describes a new state of cosmic reconciliation — God (Father, Son, and Spirit) is 'all in all' — not a permanent hierarchy.

### John 5:19 — 'The Son can do nothing of himself'

**Difficulty:** The statement 'the Son can do nothing of himself' (ou dynatai ho huios poiein aph heautou ouden) uses dynatai ('is able') with ouden ('nothing') — the most comprehensive denial of independent capacity. Read in isolation, this appears to deny not merely independent action but independent ABILITY. If the Son cannot do anything independently, how is he ontologically equal to the Father? The Augustinian middle-ground position (K.E. Johnson) reads this as reflecting 'eternal generation' — the Son derives his existence from the Father, and therefore his actions flow from the Father as their source. This would make dependence a permanent structural feature, not merely incarnational.

**Resolution:** The resolution is found in the pericope structure that immediately follows. The phrase aph heautou ('from himself') specifies the denial: the Son does not act 'from himself' — i.e., independently, on his own initiative. This denies independent initiative, not ability. The immediate context proves this: v.19b adds 'what things soever the Father doeth, these also doeth the Son likewise (homoiōs)' — the Son does the SAME things, not lesser things. V.21 attributes the divine prerogative of giving life 'to whom he will' (hous thelei — his own sovereign will). V.22 gives ALL judgment to the Son. V.23 demands EQUAL honor (kathos — 'even as'). If v.19 denied the Son's ability, the escalation to divine prerogatives in vv.21-23 would be incoherent — the same speaker cannot deny ability and then claim life-giving power, universal judgment, and equal honor. The concessive-climactic structure shows that v.19 establishes shared operation as the PREMISE for the equality claims: because the Son never acts independently, his actions perfectly mirror the Father's — proving unity, not inferiority. The K. Johnson scholarly reading confirms: v.19 refutes independent self-appointment ('he MADE himself equal'), not equality itself. On the 'eternal generation' reading: the biblical text in John 5:26 ('as the Father hath life in himself, so hath he given to the Son to have life in himself') describes the SAME quality of self-existent life in the Son — whatever the 'giving' means, the result is qualitative identity, not inferiority.

### John 14:28 — 'The Father is greater than I' within the same discourse as equality claims

**Difficulty:** Within the same Upper Room Discourse, Jesus says both 'he that hath seen me hath seen the Father' (14:9), 'I am in the Father, and the Father in me' (14:10), AND 'the Father is greater (meizon) than I' (14:28). How can the one who IS the visible manifestation of the Father also be LESS than the Father? The Eternal Functional Subordination reading sees this as evidence that even the relational ordering within the Trinity involves the Father's authority over the Son — the Father IS greater, not merely was greater during the incarnation.

**Resolution:** The resolution proceeds from three converging arguments. First, the lexical evidence: meizon (G3187, 30 occurrences) consistently compares entities of the same nature along a positional or functional dimension, never an ontological one. Matt 18:1 ('who is greatest in the kingdom'), John 13:16 ('the servant is not greater than his lord'), and critically John 14:12 in the same discourse ('greater works than these shall he do') — all same-nature, positional comparisons. Had Jesus intended an ontological comparison, kreitton (G2909) was the available word, as the author of Hebrews demonstrates when asserting Christ's nature-level superiority over angels (Heb 1:4). Second, the departure-to-Father context: 'I go unto the Father: for my Father is greater than I' — the hoti ('because') clause grounds the 'greater' in the return to the Father. Jesus is comparing his current diminished incarnational position with the Father's undiminished glory and explaining why the disciples should rejoice at his departure. Third, the grammar reference for the comparative adjective confirms that the genitive of comparison construction (meizon + mou) specifies the standard of comparison without requiring ontological difference. The EFS reading faces the problem that the same discourse contains mutual-indwelling language (14:10) and functional identity claims (14:9) — if the Father is eternally 'greater' in an authority sense, the mutual-indwelling language becomes incoherent, since mutual indwelling implies symmetrical intimacy, not asymmetrical authority.

### John 5:26 — 'Given to the Son to have life in himself'

**Difficulty:** John 5:26 says 'as the Father hath life in himself, so hath he given to the Son to have life in himself.' The 'given' (edōken, aorist active) implies the Son's self-existent life is DERIVED from the Father — a real ontological dependence. The Augustinian 'eternal generation' tradition reads this as the Son's existence being eternally derived from the Father. If the Son's very life is 'given,' is there not a permanent asymmetry between Father (source) and Son (derived)? This challenges the purely incarnational reading by suggesting a pre-incarnational ordering within the Godhead.

**Resolution:** The resolution requires careful attention to the hosper...houtōs ('just as...so also') parallel structure. The Father HAS (echei, present active — continuous possession) life in himself. The Son was GIVEN (edōken, aorist — definite act) to HAVE (echein, present active infinitive — continuous possession) life in himself. The RESULT for the Son is identical to the Father's state: possessing self-existent life. Whatever the 'giving' denotes — whether eternal generation (patristic reading), incarnational commissioning (functional reading), or the very constitution of the Son's eternal existence — the outcome is qualitative identity: the Son possesses the SAME kind of self-existent life the Father possesses. The hosper/houtōs construction demands equivalence of result. Furthermore, this statement occurs within the John 5 pericope where the escalation from apparent dependence to divine prerogative is the governing rhetorical pattern. V.26 is the peak of the escalation: the Son possesses the very attribute that defines God as God — life-in-himself, self-existent being. This is not a limitation but the highest possible claim. The 'giving' describes HOW the Son has this life (through relational derivation, if eternal generation is in view) without diminishing WHAT the Son has (identical self-existent life). The analogy: a torch lit from another torch has the same fire — derived in source but identical in nature.

### Philippians 2:9-11 — Was equality restored or something new conferred?

**Difficulty:** Phil 2:9-11 says 'God also hath highly exalted him, and given him a name which is above every name.' If the exaltation RESTORES pre-incarnation equality (per John 17:5), the subordination was temporary. But the verb hyperupsōsen ('super-exalted,' G5251 — found only here in the NT) suggests something BEYOND the original state — not merely restoration but elevation to a new height. And the passive construction ('God exalted him') means the Father acts on the Son, which could imply ongoing authority of the Father over the Son even in the exalted state.

**Resolution:** The resolution distinguishes between what was RESTORED and what was NEWLY manifested. The pre-incarnation glory (John 17:5) existed in relationship with the Father before creation — this is what Jesus asks to be restored. The 'hyper-exaltation' adds something: the universal recognition of a name above every name, the cosmic confession 'Jesus Christ is Lord,' and the vindication of the incarnational mission. The new element is not a higher ontological status (Christ was already isa theo per v.6) but universal RECOGNITION of that status, now earned through obedience unto death. Before the incarnation, Christ's deity was known to the Father (John 17:24, 'thou lovedst me before the foundation of the world') but not to creation. After the incarnation and exaltation, every knee bows and every tongue confesses — creation now recognizes what was always ontologically true. The passive construction ('God exalted him') reflects the relational pattern throughout the kenosis hymn: the Son acts on himself (ekenosen), God acts on the Son (hyperupsōsen). This describes relational action within the Godhead, not a permanent authority hierarchy. The eis doxan theou patros ('to the glory of God the Father') final clause shows the exaltation serves the glory of the Father — a relational outcome, not a subordination marker.

## Conclusion

This study demonstrates that the four subordination passages describe the Son's voluntary incarnational mission, not his eternal nature. The evidence converges from multiple independent lines: lexical (meizon vs. kreitton), grammatical (future passive hupotagēsetai, aorist ekenosen, hotan...tote temporal construction), structural (John 5:19-30 concessive-climactic pericope, Hebrews 1-2 deity-then-lowering sequence), typological (Adam-Christ framework in 1 Corinthians 15), and canonical (pre-incarnation equality texts, post-resurrection restoration texts). The convergence is significant because the evidence comes from multiple authors (John, Paul, Hebrews author, Mark), multiple genres (Gospel discourse, Pauline epistle, Christological hymn, OT psalm), and multiple analytical methods (word study, parsing, pericope analysis, cross-testament citation tracking). No single line of evidence is conclusive alone, but the cumulative weight is substantial. The Eternal Functional Subordination reading faces three critical problems the evidence highlights. First, it must explain why the NT vocabulary consistently uses positional comparison terms rather than ontological ones for the Father-Son relationship — if the subordination is a permanent structural feature, the ontological vocabulary was available and unused. Second, it must account for the total absence of subordination language in pre-incarnation references — John 1:1, 17:5, 17:24, 8:58, Phil 2:6, Col 1:15-17 describe equality, shared glory, timeless being, and creative agency without any subordination qualifier. Third, it must reconcile permanent subordination with the eschatological portrait in Revelation where the glorified Christ shares the throne (Rev 22:1,3), receives identical worship (Rev 5:12-13), and bears the title Almighty (Rev 1:8). The Augustinian middle-ground reading (eternal generation creating real ordering but not hierarchical subordination) has more biblical warrant than the EFS reading, particularly in John 5:26 ('given to the Son to have life in himself'), but the text does not resolve whether this 'giving' is a pre-temporal eternal reality or an incarnational commissioning. What the text DOES resolve is the nature of the subordination described in the four crux passages: it is purposive (Heb 2:9-17 purpose clauses), voluntary (Phil 2:7 reflexive, John 10:17-18 exousia), temporally bounded (1 Cor 15:25 achri, 15:28 hotan...tote future passive), incarnationally grounded (Luke 2:52 developmental knowledge, Col 2:9 full deity retained), and mission-directed (John 5:30 'the will of him that sent me'). The remaining unresolved question is the precise nature of the eternal Father-Son relationship APART from the incarnational mission. The biblical evidence clearly establishes that whatever that eternal relationship involves, it does not involve the kind of subordination described in the four crux passages — those belong to the incarnation. Whether there is a non-subordination ordering (taxis) within the eternal Godhead — an asymmetry of source (the Father as unoriginate, the Son as eternally generated) that does not imply inferiority — is a question the text raises (John 5:26) but does not fully resolve, and honest interpretation must acknowledge this boundary of the evidence.
