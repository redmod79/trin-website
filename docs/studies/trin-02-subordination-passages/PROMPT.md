## Question
What do John 14:28 ('the Father is greater than I'), John 5:19 ('the Son can do nothing by himself'), Mark 13:32 (the Son does not know the day or hour), and 1 Corinthians 15:28 ('the Son himself will be made subject') teach about the relationship between the Father and the Son? Do these passages describe Jesus' eternal nature or his voluntary mission?

## Prior Research Summary
Four prior studies provide relevant findings: (1) trin-01-firstborn-created-being established Christ's full deity and uncreated status via prototokos as preeminence title, the causal hoti clause in Col 1:16, and the creation paradox (John 1:3, Col 1:16, Heb 1:2). (2) jesus-never-said-i-am-god directly addresses John 14:28 as economic/positional (not ontological) and Mark 13:32 as a kenosis passage, noting Christ's claims to equal deity (John 10:30, John 20:28). (4) lgt-04b-nature-of-christ establishes the kenotic framework from Phil 2:6-7 and the Heb 2:14 verb contrast (kekoinoneken/meteschen). Web research identified three main scholarly camps: Incarnational Subordination Only (Giles, K. Johnson), Eternal Functional Subordination (Grudem, Ware, Frame), and Augustinian Middle Ground (K.E. Johnson). Key linguistic claims requiring verification include the meizon/kreitton distinction, the hotan...tote temporal construction in 1 Cor 15:28, hypotasso as salvific rather than hierarchical, and the shaliach (sending) principle for John 5:19-30.

7. **The 'will' question: one divine will or two distinct wills in Father and Son?:**
The key texts are John 5:30 ('I seek not mine own will, but the will of the Father which hath sent me'), John 6:38 ('I came down from heaven, not to do mine own will, but the will of him that sent me'), and the Gethsemane prayer in Luke 22:42 ('not my will, but thine, be done'). Run greek_parser.py --verse on John 5:30, John 6:38, and Luke 22:42. The question is: when Jesus says 'my will' vs. 'the Father's will,' is he distinguishing between two divine wills (Father's and Son's) or between his human will and the shared divine will? The Riccardi critique of EFS argues that two distinct divine wills implies either tritheism or Arianism. The Gethsemane text is particularly diagnostic: the 'my will' that shrinks from the cup is clearly the human will (no divine person would will to avoid the plan of salvation). Does this pattern extend to John 5:30 and 6:38? The EGW corpus claim is that Father and Son are 'one in nature, in will, and in purpose' (DA 208.2) — this needs verification against the biblical evidence.
