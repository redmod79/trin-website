# Web Research Findings

## Study Question
What do John 14:28 ("the Father is greater than I"), John 5:19 ("the Son can do nothing by himself"), Mark 13:32 (the Son does not know the day or hour), and 1 Corinthians 15:28 ("the Son himself will be made subject") teach about the relationship between the Father and the Son? Do these passages describe Jesus' eternal nature or his voluntary mission?

## Sources Found

### Keith E. Johnson — "Trinitarian Agency and the Eternal Subordination of the Son: An Augustinian Perspective" (Themelios, Vol. 36, Issue 1)
**URL:** [thegospelcoalition.org](https://www.thegospelcoalition.org/themelios/article/trinitarian-agency-and-the-eternal-subordination-of-the-son-an-augustinian-perspective/)
**Key claims:**
- Augustine's framework: "The divine persons act inseparably *ad extra* according to their relative properties *ad intra*." The Father generates the Son eternally; the Son eternally proceeds from the Father. This ordering does not imply ontological or functional subordination — it describes the Son's eternal mode of existence as "from the Father."
- On John 5:19 ("Son can do nothing of his own accord"): Augustine interprets this through inseparable operation — the Father and Son work together, not sequentially. The Son's inability to act independently reflects his derivative existence (eternal generation), not inferiority.
- Johnson argues that both EFS proponents and opponents misread Augustine. The relationship involves ordered but inseparable action.
- "The distinction of Persons is constituted" by eternal generation, not by authority/submission dynamics.
- Key terminology discussed: *ousia* (essence), *substantia* (substance), *principium* (source), *taxis* (ordering), *inseparabilia* (inseparable).
**Greek/Latin analysis:** Discussion of Augustine's use of *principium* (source/origin) for the Father's role and *taxis* (ordering) for Trinitarian relations. Johnson notes Augustine preferred "inseparable" (*inseparabilia*) for divine operations rather than hierarchical terminology.
**Relevance:** Provides the Augustinian middle ground between pure EFS and its denial. Directly addresses John 5:19 as reflecting eternal generation rather than subordination. The "inseparable operations" principle is a key interpretive framework for all four study passages.
**Status:** Lead to verify against Scripture

### Kevin Giles — "The Doctrine of the Trinity and Subordination" (Priscilla Papers, CBE International, July 31, 2004)
**URL:** [cbeinternational.org](https://www.cbeinternational.org/resource/doctrine-trinity-and-subordination/)
**Key claims:**
- "To eternally subordinate the Son or Spirit in work/function by necessity implies their ontological subordination." Giles rejects the modern evangelical claim of eternal functional subordination without ontological subordination, calling it "entirely novel" with "no historical antecedents."
- Athanasius established the orthodox approach: the Son has "eternal divine status" but demonstrates "voluntary and temporal subordination" during incarnation.
- Athanasius stressed that "what God does reveals who God is," meaning being and action cannot be separated — so eternal functional subordination would entail ontological subordination.
- The Nicene Council rejected "the idea that the Son was subordinated in his being to the Father," establishing the Son is "one in being (homoousios) with the Father."
- On John 14:28: Interpreted as Jesus speaking "in his state of humiliation" as the incarnate Son.
- On John 4:34; 5:30 (Son being "sent"): The *shaliach* principle means the sent one shares the sender's authority, indicating "equal authority" rather than subordination.
- On 1 Corinthians 15:28: Interpreted as the Son "freely handing back" delegated rule, suggesting "changing roles in differing epochs" rather than eternal subordination.
- On Philippians 2:5-11: Established as the interpretive key — the Son "willingly and freely chose to subordinate himself for our salvation."
- Claims Athanasius, Augustine, Calvin, and the creeds all rejected eternal subordination of the Son.
**Greek analysis:** Discussion of *shaliach* (Jewish sending principle) applied to John's "sending" language, arguing the sent one carries the sender's full authority.
**Relevance:** Major scholarly argument that all biblical subordination language is incarnational, not eternal. Directly addresses John 14:28, John 5:19-30, 1 Cor 15:28, and Phil 2:5-11. Claims patristic consensus against EFS. The *shaliach* principle for John 5:19-30 needs biblical verification.
**Status:** Lead to verify against Scripture

### Kristin Johnson — "Just as the Father, So the Son: The Implications of John 5:16-30" (Priscilla Papers, CBE International, January 31, 2005)
**URL:** [cbeinternational.org](https://www.cbeinternational.org/resource/just-father-so-son/)
**Key claims:**
- When Jewish leaders accused Jesus of "making himself equal to God" (John 5:18), Jesus did not deny equality but clarified that His equality derived from the Father's gift, not independent grasping.
- "The Son can do nothing by himself" (v.19) affirms rather than refutes equality: "Jesus is not refuting the claim that he is equal to the Father; he is refuting the claim that *he made himself equal* to the Father, as if he were independent of him."
- Cites B.B. Warfield's interpretation of "son" (*huios*) as implying "likeness" — sonship in first-century Judaism implied equality, which is why religious leaders were offended.
- The Greek *ison* (equal) in John 5:18 means "equal, the same."
- Functional equality: "Jesus' equality *in function* with the Father is what demonstrates the equality of his divine status."
- Proposes an interdependence model: "mutual dependence and submission empower rather than subordinate" — the Son depends on the Father for provision, the Father depends on the Son for extending His provision globally.
- "The Son is not inferior to the Father, nor is the Father superior to the Son."
- Their "oneness is not achieved through hierarchy, but, according to Christ, it can only be achieved through love."
**Greek analysis:** *ison* (equal) in John 5:18; *huios* (son) as implying likeness/equality in Jewish context.
**Relevance:** Directly exegetes John 5:16-30, arguing the "Son can do nothing by himself" statement proves equality rather than subordination. The full pericope argument (vv. 22-23 on equal honor and all judgment) is critical for the study. The claim that v.19 refutes independent action rather than equality needs verification from the Greek text.
**Status:** Lead to verify against Scripture

### Jon Harris — "The Father Is Greater Than I: What Did Jesus Mean?" (Pfander Center)
**URL:** [pfandercenter.org](https://www.pfandercenter.org/blog/articles/the-father-is-greater-than-i-what-did-jesus-mean)
**Key claims:**
- *Meizon* (greater) in John 14:28 refers to positional superiority, not ontological inferiority. Jesus contrasts his earthly state of humiliation with the Father's celestial position.
- The verse functions as a causal clause introduced by *hoti* (because): "the statement does not stand alone" but explains why disciples should rejoice at Jesus' departure. Focus is on Jesus' destination and motion, not his essence.
- Cites Daniel Wallace: the Genitive of Comparison construction shows "Jesus is speaking with reference to his *office, not his person*."
- Examines *meizon* across John's Gospel: Matthew 11:11 (John Baptist vs kingdom members — same human essence, different positions), John 5:20 ("greater works" = eschatological scope, not power), John 14:12 (disciples perform "greater works" through post-Ascension mission extension). Each instance demonstrates "greater" indicates functional role or scale, never essential difference.
- D.A. Carson's analogy: "Her Majesty Queen Elizabeth is greater than I" does not suggest differing human essence — only differences in "wealth, authority, majesty."
- Barrett: Jesus contrasts "heavenly state with the earthly," emphasizing the Son's "humiliation" during incarnation.
- Keener: Distinguishes Jesus from the Father (admitted in John 1:1b) while affirming deity throughout John's Gospel.
**Greek analysis:** Detailed analysis of *meizon* (μείζων) as comparative adjective indicating position/rank, distinguished from *kreitton* (κρείττων) in Hebrews 1:4 which indicates superiority in nature/essence. Wallace's Genitive of Comparison argument for office over person.
**Relevance:** Central to interpreting John 14:28. The *meizon* vs *kreitton* distinction is the core linguistic argument. The Johannine usage survey (5:20, 14:12, Matt 11:11) provides pattern evidence. Carson, Barrett, and Keener citations are major commentary voices. All claims need verification from the Greek text.
**Status:** Lead to verify against Scripture

### Wyatt Graham — "Does 1 Corinthians 15:28 Teach the Son's Eternal Subordination?" (June 6, 2019)
**URL:** [wyattgraham.com](https://www.wyattgraham.com/p/does-1-corinthians-1528-teach-the-sons-eternal-subordination-answer-no-it-teaches-our-spiritual-transformation-in-christ)
**Key claims:**

**Greek analysis:** Notes *hypotasso* has multiple valences in context (negative judgment vs positive reconciliation).
**Relevance:** Directly addresses 1 Cor 15:28 and its use in the EFS debate. The Adam-Christ typological reading and Psalm 8 connection need biblical verification. The claim that *hypotasso* is salvific rather than hierarchical needs checking against Paul's usage.

### Phil Gons — John Frame on 1 Corinthians 15:28 and Eternal Subordination (March 2008)
**URL:** [philgons.com](https://philgons.com/2008/03/john-frame-on-1-corinthians-1528-and-eternal-subordination/)
**Key claims:**
- John Frame argues the Son's subordination is eternal, grounded in Christ's perpetual humanity: "as Jesus remains both human and divine through all eternity, he will, as human servant, always be subject to the Father in these ways."
- Frame links perpetual subordination to Christ's eternal human nature. Since Christ remains both divine and human forever, and no human — even glorified — can equal God, the Son will eternally be subject to the Father regarding His humanity.
- Frame references Westminster Catechisms' doctrine of Christ's humiliation.
- Phil Gons identifies a critical tension: The Greek construction *hotan...tote* (when...then) with future tense suggests a change of state — implying the Son is not currently subordinated in the manner He will be subordinated. This temporal language suggests subordination begins at a future point, contradicting eternal subordination.
- Frame does not address how Christ's eternal humanity explains the apparently temporary character of the subordination described in 1 Cor 15:28.
**Greek analysis:** The *hotan...tote* construction with future tense indicating a change of state, not a continuing condition.
**Relevance:** Presents the strongest form of the eternal subordination argument (Frame) and its internal critique (Gons). The Greek temporal construction is a key exegetical detail for 1 Cor 15:28. Both claims need verification from the Greek text.
**Status:** Lead to verify against Scripture

**Relevance:** Shows the range of historical commentary positions on Mark 13:32. The two-natures reading (Gill, Chrysostom) vs the literal reading (Meyer, Calvin) vs the declarative reading (Macknight) represent the main interpretive options. All need biblical verification.

## Summary of Scholarly Landscape

The scholarly debate on these "subordination passages" falls into three main camps, with significant internal variation:

**Camp 1: Incarnational Subordination Only.** The dominant view in historical orthodoxy, represented by Giles, K. Johnson (Themelios), and the patristic consensus (Athanasius, Augustine, Chrysostom). All four passages — John 14:28, John 5:19, Mark 13:32, and 1 Cor 15:28 — describe the incarnate Son's voluntary self-limitation during his earthly mission, not the eternal relationship within the Godhead. Philippians 2:5-11 is the interpretive key: the Son "emptied himself" voluntarily, and all statements of limitation, subordination, and ignorance belong to this kenotic state. The Nicene *homoousios* (one in being) settles the ontological question; the functional question is purely economic (related to salvation history). Scholars in this camp argue that eternal functional subordination necessarily implies ontological subordination because being and action are inseparable in God.

**Camp 2: Eternal Functional Subordination (EFS/ERAS).** Represented by Wayne Grudem, Bruce Ware, and (with important qualifications) John Frame. These scholars affirm full ontological equality but argue the Son is eternally subordinate to the Father in role, function, and authority. The Father-Son names themselves imply an eternal authority-submission structure. Frame grounds this in Christ's permanent humanity — since the Son remains human forever, his human subordination is also permanent. This camp reads 1 Cor 15:28 as describing an eternal reality (or at least a permanent eschatological one). Critics identify two major problems: (1) the "two wills" objection — if the Son eternally submits, this implies a will distinct from the Father's, threatening divine simplicity; (2) Phil Gons' observation that the Greek *hotan...tote* construction in 1 Cor 15:28 implies a future change of state, contradicting the "eternal" claim.

**Camp 3: Augustinian Middle Ground.** Represented by K.E. Johnson's reading of Augustine. The Son's eternal generation from the Father creates a real ordering (*taxis*) within the Trinity — the Son is eternally "from" the Father — but this ordering is neither hierarchical subordination nor mere economic arrangement. The divine persons act inseparably (*ad extra*) according to their relative properties (*ad intra*). John 5:19 reflects the Son's derivative existence (he is "from" the Father), not inferiority. This position avoids the extremes of both camps by grounding distinction in eternal generation rather than authority structures.

On the specific linguistic questions, there is strong scholarly agreement that *meizon* (greater) in John 14:28 indicates positional/functional superiority rather than ontological superiority, supported by Wallace's Genitive of Comparison analysis and the contrast with *kreitton* (better in nature) used in Hebrews 1:4. On Mark 13:32, the majority of commentators (both ancient and modern) attribute the ignorance to Christ's human nature or incarnate state, though a minority propose the declarative/causative reading of *oiden* ("make known" rather than "know"). On 1 Cor 15:28, the eschatological context and Adam-Christ typology are widely recognized, but scholars disagree sharply on whether the "subjection" is a temporary mediatorial act or a permanent reality.
