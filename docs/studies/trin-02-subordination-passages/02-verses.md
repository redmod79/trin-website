# Verses

## Question
What do John 14:28 ('the Father is greater than I'), John 5:19 ('the Son can do nothing by himself'), Mark 13:32 (the Son does not know the day or hour), and 1 Corinthians 15:28 ('the Son himself will be made subject') teach about the relationship between the Father and the Son? Do these passages describe Jesus' eternal nature or his voluntary mission?

### Crux text: 'the Father is greater (meizon) than I'

> Ye have heard how I said unto you, I go away, and come [again] unto you. If ye loved me, ye would rejoice, because I said, I go unto the Father: for my Father is greater than I.

**Context:** Upper Room Discourse, evening before crucifixion. Jesus prepares disciples for his departure. The statement occurs in the context of going to the Father — a transitional, positional statement about Jesus moving FROM his incarnate state TO the Father's presence. The key word meizon (G3187, comparative of megas) is parsed as Nom Sg M comparative adjective. The genitive of comparison construction (mou) compares Jesus with the Father along some dimension of 'greatness.' The context (poreuomai pros ton Patera — 'I go to the Father') frames this as positional/locational, not ontological.

### Same meizon word: 'greater works' indicating scope, not essential difference

> Verily, verily, I say unto you, He that believeth on me, the works that I do shall he do also; and greater [works] than these shall he do; because I go unto my Father.

**Context:** Same Upper Room Discourse context. Jesus tells his disciples they will do 'greater' (meizon) works than he did. This cannot mean the disciples are ontologically superior to Jesus — it means their scope of ministry will be wider post-ascension. The same word (meizon) used for the same speaker in the same discourse context strongly suggests a consistent semantic range.

**Notes:** This is a critical control text for interpreting John 14:28. If meizon in 14:12 clearly means 'greater in scope/extent' (not 'ontologically superior'), the burden of proof falls on anyone who claims meizon means something categorically different just 16 verses later in the same discourse.

### Jewish opponents understood Jesus' claim as making himself equal (isos) with God

> Therefore the Jews sought the more to kill him, because he not only had broken the sabbath, but said also that God was his Father, making himself equal with God.

**Context:** Chapter 5 — Jesus heals on the Sabbath. The narrator (John) reports the Jewish reaction. The key word is ison (G2470, A-ASM), parsed as accusative singular masculine adjective. The construction ison heauton poion to theo ('making himself equal with God') is a present active participle — the Jews perceived Jesus as MAKING an equality claim with God. The word isos denotes full equivalence in status, not mere similarity (see word study G2470). This verse sets the context for the entire pericope that follows (vv.19-30).

The same root appears in Phil 2:6 (isa theo) where Paul affirms Christ's pre-incarnation equality.

### Crux pericope: dependence statement escalates to highest equality claims

> Then answered Jesus and said unto them, Verily, verily, I say unto you, The Son can do nothing of himself, but what he seeth the Father do: for what things soever he doeth, these also doeth the Son likewise. For the Father loveth the Son, and sheweth him all things that himself doeth: and he will shew him greater works than these, that ye may marvel. For as the Father raiseth up the dead, and quickeneth [them]; even so the Son quickeneth whom he will. For the Father judgeth no man, but hath committed all judgment unto the Son: That all [men] should honour the Son, even as they honour the Father. He that honoureth not the Son honoureth not the Father which hath sent him.

**Context:** Jesus responds to the equality charge of v.18. The pericope begins with apparent dependence ('the Son can do nothing of himself,' v.19) but immediately escalates: the Father shows the Son ALL things (v.20), the Son gives life to whom HE wills (v.21 — divine prerogative), ALL judgment is given to the Son (v.22), and men must honor the Son EVEN AS (kathos) they honor the Father (v.23). V.22 has krisin pasan dedoken (G2920 N-ASF + G3956 A-ASF + G1325 V-RAI-3S perfect: 'all judgment has given'). V.23 has timosi ton Huion kathos timosi ton Patera (G5091 V-PAS-3P: 'honor the Son just as they honor the Father'). The pempō sending verb (V-AAP-ASM, aorist participle) in v.23 identifies the Father as the sender.

**Notes:** The K. Johnson (Priscilla Papers) reading is confirmed by the Greek structure: v.19 denies independent action (aph heautou = 'from himself,' i.e., independently), not ability. NT parallels: JHN 8:28 (highest match at 0.558 — 'I do nothing of myself' repeated in same Gospel).

### Son has life in himself and authority of judgment as Son of man

> Verily, verily, I say unto you, He that heareth my word, and believeth on him that sent me, hath everlasting life, and shall not come into condemnation; but is passed from death unto life. Verily, verily, I say unto you, The hour is coming, and now is, when the dead shall hear the voice of the Son of God: and they that hear shall live. For as the Father hath life in himself; so hath he given to the Son to have life in himself; And hath given him authority to execute judgment also, because he is the Son of man.

**Context:** Continuation of the John 5 pericope. V.26 is theologically decisive: 'as the Father has life in himself, so he has given to the Son to have life in himself.' Parser confirms: hosper (G5618 adverb) gar ho Pater echei (G2192 V-PAI-3S present) zoen en heauto, houtos kai to Huio edoken (G1325 V-AAI-3S aorist) zoen echein (G2192 V-PAN infinitive) en heauto. The Father possesses self-existent life (present tense — ongoing), and gave the Son the same quality (aorist — a definite act). The 'giving' does not imply inferiority — the Son has the SAME kind of life (life-in-himself) that the Father has. V.27 adds authority (exousia) to judge because he is 'Son of man' — connecting the incarnation to the judicial role.

**Notes:** The edoken (aorist of didomi) in v.26 raises the 'eternal generation' question: does the 'giving' refer to the incarnation (the Son was granted this authority in the incarnate state) or to the eternal relation (the Father eternally communicates self-existent life to the Son)? The Augustinian middle ground (K.E. Johnson) reads this as eternal generation — the Son's existence is 'from' the Father eternally. Either reading supports the main conclusion: the Son possesses the SAME quality of life as the Father.

### Resurrection power and 'I seek not my own will but the Father's will'

> Marvel not at this: for the hour is coming, in the which all that are in the graves shall hear his voice, And shall come forth; they that have done good, unto the resurrection of life; and they that have done evil, unto the resurrection of damnation. I can of mine own self do nothing: as I hear, I judge: and my judgment is just; because I seek not mine own will, but the will of the Father which hath sent me.

**Context:** Conclusion of the pericope. V.28-29: the Son has resurrection power — ALL in the graves hear HIS voice. V.30 returns to the dependence theme: 'I can of mine own self do nothing.' Parser confirms v.30: Ou dynamai (G1410 V-PNI-1S) ego poiein (G4160 V-PAN) ap emautou (G1683 F-1GSM) ouden. Then the critical will statement: ou zeto (G2212 V-PAI-1S 'I seek not') to thelema (G2307 N-ASN) to emon (G1699 possessive) alla to thelema tou pempsantos (G3992 V-AAP-GSM 'the one having sent') me. Two distinct thelema references: 'my will' vs. 'the will of the one who sent me.' The sending verb (pempo, aorist participle) identifies this as mission language.

**Notes:** V.30's 'my will vs. Father's will' is diagnostic for the 'one will or two wills' question. The Gethsemane parallel (Luke 22:42) is crucial: there, 'my will' clearly refers to Christ's HUMAN will shrinking from the cup. The same pattern likely applies here — 'my will' = the human will, 'the Father's will' = the shared divine purpose. The word study for G2307 (thelema, 64 occurrences) confirms this as a Johannine pattern: John 4:34, 5:30, 6:38 all use the same construction.

### Crux text: 'neither the Son, but the Father' — incarnational ignorance

> But of that day and [that] hour knoweth no man, no, not the angels which are in heaven, neither the Son, but the Father.

**Context:** Olivet Discourse. Jesus speaks of the day and hour of the second coming. The hierarchical structure: no man (oudeis) < angels in heaven < the Son < the Father. Parser confirms: oiden (G1492 V-RAI-3S perfect active indicative — 'knows' with present force). The word study for G1492 (oida, 334 occurrences) confirms this is a defective perfect with present meaning — 'possesses settled knowledge.' The exclusive modifier structure: oudeis...oude hoi aggeloi...oude ho Huios, ei me ho Pater — 'no one...not even the angels...not even the Son, except the Father.' The grammar reference for perfect tense confirms oida as a stative perfect functioning as present.

- οἶδεν (G1492) — V-RAI-3S — Perf Act Ind Sg thirdPers
- Υἱός (G5207) — N-NSM — Nom Sg M
- Πατήρ (G3962) — N-NSM — Nom Sg M

**Notes:** The Macknight minority reading (oiden as causative 'make known') is not supported by any of the 334 NT uses of oida — the word study shows it consistently means 'know/be aware of' in present force. The textual variant in Matt 24:36 is significant: N1904 includes 'oude ho Huios' (neither the Son) while TR omits it. The text comparison tool confirms TEXTUAL VARIANT: N1904 has Strong's 5207 (huios) and 3761 (oude) that TR lacks. This means the Markan text (which preserves 'nor the Son' in all MSS) is the more secure witness. Compare with John 2:24-25 ('he knew all men'), John 16:30 ('thou knowest all things'), and Matt 28:18 ('all power given unto me') — these suggest the limitation was temporary and incarnational.

### Crux passage: eschatological subjection — hotan...tote temporal construction

> Then [cometh] the end, when he shall have delivered up the kingdom to God, even the Father; when he shall have put down all rule and all authority and power. For he must reign, till he hath put all enemies under his feet. The last enemy [that] shall be destroyed [is] death. For he hath put all things under his feet. But when he saith, all things are put under [him, it is] manifest that he is excepted, which did put all things under him. And when all things shall be subdued unto him, then shall the Son also himself be subject unto him that put all things under him, that God may be all in all.

**Context:** Paul's resurrection argument. The passage is embedded in Adam-Christ typology (vv.20-23, 45-49). V.24: eita to telos hotan paradidoi (G3860 V-PAS-3S present subjunctive) ten basileian (G932 N-ASF) to Theo kai Patri — 'then the end, when he delivers the kingdom to God and Father.' V.25: dei (G1163 'it is necessary') auton basileuein achri hou the (G5087 V-2AAS-3S aorist subjunctive) — 'he must reign UNTIL he has put.' The achri hou ('until') indicates a temporal endpoint. V.27: hupetaxen (G5293 V-AAI-3S aorist active — God subjected all things) + hupotetaktai (G5293 V-RPI-3S perfect passive — all things have been subjected). V.28: THE CRITICAL CONSTRUCTION — hotan (G3752 'when') de hupotagei (G5293 V-2APS-3S aorist passive subjunctive) auto ta panta, tote (G5119 'then') kai autos ho Huios hupotagēsetai (G5293 V-2FPI-3S FUTURE passive indicative). The hotan...tote construction with future passive indicative (hupotagēsetai) indicates a FUTURE CHANGE OF STATE — the Son will be subjected at a specific future point, not eternally.

- παραδιδοῖ (G3860) — V-PAS-3S — Pres Act Subj Sg thirdPers
- βασιλείαν (G932) — N-ASF — Acc Sg F
- καταργήσῃ (G2673) — V-AAS-3S — Aor Act Subj Sg thirdPers

**Notes:** The parser output is decisive for the Gons argument: hupotagēsetai is V-2FPI-3S (future passive indicative). This is NOT present tense (which would indicate ongoing state) but FUTURE tense — something that WILL happen. The hotan...tote construction marks a temporal transition: 'when X happens, THEN Y will follow.' The word study for G5293 (hupotasso, 49 occurrences) shows the 'divine cosmic ordering' category — God subjects all things to Christ, then Christ voluntarily subjects himself to God. The hina clause (hina e ho Theos panta en pasin — 'that God may be all in all') gives the PURPOSE: the subjection enables a new eschatological state. The Psalm 110:1 citation (v.25, achri = 'until,' citing 'sit at my right hand UNTIL I make your enemies your footstool') reinforces temporality. OT parallels: Isaiah 9:5 (Jesaia) scored highest among OT matches for this passage.

### Interpretive key: kenosis hymn — voluntary self-emptying and exaltation

> Let this mind be in you, which was also in Christ Jesus: Who, being in the form of God, thought it not robbery to be equal with God: But made himself of no reputation, and took upon him the form of a servant, and was made in the likeness of men: And being found in fashion as a man, he humbled himself, and became obedient unto death, even the death of the cross. Wherefore God also hath highly exalted him, and given him a name which is above every name: That at the name of Jesus every knee should bow, of [things] in heaven, and [things] in earth, and [things] under the earth; And [that] every tongue should confess that Jesus Christ [is] Lord, to the glory of God the Father.

**Context:** The kenosis hymn. Parser confirms the critical structure: V.6: hos en morphe (G3444 N-DSF) Theou (G2316 N-GSM) hyparchon (G5225 V-PAP-NSM present active participle — 'already existing in') ouch harpagmon (G725 N-ASM) hegesato (G2233 V-ADI-3S aorist middle) to einai (G1510 V-PAN infinitive) isa (G2470 A-APN) Theo (G2316 N-DSM). V.7: alla heauton (G1438 F-3ASM reflexive) ekenosen (G2758 V-AAI-3S aorist active — 'he emptied himself') morphen (G3444 N-ASF) doulou (G1401 N-GSM) labon (G2983 V-2AAP-NSM aorist participle — 'having taken'), en homoiomati (G3667 N-DSN) anthropon (G444 N-GPM) genomenos (G1096 V-2ADP-NSM aorist middle participle). The word studies for G3444 (morphe), G2758 (kenoo), G725 (harpagmos), and G2470 (isos) are all critical. The hyparchon participle (present tense = pre-existing continuous state) establishes that Christ WAS in the form of God BEFORE the self-emptying. The aorist ekenosen marks a definite act. The labon ('having taken') is an aorist of addition — he took servant form without losing divine form.

**Notes:** The word study for G725 (harpagmos, hapax legomenon) strongly favors the res rapta reading: Christ already possessed equality with God (established by hyparchon) and did not exploit it for selfish advantage. The -mos suffix indicates the act/attitude of seizing, not the thing seized. The exaltation (vv.9-11) uses hyperupsosen ('highly exalted,' G5251) + edoken ('gave,' G1325) — suggesting RESTORATION of pre-incarnation status (cf. John 17:5 'the glory which I had with you before the world was'). If the exaltation restores pre-existing glory, the subordination was temporary. OT parallels for v.7: Isaiah 52:14 (form), Isa 42:1 (servant of YHWH), Psalm 132:10 (servant). The grammar reference for active-reflexive-self-action covers heauton ekenosen — Christ acted on himself voluntarily.

### Foundational prologue: 'the Word was God' — pre-incarnation deity

> In the beginning was the Word, and the Word was with God, and the Word was God. The same was in the beginning with God. All things were made by him; and without him was not any thing made that was made.

**Context:** Johannine prologue. Parser confirms: V.1a: En (G1722 prep) arche (G746 N-DSF) en (G1510 V-IAI-3S imperfect — continuous past being) ho Logos (G3056 N-NSM). V.1b: ho Logos en (imperfect again) pros (G4314) ton Theon (G2316 N-ASM with article — 'the God'). V.1c: Theos (G2316 N-NSM WITHOUT article — anarthrous predicate nominative) en ho Logos. The verb en (imperfect of eimi, G1510) indicates continuous, timeless existence — 'was being' in the beginning. The tense contrast with egeneto (G1096, aorist — punctiliar coming into existence) in v.3 and v.14 is critical: the Word WAS (continuous being), but all things CAME INTO BEING (punctiliar creation) through him.

- ἦν (G1510) — V-IAI-3S — Impf Act Ind Sg thirdPers (3x)
- Λόγος (G3056) — N-NSM — Nom Sg M
- Θεόν (G2316) — N-ASM — Acc Sg M (with article — ton Theon)

**Notes:** The anarthrous theos in v.1c is a predicate nominative — it asserts the nature/quality of the Word ('the Word was divine/God in nature') without identifying the Word as the totality of God (which the article would imply — 'the Word was THE God' would collapse the Father-Word distinction). The grammar reference for absolute-ego-eimi is relevant here — John 8:58's ego eimi builds on this prologue foundation. The word studies for G1510 (eimi) and G1096 (ginomai) confirm the ontological divide: eimi = being, ginomai = becoming. The Word stands on the being side.

### Unity prayer: 'one as we are one' and pre-foundational love

> That they all may be one; as thou, Father, [art] in me, and I in thee, that they also may be one in us: that the world may believe that thou hast sent me. And the glory which thou gavest me I have given them; that they may be one, even as we are one: I in them, and thou in me, that they may be made perfect in one; and that the world may know that thou hast sent me, and hast loved them, as thou hast loved me. Father, I will that they also, whom thou hast given me, be with me where I am; that they may behold my glory, which thou hast given me: for thou lovedst me before the foundation of the world.

The shaliach principle from the word study: the sent one carries the sender's full authority — the sending does not establish subordination but authorized representation.

### Supporting: OT sending + Trinity language

### Supporting: pre-existence + universal authority — 'the Father had given all things into his hands'

**Context:** Before the foot-washing. Three-part statement: (1) the Father gave all things to him, (2) he came from God, (3) he went to God. Pre-existence, universal authority, and return to the Father — all in one verse.

**Notes:** The knowledge claim ('Jesus knowing') paired with 'all things into his hands' contrasts with the Mark 13:32 ignorance — this is during the same earthly ministry, yet Jesus possesses comprehensive divine knowledge alongside a specific eschatological limitation.

### Supporting: shared divine possessions — 'All things that the Father hath are mine'

> All things that the Father hath are mine: therefore said I, that he shall take of mine, and shall shew [it] unto you.

**Context:** Upper Room Discourse. Jesus claims everything the Father possesses is also his. This is an equality claim — shared possession of all divine attributes.

**Notes:** If all the Father's things belong to the Son, the subordination cannot be ontological — there is nothing the Father has that the Son lacks.

### Supporting: voluntary mission framework — came from Father, return to Father

> I came forth from the Father, and am come into the world: again, I leave the world, and go to the Father.

**Context:** Late in the Upper Room Discourse. The full mission arc: origin (came from the Father), incarnation (come into the world), departure (leave the world), return (go to the Father). The subordination belongs to the middle segment — the incarnate phase.

**Notes:** The 'came from / going to' framework is the clearest voluntary-mission statement in John. The Son's point of origin and destination are both WITH the Father — the earthly phase is the exception, not the norm.

### Supporting: Jesus knew his hour had come — incarnational knowledge claim

> Now before the feast of the passover, when Jesus knew that his hour was come that he should depart out of this world unto the Father, having loved his own which were in the world, he loved them unto the end.

**Context:** Opening of the Last Supper narrative. Jesus 'knew' (eidos, from oida G1492) that his hour had come. The same verb (oida) used in Mark 13:32 for what Jesus did NOT know is used here for what he DID know.

**Notes:** The same verb oida appears positively here and negatively in Mark 13:32 — Jesus possessed settled knowledge of some things (his hour, human hearts) while being limited regarding others (the specific day and hour of the second coming). This selective limitation is characteristic of incarnational self-limitation, not ontological inferiority.

### Supporting: Servant of YHWH — voluntary servanthood

> Behold my servant, whom I uphold; mine elect, [in whom] my soul delighteth; I have put my spirit upon him: he shall bring forth judgment to the Gentiles.

**Context:** First Servant Song. YHWH introduces his servant — upheld, chosen, endowed with the Spirit. The servant is given a mission (bring forth judgment to the Gentiles). This is the OT background for the servant Christology of Phil 2:7 and the sending vocabulary of John's Gospel.

**Notes:** The servant language in Isaiah is mission language — the servant is commissioned for a task. The servant's relationship to YHWH is one of authorized agency, not ontological inferiority. This appears in the Nave's TRINITY topic.

### Supporting: messianic enthronement — begetting language with sovereignty

> I will declare the decree: the LORD hath said unto me, Thou [art] my Son; this day have I begotten thee. Ask of me, and I shall give [thee] the heathen [for] thine inheritance, and the uttermost parts of the earth [for] thy possession. Thou shalt break them with a rod of iron; thou shalt dash them in pieces like a potter's vessel. Be wise now therefore, O ye kings: be instructed, ye judges of the earth. Serve the LORD with fear, and rejoice with trembling. Kiss the Son, lest he be angry, and ye perish [from] the way, when his wrath is kindled but a little. Blessed [are] all they that put their trust in him.

**Context:** Royal/messianic psalm. V.7: 'Thou art my Son, this day have I begotten thee' — cited in Heb 1:5 and Acts 13:33. V.12: 'Kiss the Son' — homage to the Messianic king. The 'begetting' language has been interpreted as (a) the incarnation, (b) the resurrection, or (c) the eternal generation of the Son.

**Notes:** The psalm combines sonship language with sovereignty claims: the Son receives the nations as inheritance and rules with a rod of iron. 'Blessed are all who put their trust in him' (v.12) — trust directed to a human king would be idolatry; it is appropriate only if the Son is divine.

### Supporting: mutual exclusive knowledge — 'no man knoweth the Son, but the Father'

> All things are delivered unto me of my Father: and no man knoweth the Son, but the Father; neither knoweth any man the Father, save the Son, and [he] to whomsoever the Son will reveal [him].

**Context:** Jesus claims mutual exclusive knowledge between Father and Son — only the Father fully knows the Son, and only the Son fully knows the Father. This is a reciprocal relationship, not a hierarchical one.

**Notes:** The reciprocal knowledge claim ('no one knows the Son except the Father; no one knows the Father except the Son') establishes equivalence of relationship. If the Son can be fully known only by the Father, the Son must be of infinite depth — i.e., divine. NT parallels tool scored this at 0.374 for Mark 13:32 (vocabulary: father, know).

### Supporting: the Father's works and testimony given to the Son

> But I have greater witness than [that] of John: for the works which the Father hath given me to finish, the same works that I do, bear witness of me, that the Father hath sent me. And the Father himself, which hath sent me, hath borne witness of me. Ye have neither heard his voice at any time, nor seen his shape.

**Context:** Continuation of John 5 pericope. V.36: The Father gave Jesus works to finish — these works testify that the Father sent him. V.37: The Father himself bears witness to the Son. The sending (apostello/pempo) framework is prominent.

**Notes:** The 'works given to finish' + 'Father sent me' language integrates the delegated-works framework with the mission vocabulary. The works are not lesser works but the FATHER'S works — the Son does what the Father does (v.19).
