# Verses

## Question
Does the New Testament itself — written 200+ years before the Council of Nicaea — teach the deity of Christ, or was this a 4th-century invention? Survey the Christological claims across the earliest NT documents and their dating.

### Pre-Pauline creedal formula — earliest Christian tradition (30s-40s AD, letter c. 55 AD)

> For I delivered unto you first of all that which I also received, how that Christ died for our sins according to the scriptures; And that he was buried, and that he rose again the third day according to the scriptures: And that he was seen of Cephas, then of the twelve:

**Context:** Paul transmits a received creedal formula using technical rabbinic transmission language (paralambanō/paradidōmi). This pre-dates Paul's letter — scholars date the formula to within 2-5 years of the crucifixion. 'Christ died for our sins' presupposes a salvific framework requiring more than a mere human death.

**Notes:** Date: formula 30s-40s AD; letter c. 55 AD. The technical language 'delivered/received' (paredōka/parelabon) marks this as a received tradition, not Paul's composition. The creedal core already treats Christ's death as atoning and his resurrection as scripturally necessary.

The creative prepositions ek (source) and dia (agency) attribute creation to both Father and Son.

### Maranatha — Aramaic prayer to Jesus as Lord from Palestinian community (30s-40s AD, letter c. 55 AD)

> If any man love not the Lord Jesus Christ, let him be Anathema Maranatha.

**Context:** Paul preserves an Aramaic prayer formula untranslated in a Greek letter to Gentile readers. 'Maranatha' (maran atha = 'Our Lord, come!' or marana tha = 'Come, O Lord!') is a liturgical invocation addressed to Jesus as 'Lord' (maran) from Aramaic-speaking Palestinian Jewish Christians.

**Notes:** Date: Aramaic formula 30s-40s AD; letter c. 55 AD. The fact that Paul preserves this in Aramaic without translation means Gentile Christians already knew it — it was a fixed liturgical formula. An Aramaic prayer to Jesus as 'Lord' from Palestinian Jewish Christians rules out the hypothesis that Christ-devotion was a later Gentile innovation.

### Earliest NT document identifies Jesus as God's Son, deliverer from wrath (c. 50 AD)

> And to wait for his Son from heaven, whom he raised from the dead, [even] Jesus, which delivered us from the wrath to come.

**Context:** 1 Thessalonians is the earliest extant NT document (c. 50 AD). Even in this earliest letter, Jesus is identified as God's Son from heaven who delivers from eschatological wrath — functions reserved for YHWH in the OT.

**Notes:** Date: c. 50 AD (earliest NT document). Already contains: (1) Son of God title, (2) heavenly origin, (3) resurrection, (4) deliverance from divine wrath — all high Christological categories. This is 275 years before Nicaea.

### Singular verb governing both God and Lord Jesus — grammatical co-identification (c. 50 AD)

> Now God himself and our Father, and our Lord Jesus Christ, direct our way unto you.

**Context:** In the earliest NT document, Paul uses a singular verb (kateuthynai, aorist optative 3rd singular) governing two subjects: 'God our Father AND our Lord Jesus Christ.' A singular verb with compound subjects implies a unified agency.

**Notes:** Date: c. 50 AD. The singular verb with a compound subject (God the Father + Lord Jesus Christ) is a grammatical anomaly unless Paul conceives of their agency as unified. This is the same grammatical pattern found in 2 Thess 2:16-17.

### YHWH-text transfer: Jer 9:23-24 'glory in YHWH' applied to Christ (c. 55 AD)

> That, according as it is written, He that glorieth, let him glory in the Lord.

**Context:** Paul quotes Jeremiah 9:23-24 where the subject is YHWH and applies it to Christ ('the Lord' = Christ in Pauline usage). The OT text reads 'let him that glorieth glory in this, that he understandeth and knoweth me, that I am the LORD.'

**Notes:** Date: c. 55 AD. YHWH-text transfer #1 (Hurtado): Jer 9:24 has YHWH as subject; Paul applies it to Christ as 'the Lord.' This is deliberate, not accidental — Paul knows the OT context.

### YHWH-text transfer: Isa 40:13 'mind of YHWH' applied as 'mind of Christ' (c. 55 AD)

> For who hath known the mind of the Lord, that he may instruct him? But we have the mind of Christ.

**Context:** Paul quotes Isaiah 40:13 ('Who hath directed the Spirit of YHWH, or being his counseller hath taught him?') and identifies 'the mind of the Lord' as 'the mind of Christ.'

**Notes:** Date: c. 55 AD. YHWH-text transfer #2 (Hurtado): Isaiah 40:13 has YHWH's mind as incomprehensible; Paul substitutes 'Christ' for YHWH. The transfer is syntactically explicit — 'nous kyriou' becomes 'nous Christou.'

### Christ hymn — image of God, firstborn, all things created by/through/for him, fullness dwells (c. 60 AD)

> Who is the image of the invisible God, the firstborn of every creature: For by him were all things created, that are in heaven, and that are in earth, visible and invisible, whether [they be] thrones, or dominions, or principalities, or powers: all things were created by him, and for him: And he is before all things, and by him all things consist. And he is the head of the body, the church: who is the beginning, the firstborn from the dead; that in all [things] he might have the preeminence. For it pleased [the Father] that in him should all fulness dwell; And, having made peace through the blood of his cross, by him to reconcile all things unto himself; by him, [I say], whether [they be] things in earth, or things in heaven.

**Context:** The Colossian Christ hymn presents the most comprehensive single-passage creation Christology in the NT. Christ is the eikon (image) of the invisible God, prototokos (firstborn — sovereignty title, not createdness) of all creation. The triple preposition pattern — en auto (in him), di autou (through him), eis auton (for him) — attributes creation's origin, agency, and purpose to Christ.

**Notes:** Date: c. 60 AD. prototokos (G4416) is a sovereignty title, not a createdness marker — v.16's 'by him ALL things were created' makes a partitive reading self-contradictory. The en/dia/eis preposition triad parallels what is said of God in Rom 11:36. pro panton (before all things) asserts temporal priority. synesteken (consist/hold together) asserts ongoing sustaining. pleroma (fullness) — all divine fullness dwells in Christ.

### Systematic deity argument: heir, creator, radiance, express image, sustainer, purifier, enthroned (c. 60-70 AD)

> God, who at sundry times and in divers manners spake in time past unto the fathers by the prophets, Hath in these last days spoken unto us by [his] Son, whom he hath appointed heir of all things, by whom also he made the worlds; Who being the brightness of [his] glory, and the express image of his person, and upholding all things by the word of his power, when he had by himself purged our sins, sat down on the right hand of the Majesty on high; Being made so much better than the angels, as he hath by inheritance obtained a more excellent name than they.

**Context:** Hebrews opens with the most sustained deity argument in the NT. In four verses, the Son is identified as: (1) heir of all things, (2) agent of creation ('by whom he made the worlds'), (3) apaugasma (radiance/effulgence) of God's glory (hapax G541), (4) charakter (exact representation/impress) of God's hypostasis (hapax G5481), (5) sustainer of all things by his word, (6) purifier of sins, (7) enthroned at God's right hand.

**Notes:** Date: c. 60-70 AD. apaugasma (G541, hapax) = radiance streaming from the source, not reflected light — the Son IS the outshining of the Father's glory. charakter (G5481, hapax) = exact impress, like a seal in wax — point-for-point correspondence. hypostasis (G5287) = substance/being/person — the Son is the exact representation of the Father's very being.

### Jesus's ego eimi before the Sanhedrin + Dan 7:13 — blasphemy verdict (c. 65 AD)

> But he held his peace, and answered nothing. Again the high priest asked him, and said unto him, Art thou the Christ, the Son of the Blessed? And Jesus said, I am: and ye shall see the Son of man sitting on the right hand of power, and coming in the clouds of heaven.

**Context:** Mark's Gospel (earliest, c. 65 AD) records Jesus's ego eimi before the Sanhedrin in response to 'Art thou the Christ, the Son of the Blessed?' Jesus combines the absolute 'I am' with Dan 7:13 Son of Man language — sitting at God's right hand and coming on clouds. The Sanhedrin's immediate verdict: blasphemy worthy of death (Mark 14:64, cf. **Notes:** Date: c. 65 AD (earliest Gospel). ego eimi (G1473 + G1510) in Mark 14:62 parallels the absolute ego eimi pattern of John's Gospel. The Sanhedrin — trained Torah experts — heard a deity claim deserving Lev 24:16 execution. Their contemporaneous verdict is evidence that Jesus's claim was understood as a deity claim, not merely a messianic one.

### Jesus claims authority to forgive sins — divine prerogative (c. 65 AD)

> When Jesus saw their faith, he said unto the sick of the palsy, Son, thy sins be forgiven thee. But there were certain of the scribes sitting there, and reasoning in their hearts, Why doth this [man] thus speak blasphemies? who can forgive sins but God only? And immediately when Jesus perceived in his spirit that they so reasoned within themselves, he said unto them, Why reason ye these things in your hearts? Whether is it easier to say to the sick of the palsy, [Thy] sins be forgiven thee; or to say, Arise, and take up thy bed, and walk? But that ye may know that the Son of man hath power on earth to forgive sins, (he saith to the sick of the palsy,)

**Context:** The scribes correctly identify that forgiving sins is a divine prerogative ('who can forgive sins but God only?'). Jesus does not correct their theology — instead, he proves his authority to forgive by healing the paralytic. The logic is: only God forgives sins; Jesus forgives sins; therefore Jesus exercises divine authority.

**Notes:** Date: c. 65 AD. The scribes' reasoning is theologically correct — forgiveness of sins against God belongs to God alone (Isa 43:25). Jesus's response does not deny this premise but claims the authority and proves it with a miracle. exousia (G1849) — authority/power on earth to forgive sins.

### Trinitarian baptismal formula and universal authority (c. 70-85 AD)

> And Jesus came and spake unto them, saying, All power is given unto me in heaven and in earth. Go ye therefore, and teach all nations, baptizing them in the name of the Father, and of the Son, and of the Holy Ghost:

**Context:** The post-resurrection Jesus claims 'all authority in heaven and earth' (pasa exousia) — a claim that parallels Daniel 7:14's 'dominion and glory and a kingdom.' The trinitarian baptismal formula places Father, Son, and Holy Spirit under a single 'name' (onoma, singular) — three persons sharing one name.

**Notes:** Date: c. 70-85 AD. pasa exousia = all authority in heaven and earth — a universal sovereignty claim. The baptismal formula uses singular 'name' (onoma) for three persons, implying shared identity. This is a pre-Nicene trinitarian confession embedded in the earliest Gospel traditions.

### Birth announcement — Savior, Christ, Lord (c. 70-85 AD)

> For unto you is born this day in the city of David a Saviour, which is Christ the Lord.

**Context:** The angelic birth announcement uses three titles: soter (Savior, G4990), christos (Christ/Anointed, G5547), and kyrios (Lord, G2962). In the OT, YHWH alone is Savior (Isa 43:11). kyrios is the LXX rendering of YHWH. All three divine titles applied at birth.

**Notes:** Date: c. 70-85 AD. Three divine titles at birth: soter (YHWH alone is Savior — Isa 43:11), christos (the Anointed One), kyrios (YHWH's LXX title). The concentration of divine titles in a birth announcement means deity identity was not a post-resurrection invention.

**Notes:** The two-elohim pattern: the addressee is called 'God' (elohim) while also having 'God, your God' (elohim eloheka). This OT precedent shows that being addressed as God while having God is not a contradiction — it is the pattern the NT applies to the Father-Son relationship.

### OT prophecy: Mighty God, Everlasting Father — divine titles for the messianic child

> For unto us a child is born, unto us a son is given: and the government shall be upon his shoulder: and his name shall be called Wonderful, Counseller, The mighty God, The everlasting Father, The Prince of Peace.

**Context:** Isaiah's messianic prophecy applies divine titles to the coming child: el gibbor ('Mighty God') — the same title applied to YHWH in Isaiah 10:21 — and abi-ad ('Everlasting Father' / 'Father of Eternity').

**Notes:** Hebrew: el gibbor ('Mighty God') is the same phrase used for YHWH in Isa 10:21. abi-ad ('Father of Eternity') attributes eternal existence. These are not honorary titles — they are divine-nature titles on a human child.

### Worship refusal: Peter refuses Cornelius's worship — 'I myself also am a man' (c. 62 AD)

> But Peter took him up, saying, Stand up; I myself also am a man.

**Context:** When Cornelius falls at Peter's feet and worships (proskyneo), Peter immediately refuses: 'I myself also am a man.' This establishes the creature-refusal side of the worship binary.

**Notes:** Date: c. 62 AD. Peter's refusal is automatic and immediate — a creature may not accept proskyneo. Contrast with Christ, who accepts worship repeatedly (Matt 14:33, 28:9, John 9:38, Heb 1:6, Rev 5:13-14).

### Worship refusal: Paul and Barnabas refuse worship at Lystra (c. 62 AD)

> And saying, Sirs, why do ye these things? We also are men of like passions with you, and preach unto you that ye should turn from these vanities unto the living God, which made heaven, and earth, and the sea, and all things that are therein:

**Context:** When the Lystrans attempt to worship Paul and Barnabas as gods, they tear their clothes and refuse — 'We also are men of like passions with you.' Another creature-refusal establishing the binary.

**Notes:** Date: c. 62 AD. Paul and Barnabas's violent reaction (tearing clothes) to offered worship establishes how creatures must respond. Christ never has this reaction.

### Worship refusal: Angel refuses John's worship — 'Worship God' (c. 95 AD)

> And I fell at his feet to worship him. And he said unto me, See [thou do it] not: I am thy fellowservant, and of thy brethren that have the testimony of Jesus: worship God: for the testimony of Jesus is the spirit of prophecy.

**Context:** An angel refuses John's worship: 'See thou do it not — I am thy fellowservant... worship God.' The angel directs worship exclusively to God. Christ, by contrast, receives the identical worship in Rev 5:13-14.

**Notes:** Date: c. 95 AD. The angel's refusal + redirection ('worship God') is decisive: creatures must not accept proskyneo; only God may. In the same book, the Lamb receives identical worship (Rev 5:13-14) without refusal.

### Worship refusal: Angel refuses worship again — 'Worship God' (c. 95 AD)

> And I John saw these things, and heard [them]. And when I had heard and seen, I fell down to worship before the feet of the angel which shewed me these things. Then saith he unto me, See [thou do it] not: for I am thy fellowservant, and of thy brethren the prophets, and of them which keep the sayings of this book: worship God.

**Context:** Repeated refusal scene — a second angel refuses John's worship with the same formula. The repetition emphasizes the principle: creatures refuse worship; only God accepts it.

**Notes:** Date: c. 95 AD. Two angel-refusal scenes in Revelation (19:10 and 22:8-9) bracket the book's climax. The binary is structurally reinforced: angels refuse worship → Christ receives worship → therefore Christ is not a creature.

### Counter-evidence: 'The Father is greater than I' — functional subordination (c. 90 AD)

> Ye have heard how I said unto you, I go away, and come [again] unto you. If ye loved me, ye would rejoice, because I said, I go unto the Father: for my Father is greater than I.

**Context:** Jesus says 'the Father is greater (meizon, G3187) than I.' This must be read alongside John 10:30 ('I and the Father are one') and John 5:18 ('equal with God'). The same author who records 'greater' also records 'equal' and 'one.' meizon can denote functional superiority within a voluntary arrangement (the kenotic state of Phil 2:7) rather than ontological inequality.

**Notes:** Date: c. 90 AD. meizon (G3187) = greater. Read within the kenotic framework: during the incarnation, the Son voluntarily submitted to the Father's will (Phil 2:7-8). The same Gospel that records 'greater' also records 'equal with God' (5:18), 'I and the Father are one' (10:30), and 'My Lord and my God' (20:28).

### Counter-evidence: The Son subjects himself — eschatological action (c. 55 AD)

> And when all things shall be subdued unto him, then shall the Son also himself be subject unto him that put all things under him, that God may be all in all.

**Context:** Paul describes an eschatological event: when all enemies are defeated, the Son will be subject (hupotasso, G5293) to the Father. This is a future, voluntary, functional action within the economy of redemption — not an ontological statement about the Son's nature.

**Notes:** Date: c. 55 AD. hupotasso (G5293) = arrange under/submit. The same author who writes this subordination text also writes the Shema redistribution (1 Cor 8:6), the YHWH-text transfers, and the 'Lord from heaven' (1 Cor 15:47). Functional subordination and ontological deity coexist in Paul's theology.

### Counter-evidence: The Son does not know the day/hour — kenotic limitation (c. 65 AD)

> But of that day and [that] hour knoweth no man, no, not the angels which are in heaven, neither the Son, but the Father.

**Context:** Jesus acknowledges that 'the Son' does not know the day of the Second Coming. This must be read within the kenotic framework of Phil 2:7 — during the incarnation, the Son voluntarily limited himself. The same Gospel records Jesus making deity claims (Mark 14:62, 2:5-10).

**Notes:** Date: c. 65 AD. The same author (Mark) who records this limitation also records Jesus's ego eimi before the Sanhedrin (14:62) and divine-prerogative forgiveness (2:5-10). Mark does not see a contradiction between kenotic limitation and deity.

### Counter-evidence: 'The only true God' — monotheistic confession (c. 90 AD)

> And this is life eternal, that they might know thee the only true God, and Jesus Christ, whom thou hast sent.

**Context:** Jesus calls the Father 'the only true God' (ton monon alethinon theon). Read alongside 1 John 5:20 where the same author applies 'the true God' (ho alethinos theos) to Jesus Christ. The 'only true God' language contrasts the true God with false gods (same pattern as 1 Thess 1:9), not the Father with the Son.

**Notes:** Date: c. 90 AD. monos (G3441) = only/alone. alethinos (G228) = true/genuine. The same author applies alethinos theos to Christ in 1 John 5:20. Prior study trin-03 demonstrated that 'only true God' contrasts genuine with false, not Father with Son.

### theiotes (divine quality) vs. theotes (deity) — the distinction (c. 57 AD)

> For the invisible things of him from the creation of the world are clearly seen, being understood by the things that are made, [even] his eternal power and Godhead; so that they are without excuse:

**Context:** Paul uses theiotes (G2305, divine quality/divinity) for God's attributes visible in creation — divine power and nature perceptible through the created order. Contrast with theotes (G2320, deity/Godhood) in Col 2:9, which denotes the full essence of God dwelling in Christ.

**Notes:** Date: c. 57 AD. theiotes (G2305) = divine quality, attributes — what can be observed in nature. theotes (G2320) = deity itself, the being of God — what dwells fully in Christ. The distinction matters: creation reveals divine qualities; Christ embodies deity itself.

### All fullness pleased to dwell in Christ (c. 60 AD)

> For it pleased [the Father] that in him should all fulness dwell;

**Context:** Paul states that 'all fullness' (pan to pleroma) was pleased to dwell (katoikesai) in Christ. This anticipates the more explicit Col 2:9 where the fullness is identified as theotes (deity). pleroma (G4138) = the totality/completeness.

**Notes:** Date: c. 60 AD. pleroma (G4138) = fullness/completeness. The 'all fullness' that dwells in Christ is specified in 2:9 as the fullness of theotes (deity). katoikesai (aorist infinitive of katoikeo, G2730) = to take up permanent residence.
