    Question¶

    Does the New Testament itself — written 200+ years before the Council of Nicaea — teach the deity of Christ, or was this a 4th-century invention? Survey the Christological claims across the earliest NT documents and their dating.

    Pre-Pauline creedal formula — earliest Christian tradition (30s-40s AD, letter c. 55 AD)¶

    For I delivered unto you first of all that which I also received, how that Christ died for our sins according to the scriptures; And that he was buried, and that he rose again the third day according to the scriptures: And that he was seen of Cephas, then of the twelve:

    Context: Paul transmits a received creedal formula using technical rabbinic transmission language (paralambanō/paradidōmi). This pre-dates Paul's letter — scholars date the formula to within 2-5 years of the crucifixion. 'Christ died for our sins' presupposes a salvific framework requiring more than a mere human death.

    Notes: Date: formula 30s-40s AD; letter c. 55 AD. The technical language 'delivered/received' (paredōka/parelabon) marks this as a received tradition, not Paul's composition. The creedal core already treats Christ's death as atoning and his resurrection as scripturally necessary.

    Christian Shema — Deut 6:4 reformulated to include Christ (c. 55 AD)¶

    But to us [there is but] one God, the Father, of whom [are] all things, and we in him; and one Lord Jesus Christ, by whom [are] all things, and we by him.

    Context: Paul reformulates the Jewish Shema (Deut 6:4 'YHWH our God is one YHWH') by distributing 'one God' (heis theos) to the Father and 'one Lord' (heis kyrios) to Christ, placing Christ within the divine identity. The creative prepositions ek (source) and dia (agency) attribute creation to both Father and Son.

    Maranatha — Aramaic prayer to Jesus as Lord from Palestinian community (30s-40s AD, letter c. 55 AD)¶

    If any man love not the Lord Jesus Christ, let him be Anathema Maranatha.

    Context: Paul preserves an Aramaic prayer formula untranslated in a Greek letter to Gentile readers. 'Maranatha' (maran atha = 'Our Lord, come!' or marana tha = 'Come, O Lord!') is a liturgical invocation addressed to Jesus as 'Lord' (maran) from Aramaic-speaking Palestinian Jewish Christians.

    Notes: Date: Aramaic formula 30s-40s AD; letter c. 55 AD. The fact that Paul preserves this in Aramaic without translation means Gentile Christians already knew it — it was a fixed liturgical formula. An Aramaic prayer to Jesus as 'Lord' from Palestinian Jewish Christians rules out the hypothesis that Christ-devotion was a later Gentile innovation.

    Pre-Pauline Christ hymn — morphe theou, kenosis, Isa 45:23 transfer (hymn 30s-40s AD, letter c. 60 AD)¶

    Let this mind be in you, which was also in Christ Jesus: Who, being in the form of God, thought it not robbery to be equal with God: But made himself of no reputation, and took upon him the form of a servant, and was made in the likeness of men: And being found in fashion as a man, he humbled himself, and became obedient unto death, even the death of the cross. Wherefore God also hath highly exalted him, and given him a name which is above every name: That at the name of Jesus every knee should bow, of [things] in heaven, and [things] in earth, and [things] under the earth; And [that] every tongue should confess that Jesus Christ [is] Lord, to the glory of God the Father.

    Context: Widely regarded as a pre-Pauline liturgical hymn with three-stage Christology: (1) pre-existence in morphe theou (form of God), (2) kenosis/incarnation — self-emptying by taking servant form, (3) exaltation — hyper-hypsosen (highly exalted) and the name above every name. Vv. 10-11 echo Isaiah 45:23 where YHWH says 'unto me every knee shall bow, every tongue shall swear' — the most exclusive monotheistic text in the OT, applied to Christ.

    Earliest NT document identifies Jesus as God's Son, deliverer from wrath (c. 50 AD)¶

    And to wait for his Son from heaven, whom he raised from the dead, [even] Jesus, which delivered us from the wrath to come.

    Context: 1 Thessalonians is the earliest extant NT document (c. 50 AD). Even in this earliest letter, Jesus is identified as God's Son from heaven who delivers from eschatological wrath — functions reserved for YHWH in the OT.

    Notes: Date: c. 50 AD (earliest NT document). Already contains: (1) Son of God title, (2) heavenly origin, (3) resurrection, (4) deliverance from divine wrath — all high Christological categories. This is 275 years before Nicaea.

    Singular verb governing both God and Lord Jesus — grammatical co-identification (c. 50 AD)¶

    Now God himself and our Father, and our Lord Jesus Christ, direct our way unto you.

    Context: In the earliest NT document, Paul uses a singular verb (kateuthynai, aorist optative 3rd singular) governing two subjects: 'God our Father AND our Lord Jesus Christ.' A singular verb with compound subjects implies a unified agency.

    Notes: Date: c. 50 AD. The singular verb with a compound subject (God the Father + Lord Jesus Christ) is a grammatical anomaly unless Paul conceives of their agency as unified. This is the same grammatical pattern found in 2 Thess 2:16-17.

    YHWH-text transfer: Jer 9:23-24 'glory in YHWH' applied to Christ (c. 55 AD)¶

    That, according as it is written, He that glorieth, let him glory in the Lord.

    Context: Paul quotes Jeremiah 9:23-24 where the subject is YHWH and applies it to Christ ('the Lord' = Christ in Pauline usage). The OT text reads 'let him that glorieth glory in this, that he understandeth and knoweth me, that I am the LORD.'

    Notes: Date: c. 55 AD. YHWH-text transfer #1 (Hurtado): Jer 9:24 has YHWH as subject; Paul applies it to Christ as 'the Lord.' This is deliberate, not accidental — Paul knows the OT context.

    YHWH-text transfer: Isa 40:13 'mind of YHWH' applied as 'mind of Christ' (c. 55 AD)¶

    For who hath known the mind of the Lord, that he may instruct him? But we have the mind of Christ.

    Context: Paul quotes Isaiah 40:13 ('Who hath directed the Spirit of YHWH, or being his counseller hath taught him?') and identifies 'the mind of the Lord' as 'the mind of Christ.'

    Notes: Date: c. 55 AD. YHWH-text transfer #2 (Hurtado): Isaiah 40:13 has YHWH's mind as incomprehensible; Paul substitutes 'Christ' for YHWH. The transfer is syntactically explicit — 'nous kyriou' becomes 'nous Christou.'

    Christ as 'God over all, blessed forever' — Paul's explicit theos predication (c. 57 AD)¶

    Whose [are] the fathers, and of whom as concerning the flesh Christ [came], who is over all, God blessed for ever. Amen.

    Context: Paul lists Israel's privileges, culminating in Christ 'as concerning the flesh.' The phrase ho on epi panton theos eulogetos eis tous aionas ('who is over all, God blessed forever') is the syntactic continuation and most naturally refers to Christ. Metzger and Moo hold reading A (Christ = 'God over all') as the best reading.

    Context: Paul warns against tempting Christ as Israel tempted in the wilderness. In the OT narrative (Num 21:5-6), Israel tempted YHWH.

    Notes: Date: c. 55 AD. YHWH-identification: Israel tempted YHWH (Num 21:5-6), but Paul says they tempted Christ.

    Pre-Pauline creedal fragment: Son of David, declared Son of God by resurrection (c. 57 AD, formula earlier)¶

    Concerning his Son Jesus Christ our Lord, which was made of the seed of David according to the flesh; And declared [to be] the Son of God with power, according to the spirit of holiness, by the resurrection from the dead:

    Context: Paul cites a pre-existing creedal formula with two-stage Christology: Son of David (human lineage, kata sarka) and Son of God with power (declared by resurrection, kata pneuma).

    Notes: Date: formula pre-Pauline (40s-50s AD); letter c. 57 AD. The creedal structure marks this as received tradition.

    God sent forth his Son — pre-existence implied in earliest Paul (c. 48-49 AD)¶

    But when the fulness of the time was come, God sent forth his Son, made of a woman, made under the law,

    Context: Galatians is among Paul's earliest letters (c. 48-49 AD). God sent forth (exapesteilen) his Son implies the Son existed before being sent.

    Notes: Date: c. 48-49 AD. Pre-existence is implied: God SENT his Son.

    Pre-existence: though rich, became poor for your sakes (c. 56 AD)¶

    For ye know the grace of our Lord Jesus Christ, that, though he was rich, yet for your sakes he became poor, that ye through his poverty might be rich.

    Context: Paul describes Christ's incarnation as a voluntary impoverishment: though rich (pre-incarnate glory) he became poor (incarnation).

    Notes: Date: c. 56 AD. The rich-to-poor trajectory presupposes pre-existence.

    Christ as image (eikon) of God (c. 56 AD)¶

    In whom the god of this world hath blinded the minds of them which believe not, lest the light of the glorious gospel of Christ, who is the image of God, should shine unto them.

    Context: Paul calls Christ the image (eikon, G1504) of God — the visible manifestation of the invisible God.

    Notes: Date: c. 56 AD. eikon (G1504) = image/likeness. Three independent passages call Christ God's image: 2 Cor 4:4, Col 1:15, Heb 1:3.

    God manifest in the flesh — early creedal hymn (c. 63-65 AD)¶

    And without controversy great is the mystery of godliness: God was manifest in the flesh, justified in the Spirit, seen of angels, preached unto the Gentiles, believed on in the world, received up into glory.

    Context: An early creedal hymn with six lines describing Christ's incarnation and exaltation.

    Notes: Date: c. 63-65 AD. The hymnic structure (six clauses in three couplets) marks this as a pre-existing liturgical formula.

    Father commands angels to worship the Son — proskyneo directed at Christ (c. 60-70 AD)¶

    And again, when he bringeth in the firstbegotten into the world, he saith, And let all the angels of God worship him.

    Context: The Father commands all angels to worship (proskyneo) the Son. Angels refuse worship for themselves (Rev 19:10, 22:8-9) but are COMMANDED to give it to the Son.

    Notes: Date: c. 60-70 AD. proskyneo (G4352) directed at the Son by the Father's command.

    Worthy is the Lamb — divine-attribute doxology (c. 95 AD)¶

    Saying with a loud voice, Worthy is the Lamb that was slain to receive power, and riches, and wisdom, and strength, and honour, and glory, and blessing.

    Context: The heavenly host ascribes seven divine attributes to the Lamb: power, riches, wisdom, strength, honour, glory, blessing.

    Notes: Date: c. 95 AD. Seven divine attributes ascribed to the Lamb — complete divine doxology.

    OT source: Whoever calls on the name of YHWH shall be saved — applied to Christ in Rom 10:13¶

    And it shall come to pass, [that] whosoever shall call on the name of the LORD shall be delivered: for in mount Zion and in Jerusalem shall be deliverance, as the LORD hath said, and in the remnant whom the LORD shall call.

    Context: YHWH promises salvation to all who call on his name. Paul quotes this in Rom 10:13 and applies it to Jesus.

    Notes: Hebrew: kol asher-yiqra beshem YHWH yimmalet. Three occurrences of YHWH in this verse. Paul applies this YHWH-promise to Jesus.

    OT source: Who has known the mind of YHWH — Paul applies as mind of Christ in 1 Cor 2:16¶

    Who hath directed the Spirit of the LORD, or [being] his counseller hath taught him?

    Context: Isaiah declares YHWH's mind incomprehensible. Paul quotes this and substitutes 'the mind of Christ' for 'the mind of YHWH'.

    Notes: Hebrew: mi-tikken et-ruach YHWH. Paul takes nous kyriou (LXX) and identifies it as nous Christou (1 Cor 2:16).

    OT source: Let him that glorieth glory in YHWH — Paul applies to Christ in 1 Cor 1:31¶

    Thus saith the LORD, Let not the wise [man] glory in his wisdom, neither let the mighty [man] glory in his might, let not the rich [man] glory in his riches: But let him that glorieth glory in this, that he understandeth and knoweth me, that I [am] the LORD which exercise lovingkindness, judgment, and righteousness, in the earth: for in these [things] I delight, saith the LORD.

    Context: YHWH commands that boasting be directed only toward knowing HIM. Paul quotes this in 1 Cor 1:31 and applies it to Christ.

    Notes: Hebrew: yithalel hammithalel haskel weyado oti ani YHWH. Paul transfers this YHWH-boasting text to Christ.

    OT source: The earth is YHWH's — Paul applies to Christ in 1 Cor 10:26¶

    A Psalm of David. The earth [is] the LORD'S, and the fulness thereof; the world, and they that dwell therein.

    Context: A psalm declaring YHWH's ownership of all creation. Paul quotes this in 1 Cor 10:26 with 'the Lord' = Christ.

    Notes: Hebrew: la-YHWH ha-aretz u-meloah. Paul applies this in 1 Cor 10:26 where the Lord is Christ.

    OT wisdom personification: possessed/brought forth before the world — possible pre-existence background¶

    The LORD possessed me in the beginning of his way, before his works of old. I was set up from everlasting, from the beginning, or ever the earth was. When [there were] no depths, I was brought forth; when [there were] no fountains abounding with water. Before the mountains were settled, before the hills was I brought forth:

    Context: Personified Wisdom claims to have been possessed/established by YHWH before creation. Early Christian interpreters identified this Wisdom with the pre-incarnate Christ.

    Notes: Hebrew: YHWH qanani reshit darko. The verb qanah can mean possess or create. The pre-existence language parallels NT Christological claims.

    OT source: Son of Man receives everlasting dominion and worship — Jesus's self-designation¶

    I saw in the night visions, and, behold, [one] like the Son of man came with the clouds of heaven, and came to the Ancient of days, and they brought him near before him. And there was given him dominion, and glory, and a kingdom, that all people, nations, and languages, should serve him: his dominion [is] an everlasting dominion, which shall not pass away, and his kingdom [that] which shall not be destroyed.

    Context: Daniel sees a figure like a Son of Man approaching the Ancient of Days and receiving universal, everlasting dominion. All peoples serve (pelach, Aramaic) him. Jesus adopts Son of Man as his primary self-designation.

    Notes: Aramaic: pelach (serve/worship) in Dan 7:14 is the same word used for serving God in Dan 3:12, 28; 6:16, 20. Jesus combines this with ego eimi in Mark 14:62.
