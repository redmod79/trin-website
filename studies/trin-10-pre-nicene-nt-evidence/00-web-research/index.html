    Web Research Findings¶

    Study Question¶

    Does the New Testament itself — written 200+ years before the Council of Nicaea — teach the deity of Christ, or was this a 4th-century invention? Survey the Christological claims across the earliest NT documents and their dating. Also examine extra-biblical evidence from the earliest post-apostolic writers (Ignatius c. 108 AD, Polycarp c. 110 AD, Justin Martyr c. 150 AD, Irenaeus c. 180 AD) and non-Christian sources (Pliny the Younger c. 112 AD reporting Christians singing hymns "to Christ as to a god").

    Sources Found¶

    Larry W. Hurtado — Lord Jesus Christ: Devotion to Jesus in Earliest Christianity (Eerdmans, 2003)¶

    URL: https://pursuingveritas.com/2015/11/16/book-review-lord-jesus-christ-hurtado/ Key claims: - Hurtado argues that "exalted views of Jesus emerge well within the first decades of Christianity" — not centuries later. He calls the inauguration of early Christ-devotion a "big bang, an explosively rapid and impressively substantial Christological development in the earliest stage of the Christian movement." - Paul's communities practiced "functionally binitarian worship" — treating both God the Father and Jesus as divine figures simultaneously within a monotheistic framework. - Hurtado contends that understanding Christianity's origins requires examining devotional practices (hymns, prayers, invocation), not just doctrinal titles, because "worship was the context in which christological titles and other expressions of faith were given their specific meaning." - A "binitarian monotheism and Christ-devotion was adopted by the first disciples" at the very earliest stage — perhaps within months of the crucifixion. - The review describes the work as "overwhelmingly convincing and paradigm changing." Relevance: Central thesis for dating Christ-devotion to the earliest Christian community, decades before Nicaea. Verify against NT devotional passages (Phil 2:5-11, 1 Cor 8:6, Rev 5:13-14). Status: Lead to verify against Scripture

    Larry W. Hurtado — "YHWH Texts and Jesus: A Follow-up" (Blog, 2019)¶

    URL: https://larryhurtado.wordpress.com/2019/05/07/yhwh-texts-and-jesus-a-follow-up/ Key claims: - Paul deliberately applied OT YHWH texts to Jesus in multiple letters. Specific examples: - Romans 10:13 applies Joel 2:32 (calling on the name of YHWH) to Jesus - Romans 14:11 applies Isaiah 45:23 (every knee bows to YHWH) to Jesus - 1 Corinthians 1:31 applies Jeremiah 9:23-24 (boasting in YHWH) to Jesus - 1 Corinthians 2:16 applies Isaiah 40:13 (the mind of YHWH) to Jesus - 1 Corinthians 10:26 applies Psalm 24:1 (the earth is YHWH's) to Jesus - 2 Corinthians 10:17 applies Jeremiah 9:23-24 to Jesus again - Hurtado argues that early Greek OT manuscripts preserved YHWH in Hebrew characters rather than substituting kyrios, meaning Paul's application of these texts to Jesus was "not accidental confusion" but a "fully conscious and deliberate exegetical move." - This was "intentional and fueled by powerful convictions about Jesus' exalted status." Hebrew/Greek analysis: The kyrios title in the Septuagint renders YHWH approximately 7,000 times. Paul's application of these kyrios/YHWH texts to Jesus implies identifying Jesus with the God of Israel. Relevance: Directly demonstrates that Paul (writing in the 50s AD) was identifying Jesus with YHWH through OT quotation — 270 years before Nicaea. Status: Lead to verify against Scripture — check each OT-to-NT quotation pair

    Richard Bauckham — Jesus and the God of Israel: God Crucified and Other Studies on the NT's Christology of Divine Identity (Eerdmans, 2008)¶

    URL: https://davidschrock.com/2009/03/15/bauckhams-god-crucified/ Key claims: - Central thesis: "the highest possible Christology — the inclusion of Jesus in the unique divine identity — was central to the faith of the early church even before any of the New Testament writings were written, since it occurs in all of them" (p. 19). - Bauckham employs the term "divine identity" rather than "divine nature" to reframe Christology within Second Temple Jewish monotheism. He argues this takes us "beyond the fundamentally misleading contrast between 'functional' and 'ontic' Christology" (p. 30). - Rather than Christ being elevated to divine status gradually, Bauckham insists the NT authors included Jesus within the identity of the God of Israel from the start. - "A higher Christology than Paul already expresses in 1 Corinthians 8:6 is scarcely possible" (cited by Michael Kruger). Relevance: Argues that the highest Christology predates the NT documents themselves — not merely predates Nicaea. Verify against 1 Cor 8:6, Phil 2:5-11, John 1:1-3, Col 1:15-20, Heb 1:1-3. Status: Lead to verify against Scripture

    Michael J. Kruger — "Was the Divinity of Jesus a Late Invention of the Council of Nicea?" (Canon Fodder blog)¶

    URL: https://michaeljkruger.com/was-the-divinity-of-jesus-a-late-invention-of-the-council-of-nicea/ Key claims: - 1 Corinthians 8:5-6: Paul incorporates Jesus into the Shema (Deut 6:4), applying "Lord" (kyrios) to Jesus and attributing divine creative action ("through whom are all things") to him. This "represents one of the earliest creedal statements." - Philippians 2:6-11: Contains "pre-Nicene hymnic material" affirming pre-existence, incarnation, and exaltation, applying Isaiah 45:23 (where YHWH alone receives worship) to Christ. - Both passages are identified as "earlier Christian tradition that significantly predates Paul's own letters" — meaning the high Christology is even earlier than the 50s AD. - Cites Hurtado: "Pauline Christianity is the earliest form of the Christian movement to which we have direct access from undisputed firsthand sources." - Cites Bauckham: "A higher Christology than Paul already expresses in 1 Corinthians 8:6 is scarcely possible." Relevance: Consolidates the argument from multiple scholars that high Christology predates Paul's letters, which themselves predate Nicaea by 270+ years. Status: Lead to verify against Scripture

    Bart D. Ehrman — How Jesus Became God (HarperOne, 2014) / Ehrman Blog on Nicaea¶

    URL: https://ehrmanblog.org/the-council-of-nicaea-and-the-resulting-view-of-christ/ Key claims: - Ehrman explicitly states: "everyone at the council already fully believed Christ was God. The question was: in what sense?" - He emphasizes this was "NOT when church Fathers decided... that Jesus was divine (even though that's what you read in the Da Vinci Code!!)." - The actual debate at Nicaea was between Arius (Christ was "secondary and subservient" to the Father, with the Logos "not always existing" but "begotten" at some point) and Alexander (Christ was "co-eternal with the Father" and "equally omnipotent," with there "never a time that the Logos did not exist"). - The dispute hinged on homoousios (same substance) vs. homoiousios (similar substance) — differing by a single iota. - However, Ehrman also argues that "Jesus was not originally considered to be God in any sense at all" during his lifetime, and that belief in his deity evolved through stages: exalted at resurrection, then at baptism, then at conception, then pre-existent. - Ehrman claims "for the first 300 years everyone we know was a subordinationist." - Scholarly response: In 2014, Zondervan published How God Became Jesus by five scholars (Michael Bird et al.) contesting Ehrman's reconstruction, arguing that "rather than being an invention of the church centuries after Christ's death, Jesus' divinity is clearly evident in the earliest Christian sources, immediately following his resurrection." Relevance: Critical source. Even an agnostic critical scholar concedes Nicaea did not invent Christ's deity — it debated the precise relationship between Father and Son. His evolutionary model for Christology should be tested against the actual NT evidence. The Bird et al. response should also be consulted. Status: Lead to verify against Scripture — test Ehrman's "evolutionary Christology" against actual NT dating

    Philippians 2:5-11 — Scholarly Analysis of the "Christ Hymn"¶

    URL: https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/abs/10.1177/1476993x13504167 (abstract); https://www.psephizo.com/reviews/what-does-pauls-christ-hymn-in-philippians-2-actually-tell-us/ Key claims: - Scholarly consensus holds that Philippians 2:6-11 reflects an early Christian hymn, likely pre-Pauline. Debate centers on whether Paul composed it, adapted a pre-existing hymn, or received it from the Philippians. - W. G. Kummel affirms Paul "incorporated early shaped elements from primitive traditions" including Phil 2:6-11, Rom 1:3-4, 1 Cor 15:3-5, and Col 1:15-20. - The hymn expresses a "three stage Christology": (1) pre-existence, (2) incarnation, (3) exaltation. - Not all scholars accept the hymnic designation — "there is no extant evidence that any of these texts served as hymns in the nascent church." However, the poetic, structured nature and theological sophistication point to pre-existing liturgical material. Greek analysis: The key Greek terms include morphe theou ("form of God," v. 6), kenosis/ekenosen ("emptied himself," v. 7), and the name "above every name" (v. 9) — all requiring Greek word study. Relevance: If pre-Pauline (i.e., pre-50s AD), this hymn pushes high Christology to the 30s-40s AD — within years of the crucifixion, not centuries. Status: Lead to verify against Scripture

    1 Corinthians 8:6 — The "Christian Shema"¶

    URL: https://seminaryramblings.wordpress.com/2012/09/26/the-shema-and-1-cor-86/ Key claims: - Paul has "divided the Shema between God and Christ," applying to God two prepositions describing Creator-relationship ("from" and "for/to") and the third ("through") to Christ. - The verse stands as "one of the earliest data points for a christologically reformulated monotheism: Father and Son are jointly confessed within the unique divine identity, yet personally distinguished." - This passage restructures the foundational Jewish confession of monotheism (Deut 6:4: "Hear, O Israel: the LORD our God is one LORD") to include Christ within the divine identity. Greek analysis: The reformulation of the Shema is visible in the Greek: "one God, the Father, ex hou ta panta... and one Lord, Jesus Christ, di hou ta panta." The prepositions ek (from) and dia (through) divide the creative agency between Father and Son. Relevance: Written c. 55 AD — 270 years before Nicaea. If Paul is reformulating Judaism's central monotheistic confession to include Christ, this is not a late development. Status: Lead to verify against Scripture

    Maranatha — Pre-Pauline Aramaic Prayer (1 Corinthians 16:22)¶

    URL: https://medium.com/@mjkelleyII/the-aramaic-prayer-maranatha-in-1-corinthians-16-22-a-christological-analysis-2131a53d6d40 Key claims: - "Maranatha" is Aramaic for "Our Lord, come" (maran = "Lord," atha = "come"). - "The expression certainly comes from circles of Aramaic-speaking Jewish Christians" — indicating it predates the Gentile mission and Paul's Greek-language letters. - Paul left the term untranslated in his Greek letter, and his Gentile readers appear to already know it, demonstrating how this early Aramaic prayer was "transmitted across linguistic communities." - Functions as a liturgical invocation directed toward Jesus, preserved because it "carried deep meaning in early Christian worship and identity." Relevance: An Aramaic prayer to Jesus from the earliest (pre-Pauline) Palestinian Jewish-Christian community. If Aramaic-speaking Jews were praying to Jesus as "Lord" (maran = Aramaic equivalent of kyrios), this pushes Christ-devotion to the earliest stratum of Christianity — the 30s-40s AD in Jerusalem. Status: Lead to verify against Scripture

    Pliny the Younger — Letter to Emperor Trajan (c. 112 AD)¶

    URL: https://earlychurchhistory.org/politics/plinys-letter-to-trajan-about-christians/ ; https://www.biola.edu/blogs/good-book-blog/2017/what-pliny-the-younger-learned-when-he-interrogated-christians-ca-a-d-110 Key claims: - Pliny, Roman governor of Bithynia-Pontus, wrote to Emperor Trajan c. 112 AD seeking guidance on handling Christians. - Christians "assembled on a fixed day before dawn" and "recited an antiphonal ode to Christ as to a god" (Latin: carmenque Christo quasi deo dicere secum invicem). - They bound themselves by oath to "abstain from theft, robbery, adultery, and dishonesty" and then "departed and met again to take food." - Pliny observed that "the contagion of this superstition was spread not only through towns but also villages and even rural areas" and included "many of every age and every rank and even of both sexes." - The Christian community had organized roles, including deaconesses. - Trajan responded that Christians should not be "hunted out," though those proven guilty faced punishment if they refused to recant. Relevance: A hostile pagan official independently corroborates that Christians were worshipping Christ as a divine figure by 112 AD — over 200 years before Nicaea. This is non-Christian testimony, not a claim from within the movement. Status: Lead — non-Christian historical evidence to correlate with NT devotional practices

    Ignatius of Antioch — Letters (c. 108-117 AD)¶

    URL: https://tonypetersen.wordpress.com/2021/01/19/prototrinitarianism-in-the-thought-of-ignatius-of-antioch/ Key claims (direct quotations from Ignatius): - To the Romans (greeting): "to the church... Jesus Christ, our God" - To the Ephesians: Explicitly refers to "Christ as God" in the letter opening - To Polycarp (8:3): "Constant farewell in our God Jesus Christ" - To the Magnesians (6:1): "Jesus Christ, who was with the Father before the ages and has been manifest at the end" — a statement of pre-existence - Incarnation formula: "For our God, Jesus Christ, was conceived by Mary according to the plan of God; he was from the seed of David, but also from the Holy Spirit" - Unity with Father: "our God Jesus Christ... is in the Father" ; Christ "came forth from one Father and was with the one and returned to the one" - Historian Michael Haykin notes that Ignatius "expects the Christians in Rome to be both familiar with a high christology and comfortable with it" — suggesting this was not novel teaching but established apostolic doctrine. Relevance: Ignatius was a direct disciple of the apostles (tradition links him to Peter and John). Writing c. 108-117 AD, he repeatedly and casually calls Jesus "our God" — not as if arguing a new point, but as established confession. This is only ~10-20 years after the last NT documents. Status: Lead — post-apostolic patristic evidence; verify NT passages Ignatius is echoing

    Justin Martyr — First Apology (c. 155-160 AD)¶

    URL: https://ccel.org/ccel/justin_martyr/first_apology/anf01.viii.ii.xxiii.html ; https://www.newadvent.org/fathers/0126.htm Key claims: - Justin states that Christ, "being the First-Begotten Word of God, IS EVEN GOD." - "Jesus Christ is the only proper Son who has been begotten by God, being His Word and first-begotten, and power; and, becoming man according to His will, He taught us these things for the conversion and restoration of the human race." - Justin identifies Jesus as the incarnation of the Logos (Word), arguing that anyone who spoke "with reason" connected with Christ even before the incarnation. - Justin "wholeheartedly believed that Jesus is the eternal Word of God" — an ante-Nicene theological position predating Nicaea by over 150 years. Relevance: Justin Martyr represents mid-2nd century theology. His identification of Christ as the divine Logos and explicitly "even God" demonstrates that belief in Christ's deity was not a 4th-century invention. Verify against John 1:1-18 (Logos theology). Status: Lead — ante-Nicene patristic evidence

    Kyrios Title — NT Usage and Septuagint Background¶

    URL: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kyrios ; https://larryhurtado.wordpress.com/2019/05/07/yhwh-texts-and-jesus-a-follow-up/ Key claims: - Kyrios (Greek: kurios) appears approximately 7,000 times in the Septuagint translating YHWH, and approximately 740 times in the Koine Greek NT, usually referring to Jesus. - "Most scholars agree that the use of kyrios, and hence the Lordship of Jesus, predated the Pauline Epistles, but Saint Paul expanded and elaborated on the topic." - "The kyrios title for Jesus is central to the development of New Testament Christology, for the early Christians put it at the center of their understanding." - In Paul's undisputed letters alone, kyrios is applied to Jesus over 200 times. Greek analysis: The term kurios (G2962) carries a semantic range from "sir/master" to "Lord" (divine title). When used in contexts quoting OT YHWH passages, the divine sense is unambiguous. Relevance: The sheer frequency and context of kyrios applied to Jesus in the earliest NT documents (50s AD) demonstrates that the divine lordship of Christ was foundational, not a later accretion. Status: Lead to verify against Scripture — study G2962 kurios usage patterns

    NT Document Dating — Scholarly Consensus¶

    URL: https://www.bartehrman.com/when-was-the-new-testament-written/ ; https://uncanon.app/learn/when-was-the-new-testament-written Key claims: - Earliest: 1 Thessalonians c. 50 AD (possibly Galatians, late 40s) — "the oldest surviving Christian document" - Paul's undisputed letters: 50s AD (1-2 Corinthians, Galatians, Romans, Philippians, 1 Thessalonians, Philemon) - Synoptic Gospels: Mark c. 65-70 AD, Matthew c. 80-85 AD, Luke c. 80-85 AD - Hebrews: c. 60-90 AD - John: c. 90-100 AD - Revelation: c. 95-96 AD (reign of Domitian) - Latest: 2 Peter, c. 120-130 AD - Pre-Pauline traditions embedded in Paul: Phil 2:6-11, 1 Cor 15:3-5, Rom 1:3-4 — "significantly predate Paul's own letters," pushing the evidence to the 30s-40s AD - The Council of Nicaea was convened in 325 AD — a minimum of 195 years after the latest NT document and 275 years after the earliest. Relevance: Establishes the chronological framework. Every single NT document predates Nicaea by at least two centuries. Pre-Pauline traditions push the evidence to within a decade of the crucifixion. Status: Historical framework for the study

    Thomas's Confession — John 20:28¶

    URL: https://biblehub.com/study/john/20-28.htm ; https://www.monergism.com/my-lord-and-my-god-john-2028 Key claims: - Thomas's declaration "My Lord and my God!" (ho kurios mou kai ho theos mou) is "one of the clearest and most direct acknowledgments of Jesus' divinity in the New Testament." - Described as "the highest Christological confession in any Gospel" — surpassing earlier titles like Rabbi, Messiah, and Son of God. - Jesus does not correct Thomas but affirms him: "Because you have seen Me, you have believed; blessed are those who have not seen and yet have believed" (John 20:29). Greek analysis: The Greek uses the nominative with the article — ho theos mou ("the God of me") — a direct address identifying Jesus as theos (God), not merely an exclamation. Relevance: Even dating John late (c. 90-100 AD), this explicit identification of Jesus as "my God" predates Nicaea by 225+ years. Status: Lead to verify against Scripture

    Romans 9:5 and Titus 2:13 — Paul Calling Christ "God"¶

    URL: https://trinitynotes.blogspot.com/2014/05/romans-95-and-christs-deity.html ; https://av1611.com/kjbp/faq/holland_tit2_13.html Key claims: - Romans 9:5: "whose are the fathers, and of whom as concerning the flesh Christ came, who is over all, God blessed for ever." Scholars Metzger and Moo hold that reading A (identifying Christ as "God over all") "makes best sense of Paul's syntax." However, punctuation affects interpretation — a period after "flesh" turns the next clause into a doxology to the Father. - Titus 2:13: "the glorious appearing of the great God and our Saviour Jesus Christ." Granville Sharp's rule in Greek grammar (when two nouns of the same case are joined by kai with the article before the first but not the second, they refer to the same person) makes this a direct identification of Jesus as "the great God." Greek analysis: Granville Sharp's rule applies to Titus 2:13: tou megalou theou kai soteros hemon Iesou Christou — one article governing both "God" and "Savior," identifying both as Jesus Christ. Relevance: If these readings are correct, Paul explicitly calls Christ "God" in his letters from the 50s-60s AD. Verify the Greek syntax independently. Status: Lead to verify against Scripture — check Greek parser output for both passages

    Summary of Scholarly Landscape¶

    The scholarly consensus — shared by believing and skeptical scholars alike — is that belief in Christ's deity predates the Council of Nicaea (325 AD) by centuries. Even Bart Ehrman, an agnostic critical scholar who argues that Jesus himself did not claim deity, explicitly states that "everyone at the council already fully believed Christ was God. The question was: in what sense?" The popular notion (promoted by Dan Brown's Da Vinci Code and similar works) that Nicaea "invented" Christ's deity is not held by any serious historian.

    The scholarly debate is not whether pre-Nicene Christians believed Christ was divine, but rather how early and how high the Christology was. Larry Hurtado and Richard Bauckham represent the "early high Christology" school, arguing that the highest possible Christology — the inclusion of Jesus within the unique divine identity of Israel's God — was present from the very beginning, within the first decade after the crucifixion. Hurtado points to "binitarian devotion" (worship of both Father and Jesus within a monotheistic framework) as the earliest Christian pattern, evident in pre-Pauline hymns (Phil 2:6-11), creedal confessions (1 Cor 8:6; 15:3-5), the Aramaic prayer Maranatha (1 Cor 16:22), and the systematic application of YHWH texts to Jesus throughout Paul's letters. Bauckham argues that earliest Christology was already the highest Christology, present "even before any of the New Testament writings were written, since it occurs in all of them."

    Ehrman represents the "evolutionary Christology" school, proposing stages of development from exaltation-at-resurrection to pre-existence-and-incarnation. However, even this model concedes that the earliest Christians (within a generation of the crucifixion) were attributing divine honors to Jesus. The Michael Bird et al. response (How God Became Jesus, 2014) contests Ehrman's reconstruction, arguing that the evidence for high Christology appears at the earliest accessible stratum. The extra-biblical evidence from Pliny the Younger (112 AD, a hostile non-Christian witness), Ignatius of Antioch (108-117 AD, calling Jesus "our God" as established confession), and Justin Martyr (c. 155 AD, declaring Christ "is even God") further confirms that belief in Christ's deity was widespread, established, and unremarkable well before the 4th century.

    2. Analyze Philippians 2:5-11 as pre-Pauline hymn — Run Greek parser on the full passage. Study morphe (G3444), kenosis/ekenosen (G2758), harpagmos (G725). Check whether Isaiah 45:23 LXX is quoted in vv. 10-11 via parallels tool.

    3. Trace YHWH-text applications to Jesus — For each pair Hurtado identifies (Rom 10:13/Joel 2:32; Rom 14:11/Isa 45:23; 1 Cor 1:31/Jer 9:24; 1 Cor 2:16/Isa 40:13; 1 Cor 10:26/Ps 24:1), retrieve both OT and NT text and confirm the transfer of YHWH language to Jesus.

    4. Study the Maranatha invocation (1 Cor 16:22) — Verify the Aramaic preservation. Run Strong's lookup on the term. Assess its significance as evidence for pre-Pauline, Aramaic-speaking Christ-devotion.

    5. Analyze kyrios (G2962) usage patterns — Run Strong's concordance lookup. Determine how many times it is applied to Jesus vs. God the Father in the NT. Compare with LXX usage for YHWH.

    6. Check Thomas's confession (John 20:28) — Run Greek parser on ho kurios mou kai ho theos mou. Confirm the nominative-with-article construction. Note Jesus's response in v. 29 (affirmation, not correction).

    7. Evaluate Romans 9:5 syntax — Run Greek parser. Assess whether "God blessed for ever" modifies Christ or is a separate doxology. Compare scholarly punctuation options.

    8. Apply Granville Sharp's rule to Titus 2:13 — Run Greek parser. Confirm whether one article governs both "God" and "Savior" in reference to Jesus Christ.

    9. Survey the Johannine Prologue (John 1:1-18) — Run Greek parser on "the Word was God" (theos en ho logos). Study the anarthrous theos (no article). Check parallels with Genesis 1:1.

    10. Examine Colossians 1:15-20 and Hebrews 1:1-4 — Run Greek parser on both "Christ hymn" passages. Study terms for pre-existence, creation agency, and divine attributes applied to Christ.

    11. Verify Ehrman's claim that "everyone was a subordinationist for 300 years" — Search for NT passages where Christ is presented as equal to the Father (John 5:18, 10:30, 14:9) alongside those where he is functionally subordinate (John 14:28, 1 Cor 15:28).
