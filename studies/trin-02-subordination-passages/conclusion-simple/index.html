    Does the Bible Teach That Jesus Is Permanently Less Than the Father?¶

    A Plain-English Summary¶

    Four passages in the New Testament seem to say that Jesus is less than God the Father: he says the Father is "greater," he says he "can do nothing" on his own, he admits he doesn't know when the end will come, and Paul says he will one day "be made subject" to the Father. But when you look at each passage in its full context, a consistent picture emerges: these statements describe what Jesus accepted during his earthly mission, not what he is for all eternity. Every one of them is surrounded by some of the strongest claims to full deity in the entire Bible.

    The Key Word Tells the Story¶

    When Jesus says "my Father is greater than I" in John 14:28, the Greek word he uses is meizon (meaning "greater in rank or position"). There was another word available -- kreitton (meaning "greater in nature or quality") -- and the New Testament never uses it for the Father-Son relationship.

    It does use kreitton to say Christ is better than angels in Hebrews 1:4, where a real nature-difference is the point. Jesus is comparing positions, not natures.

    Just 16 verses earlier in the same chapter, he uses the exact same word when he says the disciples will do "greater works" than he did (John 14:12). Nobody reads that as the disciples being a higher order of being than Christ.

    "The Son Can Do Nothing by Himself" -- Then What Follows?¶

    John 5:19 sounds like the most sweeping denial of Jesus' ability you could find: "The Son can do nothing of himself." But the passage doesn't stop there. It immediately escalates into a series of claims no one but God could make:

    • By verse 21, the Son gives life to whoever he chooses.
    • By verse 22, the Father has handed all judgment to the Son.
    • By verse 23, people must honor the Son "even as" they honor the Father.
    • By verse 26, the Son possesses life-in-himself -- the defining attribute of God.

    The opening denial of independent action is the premise for the equality claims, not a contradiction of them. Jesus is saying: "I never act on my own initiative -- and that's exactly why my actions perfectly mirror the Father's."

    The Son Didn't Know the Day -- But That Changed¶

    Mark 13:32 is arguably the hardest verse for anyone who holds a high view of Christ:

    "But of that day and that hour knoweth no man, no, not the angels which are in heaven, neither the Son, but the Father."

    A thorough study of the word oida ("to know" -- used 334 times in the New Testament) found zero cases where it means "to make known" rather than "to possess knowledge." The ignorance was real. Jesus genuinely did not know.

    But this is the same Jesus who "knew all men" and "knew what was in man" (John 2:24-25), who predicted specific events, and whose disciples said, "Now we know that you know all things" (John 16:30).

    That selective ignorance -- knowing human hearts but not a specific date -- points to a voluntary, bounded limitation during the incarnation, not a permanent gap. After the resurrection, Jesus claims "all power in heaven and in earth" (Matthew 28:18). The limitation was real, and then it was removed.

    The Son "Will Be Made Subject" -- A Mission Ending, Not an Eternal Arrangement¶

    In 1 Corinthians 15:28, Paul writes:

    "Then shall the Son also himself be subject unto him that put all things under him, that God may be all in all."

    The verb is future tense -- this hasn't happened yet. It describes a change of state, not a continuation of one. If the Son were permanently and eternally subordinate, you'd expect a present or past tense, not a future one.

    The context is Paul's Adam-Christ framework. Just as death came through Adam, resurrection comes through Christ. The Son's reign is the period of setting things right. When every enemy is defeated and death itself is destroyed, the Son delivers the completed kingdom to the Father -- mission accomplished. The word Paul uses for "delivers" is a voluntary transfer verb, not a word for forced submission.

    And the final state -- "God all in all" -- is not a hierarchy. Revelation fills in the picture: Christ shares the Father's throne (Revelation 22:1, 3), receives identical worship (Revelation 5:12-13), and bears the title "the Almighty" (Revelation 1:8).

    The Framework That Holds It All Together¶

    Philippians 2:5-11 provides the controlling storyline:

    1. Before the incarnation, Christ existed "in the form of God" and was "equal with God."
    2. He voluntarily "emptied himself" and took "the form of a servant."
    3. He became obedient to death on a cross.
    4. Then God "highly exalted him" and gave him a name above every name.

    The subordination passages all operate in step 3 -- after the self-emptying, before the exaltation. They describe a voluntary, temporary, mission-driven state. The emptying was not a subtraction of deity but an addition of humanity. Colossians 2:9 says "all the fulness of the Godhead dwelleth in him bodily" -- even during the incarnation, the divine nature was fully present, just not always fully exercised.

    What the Bible Never Says¶

    No pre-incarnation text in the New Testament describes the Son as subordinate to the Father.

    • John 1:1 says "the Word was God."
    • John 17:5 speaks of shared glory "before the world was."
    • John 8:58 has Jesus say "Before Abraham was, I am."
    • Philippians 2:6 affirms full equality.

    The subordination language appears only within the incarnation -- never before it and never after it in the final picture.

    The Honest Boundary¶

    There is one question this evidence raises but does not fully settle. John 5:26 says the Father "gave" the Son to have life in himself. That word "gave" implies some kind of ordering -- the Father as source, the Son as derived.

    But the text insists the result is identical: the Son possesses the same self-existent life the Father has. Whatever the eternal Father-Son relationship involves, it does not involve the kind of subordination described in these four passages. Those belong to the incarnation. Whether there is a non-hierarchical ordering within the eternal Godhead -- an asymmetry of origin that does not imply inferiority -- is a question honest interpretation must leave open.
