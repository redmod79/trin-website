    Reference Gathering: Subordination Passages and the Father-Son Relationship¶

    Question¶

    What do John 14:28 ("the Father is greater than I"), John 5:19 ("the Son can do nothing by himself"), Mark 13:32 (the Son does not know the day or hour), and 1 Corinthians 15:28 ("the Son himself will be made subject") teach about the relationship between the Father and the Son? Do these passages describe Jesus' eternal nature or his voluntary mission?

    Study Plan Context¶

    No dedicated study plan entry found for the Trinity series. This is Study 2 (trin-02) of a 10-study series examining the Trinity debate. Study 1 (trin-01-firstborn-created-being) established that "firstborn of all creation" and "beginning of the creation of God" are preeminence titles, not createdness indicators.

    Prior Studies¶

    From Series (trin-01)¶

    trin-01-firstborn-created-being: - Question: Does "firstborn of all creation" (Col 1:15) teach Christ was the first created being? - Key finding 1: Prototokos (G4416) functions as a title of sovereign preeminence grounded in creative agency, not as a chronological marker of createdness. OT precedent: Psalm 89:27 (David = youngest, made "firstborn" = highest), Exodus 4:22 (Israel = "firstborn"), Jeremiah 31:9 (Ephraim = second-born, called firstborn). - Key finding 2: The causal hoti clause in Col 1:16 grounds the firstborn title in creative agency — "He is firstborn BECAUSE by him all things were created" — making the partitive genitive (firstborn within creation) incoherent. - Key finding 3: The creation paradox (John 1:3, Col 1:16, Heb 1:2, 1 Cor 8:6) — if Christ created ALL things, he cannot be a created thing without creating himself. - Key finding 4: John 1:1-3 verb distinction — en (eimi, continuous being) for the Word vs. egeneto (ginomai, coming-into-being) for created things places Christ on the uncreated side of the ontological divide. - Key finding 5: Hebrews 1:6-12 calls Christ prototokos (v.6), theos ("God," v.8), and kyrios who created heaven and earth (vv.10-12) within one chapter — proving the title is compatible with full deity. - Relevance: Establishes Christ's full deity and uncreated status. The subordination passages in trin-02 must be interpreted against this established background — whatever John 14:28, John 5:19, Mark 13:32, and 1 Cor 15:28 mean, they cannot mean Christ is a created being ontologically inferior to the Father.

    From Semantic Search (additional)¶

    jesus-never-said-i-am-god: (score: 0.453) - Question: Why is there no sentence "I am God" on Jesus' lips? - Relevance: Directly discusses John 14:28 and Mark 13:32 as "difficult passages" - Key finding on John 14:28: "The context is the Son's outgoing-to-the-Father (return after incarnation) — i.e., relational/economic/positional, not ontological. The same Gospel says 'the Word was God' (Joh 1:1), records Thomas's 'My Lord and my God' without rebuke (Joh 20:28), and affirms 'I and my Father are one' (Joh 10:30). Read in canonical context, 'greater' describes the Father's economic priority within the Father-Son relation, not deity-vs-non-deity." - Key finding on Mark 13:32: "This is a kenosis passage (Phi 2:7). The incarnate Son in his economic mission has voluntarily limited access to certain divine knowledge as part of the 'form of a servant' he took on. The same Jesus elsewhere knows hearts (Joh 2:24-25), pre-knows the betrayal (Joh 13:19), and after the resurrection openly claims 'all power... in heaven and in earth' (Mat 28:18)." - Key finding on John 5:18: "The Jews sought the more to kill him, because he... said also that God was his Father, making himself equal with God" — the contemporaries heard an equality claim. - Pattern: Jesus' indirect deity-claim matrix (ego eimi sayings, divine-prerogative actions, acceptance of worship, YHWH-title reception) demonstrates full deity; subordination language belongs to the kenotic/incarnational register.

    cry-of-dereliction: (score: 0.431) - Question: What does the cry "My God, my God, why hast thou forsaken me?" reveal? - Relevance: Establishes the framework of Christ's voluntary self-emptying and its experiential consequences - Key finding 1: Christ's forsakenness was experientially real but temporally bounded — "Father" on both sides (Luke 23:34, 46). The suffering belongs to the incarnational mission, not eternal nature. - Key finding 2: John 10:17-18 — "No man taketh [my life] from me, but I lay it down of myself" — establishes the VOLUNTARY nature of Christ's submission. "Both Father and Son acted voluntarily — the Father delivering, the Son laying down." - Key finding 3: The imperfect tense of paredidou in 1 Peter 2:23 ("kept on entrusting") shows Christ maintained trust and relationship throughout — the subordination was within a shared mission. - Key finding 4: Phil 2:6-8 (morphe theou → morphe doulou, heauton ekenosen) frames the kenosis as voluntary movement from the "form of God" to the "form of a servant" — the unveiling comes post-resurrection (Phil 2:9-11).

    lgt-04b-nature-of-christ: (score: 0.433) - Question: Did Christ take fallen or unfallen human nature? - Relevance: Establishes the kenotic framework — Christ entering the fallen human CONDITION while retaining unfallen moral DISPOSITION - Key finding 1: Philippians 2:6-7 — "Who, being in the form of God (morphe theou), thought it not robbery to be equal with God (isa theo), but made himself of no reputation (heauton ekenosen)" — Christ was genuinely equal with God before the incarnation. - Key finding 2: Hebrews 2:14 verb contrast — kekoinoneken (perfect, natural ongoing participation for humans) vs. meteschen (aorist, voluntary punctiliar act for Christ). Same substance, different mode of entry. - Key finding 3: The NT consistently presents Christ's limitations (hunger, fatigue, susceptibility to temptation, mortality) as belonging to the incarnational condition, not to his eternal nature. - Key finding 4: Homoioma (G3667) in Rom 8:3 and Phil 2:7 denotes genuine resemblance without full identity — Christ genuinely resembled sinful/human flesh without being reducible to it.

    Three Main Scholarly Camps¶

    Camp 1: Incarnational Subordination Only (Giles, K. Johnson/Themelios, patristic consensus) - All four passages describe the incarnate Son's voluntary self-limitation - Philippians 2:5-11 is the interpretive key - "Eternal functional subordination necessarily implies ontological subordination because being and action are inseparable in God"

    Camp 2: Eternal Functional Subordination (EFS/ERAS) (Grudem, Ware, Frame) - Full ontological equality but eternal subordination in role/function/authority - Father-Son names imply eternal authority-submission structure - Frame: Christ's permanent humanity grounds permanent subordination

    Camp 3: Augustinian Middle Ground (K.E. Johnson's reading of Augustine) - Eternal generation creates real ordering (taxis) but not hierarchical subordination - Divine persons act inseparably (ad extra) according to relative properties (ad intra) - John 5:19 reflects derivative existence ("from the Father"), not inferiority

    Key Linguistic Claims to Verify¶

    1. Meizon vs. kreitton distinction (Harris, Alexios): John 14:28 uses meizon (positional superiority) not kreitton (Heb 1:4, nature-level superiority). Needs Greek verification.
    2. John 5:19 grammatical reading (K. Johnson/Priscilla Papers): "The Son can do nothing by himself" refutes independent action, not ability — the pericope (vv. 22-23: all judgment, equal honor) proves equality.
    3. Shaliach principle (Giles): Jewish "sending" concept means the sent one carries the sender's full authority.
    4. 1 Cor 15:28 hotan...tote construction (Gons): Greek temporal language indicates future change of state, contradicting "eternal" subordination claim.
    5. Hypotasso in 1 Cor 15:28 (Graham): Carries salvific/reconciliation meaning in Adam-Christ typology context, not hierarchical meaning.
    6. Oiden in Mark 13:32 (Macknight minority): Could carry causative sense "make known" rather than "possess knowledge."
    7. Kenoo in Phil 2:7 (Barclay/Gaventa): Christ did not relinquish divine power but expressed it through human weakness.

    Claims to verify biblically: 1. EGW interprets John 5:19 ("Son can do nothing of Himself") as demonstrating that Christ is "one with [the Father] in nature, in will, and in purpose" — i.e., the statement proves unity and cooperation, not inferiority. Verify this reading against the Greek text and full pericope (John 5:19-30). 2. EGW presents Christ's dependence on the Father as a feature of His incarnational mission ("sent into the world to do"), not His eternal nature — "So utterly was Christ emptied of self that He made no plans for Himself." Verify Phil 2:6-7 supports this kenotic framework. 3. EGW distinguishes oneness "in nature, in will, and in purpose" from oneness "in person" (MH 422.1). This is relevant to the "two wills" debate — does the Bible portray Father and Son as having one nature but distinct personal wills? 4. EGW's treatment of Mark 13:32 implies the limitation was temporary (related to incarnational state). Verify whether biblical evidence supports temporary vs. permanent ignorance.
