    Verses¶

    Question¶

    What do John 14:28 ('the Father is greater than I'), John 5:19 ('the Son can do nothing by himself'), Mark 13:32 (the Son does not know the day or hour), and 1 Corinthians 15:28 ('the Son himself will be made subject') teach about the relationship between the Father and the Son? Do these passages describe Jesus' eternal nature or his voluntary mission?

    Crux text: 'the Father is greater (meizon) than I'¶

    Ye have heard how I said unto you, I go away, and come [again] unto you. If ye loved me, ye would rejoice, because I said, I go unto the Father: for my Father is greater than I.

    Context: Upper Room Discourse, evening before crucifixion. Jesus prepares disciples for his departure. The statement occurs in the context of going to the Father — a transitional, positional statement about Jesus moving FROM his incarnate state TO the Father's presence. The key word meizon (G3187, comparative of megas) is parsed as Nom Sg M comparative adjective. The genitive of comparison construction (mou) compares Jesus with the Father along some dimension of 'greatness.' The context (poreuomai pros ton Patera — 'I go to the Father') frames this as positional/locational, not ontological.

The contrast with kreitton (G2909) in Heb 1:4 is decisive: kreitton compares qualitative/nature-level superiority (Christ better than angels), while meizon compares positional/functional rank. John chose meizon deliberately. NT parallels: John 17:24, John 16:10 (Father language in departure context).

    Same meizon word: 'greater works' indicating scope, not essential difference¶

    Verily, verily, I say unto you, He that believeth on me, the works that I do shall he do also; and greater [works] than these shall he do; because I go unto my Father.

    Context: Same Upper Room Discourse context. Jesus tells his disciples they will do 'greater' (meizon) works than he did. This cannot mean the disciples are ontologically superior to Jesus — it means their scope of ministry will be wider post-ascension. The same word (meizon) used for the same speaker in the same discourse context strongly suggests a consistent semantic range.

    Notes: This is a critical control text for interpreting John 14:28. If meizon in 14:12 clearly means 'greater in scope/extent' (not 'ontologically superior'), the burden of proof falls on anyone who claims meizon means something categorically different just 16 verses later in the same discourse.

    Jewish opponents understood Jesus' claim as making himself equal (isos) with God¶

    Therefore the Jews sought the more to kill him, because he not only had broken the sabbath, but said also that God was his Father, making himself equal with God.

    Context: Chapter 5 — Jesus heals on the Sabbath. The narrator (John) reports the Jewish reaction. The key word is ison (G2470, A-ASM), parsed as accusative singular masculine adjective. The construction ison heauton poion to theo ('making himself equal with God') is a present active participle — the Jews perceived Jesus as MAKING an equality claim with God. The word isos denotes full equivalence in status, not mere similarity (see word study G2470). This verse sets the context for the entire pericope that follows (vv.19-30).

    • ἑαυτὸν (G1438) — F-3ASM — Acc Sg M
    • ποιῶν (G4160) — V-PAP-NSM — Pres Act Ptcp Nom Sg M
    • τῷ (G3588) — T-DSM — Dat Sg M
    • Θεῷ (G2316) — N-DSM — Dat Sg M

    Notes: Parser confirms: ison (G2470, A-ASM), heauton (G1438, F-3ASM reflexive), poion (G4160, V-PAP-NSM present active participle). The present participle indicates an ongoing action perceived by the Jews. The word study for G2470 shows isos is used 7x in NT, with the theological core in John 5:18 and Phil 2:6 — both asserting ontological equality with God. The same root appears in Phil 2:6 (isa theo) where Paul affirms Christ's pre-incarnation equality.

    Crux pericope: dependence statement escalates to highest equality claims¶

    Then answered Jesus and said unto them, Verily, verily, I say unto you, The Son can do nothing of himself, but what he seeth the Father do: for what things soever he doeth, these also doeth the Son likewise. For the Father loveth the Son, and sheweth him all things that himself doeth: and he will shew him greater works than these, that ye may marvel. For as the Father raiseth up the dead, and quickeneth [them]; even so the Son quickeneth whom he will. For the Father judgeth no man, but hath committed all judgment unto the Son: That all [men] should honour the Son, even as they honour the Father. He that honoureth not the Son honoureth not the Father which hath sent him.

    Context: Jesus responds to the equality charge of v.18. The pericope begins with apparent dependence ('the Son can do nothing of himself,' v.19) but immediately escalates: the Father shows the Son ALL things (v.20), the Son gives life to whom HE wills (v.21 — divine prerogative), ALL judgment is given to the Son (v.22), and men must honor the Son EVEN AS (kathos) they honor the Father (v.23). Parser confirms: v.19 ou dynatai (G1410, V-PNI-3S) + poiein (G4160, V-PAN infinitive) + aph heautou (G1438, F-3GSM 'from himself') + ouden (G3762, A-ASN-N 'nothing'). V.22 has krisin pasan dedoken (G2920 N-ASF + G3956 A-ASF + G1325 V-RAI-3S perfect: 'all judgment has given'). V.23 has timosi ton Huion kathos timosi ton Patera (G5091 V-PAS-3P: 'honor the Son just as they honor the Father'). The pempō sending verb (V-AAP-ASM, aorist participle) in v.23 identifies the Father as the sender.

    • οὐδὲν (G3762) — A-ASN-N — Acc Sg N
    • ὁμοίως (G3668) — ADV — ποιεῖ ποιέω G4160 [V-PAI-3S] Pres Act Ind Sg thirdPers

    Notes: The K. Johnson (Priscilla Papers) reading is confirmed by the Greek structure: v.19 denies independent action (aph heautou = 'from himself,' i.e., independently), not ability. The pericope then proves this by showing the Son exercises EVERY divine prerogative: life-giving (v.21), all judgment (v.22), equal honor (v.23). The homoios ('likewise,' G3668 adverb) in v.19 is critical — the Son does the SAME things the Father does, not lesser things. The escalation pattern refutes reading v.19 as subordination. NT parallels: JHN 8:28 (highest match at 0.558 — 'I do nothing of myself' repeated in same Gospel).

    Son has life in himself and authority of judgment as Son of man¶

    Verily, verily, I say unto you, He that heareth my word, and believeth on him that sent me, hath everlasting life, and shall not come into condemnation; but is passed from death unto life. Verily, verily, I say unto you, The hour is coming, and now is, when the dead shall hear the voice of the Son of God: and they that hear shall live. For as the Father hath life in himself; so hath he given to the Son to have life in himself; And hath given him authority to execute judgment also, because he is the Son of man.

    Context: Continuation of the John 5 pericope. V.26 is theologically decisive: 'as the Father has life in himself, so he has given to the Son to have life in himself.' Parser confirms: hosper (G5618 adverb) gar ho Pater echei (G2192 V-PAI-3S present) zoen en heauto, houtos kai to Huio edoken (G1325 V-AAI-3S aorist) zoen echein (G2192 V-PAN infinitive) en heauto. The Father possesses self-existent life (present tense — ongoing), and gave the Son the same quality (aorist — a definite act). The 'giving' does not imply inferiority — the Son has the SAME kind of life (life-in-himself) that the Father has. V.27 adds authority (exousia) to judge because he is 'Son of man' — connecting the incarnation to the judicial role.

    Notes: The edoken (aorist of didomi) in v.26 raises the 'eternal generation' question: does the 'giving' refer to the incarnation (the Son was granted this authority in the incarnate state) or to the eternal relation (the Father eternally communicates self-existent life to the Son)? The Augustinian middle ground (K.E. Johnson) reads this as eternal generation — the Son's existence is 'from' the Father eternally. Either reading supports the main conclusion: the Son possesses the SAME quality of life as the Father.

    Resurrection power and 'I seek not my own will but the Father's will'¶

    Marvel not at this: for the hour is coming, in the which all that are in the graves shall hear his voice, And shall come forth; they that have done good, unto the resurrection of life; and they that have done evil, unto the resurrection of damnation. I can of mine own self do nothing: as I hear, I judge: and my judgment is just; because I seek not mine own will, but the will of the Father which hath sent me.

    Context: Conclusion of the pericope. V.28-29: the Son has resurrection power — ALL in the graves hear HIS voice. V.30 returns to the dependence theme: 'I can of mine own self do nothing.' Parser confirms v.30: Ou dynamai (G1410 V-PNI-1S) ego poiein (G4160 V-PAN) ap emautou (G1683 F-1GSM) ouden. Then the critical will statement: ou zeto (G2212 V-PAI-1S 'I seek not') to thelema (G2307 N-ASN) to emon (G1699 possessive) alla to thelema tou pempsantos (G3992 V-AAP-GSM 'the one having sent') me. Two distinct thelema references: 'my will' vs. 'the will of the one who sent me.' The sending verb (pempo, aorist participle) identifies this as mission language.

    Notes: V.30's 'my will vs. Father's will' is diagnostic for the 'one will or two wills' question. The Gethsemane parallel (Luke 22:42) is crucial: there, 'my will' clearly refers to Christ's HUMAN will shrinking from the cup. The same pattern likely applies here — 'my will' = the human will, 'the Father's will' = the shared divine purpose. The word study for G2307 (thelema, 64 occurrences) confirms this as a Johannine pattern: John 4:34, 5:30, 6:38 all use the same construction.

    Crux text: 'neither the Son, but the Father' — incarnational ignorance¶

    But of that day and [that] hour knoweth no man, no, not the angels which are in heaven, neither the Son, but the Father.

    Context: Olivet Discourse. Jesus speaks of the day and hour of the second coming. The hierarchical structure: no man (oudeis) < angels in heaven < the Son < the Father. Parser confirms: oiden (G1492 V-RAI-3S perfect active indicative — 'knows' with present force). The word study for G1492 (oida, 334 occurrences) confirms this is a defective perfect with present meaning — 'possesses settled knowledge.' The exclusive modifier structure: oudeis...oude hoi aggeloi...oude ho Huios, ei me ho Pater — 'no one...not even the angels...not even the Son, except the Father.' The grammar reference for perfect tense confirms oida as a stative perfect functioning as present.

    • οἶδεν (G1492) — V-RAI-3S — Perf Act Ind Sg thirdPers
    • Υἱός (G5207) — N-NSM — Nom Sg M
    • Πατήρ (G3962) — N-NSM — Nom Sg M

    Notes: The Macknight minority reading (oiden as causative 'make known') is not supported by any of the 334 NT uses of oida — the word study shows it consistently means 'know/be aware of' in present force. The textual variant in Matt 24:36 is significant: N1904 includes 'oude ho Huios' (neither the Son) while TR omits it. The text comparison tool confirms TEXTUAL VARIANT: N1904 has Strong's 5207 (huios) and 3761 (oude) that TR lacks. This means the Markan text (which preserves 'nor the Son' in all MSS) is the more secure witness. Compare with John 2:24-25 ('he knew all men'), John 16:30 ('thou knowest all things'), and Matt 28:18 ('all power given unto me') — these suggest the limitation was temporary and incarnational.

    Synoptic parallel: textual variant — some MSS omit 'nor the Son'¶

    But of that day and hour knoweth no [man], no, not the angels of heaven, but my Father only.

    Context: Synoptic parallel to Mark 13:32. N1904 (critical text) includes 'nor the Son' (oude ho Huios) while TR omits it, adding 'my' (mou) before Father and monos (G3441 'only') after Father. Parser confirms: oiden (G1492 V-RAI-3S), monos (G3441 A-NSM). The text comparison confirms 5 Strong's numbers present in N1904 but absent from TR: G5610 (hora), G2250 (hemera), G1492 (oida), G3761 (oude), G5207 (huios). The TR reading 'but my Father only' omits the Son from the ignorance chain entirely.

    • οἶδεν (G1492) — V-RAI-3S — Perf Act Ind Sg thirdPers
    • μόνος (G3441) — A-NSM — Nom Sg M

    Notes: The textual variant confirms that even ancient scribes found 'nor the Son' theologically difficult. The fact that Mark includes it in all manuscripts (the harder reading) supports its authenticity. This is relevant to the study question: the existence of a scribal tradition that removed 'nor the Son' shows ancient awareness that this passage creates tension with other deity claims.

    Crux passage: eschatological subjection — hotan...tote temporal construction¶

    Then [cometh] the end, when he shall have delivered up the kingdom to God, even the Father; when he shall have put down all rule and all authority and power. For he must reign, till he hath put all enemies under his feet. The last enemy [that] shall be destroyed [is] death. For he hath put all things under his feet. But when he saith, all things are put under [him, it is] manifest that he is excepted, which did put all things under him. And when all things shall be subdued unto him, then shall the Son also himself be subject unto him that put all things under him, that God may be all in all.

    Context: Paul's resurrection argument. The passage is embedded in Adam-Christ typology (vv.20-23, 45-49). V.24: eita to telos hotan paradidoi (G3860 V-PAS-3S present subjunctive) ten basileian (G932 N-ASF) to Theo kai Patri — 'then the end, when he delivers the kingdom to God and Father.' V.25: dei (G1163 'it is necessary') auton basileuein achri hou the (G5087 V-2AAS-3S aorist subjunctive) — 'he must reign UNTIL he has put.' The achri hou ('until') indicates a temporal endpoint. V.27: hupetaxen (G5293 V-AAI-3S aorist active — God subjected all things) + hupotetaktai (G5293 V-RPI-3S perfect passive — all things have been subjected). V.28: THE CRITICAL CONSTRUCTION — hotan (G3752 'when') de hupotagei (G5293 V-2APS-3S aorist passive subjunctive) auto ta panta, tote (G5119 'then') kai autos ho Huios hupotagēsetai (G5293 V-2FPI-3S FUTURE passive indicative). The hotan...tote construction with future passive indicative (hupotagēsetai) indicates a FUTURE CHANGE OF STATE — the Son will be subjected at a specific future point, not eternally.

    • παραδιδοῖ (G3860) — V-PAS-3S — Pres Act Subj Sg thirdPers
    • βασιλείαν (G932) — N-ASF — Acc Sg F
    • καταργήσῃ (G2673) — V-AAS-3S — Aor Act Subj Sg thirdPers

    Notes: The parser output is decisive for the Gons argument: hupotagēsetai is V-2FPI-3S (future passive indicative). This is NOT present tense (which would indicate ongoing state) but FUTURE tense — something that WILL happen. The hotan...tote construction marks a temporal transition: 'when X happens, THEN Y will follow.'     Notes: Pre-existence + mission language + will distinction — all in one verse. The 'came down from heaven' (katabebeka ek tou ouranou) is an aorist of voluntary descent, not a statement about ontological inferiority.

    Supporting: 'My meat is to do the will of him that sent me'¶

    Jesus saith unto them, My meat is to do the will of him that sent me, and to finish his work.

    Context: Samaritan woman encounter. Jesus frames his entire earthly ministry as executing the sender's will and finishing the sender's work.

    Notes: The 'finishing' (teleioso) his work connects to John 17:4 ('I have finished the work') and John 19:30 ('It is finished') — a completed mission, not an ongoing subordination.

    Gethsemane: 'not my will but thine' — human will vs. divine purpose¶

    Saying, Father, if thou be willing, remove this cup from me: nevertheless not my will, but thine, be done.

    Context: Parser confirms: plen (G4133 'nevertheless') me (G3361) to (G3588) thelema (G2307 N-NSN) mou alla to son (G4674 possessive) ginestho (G1096 V-PNM-3S present middle/passive imperative — 'let it come to be'). The 'my will' (to thelema mou) vs. 'thine' (to son) is the clearest NT expression of the two-wills distinction. The 'my will' here is unambiguously the HUMAN will shrinking from death. No divine person would will to abort the plan of salvation.

    Notes: This is the key diagnostic for the 'one will or two wills' question. If 'my will' in Gethsemane is clearly the human will, the same pattern likely applies to 'my will' in John 5:30 and 6:38 — these are the incarnate Christ's human will being submitted to the shared divine purpose, not two divine wills in conflict.

    Supporting: 'I am Alpha and Omega... the Almighty'¶

    I am Alpha and Omega, the beginning and the ending, saith the Lord, which is, and which was, and which is to come, the Almighty.

    Context: Opening of Revelation. The speaker claims to be Alpha and Omega, the Almighty (pantokrator). This title is applied to Christ in Revelation's context (cf. Rev 22:13 where Christ says 'I am Alpha and Omega, the first and the last').

    Notes: Pantokrator ('the Almighty') is an OT divine title (LXX for Shaddai/Sabaoth). Its application to Christ in Revelation contradicts permanent subordination — the Almighty is not subordinate to anyone.

    Notes: Combined with Rev 22:1,3 ('the throne of God AND of the Lamb'), this shows the glorified Christ sharing the Father's throne as equals, not occupying a lesser position.

    Notes: The Hebrew qanah ('possessed/created/acquired') in v.22 is debated. The broader context establishes Wisdom's pre-existence and intimate participation in creation, paralleling John 1:1-3 and Colossians 1:15-17.

    Supporting: meizon usage — 'greater than John the Baptist' is positional¶

    Verily I say unto you, Among them that are born of women there hath not risen a greater than John the Baptist: notwithstanding he that is least in the kingdom of heaven is greater than he.

    Context: Jesus compares John the Baptist with kingdom members. The 'least in the kingdom' is meizon (greater) than John — same human nature, different positional standing.

    Notes: This is a key control text for the meizon word study. The 'least in the kingdom' is not ontologically superior to John the Baptist — they share the same human nature. The comparison is purely positional/dispensational. This confirms the positional sense of meizon in John 14:28.

    Supporting: 'God sending his own Son in the likeness of sinful flesh'¶

    For what the law could not do, in that it was weak through the flesh, God sending his own Son in the likeness of sinful flesh, and for sin, condemned sin in the flesh:

    Context: Sending + incarnation language. 'His own Son' (ton heautou huion) implies pre-existing relationship. 'In the likeness (homoioma G3667) of sinful flesh' — genuine resemblance without identity.

    Notes: The word study for G3667 (homoioma) confirms genuine resemblance: real participation in human flesh, but not reducible to sinful flesh. The sending language (pempsas) uses the mission framework.

    Supporting: 'God sent forth his Son, made of a woman, made under the law'¶

    But when the fulness of the time was come, God sent forth his Son, made of a woman, made under the law, To redeem them that were under the law, that we might receive the adoption of sons.

    Context: Paul's sending language. 'Made of a woman' (genomenon ek gynaikos) = incarnation. 'Made under the law' (genomenon hypo nomon) = voluntarily placing himself under legal constraints. The purpose: 'to redeem.'

    Notes: The 'made under the law' parallels the subordination passages — the Son voluntarily placed himself under constraints (law, ignorance, death, subjection) FOR the purpose of redemption. The subjection is purposive and mission-bound.

    Supporting: hypotasso in BOTH directions — angels subject to Christ¶

    Who is gone into heaven, and is on the right hand of God; angels and authorities and powers being made subject unto him.

    Context: Post-ascension Christ with angels/authorities/powers 'made subject' (hupotagenton, aorist passive participle of G5293) to HIM. The same verb (hypotasso) used for the Son's subjection in 1 Cor 15:28 is here used for creation's subjection to the Son.

    Notes: This demonstrates that hypotasso is not inherently about ontological inferiority — angels subjected to Christ are not superior to Christ who later subjects himself to the Father. The word describes functional arrangement, not essential hierarchy.

    Supporting: 'Though he were a Son, yet learned he obedience'¶

    Though he were a Son, yet learned he obedience by the things which he suffered;

    Context: Incarnational learning. The concessive 'though a Son' (kaiper on huios) implies sonship would NORMALLY preclude the need to learn obedience — the learning is a feature of the incarnate state, not the eternal nature.

    Notes: The learning was incarnational and experiential — not that the eternal Son was ignorant of obedience, but that he experienced it in human form for the first time.

    Supporting: 'God was manifest in the flesh'¶

    And without controversy great is the mystery of godliness: God was manifest in the flesh, justified in the Spirit, seen of angels, preached unto the Gentiles, believed on in the world, received up into glory.

    Context: Creedal/hymnic statement. 'God was manifest in the flesh' (theos ephanerothe en sarki) — the movement is from divine to human, not the other way around. God appeared in flesh, was received up into glory — a kenosis-exaltation sequence.

    Notes: The sequence mirrors Phil 2:5-11: divine status → incarnation → exaltation. The subordination belongs to the incarnational phase.

    Supporting: 'He that hath seen me hath seen the Father'¶

    Jesus saith unto him, Have I been so long time with you, and yet hast thou not known me, Philip? he that hath seen me hath seen the Father; and how sayest thou [then], Shew us the Father? Believe me that I [am] in the Father, and the Father in me: or else believe me for the very works' sake.

    Context: Same Upper Room Discourse as John 14:28. Jesus claims functional identity with the Father — seeing Jesus IS seeing the Father. The mutual indwelling ('I in the Father, the Father in me') indicates inseparable operation.

    Notes: This context is critical for John 14:28 interpretation. In the SAME discourse, Jesus both claims to be the visible manifestation of the Father (v.9) and says 'the Father is greater than I' (v.28). The only coherent reading is that v.28 refers to the Father's positional priority within the economic arrangement, while v.9 refers to the essential unity of nature.

    Supporting: mission-subordination language — 'the Father gave me a commandment'¶

    For I have not spoken of myself; but the Father which sent me, he gave me a commandment, what I should say, and what I should speak. And I know that his commandment is life everlasting: whatsoever I speak therefore, even as the Father said unto me, so I speak.

    Context: Mission language. The 'commandment' (entole) framework is the same as John 10:18 — the Father's commandment establishes the mission parameters within which the Son operates.

    Notes: The word study for G649 (apostello, 144 occurrences) and G3992 (pempo, 82 occurrences) shows that John uses both sending verbs interchangeably for the Father-Son relationship. The shaliach principle from the word study: the sent one carries the sender's full authority — the sending does not establish subordination but authorized representation.

    Supporting: exaltation + Psalm 8/110 application¶

    Which he wrought in Christ, when he raised him from the dead, and set [him] at his own right hand in the heavenly [places], Far above all principality, and power, and might, and dominion, and every name that is named, not only in this world, but also in that which is to come: And hath put all [things] under his feet, and gave him [to be] the head over all [things] to the church,

    Context: Paul's exaltation language applying Psalm 8:6 ('put all things under his feet') and Psalm 110:1 (right hand) to Christ. 'Far above ALL' — no exception, no subordination indicated in the exalted state.

    Supporting: OT plurality — 'Let us make man in our image'¶

    Supporting: OT sending + Trinity language¶

    Supporting: pre-existence + universal authority — 'the Father had given all things into his hands'¶

    Jesus knowing that the Father had given all things into his hands, and that he was come from God, and went to God;

    Context: Before the foot-washing. Three-part statement: (1) the Father gave all things to him, (2) he came from God, (3) he went to God. Pre-existence, universal authority, and return to the Father — all in one verse.

    Notes: The knowledge claim ('Jesus knowing') paired with 'all things into his hands' contrasts with the Mark 13:32 ignorance — this is during the same earthly ministry, yet Jesus possesses comprehensive divine knowledge alongside a specific eschatological limitation.

    Supporting: shared divine possessions — 'All things that the Father hath are mine'¶

    All things that the Father hath are mine: therefore said I, that he shall take of mine, and shall shew [it] unto you.

    Context: Upper Room Discourse. Jesus claims everything the Father possesses is also his. This is an equality claim — shared possession of all divine attributes.

    Notes: If all the Father's things belong to the Son, the subordination cannot be ontological — there is nothing the Father has that the Son lacks.

    Supporting: voluntary mission framework — came from Father, return to Father¶

    I came forth from the Father, and am come into the world: again, I leave the world, and go to the Father.

    Context: Late in the Upper Room Discourse. The full mission arc: origin (came from the Father), incarnation (come into the world), departure (leave the world), return (go to the Father). The subordination belongs to the middle segment — the incarnate phase.

    Notes: The 'came from / going to' framework is the clearest voluntary-mission statement in John. The Son's point of origin and destination are both WITH the Father — the earthly phase is the exception, not the norm.

    Notes: The disciples' testimony ('thou knowest all things') during the same evening as the Olivet Discourse (where Mark 13:32 was spoken) suggests the ignorance of the day and hour was a specific, bounded limitation, not a general cognitive deficit.

    Supporting: Jesus knew his hour had come — incarnational knowledge claim¶

    Notes: The same verb oida appears positively here and negatively in Mark 13:32 — Jesus possessed settled knowledge of some things (his hour, human hearts) while being limited regarding others (the specific day and hour of the second coming). This selective limitation is characteristic of incarnational self-limitation, not ontological inferiority.
