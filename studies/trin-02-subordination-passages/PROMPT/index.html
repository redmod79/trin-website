    Scope

    Question¶

    What do John 14:28 ('the Father is greater than I'), John 5:19 ('the Son can do nothing by himself'), Mark 13:32 (the Son does not know the day or hour), and 1 Corinthians 15:28 ('the Son himself will be made subject') teach about the relationship between the Father and the Son? Do these passages describe Jesus' eternal nature or his voluntary mission?

    Prior Research Summary¶

    Four prior studies provide relevant findings: (1) trin-01-firstborn-created-being established Christ's full deity and uncreated status via prototokos as preeminence title, the causal hoti clause in Col 1:16, and the creation paradox (John 1:3, Col 1:16, Heb 1:2). (2) jesus-never-said-i-am-god directly addresses John 14:28 as economic/positional (not ontological) and Mark 13:32 as a kenosis passage, noting Christ's claims to equal deity (John 10:30, John 20:28). (3) cry-of-dereliction establishes the voluntary nature of Christ's submission via John 10:17-18 and the 1 Peter 2:23 imperfect tense. (4) lgt-04b-nature-of-christ establishes the kenotic framework from Phil 2:6-7 and the Heb 2:14 verb contrast (kekoinoneken/meteschen). Web research identified three main scholarly camps: Incarnational Subordination Only (Giles, K. Johnson), Eternal Functional Subordination (Grudem, Ware, Frame), and Augustinian Middle Ground (K.E. Johnson). Key linguistic claims requiring verification include the meizon/kreitton distinction, the hotan...tote temporal construction in 1 Cor 15:28, hypotasso as salvific rather than hierarchical, and the shaliach (sending) principle for John 5:19-30.

    Primary Verses (31 — full analysis required)¶

    Four disputed passages (crux texts): The four subordination passages with FULL pericope context - John 14:28 — Crux text — 'the Father is greater than I'; meizon (G3187) usage central to the positional vs. ontological question - John 5:19-23 — Crux pericope — 'Son can do nothing by himself' (v.19) but 'all judgment committed to the Son' (v.22), 'all men should honour the Son even as they honour the Father' (v.23) - John 5:24-27 — Continuation of pericope — Son has life in himself (v.26), authority of judgment because he is Son of man (v.27) - John 5:28-30 — Close of pericope — resurrection power (vv.28-29), 'I seek not my own will but the will of the Father who sent me' (v.30) - Mark 13:32 — Crux text — 'neither the Son, but the Father' in terms of knowing the day/hour; oida (G1492) usage - 1 Corinthians 15:24-28 — Crux passage — 'the Son himself will be made subject' (hypotasso G5293); kingdom delivered to the Father; eschatological context

    Kenosis / incarnation framework: Philippians 2 kenosis hymn and Hebrews 2 incarnation purpose - Philippians 2:5-11 — Interpretive key for all four passages — kenosis (kenoo G2758), morphe theou (G3444), voluntary self-emptying, exaltation sequence - Hebrews 2:9 — 'Made a little lower than the angels FOR the suffering of death' — temporary lowering with purpose clause - Hebrews 2:14-17 — Christ 'took part of the same' (meteschen aorist, voluntary), 'in all things made like his brethren' — incarnational framework

    Equality / deity claims: Passages asserting Christ's full deity and equality with the Father - John 1:1-3 — Foundational Johannine prologue — 'the Word was God' (theos), continuous being (en/eimi) vs. coming-into-being (egeneto/ginomai) - John 5:18 — Jews sought to kill him because he 'said also that God was his Father, making himself equal (isos G2470) with God' - John 10:30 — 'I and my Father are one' — direct equality claim; triggers stoning attempt - John 10:33 — Jewish interpretation: 'thou, being a man, makest thyself God' — confirms audience understood equality claim - John 20:28 — Thomas's confession — 'My Lord and my God' accepted without correction - Hebrews 1:3-4 — Son is 'brightness of his glory, express image of his person' (v.3), then 'made so much better (kreitton G2909) than the angels' (v.4) — nature-superiority word vs. John 14:28's meizon - Hebrews 1:8-12 — Father addresses Son as 'God' (v.8 theos), 'Lord who laid the foundation of the earth' (v.10) — full deity attribution - Colossians 1:15-19 — 'Image of the invisible God, firstborn of every creature... all things created by him and for him... in him all things consist' — ontological supremacy context - Colossians 2:9 — 'In him dwelleth all the fulness of the Godhead bodily' — full deity even during incarnation - John 8:58 — Absolute ego eimi — 'Before Abraham was, I am'; tense asymmetry (genesthai aorist vs. eimi present)

    Pre-incarnation glory and voluntary mission: Pre-existence, glory, voluntary nature of incarnation - John 17:5 — Pre-incarnation glory — 'the glory which I had with thee before the world was'; para soi + eichon - John 17:21-24 — Unity prayer — 'that they may be one, as we are one' (v.22); 'thou lovedst me before the foundation of the world' (v.24) - John 10:17-18 — Voluntary power over his own life — exousia (G1849) to lay down and take up life; 'this commandment I received from my Father'

    Post-resurrection and eschatological evidence: Post-resurrection authority and eschatological portrayal - Matthew 28:18 — 'All power is given unto me in heaven and in earth' — post-resurrection claim of universal authority

    OT messianic enthronement background: Psalm 110 and Psalm 8 as cited in 1 Cor 15 and Hebrews - Psalm 110:1 — OT background — 'The LORD said unto my Lord, Sit thou at my right hand'; messianic enthronement; cited in 1 Cor 15:25 - Psalm 8:4-6 — OT background — 'Thou madest him a little lower than the angels'; cited in Heb 2:6-8 and 1 Cor 15:27 for 'all things under his feet' - Isaiah 9:6 — OT messianic prophecy — 'Mighty God, Everlasting Father, Prince of Peace' — deity titles for the child/son; critical for evaluating whether OT already presents the Son as fully divine

    Adam-Christ typology in 1 Corinthians 15: The broader context for the subjection passage - 1 Corinthians 15:20-23 — Adam-Christ typology — 'firstfruits' and 'every man in his own order'; framework for understanding v.28 - 1 Corinthians 15:45-49 — Second Adam/last Adam typology — 'first man Adam/last Adam'; anthropological context for subjection passage

    Knowledge and omniscience texts: Passages relevant to Mark 13:32 ignorance question - John 2:24-25 — Jesus 'knew all men... knew what was in man' — omniscience claim contradicting permanent ignorance reading of Mark 13:32

    Additional Primary Verses: - John 14:12 — Same meizon — 'greater works' shows meizon indicates scope/role, not essential difference - Matthew 24:36 — Synoptic parallel to Mark 13:32 — some MSS omit 'nor the Son'

    Focus Areas¶

    1. The meizon (G3187) vs. kreitton (G2909) distinction in John 14:28 and Hebrews 1:4: READ the existing word study for G1492-oida.md (relevant to Mark 13:32). Run greek_parser.py --verse on John 14:28 to parse the full clause and identify the genitive of comparison construction. Run greek_parser.py --verse on Hebrews 1:4 to parse kreitton. Then survey meizon across John's Gospel: John 5:20 ('greater works'), John 14:12 ('greater works than these'), Matthew 11:11 (John Baptist least-in-kingdom comparison). In each case, meizon compares entities of the SAME nature in different positions or scope. Now check kreitton in Hebrews: 1:4 (better than angels — nature-level), 7:7 ('the less is blessed of the better'), 8:6 ('a better covenant'). Does kreitton consistently indicate qualitative superiority? If so, the word choice in John 14:28 is deliberate. The research agent should also run cross_testament_parallels_v2.py --hybrid-ot and --hybrid-nt on John 14:28 to find parallel 'greater' comparisons. Check the grammar reference entry for comparative-adjective-with-article.md which covers kreitton. Verses: John 14:28, Hebrews 1:4, John 5:20, John 14:12, Matthew 11:11, Hebrews 7:7, Hebrews 8:6 Strong's: G3187, G2909

    2. John 5:19-30 full pericope: dependence as proof of equality, not inferiority: The pericope begins with the Jews' charge that Jesus was 'making himself equal (isos G2470) with God' (John 5:18). Jesus' response in v.19 has been read two ways: (a) denying equality by affirming dependence, or (b) denying INDEPENDENT action while affirming shared operation. The scholarly claim (K. Johnson in Priscilla Papers) is that v.19 refutes the claim that Jesus made himself equal — denying not his equality but his independent self-appointment. Run greek_parser.py --verse on John 5:18 to parse isos. Then parse vv.19-23 to trace the escalation: v.19 dependence → v.20 Father shows Son → v.21 Son gives life to whom he will → v.22 all judgment to Son → v.23 equal honor. The key question: does the voluntative clause in v.21 ('the Son quickeneth whom he will') indicate divine will? READ the library entry for G2919-krino.md (judgment). Run cross_testament_parallels_v2.py --hybrid-ot and --hybrid-nt on John 5:19 and John 5:22. Also investigate the shaliach (sending) principle: does John's 'sending' vocabulary (apostello G649, pempo G3992) in this passage support the claim that the sent one carries the sender's full authority? READ the library entry for G3992-pémpō.md. Verses: John 5:18, John 5:19-23, John 5:24-27, John 5:28-30, John 5:36-37 Strong's: G2470, G2919, G649, G3992, G1410

    3. Mark 13:32 and the scope of incarnational ignorance: temporary or permanent?: READ the library entry for G1492-oida.md — this is a critical word study for this focus area, as oida has 334 NT occurrences and its semantic range must be evaluated. Run greek_parser.py --verse on Mark 13:32 and Matthew 24:36 (synoptic parallel — some MSS omit 'nor the Son'). The research agent should check the textual variant in Matthew 24:36 using greek_text_compare.py --verse 'MAT 24:36'. The Macknight minority reading proposes that oiden carries a causative/Hiphil-like sense ('make known') — check whether any NT usage of oida supports this. Then compare with Christ's knowledge claims elsewhere: John 2:24-25 ('knew all men... knew what was in man'), John 13:1 ('Jesus knew that his hour was come'), John 16:30 (disciples say 'we are sure that thou knowest all things'). Does Luke 2:52 ('increased in wisdom') support genuinely growing knowledge in the incarnate state? Finally, does Matthew 28:18 ('all power in heaven and earth') post-resurrection indicate the limitation was temporary? READ also the grammar reference entry for the absolute-ego-eimi pattern, since John 8:58 presents a divine knowledge claim in the same Gospel tradition. Verses: Mark 13:32, Matthew 24:36, John 2:24-25, John 13:1, John 16:30, Luke 2:52, Matthew 28:18, John 8:58 Strong's: G1492, G3441, G1097

    4. 1 Corinthians 15:24-28: eschatological subjection — temporary mediatorial or eternally subordinate?: This is the most complex passage in the study. Run greek_parser.py --verse on 1 Corinthians 15:24-28 to parse the key verbs, especially hypotasso. The scholarly claim from Phil Gons is that the hotan...tote construction with future tense indicates a CHANGE of state — the Son is not currently in this subjection, but will be at a future point. This directly contradicts the 'eternal subordination' reading. The research agent should verify this by checking other hotan...tote constructions in Paul. Then examine hypotasso across its 49 NT uses: does it carry a salvific/reconciliation meaning (Graham's claim) in this Adam-Christ typological context (vv.21-22, 45-49)? Or is it consistently hierarchical? Compare 1 Peter 3:22 where the same word describes angels/authorities being subject to Christ — does subjection run in BOTH directions? The Psalm 8 and Psalm 110 citations are critical: v.25 cites Psalm 110:1 ('till he hath put all enemies under his feet'), v.27 cites Psalm 8:6 ('he hath put all things under his feet'). Run cross_testament_parallels_v2.py --hybrid-ot on 1 Cor 15:28. The key interpretive question: when 'all things are subdued,' is the Son VOLUNTARILY delivering the completed mediatorial kingdom back to the Father (mission completion), or is the Son eternally remaining in a subordinate position? Verses: 1 Corinthians 15:24-28, 1 Corinthians 15:20-23, 1 Corinthians 15:45-49, Psalm 110:1, Psalm 8:4-6, 1 Peter 3:22 Strong's: G5293, G932, G5021

    5. Philippians 2:5-11 as the interpretive key: kenosis, morphe, and the voluntary structure of self-emptying: READ the existing library entries for G2758-kenóō.md and G3444-morphe.md — these are critical resources. Run greek_parser.py --verse on Phil 2:6-8 to parse the full hymn structure. The key grammatical questions: (1) What is the relationship between morphe theou (v.6) and morphe doulou (v.7)? If morphe means inner essence/nature (not just outward appearance), then Christ had the divine nature and took on the servant nature. (2) What does harpagmos (G726) mean — something to be 'grasped' or 'exploited'? The scholarly claim (Barclay/Gaventa) is that Christ did not relinquish divine power but expressed it through human weakness. (3) The voluntary structure: heauton ekenosen ('emptied himself') is active reflexive — Christ acted on himself. This parallels John 10:17-18 ('I lay down my life of myself'). Run cross_testament_parallels_v2.py --hybrid-ot and --hybrid-nt on Phil 2:7. The exaltation sequence (vv.9-11) is equally important: 'God also hath highly exalted him, and given him a name above every name' — does the exaltation restore a pre-existing status or confer a new one? If restoration, then the subordination was temporary. READ G1391-doxa.md for the glory concept. Verses: Philippians 2:5-11, John 10:17-18, John 17:5 Strong's: G2758, G3444, G726, G1391, G2470

    6. John's 'sending' vocabulary and the shaliach principle: does being 'sent' imply subordination or authorization?: READ the library entry for G3992-pémpō.md. The research agent should survey John's use of apostello and pempo across the Gospel — John uses both verbs for the Father sending the Son (e.g., John 3:17 apostello, John 5:23 pempo). Is there a semantic distinction, or are they interchangeable? The shaliach principle from Jewish law holds that 'the one sent is as the one who sends' — i.e., the agent carries the principal's full authority. Verify this against the actual biblical text: does Jesus claim to act with the Father's full authority (John 5:22-23 all judgment, equal honor) or with delegated and therefore lesser authority? Key test passages: John 12:44-45 ('he that believeth on me, believeth not on me, but on him that sent me'), John 13:20 ('he that receiveth whomsoever I send receiveth me; and he that receiveth me receiveth him that sent me'). Run concept_context.py on 'JHN 5:23' to find other verses with the same theological concepts. Verses: John 5:23, John 3:17, John 6:38, John 12:44-45, John 13:20, John 12:49-50 Strong's: G649, G3992

    7. The 'will' question: one divine will or two distinct wills in Father and Son?: The key texts are John 5:30 ('I seek not mine own will, but the will of the Father which hath sent me'), John 6:38 ('I came down from heaven, not to do mine own will, but the will of him that sent me'), and the Gethsemane prayer in Luke 22:42 ('not my will, but thine, be done'). Run greek_parser.py --verse on John 5:30, John 6:38, and Luke 22:42. The question is: when Jesus says 'my will' vs. 'the Father's will,' is he distinguishing between two divine wills (Father's and Son's) or between his human will and the shared divine will? The Riccardi critique of EFS argues that two distinct divine wills implies either tritheism or Arianism. The Gethsemane text is particularly diagnostic: the 'my will' that shrinks from the cup is clearly the human will (no divine person would will to avoid the plan of salvation). Does this pattern extend to John 5:30 and 6:38? Also check John 4:34 ('My meat is to do the will of him that sent me, and to finish his work') and John 17:4 ('I have finished the work which thou gavest me to do'). The EGW corpus claim is that Father and Son are 'one in nature, in will, and in purpose' (DA 208.2) — this needs verification against the biblical evidence. Verses: John 5:30, John 6:38, Luke 22:42, John 4:34, John 17:4 Strong's: G2307, G5426

    8. OT background: Psalm 110, Psalm 8, and the messianic enthronement pattern: Run hebrew_parser.py --verse on Psalm 110:1 and Psalm 8:5-6. Then trace the NT citation chain: Psalm 110:1 is cited in 1 Cor 15:25, Heb 1:13, Matt 22:44, Acts 2:34-35 — it describes a TEMPORARY state ('sit... till I make thine enemies thy footstool'). The 'till' (ad/heos) implies an endpoint. Psalm 8:4-6 is cited in 1 Cor 15:27, Heb 2:6-8, Eph 1:22 — it describes humanity's dominion ('thou hast put all things under his feet'). Hebrews 2:8-9 notes 'we see not yet all things put under him' but 'we see Jesus, who was made a little lower than the angels FOR the suffering of death.' The key question: does the 'all things under his feet' in 1 Cor 15:27 apply to Christ as the second Adam (mediatorial role) or as the eternal Son? If the former, then v.28's subjection marks the completion of the mediatorial role and the return to the pre-incarnation state of 'God all in all.' Run cross_testament_parallels_v2.py --hybrid-nt on Psalm 110:1 and Psalm 8:6. Verses: Psalm 110:1, Psalm 8:4-6, Hebrews 2:6-9, 1 Corinthians 15:25-27, Ephesians 1:20-22, Acts 2:34-35 Strong's: H3427, H3225, H7896

    9. Post-resurrection and eschatological evidence: does subordination continue after the incarnate mission?: The key post-resurrection texts include: Matthew 28:18 ('All power is given unto me in heaven and in earth'), Philippians 2:9-11 (hyper-exaltation, name above every name, universal confession as Lord), Revelation 5:12-13 (Lamb receives identical worship to 'him that sitteth upon the throne'), Revelation 22:1,3 ('the throne of God and of the Lamb' — single throne), and Colossians 2:9 ('all the fulness of the Godhead bodily' — present tense). Does the exaltation in Phil 2:9-11 restore the pre-incarnation glory (cf. John 17:5) or confer something new? READ G1391-doxa.md for the glory/doxa concept. The EFS reading of 1 Cor 15:28 claims the Son remains subject even after all enemies are defeated. But does the temporal language (hotan...tote) indicate a transition FROM the current mediatorial reign TO a new eschatological state where 'God may be all in all'? The Frame argument grounds permanent subordination in Christ's eternal humanity — check whether Revelation portrays the glorified Christ as still in a subordinate role (Rev 1:8, 22:13 'Alpha and Omega' applied to Christ) or as sharing the throne equally (Rev 3:21, 22:1,3). Verses: Matthew 28:18, Philippians 2:9-11, Revelation 5:12-13, Revelation 22:1, Revelation 22:3, Revelation 1:8, Revelation 3:21, Colossians 2:9, John 17:5 Strong's: G1849, G1391, G2362

    10. The pre-incarnation state: evidence for equality before the mission began: The primary pre-incarnation texts include: John 1:1-3 ('the Word was God' — theos without the article, but in the predicate position), John 17:5 ('the glory which I had with thee before the world was' — eichon imperfect = continuous past possession), John 17:24 ('thou lovedst me before the foundation of the world'), Philippians 2:6 ('being in the form of God, thought it not robbery to be equal with God' — huparchon present participle = pre-existing state), Colossians 1:15-17 ('by him were all things created... he is before all things'), Hebrews 1:2-3 ('by whom also he made the worlds... the brightness of his glory'), and John 8:58 ('Before Abraham was, I am'). READ G2758-kenóō.md and G3444-morphe.md. Run greek_parser.py --verse on John 1:1 to parse the predicate nominative construction (theos without article). The research agent should also parse John 17:5 for the para soi + eichon construction — does 'the glory I had with you' indicate shared personal glory? Run cross_testament_parallels_v2.py --hybrid-ot on John 1:1 to trace OT background for the Word's deity. The key question: do ANY pre-incarnation texts describe the Son as subordinate to the Father? If the answer is no, the subordination is necessarily incarnational. Verses: John 1:1-3, John 17:5, John 17:24, Philippians 2:6, Colossians 1:15-17, Hebrews 1:2-3, John 8:58, Proverbs 8:22-31 Strong's: G2316, G3056, G1510, G1096, G3444

    11. Hebrews 2:9-18: 'made lower than the angels' as the incarnational paradigm: READ G3667-homoioma.md (relevant for the 'likeness' concept). Run greek_parser.py --verse on Heb 2:9, 2:14, 2:17. The structure of Hebrews 1-2 is decisive: Chapter 1 establishes the Son's deity (theos in v.8, Lord/creator in vv.10-12, better/kreitton than angels in v.4), then Chapter 2 immediately explains WHY this same Son was 'made a little lower than the angels' — the purpose clauses are stacked: dia to pathema tou thanatou ('for the suffering of death,' 2:9), hina dia tou thanatou katargese ('that through death he might destroy,' 2:14), hopōs hileos genētai ('that he might be a merciful high priest,' 2:17). Each purpose clause grounds the lowering in a MISSION, not a nature. The verb meteschen in 2:14 (aorist — voluntary, punctiliar act) contrasts with kekoinoneken (perfect — natural ongoing participation for humans). This Heb 2:14 verb contrast was established in the lgt-04b-nature-of-christ prior study. Run cross_testament_parallels_v2.py --hybrid-ot on Heb 2:9 to connect with the Psalm 8 citation. Verses: Hebrews 2:9, Hebrews 2:14-17, Hebrews 1:4, Hebrews 1:8-12 Strong's: G2909, G1642, G3348, G2841
