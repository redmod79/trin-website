    What Falls Apart If Jesus Is Not God?¶

    A Plain-English Summary¶

    If Jesus is not God, the Bible does not merely lose one doctrine -- it collapses into self-contradiction across six major areas at once. The same Jesus who told Satan "worship God only" becomes the greatest violator of that command. The same Jesus who agreed that only God can forgive sins becomes history's most brazen blasphemer. No alternative explanation can hold all six areas together without the Bible contradicting itself.

    The Worship Problem¶

    The Bible draws a hard line on worship. When the apostle John falls down to worship an angel in Revelation 19:10, the angel stops him immediately: "See thou do it not ... worship God." When Cornelius bows before Peter in Acts 10:25-26, Peter pulls him up: "I myself also am a man." Every righteous creature in the New Testament who receives worship refuses it and redirects it to God. Every single time.

    Jesus never does this. Not once. Disciples worship him after he walks on water (Matt 14:33). Women grab his feet and worship him after the resurrection (Matt 28:9). Thomas falls before him and says, "My Lord and my God" (John 20:28). Jesus accepts it all without a word of correction.

    And here is the sharpest edge: Hebrews 1:6 says God himself commands the angels to worship Jesus.

    If Jesus is a created being, then God -- the one who said "worship God only" -- is ordering his angels to commit idolatry. God becomes the author of the very sin he forbids.

    The Forgiveness Problem¶

    When Jesus tells the paralyzed man "Thy sins are forgiven" in Mark 2:5, the scribes react instantly: "Why doth this man thus speak blasphemies? Who can forgive sins but God only?" (Mark 2:7). They are theologically correct. In the Old Testament, the Hebrew word for divine pardon (calach) is used exclusively with God as the subject. No human ever calach's another human. Forgiveness of sins is God's business alone.

    Jesus does not correct the scribes. He does not say, "You're wrong -- God delegates this authority to people." Instead, he heals the man to prove he has the authority they just said belongs only to God. He validates their premise and then demonstrates that he meets it.

    The Creation Problem¶

    John 1:3 says of Jesus: "All things were made by him; and without him was not any thing made that was made." Colossians 1:16 expands it: "by him were all things created, that are in heaven, and that are in earth." The scope is total -- everything that exists was created through him.

    Now apply the "not God" hypothesis. If Jesus is a created being, he is one of the "all things" that were made. But the text says he made all things. So he made himself -- a logical impossibility.

    The only escape is to say he is outside the category of created things. But Isaiah 44:24 says YHWH created "alone" and "by myself." If Jesus is a separate being who helped, YHWH's claim to have worked alone is false. The only way both statements survive: Jesus is within YHWH's own identity, not a separate assistant.

    The Savior Problem¶

    Isaiah 43:11 is blunt: "I, even I, am the LORD; and beside me there is no saviour." YHWH claims exclusive saving power -- not just military rescue, but the kind tied to the declaration "before me there was no God formed, neither shall there be after me" (Isa 43:10). This is cosmic and absolute.

    Yet Acts 4:12 says of Jesus: "Neither is there salvation in any other: for there is none other name under heaven given among men, whereby we must be saved." Titus 2:13 calls him "the great God and our Saviour Jesus Christ." The same exclusive title YHWH claims for himself is given to Jesus.

    Some point out that the Old Testament calls human judges "saviors" (Neh 9:27). True -- but those were local military deliverers. Jesus' saving work is universal and eternal: "He is able to save to the uttermost" (Heb 7:25). The scale matches Isaiah's claim, not Nehemiah's judges.

    The Prayer Problem¶

    Stephen, dying under a hail of stones, prays: "Lord Jesus, receive my spirit" (Acts 7:59). Paul prays to Jesus three times about his thorn in the flesh and receives a direct answer (2 Cor 12:8-9).

    The entire early church is defined as "those who call upon the name of our Lord Jesus Christ" (1 Cor 1:2) -- using the exact Greek verb (epikaleomai, meaning "to invoke") that the Old Testament reserves for calling on the name of YHWH.

    Jesus also promises omnipresence: "Where two or three are gathered together in my name, there am I in the midst of them" (Matt 18:20), and "I am with you always, even unto the end of the world" (Matt 28:20). A creature cannot be present with every group of believers on earth simultaneously. Either these are lies, or Jesus is not a creature.

    The Atonement Problem¶

    Psalm 49:7-8 states flatly that no human being can redeem his brother or pay God the ransom price for a soul:

    "For the redemption of their soul is precious, and it ceaseth for ever." (Psa 49:8)

    The cost is categorically beyond human capacity.

    Yet Hebrews claims Jesus obtained "eternal redemption" (Heb 9:12) through a sacrifice offered "through the eternal Spirit" (Heb 9:14). His priesthood rests on "the power of an indestructible life" (Heb 7:16) -- the Greek akatalytos means not just long-lasting but inherently incapable of destruction. A created being's existence is contingent; the Creator could end it. Only an uncreated life is truly indestructible.

    If Jesus is merely human, Psalm 49 has already ruled his ransom attempt impossible. If his life is merely creaturely, "indestructible" is the wrong word. And if his sacrifice is finite, it cannot produce the eternal redemption Hebrews claims it did.

    Why It All Collapses Together¶

    These six areas are not isolated proof texts you can pick off one at a time. They interlock. The same person is the subject in all six.

    Any explanation that removes deity from one domain breaks the others:

    • Say Jesus is a created being who was delegated the right to receive worship. Then God is sharing his glory with "another," which Isaiah 42:8 says he will never do.
    • Say the forgiveness scenes are just prophetic announcements. Then why did the scribes charge blasphemy, and why did Jesus validate their reasoning instead of correcting it?
    • Say God sovereignly amplified a finite sacrifice to produce eternal results. Then Hebrews is wrong to ground the atonement's power in Christ's own indestructible nature rather than in an external divine boost.

    Every alternative explanation patches one hole while tearing open another. No single non-deity account of Jesus can hold all six domains together without the Bible contradicting itself.

    The Bible either presents a figure who stands within YHWH's identity -- making all six areas coherent -- or it presents a figure who is simultaneously its greatest hero and its greatest disaster: an idolater who accepted worship, a blasphemer who claimed to forgive sins, a logical impossibility who created himself, a fraud who stole YHWH's title, a deceiver who promised omnipresence he could not deliver, and a failed redeemer whose sacrifice could never pay a ransom the Psalms declared beyond human reach.

    Thomas had it right: "My Lord and my God" (John 20:28).
