    Web Research Findings¶

    Study Question¶

    If Jesus is not God but a created being or merely a perfect human, what logical contradictions arise within the Bible's own framework? Trace the consequences across six domains: (1) worship, (2) forgiveness, (3) creation, (4) savior exclusivity, (5) prayer/omnipresence, (6) atonement sufficiency.

    Sources Found¶

    Street Theologian — "Unlike Any Other Name: 10 Distinct Ways the New Testament Treats the Name of Jesus" (Substack, undated)¶

    URL: https://streettheologian.substack.com/p/unlike-any-other-name-10-distinct Key claims: - NT writers transfer YHWH-exclusive prerogatives to Jesus across 10 categories: exclusive salvation name (Acts 4:11-12 applying Joel 2:32), invocation in prayer (1 Cor 1:2), "for my name's sake" language (Matt 10:22, 19:29, 24:9), trinitarian name unity (Matt 28:19 singular onoma for three persons), authority over demonic forces (Luke 10:17), cosmic homage (Phil 2:9-11 quoting Isa 45:21-23), forgiveness authority (Acts 2:38, 10:43 vs. Isa 43:25), prayer efficacy and omnipresence (John 14:13-14, Matt 18:20), life's governing principle (Col 3:17), healing power (Acts 3:6, 16) - "Paul transfers the very cosmic homage due to YHWH alone directly onto Jesus" via Phil 2:9-11 citing Isa 45:23 - The singular Greek onoma in Matt 28:19 despite three persons indicates semantic unity of the divine designation Greek analysis: Notes onoma (name) appears in singular despite three persons in the baptismal formula Relevance: Directly addresses multiple domains — provides a systematic framework showing how the NT consistently applies YHWH-exclusive functions to Jesus across worship, prayer, forgiveness, and savior titles Status: Lead to verify against Scripture

    Dr. Joshua Greever — "Forgiveness and Faith in Mark 2" (Bethlehem College and Seminary)¶

    URL: https://bcsmn.edu/mark2/ Key claims: - Jesus pronounces the paralytic's sins forgiven before healing him, and the scribes understand this as arrogating "the divine prerogative of definitive forgiveness of sins" - The scribes' objection centers on Jesus overstepping human boundaries by exercising a function "traditionally God's exclusive" - "Jesus proved his authority to forgive by healing the man" — the physical healing serves as external evidence of the internal spiritual reality of forgiveness - Faith operates as the means through which forgiveness is received, manifesting in concrete actions Relevance: Domain 2 (forgiveness) — establishes the scholarly consensus that the scribes correctly identified forgiveness as divine prerogative; Jesus validated rather than corrected their theological premise Status: Lead to verify against Scripture

    Kermit Zarley — "Did Jesus' Authority To Forgive Sins Indicate He Was God?" (Patheos, 2017)¶

    URL: https://www.patheos.com/blogs/kermitzarleyblog/2017/03/jesus-authority-forgive-sins-indicate-god/ Key claims (anti-deity position): - Zarley (author of The Restitution of Jesus Christ, formerly Trinitarian for 22 years) argues Jesus' forgiveness does not prove deity - Cites E.P. Sanders: "such a pronouncement would not be regarded as blasphemy by any known Jewish law" - Argues Mark 2:5 uses passive voice, creating ambiguity about whether Jesus forgave directly or announced God's forgiveness - Points to delegated authority: Angel of the LORD (Exod 23:21), Peter's binding/loosing (Matt 16:19), disciples (John 20:23) - Notes Matt 9:8 says crowds "glorified God, who had given such authority to men" — indicating delegated, not inherent, power - Claims Jesus bases forgiveness authority on being "the Son of Man" (Mark 2:10), which church fathers associated with humanity Relevance: Domain 2 counterargument — strongest anti-deity rebuttal on forgiveness; downstream agents should test whether the "delegation" model holds up when Jesus forgives sins committed against God (not against himself), and whether passive voice actually dissolves the problem Status: Lead to verify against Scripture — note this is an anti-deity source presenting the opposition case

    Precept Austin — Hebrews 9:13-14 Commentary (compilation of commentators)¶

    URL: https://www.preceptaustin.org/hebrews_913-14 Key claims: - F.F. Bruce: The phrase "eternal Spirit" (aioniou pneumatos) draws on Isaianic Servant imagery; "It is in the power of the Divine Spirit, accordingly, that the Servant accomplishes every phase of his ministry" - Spurgeon: The phrase indicates Christ's "eternal Godhead" gave His offering "extreme value that otherwise could not have been attached to it" - Leon Morris: "Christ's own spirit was indeed involved in his voluntary sacrifice, but so is the divine Spirit" - Kenneth Wuest: Greek amomos (without blemish) carries legal-cultic weight; Christ's unblemished offering superior because He "was a man without blemish in all that He did" - Steven Cole: "The difference between the Levitical offerings and Christ's self-offering was infinite rather than relative" - Andrew Murray: "It was the Word that became flesh... That life gave His blood... an infinite value" - Spurgeon: "The blood that He shed was the blood of God" because His Godhead consented to the manhood's suffering - Paul Ellingworth: Christ uniquely qualified as both "high priest and offering" simultaneously - The passage employs rabbinic qal wahomer (a fortiori) reasoning: if animal blood sanctified externally, Christ's blood infinitely purifies internally Greek analysis: amomos = without blemish (legal-cultic term); aioniou pneumatos = eternal Spirit (debated: Holy Spirit vs. Christ's eternal divine nature) Relevance: Domain 6 (atonement sufficiency) — multiple scholars argue the atonement's efficacy depends on Christ's divine nature; a finite creature's sacrifice cannot produce infinite/eternal redemption Status: Lead to verify against Scripture

    "18 Passages From Mark's Gospel That Prove Jesus Is God" (IsJesusAlive.com)¶

    URL: https://isjesusalive.com/18-passages-in-marks-gospel-that-prove-jesus-is-god/ Key claims: - Mark 2:5-10: Scribes explicitly reason "Who can forgive sins but God alone?" — Jesus does not deny this premise but validates it by healing - Mark 4:39-41, 6:48-50: Jesus exercises authority over natural law (calming storm, walking on water) - Mark 6:48-50: Jesus uses "ego eimi" (I am), echoing the divine name from Exodus 3:14, while walking on water - Mark 7:18-19, 10:2-6: Jesus modifies God's Law (declaring foods clean, reformulating divorce law) — only the Lawgiver can modify the Law - Mark 13:31: "Heaven and earth will pass away, but my words will not pass away" — claiming his words are as eternal as God's - Mark 14:61-64: Claims to be coming "in the clouds" as judge — the high priest tears his robes, calling it blasphemy Greek analysis: ego eimi in Mark 6:50 parallels Exodus 3:14 LXX Relevance: Domains 1-2 — Mark's Gospel alone presents Jesus exercising multiple divine-exclusive functions; the "self-defeating" argument is strengthened because Mark's Jesus would be a blasphemer if not God Status: Lead to verify against Scripture

    Granville Sharp's Rule and Titus 2:13 — Multiple Sources¶

    URL (primary): https://christianpublishinghouse.co/2019/08/04/titus-213-and-2-peter-11-what-is-the-long-debated-controversial-granville-sharp-rule/ URL (secondary): https://theopedia.com/granville-sharps-rule Key claims: - Granville Sharp (1798) formulated the rule: when two singular, personal nouns appear under one article linked by "and" (article-noun-kai-noun), they designate the same individual - Applied to Titus 2:13 Greek: "tou megalou theou kai soteros" (the great God and Savior) — under the rule, both "God" and "Savior" refer to Jesus Christ - Applied to 2 Peter 1:1 Greek: "tou theou kai soteros Iesou Christou" (the God and Savior Jesus Christ) - The primary source notes Sharp worked with "a limited sample size" and later papyri discoveries showed "Koine authors often omitted the second article even if they were speaking of distinct subjects" - The primary source also notes Paul's typical pattern in Titus maintains distinction between God (Father) and Christ/Savior (Son), citing Titus 1:4 and 3:4-6 - The secondary source (Theopedia) notes modern research has confirmed Sharp's observation in "some eighty New-Testament instances with no exceptions" Greek analysis: The rule concerns the article-noun-kai-noun construction; debate centers on whether Titus 2:13 follows the standard pattern or represents an exception Relevance: Domain 4 (savior exclusivity) — if the rule holds, Paul explicitly calls Jesus "God and Savior" in the same breath; downstream agents should verify the Greek construction and examine Daniel Wallace's research Status: Lead to verify against Scripture

    Biblical Unitarian — Isaiah 43:11 (BiblicalUnitarian.com)¶

    URL: https://www.biblicalunitarian.com/verses/isaiah-43-11 Key claims (anti-deity position): - Rejects the syllogism "Jesus is savior + God is the only savior = Jesus is God" as flawed reasoning - Argues "no savior" refers to specific types of salvation, not all rescue universally - Claims the immediate context (Isa 43:16) refers to the Red Sea deliverance, making it historically specific - Points to other biblical figures called "saviors": Nehemiah 9:27, Isaiah 19:20, 2 Kings 13:5, Obadiah 21 - Offers reductio: if the Trinitarian logic holds, these human "saviors" would also be divine Relevance: Domain 4 counterargument — strongest anti-deity rebuttal on savior exclusivity; downstream agents should examine whether the Hebrew word used for these human "saviors" (moshia) is the same as in Isa 43:11, and whether there is a qualitative difference between temporal/national deliverance and eternal salvation Status: Lead to verify against Scripture — note this is an anti-deity source presenting the opposition case

    "Did Jesus Accept Worship in the Bible?" (AccordingToTheBible)¶

    URL: https://accordingtothebiblebooks.com/did-jesus-accept-worship-in-the-bible/ Key claims: - Catalogues seven specific instances where Jesus accepted worship: Matt 8:2 (leper), 9:18 (ruler), 14:33 (disciples after walking on water), John 9:35-38 (blind man), Matt 28:9 (post-resurrection), Matt 28:17 (disciples), Luke 24:52 (ascension) - Contrasts with two refusal instances: Rev 22:8-9 (angel), Acts 10:25-26 (Peter) - Notes that "when someone else in the Bible is worshiped, they immediately reject it. Yet Jesus stands in the middle of this pattern — and does not refuse it" Relevance: Domain 1 (worship) — establishes the accept/refuse contrast pattern; downstream agents should expand the refusal catalog (add Rev 19:10, Acts 14:11-15) and verify every instance with the Greek proskuneo Status: Lead to verify against Scripture

    Philippians 2:9-11 and Isaiah 45:23 — Multiple Sources¶

    URL (primary): https://appliedapologetics.wordpress.com/2012/03/15/exegesis-of-philippians-25-11-verses-10-11/ Key claims: - Paul directly transfers Isaiah 45:23's YHWH-exclusive language ("every knee shall bow") to Jesus in Phil 2:10-11 - In Isaiah, YHWH is the kyrios to whom every knee bows; in Philippians, Jesus is the kyrios - Two scholarly positions: (a) direct identification of Jesus with YHWH, (b) unique exaltation to share in divine attributes/authority - The exaltation view sees Paul presenting Jesus as "an exalted human being, enthroned in heaven alongside YHWH, exercising the supreme but delegated authority" Relevance: Domains 1 and 4 — the transfer of YHWH-exclusive cosmic homage to Jesus is either identification (deity) or delegation (exaltation); downstream agents should examine whether Isaiah 45:21-23 context permits the "delegation" reading, since YHWH explicitly says "there is no God else beside me" Status: Lead to verify against Scripture

    John 20:28 — Thomas's Confession (Multiple Commentary Sources)¶

    URL: https://biblehub.com/commentaries/john/20-28.htm Key claims: - Thomas's confession "My Lord and my God" (ho kyrios mou kai ho theos mou) is described as "the strongest, most explicit affirmation of Jesus's full deity in the entire Gospel of John" - The repeated pronoun "my" makes this a personal confession directed TO Jesus, not an exclamation to the Father - Thomas's recognition arises in the presence of Christ's wounds — binding together suffering and glory, death and life - Some anti-deity scholars attempt to read this as an exclamation of surprise (like "Oh my God!") rather than a confession, but the Greek construction with the vocative direction "said to him" (eipen auto) refutes this — Thomas is addressing Jesus directly - Jesus does not correct Thomas but accepts the confession and pronounces blessing on those who believe without seeing Relevance: Domain 1 (worship) — if Jesus is not God, he accepts a declaration that he is God without correction; this makes him either a deceiver or delusional within the Bible's own moral framework Status: Lead to verify against Scripture

    Hebrews 7:16 — "Indestructible Life" (Academia.edu, paywalled)¶

    URL: https://www.academia.edu/87583353/_You_are_a_Priest_Forever_Jesus_Indestructible_Life_in_Hebrews_7_16 Key claims (from abstract/preview): - Academic paper analyzing the phrase "power of an indestructible life" (dynamis zoes akatalutou) - Scholars propose interpretations: (a) exalted life, (b) resurrection life, (c) divine life — all crucial for Jesus' eternal priesthood - The Greek akatalutos means "indissoluble, indestructible" — applied to Jesus' life, it implies a life that cannot be destroyed - This quality is what makes Jesus' priesthood permanent (unlike Levitical priests who die) Greek analysis: akatalutos = indissoluble/indestructible; dynamis = power; zoes = life Relevance: Domain 6 (atonement sufficiency) — if Jesus is a created being, his life is by definition dissoluble (God could un-create it); "indestructible life" implies a life that is inherently, not delegatedly, beyond destruction Status: Lead to verify against Scripture — full text paywalled

    Hebrews 9:12 "Eternal Redemption" and 7:25 "Ever Lives" — BibleRef/BibleStudyTools¶

    URL: https://www.bibleref.com/Hebrews/7/Hebrews-7-25.html Key claims: - Heb 7:25: Jesus "is able to save completely those who draw near to God through Him, since He always lives to intercede for them" - "Where a human priest would fall short, Christ can save us entirely, since His priesthood is unending" - The High Priest holds office "by the power of endless life in himself; not only to keep himself alive, but to give spiritual and eternal life to all who rely upon his sacrifice and intercession" - Heb 9:12: Christ obtained "eternal redemption" — not temporary covering but permanent resolution Relevance: Domain 6 — the argument chain: eternal redemption requires eternal priesthood, eternal priesthood requires indestructible life, indestructible life requires a nature that is inherently beyond destruction; a created being's existence is contingent, therefore its priesthood is contingent, therefore its redemption is contingent Status: Lead to verify against Scripture

    Summary of Scholarly Landscape¶

    The scholarly landscape on this question divides into two primary camps with distinct methodological approaches.

    The pro-deity position argues that the Bible's own internal logic requires Jesus' deity across all six domains. Scholars like F.F. Bruce, Spurgeon, Andrew Murray, Leon Morris, and Kenneth Wuest emphasize that the atonement's infinite efficacy depends on the divine nature of the one offering it. The worship acceptance/refusal pattern (angels refuse, apostles refuse, Jesus alone accepts) creates an inescapable dilemma: either Jesus is God and rightly receives worship, or he is the Bible's greatest moral contradiction — a righteous man who accepts idolatry. The Granville Sharp rule in Greek grammar provides a linguistic argument that Paul directly identifies Jesus as "God and Savior" in Titus 2:13. The transfer of YHWH-exclusive language (Isa 45:23 to Phil 2:10-11, Isa 43:11 to Acts 4:12) is treated as identification, not mere delegation.

    The anti-deity position (represented by Kermit Zarley, Biblical Unitarian sources, and some NT scholars) offers counterarguments focused on delegation: forgiveness authority was delegated (Matt 9:8, John 20:23), the title "savior" was applied to human judges (Neh 9:27, Obad 21), worship (proskuneo) could mean "homage" rather than divine worship, and Phil 2:9-11 represents exaltation to shared authority rather than identification with YHWH. E.P. Sanders is cited arguing that pronouncing forgiveness would not have been blasphemy under any known Jewish law.

    The critical scholarly battleground is whether the "delegation model" can sustain itself across all six domains simultaneously. Individual counterarguments exist for each domain, but the cumulative force of all six — Jesus accepts worship, forgives sins, creates all things, is the exclusive savior, receives prayer while claiming omnipresence, and provides eternal redemption through indestructible life — creates a weight that the delegation model must bear across every domain without exception. The downstream research should test whether a single coherent non-deity Christology can account for all six without internal contradiction.

    Research Directives for Downstream Agents¶

    1. Worship pattern verification: Verify all instances where Jesus accepted proskuneo (Matt 8:2, 9:18, 14:33, 28:9, 28:17, John 9:38, Luke 24:52) and all refusal instances (Rev 19:10, 22:8-9, Acts 10:25-26, 14:11-15). Confirm the Greek term is identical in accept and refuse cases. Check whether Jesus ever redirected worship to the Father.

    2. John 20:28 Greek construction: Verify that "eipen auto" (said to him) proves Thomas addressed Jesus directly, refuting the "exclamation" theory. Check whether Jesus' response (John 20:29) constitutes acceptance of the title "my God."

    3. Mark 2:5-7 delegation rebuttal: Zarley claims Matt 9:8 ("given such authority to men") proves delegation. Verify the Greek of Matt 9:8 — does "men" (anthropois, plural) refer to Jesus specifically or to humanity generally? Test whether the passive voice in Mark 2:5 actually dissolves the divine prerogative claim. Verify whether Jesus forgives sins committed against God (not just interpersonal offenses), which would exceed any delegation model.

    4. Isaiah 43:11 / savior exclusivity: Verify the Hebrew of Isa 43:11 (moshia). Compare with the "saviors" in Neh 9:27, Judg 3:9, 3:15, Obad 21. Test whether there is a qualitative difference between temporal national deliverance and eternal salvation. Check whether Isa 43:11 context (Isa 43:10-13) makes broader claims than just Red Sea deliverance.

    5. Isaiah 44:24 creation paradox: Verify YHWH's claim to have created "alone" and "by myself" (Isa 44:24). Cross-reference with John 1:3 ("all things were made through him; without him was not anything made") and Col 1:16. If Jesus is a created being, he is part of "all things" that were made through him — a logical impossibility.

    6. Philippians 2:9-11 / Isaiah 45:21-23: Verify the full Isaiah context — does YHWH say "there is no God else beside me" in the same breath as "every knee shall bow"? If so, can the "exaltation/delegation" reading survive the exclusive monotheistic claim in the source text?

    7. Titus 2:13 Granville Sharp: Verify the Greek construction. Check Daniel Wallace's research (if available in tools) on how many NT instances follow the article-noun-kai-noun pattern. Note whether exceptions exist for singular, personal, non-proper nouns.

    8. Hebrews 7:16 akatalutos: Verify the Greek akatalutos (indestructible). Check how this word is used elsewhere in the NT/LXX. Test whether "indestructible life" is compatible with a created nature (all created things are by definition destructible by their Creator).

    9. Hebrews 9:12-14 eternal redemption: Verify the argument chain: eternal redemption (aionion lutrosin) requires more than a finite sacrifice. Check whether "eternal Spirit" (aioniou pneumatos) refers to the Holy Spirit or Christ's own eternal nature. Verify Spurgeon's claim that "the blood that He shed was the blood of God" against Acts 20:28.

    10. Self-defeating moral argument: If Jesus is not God, trace the consequences for his moral character: he accepted worship (idolatry by OT standards), claimed to forgive sins against God (blasphemy by the scribes' correct reasoning), accepted the title "my God" (deception), and claimed omnipresence (falsehood). The Bible's greatest moral hero becomes simultaneously its greatest violator of the first two commandments.

    11. Prayer to Jesus: Verify Acts 7:59 (Stephen prays to Jesus while dying), 2 Cor 12:8 (Paul prays to "the Lord" three times — verify whether "Lord" here is Christ from context), and 1 Cor 1:2 (Christians defined as those who "call upon the name of our Lord Jesus Christ"). If Jesus is not God, prayer to him violates the first commandment.

    12. Matt 18:20 and 28:20 omnipresence: Verify Jesus' claims to be present "where two or three are gathered" and "with you always, to the end of the age." If believers are scattered across the globe, simultaneous presence requires omnipresence — an attribute of God alone. Test whether any created being in Scripture claims omnipresence.
