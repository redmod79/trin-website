    Word Studies¶

    Question¶

    What does Scripture teach about the nature of Christ's death and its sufficiency as a ransom (lutron/antilutron)? Does the ransom require exact equivalence between Adam and Christ, and to whom is the ransom paid? Can God die, and did Jesus truly die?

    H3724 — kopher¶

    Source: D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/H3724-kopher.md

    Occurrences: 18

    Hebrew ransom price derived from kaphar (H3722, to cover/atone). Appears in 17 unique verses across 10 books. Dominant usage is as ransom price for life (Exo 21:30, 30:12, Job 33:24, Pro 13:8). Key theological contexts include: ransom prohibited for murder (Num 35:31-32), impossibility of human ransom (Psa 49:7), God providing ransom (Job 33:24, Isa 43:3). LXX maps kopher to Greek lytron (G3083) with PMI 8.83

    Semantic categories: ransom price for life, ransom prohibited/insufficient, God-provided ransom, covering/pitch

    Key verses: Exo 21:30, Exo 30:12, Psa 49:7, Job 33:24, Isa 43:3, Num 35:31

    Relevance: Foundation of the kopher→lytron→antilytron chain. Establishes that (1) ransom is a covering price for life, (2) no human can provide adequate ransom (Psa 49:7), (3) God himself provides the ransom (Job 33:24, Isa 43:3), and (4) ransom can be an alternative to strict lex talionis (Exo 21:30).

    G3083 — lytron¶

    Source: D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/G3083-lytron.md

    Occurrences: 2

    Greek ransom price from lyo (to loose). Appears only in Matt 20:28 and Mark 10:45, both recording the same logion: 'to give his life a ransom (lytron) for (anti) many.' The LXX uses lytron to translate kopher (H3724) in Exo 21:30, Lev 19:20, Num 35:31, Isa 45:13. The construction dounai ten psychen autou lytron anti pollon places lytron as the payment and anti as the substitution marker.

    Semantic categories: substitutionary price for life, OT redemption price via LXX

    Key verses: Matt 20:28, Mark 10:45

    Relevance: The direct ransom term Jesus used. The anti pollon ('for many') establishes that the ransom is one life for MANY — not one for one. Connected to kopher via LXX translation bridge.

    G487 — antilytron¶

    Source: D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/G487-antilytron.md

    Occurrences: 1

    Hapax legomenon in 1 Tim 2:6. Compound of anti (G473, 'in place of') + lytron (G3083, 'ransom'). The JW argument claims anti- adds 'exact equivalence/correspondence,' but modern lexicons define it simply as 'ransom' or 'substitute-ransom' without quantitative correspondence. The construction dous heauton antilytron hyper panton chains three substitutionary markers: heauton (himself as content), anti- (exchange prefix), hyper (on behalf of). Used with hyper panton ('on behalf of all') — expanding scope beyond Matt 20:28's 'many.'

    Semantic categories: substitutionary ransom, self-giving of Christ

    Key verses: 1 Tim 2:6

    Relevance: Central to the exact-equivalence debate. The JW position depends on antilytron meaning 'corresponding/equivalent ransom,' but the word simply means 'ransom given in exchange.' The scope 'for all' contradicts one-for-one equivalence.

    G629 — apolytrosis¶

    Source: D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/G629-apolytrosis.md

    Occurrences: 10

    Complete redemption/liberation, from apo (from) + lytrosis (ransoming). Used for redemption through Christ's blood (Eph 1:7, Col 1:14), the redemption in Christ Jesus (Rom 3:24), eternal redemption (Heb 9:12), and the future redemption of the body (Rom 8:23). The fullest term in the lytron family — emphasizes the completeness of the liberation.

    Semantic categories: complete redemption through blood, eschatological liberation

    Key verses: Rom 3:24, Eph 1:7, Heb 9:12, Rom 8:23

    Relevance: The culmination of the ransom vocabulary — not merely price-paid but full liberation achieved. The 'eternal' quality (Heb 9:12) shows the ransom exceeds temporal limitations.

    G3084 — lytroo¶

    Source: D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/G3084-lytroo.md

    Occurrences: 4

    Verbal form 'to redeem/ransom.' Used in 1 Pet 1:18 (redeemed not with corruptible things), Tit 2:14 (redeem us from all iniquity), Luke 24:21 (hoped he would redeem Israel). The passive elytrothete in 1 Pet 1:18 emphasizes believers as recipients of redemption.

    Semantic categories: act of ransoming, redemption from iniquity

    Key verses: 1 Pet 1:18, Tit 2:14, Luke 24:21

    Relevance: The verbal action of ransom — Christ actively ransoms/redeems. Connected to the noun lytron and the compound antilytron.

    G3085 — lytrosis¶

    Source: D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/G3085-lytrosis.md

    Occurrences: 3

    Ransoming/redemption noun from lytroo (G3084). Three occurrences: Luke 1:68 (messianic redemption of God's people), Luke 2:38 (looking for redemption of Jerusalem), Heb 9:12 (eternal redemption obtained through Christ's blood). The Heb 9:12 usage is critical — lytrosis aionion ('eternal redemption') connects the ransom word family to divine/eternal quality.

    Semantic categories: messianic redemption, sacrificial atonement

    Key verses: Luke 1:68, Luke 2:38, Heb 9:12

    Relevance: Links the lytron family to 'eternal' quality in Heb 9:12. The ransom produces not temporary relief but eternal redemption — requiring more than a finite human life.

    G1805 — exagorazo¶

    Source: D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/G1805-exagorazo.md

    Occurrences: 4

    To buy up/out, redeem from the marketplace. Used in Gal 3:13 (redeemed from the curse of the law), Gal 4:5 (redeem those under law), Eph 5:16 and Col 4:5 (redeeming the time). Adds the marketplace dimension to ransom vocabulary — Christ purchased our freedom.

    Semantic categories: marketplace redemption, liberation from legal bondage

    Key verses: Gal 3:13, Gal 4:5

    Relevance: Substitutionary mechanism — Christ became a curse (Gal 3:13) to buy us out of the curse's domain.

    H6299 — padah¶

    Source: D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/H6299-padah.md

    Occurrences: 67

    Hebrew verb to ransom/redeem. Used in the emphatic impossibility construction padoh yipdeh in Psa 49:7 (infinitive absolute + imperfect = 'can by no means redeem'). Also used of God's redemptive activity: Psa 49:15 (God will redeem), Hos 13:14 (I will ransom them), 2 Sam 4:9 (the LORD who redeemed my soul).

    Semantic categories: human ransom impossibility, divine redemption, firstborn redemption

    Key verses: Psa 49:7, Psa 49:15, Hos 13:14

    Relevance: Establishes the impossibility of human ransom (Psa 49:7) and the necessity of divine intervention (Psa 49:15). The infinitive absolute construction intensifies the impossibility.

    H1350 — gaal¶

    Source: D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/H1350-gaal.md

    Occurrences: 116

    Kinsman-redeemer verb. The goel must be a blood relative who has the means to redeem. Used of God as Israel's Redeemer (Isa 44:24, 54:5) and of Boaz redeeming Ruth's family property. The kinship requirement means the redeemer must share the nature of those redeemed.

    Semantic categories: kinsman redemption, divine redemption, property restoration

    Key verses: Isa 44:24, Ruth 4:4-6

    Relevance: The kinship requirement explains why the divine Son had to become human (Heb 2:14-17) — to be a kinsman-redeemer. But the goel must also have means — a merely human goel could not ransom from death (Psa 49:7).

    H3722 — kaphar¶

    Source: D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/H3722-kaphar.md

    Occurrences: 130

    To cover/atone. Root of kopher (H3724, ransom) and kapporeth (mercy seat/propitiatory). The Piel form lekhapper ('to make atonement') dominates Levitical usage. Lev 17:11 establishes the principle: blood makes atonement for the soul.

    Semantic categories: atonement/covering, blood sacrifice

    Key verses: Lev 17:11, Lev 16:30

    Relevance: Root of the ransom vocabulary chain. Connects the concept of covering/atonement to the ransom price and the mercy seat where propitiation occurs.

    G110 — athanasia¶

    Source: D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/G110-athanasia.md

    Occurrences: 3

    Immortality/deathlessness. Alpha-privative of thanatos (G2288, death). Three NT occurrences: 1 Cor 15:53-54 (mortals put on immortality at resurrection), 1 Tim 6:16 (God alone has immortality). The word denotes inherent deathlessness as divine attribute and acquired deathlessness for resurrected believers.

    Semantic categories: divine essential attribute, eschatological acquisition by believers

    Key verses: 1 Tim 6:16, 1 Cor 15:53-54

    Relevance: Central to the 'can God die?' question. If God ALONE has immortality (1 Tim 6:16), then Christ's genuine death requires the incarnation mechanism — the divine nature remains immortal while the human nature dies.

    G862 — aphthartos¶

    Source: D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/G862-aphthartos.md

    Occurrences: 7

    Incorruptible/imperishable. Alpha-privative of phtheiro (G5351, to corrupt). Applied to God (1 Tim 1:17, Rom 1:23), to the resurrection body (1 Cor 15:52), and to the imperishable inheritance (1 Pet 1:4). When applied to God, it denotes a nature beyond decay and destruction.

    Semantic categories: divine attribute — beyond corruption, resurrection body quality

    Key verses: 1 Tim 1:17, 1 Cor 15:52, 1 Pet 1:4

    Relevance: Another alpha-privative divine attribute. The incorruptible God cannot be permanently destroyed by death — the divine nature persists through the death of the human nature.

    G861 — aphtharsia¶

    Source: D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/G861-aphtharsia.md

    Occurrences: 8

    Incorruptibility/immortality. Noun form corresponding to aphthartos (G862). Used for what believers put on at resurrection (1 Cor 15:42,50,53-54), what Christ brought to light (2 Tim 1:10), and what those who love Christ seek (Rom 2:7, Eph 6:24).

    Semantic categories: resurrection quality, eschatological hope, divine attribute

    Key verses: 1 Cor 15:42, 1 Cor 15:53-54, 2 Tim 1:10

    Relevance: Christ 'brought immortality to light' (2 Tim 1:10) — the ransom's ultimate result is the gift of incorruptibility to mortal humans.

    G2434 — hilasmos¶

    Source: D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/G2434-hilasmos.md

    Occurrences: 2

    Propitiation/atoning sacrifice. Used in 1 John 2:2 (propitiation for our sins and the whole world) and 1 John 4:10 (God sent his Son as propitiation). The word denotes the means by which divine wrath is averted and sin is covered.

    Semantic categories: atoning sacrifice, divine initiative in atonement

    Key verses: 1 John 2:2, 1 John 4:10

    Relevance: Shows the ransom is directed toward God's justice (propitiation), not toward Satan. God PROVIDES the propitiation (1 John 4:10) — God is both provider and recipient of the ransom.

    G2435 — hilasterion¶

    Source: D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/G2435-hilasterion.md

    Occurrences: 2

    Propitiation/mercy seat. Two NT uses: Rom 3:25 (God set forth Christ as hilasterion through faith in his blood) and Heb 9:5 (the mercy seat over the ark). The same Greek word denotes both the OT mercy seat (kapporeth) and Christ as the antitype propitiation. God is the agent who 'set forth' (proetheto) Christ publicly.

    Semantic categories: mercy seat/kapporeth, Christ as propitiatory sacrifice

    Key verses: Rom 3:25, Heb 9:5

    Relevance: Answers the 'to whom is the ransom paid?' question — it is directed toward God's justice as propitiation (Rom 3:25). God SET FORTH Christ — God is the initiator. The same word for the mercy seat ties Christ's sacrifice to the Day of Atonement ritual.

    G2673 — katargeo¶

    Source: D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/G2673-katargeo.md

    Occurrences: 32

    Render powerless/destroy/abolish. 27 tokens across 26 verses, predominantly Pauline. Used in Heb 2:14 (Christ through death rendered powerless the devil), 2 Tim 1:10 (Christ abolished death), 1 Cor 15:26 (last enemy destroyed is death). The word does not mean 'annihilate' but 'render idle/ineffective.'

    Semantic categories: render powerless, abolish/do away with, cease/pass away

    Key verses: Heb 2:14, 2 Tim 1:10, 1 Cor 15:26

    Relevance: The ransom's effect on the devil is katargeo — rendering him powerless, not paying him. The ransom's effect on death is also katargeo — abolishing death's power. This is victory language, not transaction language.

    G129 — haima¶

    Source: D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/G129-haima.md

    Occurrences: 99

    Blood. Central to atonement theology. Used for Christ's blood as ransom price (Acts 20:28, 1 Pet 1:19), propitiation medium (Rom 3:25), covenant seal (Heb 13:20), cleansing agent (1 John 1:7, Rev 12:11). Acts 20:28 attributes blood to God himself.

    Semantic categories: sacrificial blood, covenant blood, cleansing blood

    Key verses: Acts 20:28, Rom 3:25, Heb 9:14, 1 Pet 1:19

    Relevance: The ransom medium. Acts 20:28 calls it 'God's own blood' — attributing divine value to the ransom price.

    G2288 — thanatos¶

    Source: D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/G2288-thanatos.md

    Occurrences: 119

    Death. 119 NT occurrences. Used for physical death, spiritual death, and the personified power of death (Heb 2:14). Christ's death is described as genuine (Phil 2:8 'death of the cross'), voluntary (John 10:17-18), and victorious (1 Cor 15:55-57). The base word negated in a-thanasia (G110).

    Semantic categories: physical death, spiritual death, death as power/enemy

    Key verses: Phil 2:8, Heb 2:14, 1 Cor 15:26, Rev 1:18

    Relevance: The ransom price IS death — Christ's genuine death. Death is also the ENEMY destroyed by the ransom (Heb 2:14, 2 Tim 1:10).

    H817 — asham¶

    Source: D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/H817-asham.md

    Occurrences: 47

    Guilt offering/trespass offering. Distinguished from chattat (H2403, sin offering) by the requirement of restitution plus one-fifth surplus (Lev 5:15-16, 6:5). Isaiah 53:10 deliberately uses asham rather than chattat to describe the servant's offering, invoking the surplus principle — the ransom MORE than compensates for what was lost.

    Semantic categories: guilt offering with surplus, restitution plus penalty

    Key verses: Isa 53:10, Lev 5:15-16, Lev 6:5

    Relevance: The asham surplus principle directly contradicts exact equivalence. If Christ is an asham, his ransom is designed to EXCEED the loss — restoration plus one-fifth. This connects to Paul's 'much more' (pollo mallon) language in Rom 5:15-17.

    G1849 — exousia¶

    Source: D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/G1849-exousia.md

    Occurrences: 103

    Authority/power — the right and ability to act. In John 10:18, Christ claims exousia to both lay down and take up his life. This is divine prerogative — no mere human has authority over their own resurrection.

    Semantic categories: authority/right to act, divine prerogative

    Key verses: John 10:18

    Relevance: Christ's authority over his own death and resurrection demonstrates that the one paying the ransom is more than human. A mere human ransom would have no power to self-resurrect.

    G3348 — metecho¶

    Source: D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/G3348-metecho.md

    Occurrences: 10

    To share/partake voluntarily. In Heb 2:14 (aorist meteschen), contrasted with koinoneo (G2841, perfect kekoinoneken). The aorist marks Christ's bounded voluntary entry into human mortality; the perfect marks the children's ongoing permanent state. Christ partook of flesh and blood deliberately to enable his death.

    Semantic categories: voluntary participation, deliberate sharing

    Key verses: Heb 2:14

    Relevance: The incarnation mechanism — Christ voluntarily entered mortal humanity (metecho, punctiliar) while the children permanently share in it (koinoneo, perfect). This explains how the immortal God could die: by voluntarily assuming mortal nature.

    G2841 — koinoneo¶

    Source: D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/G2841-koinoneo.md

    Occurrences: 8

    To share/participate naturally. Perfect tense kekoinoneken in Heb 2:14 describes the children's permanent, ongoing state of being flesh-and-blood beings. Contrasted with metecho's aorist to show the fundamental difference between Christ's voluntary entry and humanity's natural state.

    Semantic categories: natural participation, permanent shared state

    Key verses: Heb 2:14

    Relevance: The grammatical contrast with metecho (G3348) in Heb 2:14 is theologically critical: children ARE flesh (permanent perfect); Christ TOOK ON flesh (bounded aorist). The incarnation is voluntary, not natural.

    G166 — aionios¶

    Source: D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/G166-aionios.md

    Occurrences: 71

    Eternal/age-long. Used to qualify the Spirit through whom Christ offered himself (Heb 9:14, 'eternal Spirit'), the redemption Christ obtained (Heb 9:12, 'eternal redemption'), and the covenant sealed by Christ's blood (Heb 13:20, 'everlasting covenant'). The 'eternal' quality of the sacrifice elevates it beyond any temporal/finite transaction.

    Semantic categories: eternal divine quality, age-lasting

    Key verses: Heb 9:12, Heb 9:14, Heb 13:20

    Relevance: The ransom has eternal quality — eternal Spirit, eternal redemption, everlasting covenant. A merely finite human sacrifice could not produce eternal results.

    G4151 — pneuma¶

    Source: D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/G4151-pneuma.md

    Occurrences: 385

    Spirit. In Heb 9:14, 'through the eternal Spirit' (dia pneumatos aioniou) describes the means by which Christ offered himself. Whether this refers to the Holy Spirit or Christ's own eternal divine nature, the phrase attributes transcendent, non-temporal quality to the sacrifice.

    Semantic categories: Holy Spirit, divine nature, human spirit

    Key verses: Heb 9:14

    Relevance: The 'eternal Spirit' in Heb 9:14 is the key to the sacrifice's infinite value — it transcends finite human capacity regardless of whether pneuma here refers to the Holy Spirit or Christ's divine nature.

    G5248 — huperperisseuo¶

    Source: D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/G5248-huperperisseno.md

    Occurrences: 2

    To superabound — compound of hyper (over/beyond) + perisseuo (to abound). Two NT occurrences: Rom 5:20 (grace superabounded where sin abounded) and 2 Cor 7:4 (Paul's exceeding joy). The Rom 5:20 usage is the climax of Paul's surplus argument — grace does not merely equal sin but hyper-exceeds it.

    Semantic categories: super-abundance of grace

    Key verses: Rom 5:20

    Relevance: The strongest surplus term in Paul's vocabulary. If grace merely equaled sin (exact equivalence), Paul would use perisseuo. Instead he uses the hyper- intensifier — grace goes BEYOND equivalence.

    G5250 — huperpleonazo¶

    Source: D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/G5250-huperpleonazo.md

    Occurrences: 1

    To exceed abundantly — compound of hyper + pleonazo (to increase). Used in 1 Tim 1:14 (grace exceeding abundantly with faith and love). Another hyper-compound denoting grace's excess beyond any measurable limit.

    Semantic categories: hyper-abundant grace

    Key verses: 1 Tim 1:14

    Relevance: Part of Paul's vocabulary of surplus. Grace does not merely cover sin — it exceeds abundantly.

    H2403 — chattat¶

    Source: D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/H2403-chattaah.md

    Occurrences: 306

    Sin offering. The standard purification sacrifice in the Levitical system. Distinct from asham (H817) in not requiring the surplus/restitution element. Isaiah 53:10's choice of asham OVER chattat is theologically deliberate — invoking debt-resolution with surplus rather than mere purification.

    Semantic categories: sin offering/purification, standard atonement sacrifice

    Key verses: Lev 4:3, Lev 4:14

    Relevance: The contrast with asham (H817) illuminates why Isaiah chose asham for the servant's offering — the ransom is designed as MORE than equivalence (asham surplus), not mere purification (chattat).

    G3316 — mesites¶

    Source: D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/G3316-mesites.md

    Occurrences: 6

    Mediator — one who stands between two parties to reconcile them. Used in 1 Tim 2:5 (one mediator between God and men, the man Christ Jesus), Heb 9:15 (mediator of the new testament), Heb 8:6 (mediator of a better covenant). The mediator must have connection to BOTH parties — divine connection to God and human connection to mankind.

    Semantic categories: covenant mediator, reconciliation agent

    Key verses: 1 Tim 2:5, Heb 9:15, Heb 8:6

    Relevance: The mediator's dual qualification — must be connected to both parties. 'The man Christ Jesus' establishes the human connection; other texts establish the divine connection. The ransom is paid BY a mediator who belongs to both sides.

    G2643 — katallage¶

    Source: D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/G2643-katallage.md

    Occurrences: 4

    Reconciliation — the restoration of a right relationship between estranged parties. Used in Rom 5:11, 11:15, and 2 Cor 5:18-19. God initiates the reconciliation (2 Cor 5:18-19) — 'God was in Christ reconciling the world to himself.'

    Semantic categories: divine-human reconciliation, cosmic restoration

    Key verses: Rom 5:11, 2 Cor 5:18-19

    Relevance: The ransom's purpose is reconciliation. God is both the initiator ('all things are of God, who reconciled us') and the one reconciled TO ('reconciling the world to himself'). This is the 'self-payment' — God provides what satisfies his own justice.

    G473 — anti¶

    Source: D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/G473-antí.md

    Occurrences: 22

    Primary preposition meaning 'instead of, in place of, in exchange for.' 22 NT occurrences. Core meaning is substitution/exchange (Mat 2:22 'in the room of'; Mat 5:38 'eye for eye'; Mat 20:28 'ransom for many'). Also used causally ('because of'). As prefix in antilytron, it makes the exchange idea explicit but does NOT add mathematical exactitude — Mat 5:38 uses anti for lex talionis, but Exo 21:30 in the same legal context allows ransom as alternative to strict anti.

    Semantic categories: substitution/exchange, cause/reason, correspondence/requital

    Key verses: Mat 20:28, Mat 5:38, Mat 2:22, Heb 12:16

    Relevance: Critical for the antilytron debate. Anti marks exchange/substitution but does NOT mandate mathematical exactitude. The preposition's range includes proportional (eye for eye) AND disproportionate exchange (Heb 12:16, birthright for one morsel). The JW claim that anti demands exact equivalence is not supported by the word's usage.

    G5228 — hyper¶

    Source: D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/G5228-huper.md

    Occurrences: 168

    Preposition 'on behalf of, for the sake of' (with genitive) or 'above, beyond' (with accusative). 168 NT occurrences. In 1 Tim 2:6, hyper panton ('on behalf of all') specifies the ransom's beneficiaries. The genitive-case 'on behalf of' dominates NT usage (approx. 150 of 168 occurrences).

    Semantic categories: representation/benefit, substitution, superiority/excess

    Key verses: 1 Tim 2:6, 2 Cor 5:14, Gal 3:13

    Relevance: The preposition connecting the ransom to its beneficiaries. In 1 Tim 2:6, hyper panton ('for all') establishes universal scope — the ransom covers everyone, not just one man.

    H6306 — pidyom¶

    Source: D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/H6306-pidyôwm.md

    Occurrences: 3

    Ransom price from padah (H6299). Three occurrences: Exo 21:30 (ransom for life as alternative to death penalty), Num 3:49 (redemption money for firstborn), Psa 49:8 (redemption of the soul is too precious). The Exo 21:30 usage is critical — pidyon is explicitly an alternative to lex talionis life-for-life.

    Semantic categories: redemption payment for life, impossibility of self-redemption

    Key verses: Exo 21:30, Psa 49:8, Num 3:49

    Relevance: Exo 21:30 proves the Torah itself allowed ransom (pidyon/kopher) as alternative to strict life-for-life, undermining the JW claim that lex talionis demands exact equivalence. Psa 49:8 declares the pidyon of a soul is too precious for any human to pay.
