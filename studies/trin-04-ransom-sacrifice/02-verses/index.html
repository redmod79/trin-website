    Verses¶

    Question¶

    What does Scripture teach about the nature of Christ's death and its sufficiency as a ransom (lutron/antilutron)? Does the ransom require exact equivalence between Adam and Christ, and to whom is the ransom paid? Can God die, and did Jesus truly die?

    Christ gave his life as lutron anti pollon — ransom for many¶

    Even as the Son of man came not to be ministered unto, but to minister, and to give his life a ransom for many.

    Context: Jesus teaches the disciples about servant leadership, concluding with the purpose of his coming — to serve and to give his life as a ransom price (lutron) in exchange for (anti) many.

    • λύτρον (G3083) — N-ASN — Acc Sg N
    • ἀντὶ (G473) — PREP — πολλῶν. πολύς G4183 [A-GPM] Gen Pl M

    Notes: The construction lutron anti pollon establishes two key points: (1) the ransom is one life for MANY lives, not one for one; (2) anti marks substitution/exchange. The scope 'for many' undermines exact equivalence with one man (Adam). Paralleled in Mark 10:45.

    Synoptic parallel — Son of man gives his life a ransom for many¶

    For even the Son of man came not to be ministered unto, but to minister, and to give his life a ransom for many.

    Context: Identical logion to Matt 20:28 in the Markan account, following the request of James and John for positions of honor.

    Notes: Same construction as Matt 20:28. Both Synoptic accounts preserve lutron anti pollon. Luke's parallel (22:27) rephrases without the ransom vocabulary.

    The man Christ Jesus gave himself as antilutron for all¶

    For [there is] one God, and one mediator between God and men, the man Christ Jesus; Who gave himself a ransom for all, to be testified in due time.

    Context: Paul urges prayer for all men, grounding it in God's desire for all to be saved. Christ's mediatorial role requires both deity (connection to the one God) and humanity (the man Christ Jesus).

    • μεσίτης (G3316) — N-NSM — Nom Sg M
    • ἄνθρωπος (G444) — N-NSM — Nom Sg M

    Notes: Antilutron (G487, hapax) is used with hyper panton ('on behalf of all') — expanding the scope from Matt 20:28's 'many' to 'all.' The anti- prefix makes the exchange idea explicit but does NOT add 'exact equivalence' — modern lexicons define it simply as 'ransom.' Christ is called 'the man' (anthropos) to establish mediatorial qualification, not to limit his nature. The active reflexive 'gave himself' (dous heauton) emphasizes voluntary self-offering.

    Adam-Christ parallel — the free gift is NOT like the offense¶

    Wherefore, as by one man sin entered into the world, and death by sin; and so death passed upon all men, for that all have sinned: (For until the law sin was in the world: but sin is not imputed when there is no law. Nevertheless death reigned from Adam to Moses, even over them that had not sinned after the similitude of Adam's transgression, who is the figure of him that was to come. But not as the offence, so also [is] the free gift. For if through the offence of one many be dead, much more the grace of God, and the gift by grace, [which is] by one man, Jesus Christ, hath abounded unto many. And not as [it was] by one that sinned, [so is] the gift: for the judgment [was] by one to condemnation, but the free gift [is] of many offences unto justification. For if by one man's offence death reigned by one; much more they which receive abundance of grace and of the gift of righteousness shall reign in life by one, Jesus Christ.) Therefore as by the offence of one [judgment came] upon all men to condemnation; even so by the righteousness of one [the free gift came] upon all men unto justification of life. For as by one man's disobedience many were made sinners, so by the obedience of one shall many be made righteous. Moreover the law entered, that the offence might abound. But where sin abounded, grace did much more abound: That as sin hath reigned unto death, even so might grace reign through righteousness unto eternal life by Jesus Christ our Lord.

    Context: Paul develops the Adam-Christ typology in Romans 5. Adam is 'the figure of him that was to come' (v.14), but Paul immediately breaks the parallel in v.15 with ouch hos ('not as') — the gift is NOT like the offense. Three times Paul uses pollo mallon ('much more') to assert grace's surplus over sin.

    Notes: The critical phrase ouch hos to paraptoma houtos kai to charisma ('not as the offense, so also the free gift') in v.15 is Paul's explicit denial of equivalence. The a fortiori reasoning (pollo mallon) in vv.15,17 argues from lesser to greater — if one offense brought death to many, MUCH MORE does grace abound. This is surplus language, not equivalence. The verb eperisseuen (G4052, aorist of perisseuo) in v.15 means 'abounded.' In v.17, perisseia (G4050, 'abundance') of grace exceeds the offense. In v.20, huperperisseuen (G5248, 'superabounded') — grace hyper-exceeds sin. This demolishes the JW claim of exact one-for-one equivalence.

    Pollo mallon surplus language — grace exceeds the offense¶

    But not as the offence, so also [is] the free gift. For if through the offence of one many be dead, much more the grace of God, and the gift by grace, [which is] by one man, Jesus Christ, hath abounded unto many. And not as [it was] by one that sinned, [so is] the gift: for the judgment [was] by one to condemnation, but the free gift [is] of many offences unto justification. For if by one man's offence death reigned by one; much more they which receive abundance of grace and of the gift of righteousness shall reign in life by one, Jesus Christ.)

    Context: The specific verses within Rom 5:12-21 that contain Paul's three pollo mallon statements and the denial of equivalence.

    • οὐχ (G3756) — PRT-N — ὡς ὡς G5613 [ADV]
    • πολλῷ (G4183) — A-DSN — Dat Sg N
    • μᾶλλον (G3123) — ADV — ἐπερίσσευσεν. περισσεύω G4052 [V-AAI-3S] Aor Act Ind Sg thirdPers

    Notes: v.15: ouch hos + pollo mallon + eperisseuen — denial + surplus + abounding. v.16: ouk hos — second denial. v.17: pollo mallon + perisseia — surplus + abundance. The pattern is unmistakable: Paul insists the ransom exceeds what was lost.

    The church of God purchased with his own blood¶

    Take heed therefore unto yourselves, and to all the flock, over the which the Holy Ghost hath made you overseers, to feed the church of God, which he hath purchased with his own blood.

    Context: Paul's farewell address to the Ephesian elders at Miletus. He charges them to shepherd the church, identifying it as God's church purchased by God's own blood.

    • Θεοῦ (G2316) — N-GSM — Gen Sg M
    • περιεποιήσατο (G4046) — V-AMI-3S — Aor Mid Ind Sg thirdPers
    • αἵματος (G129) — N-GSN — Gen Sg N
    • ἰδίου (G2398) — A-GSM — Gen Sg M

    Notes: The N1904 reads 'ten ekklesian tou Theou' (church of God) and 'dia tou haimatos tou idiou' (through/by his own blood). Textual variant: some MSS read kyriou (Lord) instead of Theou (God). Both N1904 and TR read Theou. The phrase attributes blood to God — theologically significant for the divine value of the ransom. The verb peripoieomai (G4046, aorist middle) means 'acquired/purchased for himself.'

    Christ partook of flesh and blood to taste death and destroy the devil¶

    But we see Jesus, who was made a little lower than the angels for the suffering of death, crowned with glory and honour; that he by the grace of God should taste death for every man. For it became him, for whom [are] all things, and by whom [are] all things, in bringing many sons unto glory, to make the captain of their salvation perfect through sufferings. For both he that sanctifieth and they who are sanctified [are] all of one: for which cause he is not ashamed to call them brethren, Saying, I will declare thy name unto my brethren, in the midst of the church will I sing praise unto thee. And again, I will put my trust in him. And again, Behold I and the children which God hath given me. Forasmuch then as the children are partakers of flesh and blood, he also himself likewise took part of the same; that through death he might destroy him that had the power of death, that is, the devil; And deliver them who through fear of death were all their lifetime subject to bondage. For verily he took not on [him the nature of] angels; but he took on [him] the seed of Abraham. Wherefore in all things it behoved him to be made like unto [his] brethren, that he might be a merciful and faithful high priest in things [pertaining] to God, to make reconciliation for the sins of the people.

    Context: Hebrews 2 explains why the divine Son had to become human. The passage moves from the incarnation's purpose (tasting death for every man) through the mechanism (partaking of flesh and blood) to the result (destroying the devil's death-power).

    Notes: v.9: 'taste death for every man' (hyper pantos) — universal scope. v.14: kekoinoneken (G2841, perfect active = permanent state of children sharing in flesh/blood) vs meteschen (G3348, aorist active = Christ's bounded voluntary entry). This grammatical contrast shows Christ voluntarily entered mortal humanity, not that he was always mortal. v.14: katargese (G2673, aorist subjunctive of katargeo) — purpose clause: 'that through death he might render powerless' the devil. The ransom is not PAID to the devil but DEFEATS him. v.17: 'to make reconciliation (hilaskesthai) for the sins of the people' — the sacrifice is directed toward God, not Satan.

    Eternal redemption through the eternal Spirit¶

    Neither by the blood of goats and calves, but by his own blood he entered in once into the holy place, having obtained eternal redemption [for us]. For if the blood of bulls and of goats, and the ashes of an heifer sprinkling the unclean, sanctifieth to the purifying of the flesh: How much more shall the blood of Christ, who through the eternal Spirit offered himself without spot to God, purge your conscience from dead works to serve the living God?

    Context: The author of Hebrews contrasts the Levitical system with Christ's superior sacrifice. The 'eternal Spirit' (pneumatos aioniou) imparts divine quality to the offering.

    Notes: v.12: lytrosis (G3085) aionion — 'eternal redemption,' connecting the lytron word family to eternal/divine quality. v.14: poso mallon — 'how much more,' another a fortiori argument (same pattern as Rom 5:15-17). dia Pneumatos aioniou — 'through the eternal Spirit' — whether this refers to the Holy Spirit or Christ's divine nature, it means the sacrifice transcends finite human capacity. The offering is amomon ('without blemish,' G299) and directed to Theo ('to God,' G2316) — God is the recipient, not Satan. prosenengken (G4374, aorist active of prosphero) — 'offered himself' — active reflexive, voluntary self-offering.

    The power of an indestructible life¶

    Who is made, not after the law of a carnal commandment, but after the power of an endless life.

    Context: Hebrews argues Christ's priesthood is of a different order than the Levitical — based not on genealogical law but on the quality of his life.

    • ζωῆς (G2222) — N-GSF — Gen Sg F
    • ἀκαταλύτου (G179) — A-GSF — Gen Sg F

    Notes: akatalytou (G179) — alpha-privative of katalyo ('to destroy/dissolve'), meaning 'indestructible/indissoluble.' This is the life that backs the ransom — an indestructible life that death cannot permanently hold. Christ's priesthood depends on a nature (zoe akatalytou) that transcends mortal limitations. Connects to Acts 2:24 where death 'could not hold him.'

    He ever lives to make intercession¶

    Wherefore he is able also to save them to the uttermost that come unto God by him, seeing he ever liveth to make intercession for them.

    Context: The conclusion of the Melchizedek priesthood argument — Christ's ongoing life validates and applies the once-for-all sacrifice.

    Notes: panteles ('to the uttermost/completely') — the ransom's effect is total, not partial. 'Ever liveth' (pantote zon) — present active participle, continuous state. The ransom is sufficient because the one who paid it lives forever to apply it.

    Christ's voluntary authority over his own death and resurrection¶

    Therefore doth my Father love me, because I lay down my life, that I might take it again. No man taketh it from me, but I lay it down of myself. I have power to lay it down, and I have power to take it again. This commandment have I received of my Father.

    Context: Jesus speaks as the Good Shepherd who lays down his life for the sheep. His death is entirely voluntary — no one takes his life; he lays it down with authority.

    • τίθημι (G5087) — V-PAI-1S — Pres Act Ind Sg firstPers
    • ἵνα (G2443) — CONJ — λάβω λαμβάνω G2983 [V-2AAS-1S] Aor Act Subj Sg firstPers
    • ἐξουσίαν (G1849) — N-ASF — Acc Sg F
    • λαβεῖν (G2983) — V-2AAN — Aor Act Inf

    Notes: exousian (G1849) appears twice — authority to lay down AND authority to take up again. tithemi (G5087, present active) — 'I lay down' — continuous willingness. hina palin labo (G2983, aorist active subjunctive) — purpose clause: 'that I might take it again.' This claim is unique to Christ — no mere human has authority over his own resurrection. The voluntariness of the death is itself evidence that the one dying is more than human.

    God alone possesses immortality¶

    Who only hath immortality, dwelling in the light which no man can approach unto; whom no man hath seen, nor can see: to whom [be] honour and power everlasting. Amen.

    Context: Paul's doxology to the 'blessed and only Potentate, King of kings and Lord of lords' (v.15). The divine attributes listed are exclusive: only (monos) immortality, unapproachable light, invisibility.

    • μόνος (G3441) — A-NSM — Nom Sg M
    • ἔχων (G2192) — V-PAP-NSM — Pres Act Ptcp Nom Sg M
    • ἀθανασίαν (G110) — N-ASF — Acc Sg F

    Notes: monos (G3441) echon (G2192, present active participle) athanasiан (G110) — 'the only one having immortality.' God alone possesses deathlessness as an inherent attribute. This creates the tension: if God alone has immortality, and Christ is God, how can Christ die? The resolution is the incarnation — the divine nature remains immortal while the assumed human nature genuinely dies. aprostion (G676) — 'unapproachable' — another alpha-privative term.

    The King eternal, immortal, invisible — divine attributes¶

    Now unto the King eternal, immortal, invisible, the only wise God, [be] honour and glory for ever and ever. Amen.

    Context: Paul's doxology after recounting his own conversion. Three alpha-privative adjectives describe God: aphthartos (incorruptible), aoratos (invisible), combined with monos (only).

    • ἀφθάρτῳ (G862) — A-DSM — Dat Sg M
    • ἀοράτῳ (G517) — A-DSM — Dat Sg M
    • μόνῳ (G3441) — A-DSM — Dat Sg M

    Notes: aphtharto (G862, dative singular) — 'incorruptible/imperishable.' aorato (G517) — 'invisible.' mono (G3441) — 'only.' These are alpha-privative constructions denying corruption, visibility, and plurality. The 'incorruptible' attribute connects to the death question — the divine nature cannot be corrupted or destroyed.

    Death could not hold him — impossibility language¶

    Whom God hath raised up, having loosed the pains of death: because it was not possible that he should be holden of it.

    Context: Peter's Pentecost sermon. He declares that God raised Jesus, and then explains WHY resurrection was inevitable — it was impossible (ouk en dynaton) for death to hold him.

    • οὐκ (G3756) — PRT-N — ἦν εἰμί G1510 [V-IAI-3S] Impf Act Ind Sg thirdPers
    • δυνατὸν (G1415) — A-NSN — Nom Sg N
    • κρατεῖσθαι (G2902) — V-PPN — Pres Pass Inf

    Notes: ouk en dynaton (G1415) — 'it was not possible.' krateisthai (G2902, present passive infinitive) — 'to be held/mastered.' The impossibility language is remarkable — death's inability to hold Christ is treated as an inherent impossibility based on his nature, not merely a divine intervention. This connects to the 'power of an indestructible life' in Heb 7:16. The pains/birth-pangs (odinas, G5604) of death were 'loosed' (lysas, from lyo — the same root as lytron) — wordplay connecting ransom and release.

    Triumph over principalities — the ransom defeats, not pays, Satan¶

    Blotting out the handwriting of ordinances that was against us, which was contrary to us, and took it out of the way, nailing it to his cross; [And] having spoiled principalities and powers, he made a shew of them openly, triumphing over them in it.

    Context: Paul describes what happened at the cross in terms of legal cancellation and military triumph. The 'handwriting of ordinances' (cheirographon) is cancelled, and the hostile powers are publicly defeated.

    • θριαμβεύσας (G2358) — V-AAP-NSM — Aor Act Ptcp Nom Sg M
    • ἀπεκδυσάμενος (G554) — V-ADP-NSM — Aor Mid Ptcp Nom Sg M

    Notes: thriambeusas (G2358, aorist active participle) — 'having triumphed over.' This is Roman triumphal procession language — the defeated enemy paraded in humiliation. apekdysamenos (G554, aorist middle participle) — 'having disarmed/stripped.' The cross does not pay Satan; it defeats and humiliates him. The ransom's EFFECT on the devil is destruction of his power (cf. Heb 2:14), not payment to him.

    No man can by any means redeem his brother — emphatic impossibility¶

    None [of them] can by any means redeem his brother, nor give to God a ransom for him: (For the redemption of their soul [is] precious, and it ceaseth for ever:)

    Context: Psalm 49 contrasts the foolish trust in wealth with the reality that no amount of money can buy immortality. The emphatic construction padoh yipdeh uses the infinitive absolute to intensify the impossibility.

    Notes: padoh yipdeh — infinitive absolute (padoh) + imperfect (yipdeh) of padah (H6299).     Notes: di' heautou katharismon poiesamenos ton hamartion — 'having by himself made purification of sins.' The one who purges sins is the same one who upholds all things by the word of his power — divine agency in the atonement. 'Sat down' (ekathisen) — completed work, the ransom fully paid.

    Bore our sins in his own body on the tree¶

    Who his own self bare our sins in his own body on the tree, that we, being dead to sins, should live unto righteousness: by whose stripes ye were healed.

    Context: Peter describes Christ's substitutionary sin-bearing, echoing Isaiah 53:4-5.

    Notes: anenegken (G399, aorist active of anaphero) — 'bore/carried up.' The sin-bearing is in his body (en to somati autou) — genuine physical death. The healing comes 'by whose stripes' — echoing Isa 53:5.

    Christ suffered once for sins, the just for the unjust¶

    For Christ also hath once suffered for sins, the just for the unjust, that he might bring us to God, being put to death in the flesh, but quickened by the Spirit:

    Context: Peter summarizes the atonement: Christ (the just) suffered as substitute for the unjust. He was genuinely 'put to death in the flesh' but 'quickened by the Spirit.'

    Notes: hapax — 'once' — the ransom is unrepeatable. dikaios hyper adikon — 'just one on behalf of unjust ones.' thanatotheis men sarki — 'put to death in the flesh' — genuine death. zoopoietheis de pneumati — 'made alive in the spirit' — genuine resurrection. The flesh/spirit contrast maps onto the incarnation: the human nature died; the divine/spiritual nature is the basis of resurrection.

    One died for all, then were all dead — scope of death¶

    For the love of Christ constraineth us; because we thus judge, that if one died for all, then were all dead: And [that] he died for all, that they which live should not henceforth live unto themselves, but unto him which died for them, and rose again.

    Context: Paul states the scope: one died for ALL (hyper panton). If one life pays for all, the payment infinitely exceeds a one-for-one exchange.

    Notes: heis hyper panton apethanen — 'one died on behalf of all.' This is the scope argument against exact equivalence: if the ransom were one-for-one, Christ's death could only cover Adam. But Paul says it covers ALL.

    God was in Christ reconciling the world¶

    And all things [are] of God, who hath reconciled us to himself by Jesus Christ, and hath given to us the ministry of reconciliation; To wit, that God was in Christ, reconciling the world unto himself, not imputing their trespasses unto them; and hath committed unto us the word of reconciliation.

    Context: God's presence IN Christ during the reconciling work. God is not absent from the sacrifice but present in it.

    Notes: Theos en en Christo kosmon katallason heauto — 'God was in Christ reconciling the world to himself.' God is the initiator (all things are OF God), the agent (reconciling), and the beneficiary (to himself). This is the 'self-payment' — God provides what God's justice requires.

    The Son of God who loved me and gave himself for me¶

    I am crucified with Christ: nevertheless I live; yet not I, but Christ liveth in me: and the life which I now live in the flesh I live by the faith of the Son of God, who loved me, and gave himself for me.

    Context: Paul's personal appropriation of the ransom. The 'Son of God' is the one who gave himself — divine identity is inseparable from the self-giving.

    Notes: tou huiou tou theou — 'the Son of God' — divine identity. paradontos heauton hyper emou — 'who gave himself on behalf of me.' Personal appropriation of the universal ransom.

    Christ gave himself an offering and sacrifice TO God¶

    And walk in love, as Christ also hath loved us, and hath given himself for us an offering and a sacrifice to God for a sweetsmelling savour.

    Context: Paul explicitly states the direction of the sacrifice: it is offered TO God (to Theo), not to Satan or any other recipient.

    Notes: prosphoran kai thysian to Theo — 'an offering and a sacrifice to God.' The ransom is directed TO God. eis osmen euodias — 'for a sweet-smelling savor' — sacrificial language from the Levitical system. The sacrifice satisfies God, not Satan.

    Jesus suffered without the gate to sanctify with his blood¶

    Wherefore Jesus also, that he might sanctify the people with his own blood, suffered without the gate.

    Context: Jesus suffered outside Jerusalem, paralleling the sin offering whose body was burned outside the camp (Lev 16:27).

    Notes: dia tou idiou haimatos — 'through his own blood.' The blood is Christ's own — personal, not borrowed. The suffering 'without the gate' connects to the sin offering typology.

    Blood of the everlasting covenant — eternal quality of the sacrifice¶

    Now the God of peace, that brought again from the dead our Lord Jesus, that great shepherd of the sheep, through the blood of the everlasting covenant, Make you perfect in every good work to do his will, working in you that which is wellpleasing in his sight, through Jesus Christ; to whom [be] glory for ever and ever. Amen.

    Context: The benediction identifies the blood as belonging to an 'everlasting covenant' (diatheke aioniou) — the ransom has eternal quality.

    Notes: en haimati diathekes aioniou — 'in/through the blood of the eternal covenant.' The 'eternal' (aionios) quality of the covenant matches the 'eternal Spirit' (Heb 9:14) and 'eternal redemption' (Heb 9:12). The blood price has everlasting efficacy.

    The blood of Jesus Christ cleanses from all sin¶

    But if we walk in the light, as he is in the light, we have fellowship one with another, and the blood of Jesus Christ his Son cleanseth us from all sin.

    Context: John affirms the ongoing cleansing power of Christ's blood — present tense, continuous action.

    Notes: katharizei (G2511, present active indicative) — 'cleanses' — continuous present. apo pases hamartias — 'from all sin.' The ransom's effect is comprehensive ('all sin') and ongoing ('cleanses').

    Overcame by the blood of the Lamb¶

    And they overcame him by the blood of the Lamb, and by the word of their testimony; and they loved not their lives unto the death.

    Context: The saints overcome Satan through the blood of the Lamb — the ransom defeats the accuser.

    Notes: enikesan (G3528, aorist active of nikao) — 'they conquered/overcame.' dia to haima tou arniou — 'through the blood of the Lamb.' The blood is the instrument of VICTORY over Satan, not PAYMENT to him.

    Against thee only have I sinned — sin is offense against God¶

    Against thee, thee only, have I sinned, and done [this] evil in thy sight: that thou mightest be justified when thou speakest, [and] be clear when thou judgest.

    Context: David's confession after the Bathsheba affair. Despite sinning against Uriah and Bathsheba, David declares his sin is ultimately 'against thee only' — against God.

    Notes: lekha lebaddekha chatati — 'against you, you alone, have I sinned.' If sin is fundamentally an offense against the infinite God (not merely loss of human perfection), then a finite ransom is insufficient. The infinite character of the offense requires an infinite ransom — which is what the divine nature of the sacrifice provides.

    God ransoms from death and destroys the grave¶

    I will ransom them from the power of the grave; I will redeem them from death: O death, I will be thy plagues; O grave, I will be thy destruction: repentance shall be hid from mine eyes.

    Context: God declares himself the ransomer from death and the destroyer of the grave. God is the active agent against death.

    Notes: ephdem (H6299, imperfect of padah) — 'I will ransom.' egoalem (H1350, imperfect of gaal) — 'I will redeem.' Both ransom verbs with GOD as subject. God is not receiving a ransom from outside — God IS the ransomer. Quoted by Paul in 1 Cor 15:55.

    He will swallow up death in victory¶

    He will swallow up death in victory; and the Lord GOD will wipe away tears from off all faces; and the rebuke of his people shall he take away from off all the earth: for the LORD hath spoken [it].

    Context: Isaiah's prophecy of God's ultimate victory over death — God destroys death itself.

    Notes: billa ha-mavet la-netsach — 'he will swallow up death forever.' God destroys death — the ransom's ultimate effect is the abolition of death itself (cf. 2 Tim 1:10; 1 Cor 15:26).

    Where sin abounded, grace superabounded¶

    Moreover the law entered, that the offence might abound. But where sin abounded, grace did much more abound: That as sin hath reigned unto death, even so might grace reign through righteousness unto eternal life by Jesus Christ our Lord.

    Context: Paul's climactic statement on the surplus of grace over sin. Grace does not merely match sin — it superabounds.

    Notes: huperperisseuen (G5248, aorist active of huperperisseuo) — 'superabounded.' This is the intensified form: hyper + perisseuo = grace exceeds sin BEYOND measure. If the ransom were exact equivalence, grace could only EQUAL sin. Paul says it SUPERABOUNDS.

    O death, where is thy sting — victory through Jesus Christ¶

    O death, where [is] thy sting? O grave, where [is] thy victory? The sting of death [is] sin; and the strength of sin [is] the law. But thanks [be] to God, which giveth us the victory through our Lord Jesus Christ.

    Context: Paul's triumphant conclusion to the resurrection chapter. Death's power (sting) is sin, and the law strengthens sin's hold. But God gives victory through Christ.

    Notes: nikos (G3529) — 'victory.' The ransom produces VICTORY over death, not merely equivalence with it. God gives (didonti, present active participle) the victory — ongoing divine action through Christ.

    Full suffering servant passage — vicarious bearing¶

    Surely he hath borne our griefs, and carried our sorrows: yet we did esteem him stricken, smitten of God, and afflicted. But he [was] wounded for our transgressions, [he was] bruised for our iniquities: the chastisement of our peace [was] upon him; and with his stripes we are healed. All we like sheep have gone astray; we have turned every one to his own way; and the LORD hath laid on him the iniquity of us all. He was oppressed, and he was afflicted, yet he opened not his mouth: he is brought as a lamb to the slaughter, and as a sheep before her shearers is dumb, so he openeth not his mouth. He was taken from prison and from judgment: and who shall declare his generation? for he was cut off out of the land of the living: for the transgression of my people was he stricken. And he made his grave with the wicked, and with the rich in his death; because he had done no violence, neither [was any] deceit in his mouth.

    Context: The core suffering servant passage describing vicarious sin-bearing. The servant bears 'our' griefs, 'our' sorrows, is wounded for 'our' transgressions — the substitutionary dimension is emphatic.

    Notes: The scope is universal — 'the iniquity of us ALL' (kullanu, v.6). The servant is sinless ('no violence... no deceit,' v.9) while bearing the sins of others. 'Cut off out of the land of the living' (v.8) — genuine death. This passage provides the theological foundation for the NT ransom statements.

    Ram substituted for Isaac — typological ransom¶

    And Abraham lifted up his eyes, and looked, and behold behind [him] a ram caught in a thicket by his horns: and Abraham went and took the ram, and offered him up for a burnt offering in the stead of his son.

    Context: The Aqedah — God provides a substitute for Isaac. The ram dies 'in the stead of' (tachat) Isaac.

    Notes: tachat beno — 'in the place of his son.' The substitutionary principle is established typologically: God provides a substitute to prevent death. The ram is NOT equivalent to Isaac — it is a different creature entirely. The substitute need not be identical to what it replaces.

    Wages of sin is death, gift of God is eternal life¶

    For the wages of sin [is] death; but the gift of God [is] eternal life through Jesus Christ our Lord.

    Context: Paul contrasts what sin earns (death) with what God gives freely (eternal life through Christ).

    Notes: opsonia (G3800) — 'wages/rations' — what is earned. charisma (G5486) — 'gift/grace-gift' — what is freely given. The ransom transforms wages into gift, death into life.

    Behold the Lamb of God that takes away the sin of the world¶

    The next day John seeth Jesus coming unto him, and saith, Behold the Lamb of God, which taketh away the sin of the world.

    Context: John the Baptist identifies Jesus as the Lamb of God. The scope is 'the sin of the world' — universal.

    Notes: ho amnos tou theou — 'the Lamb of God.' God provides the lamb (cf. Gen 22:8 'God will provide himself a lamb'). ho airon — 'the one taking away/bearing.' The scope is 'the sin of the world' (ten hamartian tou kosmou) — singular sin, universal scope.

    Scapegoat bearing iniquities — substitutionary type¶

    And Aaron shall lay both his hands upon the head of the live goat, and confess over him all the iniquities of the children of Israel, and all their transgressions in all their sins, putting them upon the head of the goat, and shall send [him] away by the hand of a fit man into the wilderness: And the goat shall bear upon him all their iniquities unto a land not inhabited: and he shall let go the goat in the wilderness.

    Context: The scapegoat ritual on the Day of Atonement. Sin is transferred to the goat, which bears it away into the wilderness.

    Notes: nasa (H5375) — 'to bear/carry.' The scapegoat bears ALL iniquities — comprehensive scope. The mechanism is transfer and removal, not payment to a recipient.

    Mediator of new testament, by means of death for redemption¶

    And for this cause he is the mediator of the new testament, that by means of death, for the redemption of the transgressions [that were] under the first testament, they which are called might receive the promise of eternal inheritance.

    Context: Christ's death serves as the ransom not only for future sins but retroactively for transgressions under the old covenant.

    Notes: mesites (G3316) — 'mediator.' thanatou genomenou — 'a death having occurred.' apolytrosis (G629) — 'redemption/ransoming.' The death-ransom secures an 'eternal inheritance' (aionion kleronomian) — the ransom's benefit is eternal, not temporal.

    Messiah cut off but not for himself — prophetic ransom¶

    Seventy weeks are determined upon thy people and upon thy holy city, to finish the transgression, and to make an end of sins, and to make reconciliation for iniquity, and to bring in everlasting righteousness, and to seal up the vision and prophecy, and to anoint the most Holy. Know therefore and understand, [that] from the going forth of the commandment to restore and to build Jerusalem unto the Messiah the Prince [shall be] seven weeks, and threescore and two weeks: the street shall be built again, and the wall, even in troublous times. And after threescore and two weeks shall Messiah be cut off, but not for himself: and the people of the prince that shall come shall destroy the city and the sanctuary; and the end thereof [shall be] with a flood, and unto the end of the war desolations are determined.

    Context: Daniel's 70 weeks prophecy. Messiah is 'cut off' (yikkaret) 'but not for himself' — his death benefits others, not himself.

    Notes: yikkaret — 'shall be cut off' (H3772, niphal imperfect). ve-ein lo — 'but not for himself' — the death is vicarious, for others. This prophetic text confirms the ransom is substitutionary.

    God so loved the world he gave his only Son¶

    For God so loved the world, that he gave his only begotten Son, that whosoever believeth in him should not perish, but have everlasting life.

    Context: The quintessential statement of divine initiative in redemption. God GIVES the Son — God provides the ransom.

    Notes: edoken (G1325, aorist active of didomi) — 'gave.' God is the giver of the ransom price (his Son). The scope is 'the world' (ton kosmon) and 'whosoever' (pas ho pisteuon) — universal. ton monogene — 'the only-begotten' — the uniqueness and preciousness of the gift underscores the ransom's infinite value.

    YHWH created alone — if Christ is Creator he is within YHWH¶

    Thus saith the LORD, thy redeemer, and he that formed thee from the womb, I [am] the LORD that maketh all [things]; that stretcheth forth the heavens alone; that spreadeth abroad the earth by myself;

    Context: YHWH identifies himself as both Creator and Redeemer (goel). He created alone (lebaddi) — no other being assisted.

    Notes: goelekha — 'your redeemer' (H1350, participle of gaal). YHWH is both Creator and Redeemer. If Christ is the agent of creation (John 1:3, Col 1:16), he must be within YHWH's identity. The redeemer IS the creator — the ransom is paid by the one who made everything.

    Whether we live or die we are the Lord's — ownership through ransom¶

    For none of us liveth to himself, and no man dieth to himself. For whether we live, we live unto the Lord; and whether we die, we die unto the Lord: whether we live therefore, or die, we are the Lord's.

    Context: Paul affirms that the ransom establishes Christ's ownership over believers in both life and death.

    Notes: tou kyriou esmen — 'we are the Lord's.' Ownership established through the ransom purchase (cf. 1 Cor 6:20, 7:23).

    Christ our passover sacrificed for us¶

    Purge out therefore the old leaven, that ye may be a new lump, as ye are unleavened. For even Christ our passover is sacrificed for us:

    Context: Paul applies the Passover typology to Christ. The Passover lamb's blood protected from the destroyer — Christ's blood ransoms from death.

    Notes: to pascha hemon etuthe Christos — 'Christ our passover was sacrificed.' The Passover lamb was not an exact equivalent of the firstborn — it was a substitute provided by God. The type reinforces that the ransom need not be identical to what it replaces.

    Taste death for every man¶

    But we see Jesus, who was made a little lower than the angels for the suffering of death, crowned with glory and honour; that he by the grace of God should taste death for every man.

    Context: Jesus was made lower than the angels specifically FOR the purpose of dying. The scope is 'every man' (pantos) — universal.

    Notes: hyper pantos geusetai thanatou — 'he might taste death on behalf of everyone.' Universal scope again. 'Taste death' — genuine experience of death, not merely apparent.

    Asham surplus — restore in full plus one-fifth¶

    Or all that about which he hath sworn falsely; he shall even restore it in the principal, and shall add the fifth part more thereto, [and] give it unto him to whom it appertaineth, in the day of his trespass offering.

    Context: The restitution requirement for the asham: full restoration PLUS one-fifth. This is the surplus principle that Isa 53:10 invokes.

    Notes: chamishitav yoseph alav — 'its fifth part he shall add to it.' The asham is NOT exact equivalence — it requires MORE than what was lost. Christ as asham (Isa 53:10) pays more than Adam lost.
