    Does the New Testament Break Old Testament Monotheism by Making Jesus God?¶

    A Plain-English Summary¶

    No. The New Testament writers did not abandon the Old Testament's insistence that there is only one God. Instead, they showed that Jesus belongs inside that one God's identity -- not as a second deity added on, but as someone who shares the same divine identity as the Father.

    At least four independent NT authors reach this same conclusion through different evidence, making it one of the most widely attested claims in the entire New Testament.

    The Core Problem¶

    The Old Testament is unmistakably clear: there is one God, and his name is YHWH. "Hear, O Israel: the LORD our God is one LORD" (Deuteronomy 6:4). "I am the LORD, and there is none else, there is no God beside me" (Isaiah 45:5). This is not ambiguous. Israel's God tolerates no rivals.

    Then the New Testament writers do something extraordinary. They give Jesus the titles that belong exclusively to YHWH. They record people worshiping Jesus -- and Jesus accepting it. They say Jesus created everything. They say he will judge the world. These are things only God does.

    So did they contradict the Old Testament? Or did they see something in the Old Testament that pointed to this all along?

    The Shema Was Expanded, Not Discarded¶

    Paul takes the Shema -- Israel's most foundational monotheistic prayer -- and redistributes it. In 1 Corinthians 8:6 he writes: "But to us there is but one God, the Father, of whom are all things, and we in him; and one Lord Jesus Christ, by whom are all things, and we by him."

    Notice what Paul did. He took "one God" and "one Lord" from the Shema and assigned "one God" to the Father and "one Lord" to Christ. He did not add a second god. He placed Christ inside the confession itself. The Shema now has two persons named in it, but it is still declaring one God.

    YHWH's Own Titles Are Given to Jesus¶

    This is not a case of one writer making a bold claim. At least four independent authors transfer titles and texts that belong exclusively to YHWH directly to Jesus:

    • Paul takes Isaiah 45:23 ("every knee shall bow to me") -- a passage where YHWH swears by himself -- and applies it to Jesus in Philippians 2:10-11.
    • Paul takes Joel 2:32 ("whoever calls on the name of the LORD shall be saved") and applies it to calling on Jesus in Romans 10:13.
    • The author of Hebrews takes Psalm 102:25-27, a prayer addressed to YHWH as Creator, and applies it directly to the Son in Hebrews 1:10-12.
    • John records Christ claiming "I am the First and the Last" in Revelation 1:17-18 -- a title that in Isaiah 44:6 is paired with "beside me there is no God."

    These are not vague similarities. These are direct quotations of YHWH-exclusive texts, transferred to Jesus on purpose.

    Jesus Created Everything -- But YHWH Created Alone¶

    Isaiah 44:24 has YHWH declaring: "I am the LORD that maketh all things; that stretcheth forth the heavens alone; that spreadeth abroad the earth by myself." No help. No assistant.

    Then four independent NT authors say Jesus made everything: "All things were made by him" (John 1:3), "by him were all things created" (Colossians 1:16), and the Father made the worlds "by" the Son (Hebrews 1:2, 1 Corinthians 8:6).

    If YHWH created alone and Jesus created everything, then Jesus must be inside YHWH's identity. There is no other way to hold both statements together.

    The Worship Test¶

    The New Testament draws a hard line between Creator and creature, and worship is where that line shows up most clearly. The pattern is absolute: every righteous creature who is offered worship refuses it. Peter says "Stand up; I myself also am a man" (Acts 10:26). An angel says "See thou do it not: I am thy fellowservant" (Revelation 22:9).

    Jesus never refuses worship. Not once. Across seven recorded scenes, he accepts it every time. And in Hebrews 1:6, God himself commands the angels to worship the Son.

    If Jesus is a creature -- even the highest creature -- this pattern makes no sense. It only works if Jesus belongs on the Creator side of the line.

    "The Only True God" Does Not Exclude Jesus¶

    The strongest-sounding argument against all of this is John 17:3, where Jesus calls the Father "the only true God." If the Father is the only true God, how can Jesus also be God?

    The answer becomes clear when you apply the same logic consistently. First Timothy 6:15-16 says God "alone has immortality" -- but Revelation 1:18 says Jesus is "alive forevermore." First Timothy 1:17 calls God "the only wise God" -- but Colossians 2:3 says all the treasures of wisdom are in Christ. If "only" absolutely excludes Jesus, he has no immortality and no wisdom. The word "only" distinguishes the true God from false gods, not the Father from the Son.

    The Old Testament Already Contained the Seeds¶

    The study found that the Old Testament itself leaves room for this complexity. The Hebrew word used in the Shema for "one" is echad (meaning a composite unity -- like "one flesh" in Genesis 2:24), not yachid (meaning solitary, alone). Yachid is never used of God anywhere in Scripture.

    Genesis 19:24 says "the LORD rained upon Sodom brimstone and fire from the LORD out of heaven" -- two persons called YHWH in the same sentence. Psalm 45:6-7 addresses someone as "O God" while also saying "God, your God, has anointed you" -- one God speaking to another God. The author of Hebrews applies this directly to the Father addressing the Son.

    These are not proof-texts invented by later theology. They are patterns already present in the Hebrew Bible.

    What About the Passages Where Jesus Seems Less Than God?¶

    The study examined the strongest counter-evidence honestly.

    In 1 Corinthians 15:28, Paul says the Son will one day "be subject" to the Father. But the future tense ("will be subject") means this is a change of state -- the Son is not currently in permanent subordination. This describes the completion of a rescue mission, not a permanent demotion. Before the incarnation, every text describes equality: "the Word was God" (John 1:1), glory shared "before the world was" (John 17:5).

    In Revelation 3:12, the glorified Jesus calls the Father "my God" four times. But Psalm 45:6-7 already established this pattern: someone addressed as God can simultaneously have God as his God. The Son relates to the Father in a way that includes "my God" language without canceling his own deity -- just as Thomas, eleven verses after Jesus says "my God," calls Jesus "my Lord and my God" (John 20:28).

    The Holy Spirit Extends the Pattern¶

    The study also found that the same logic applies to the Holy Spirit. Three independent authors identify the Spirit with YHWH: Paul writes "the Lord is that Spirit" (2 Corinthians 3:17); Luke records the Spirit saying what YHWH said in Isaiah 6:9-10 (Acts 28:25); the author of Hebrews attributes YHWH's words from Psalm 95 to the Holy Spirit (Hebrews 3:7-11). The baptismal formula in Matthew 28:19 places all three -- Father, Son, and Holy Spirit -- under a single shared name, not three separate names. This extends the conclusion from a two-person pattern to a three-person one.

    What This Study Does and Does Not Claim¶

    This study does not claim to explain the full mechanism of how one God can be three persons. The New Testament provides the evidence but does not provide a complete philosophical explanation. The "my God" language, the future subjection, the relational dynamics between Father, Son, and Spirit -- these contain genuine mystery.

    What the evidence does establish, across multiple independent authors, is that "one God" in the New Testament means one divine identity -- YHWH -- shared by the Father, Son, and Holy Spirit through title-transfer, worship-reception, creation-agency, and direct identification as God. The monotheism is not broken. It is revealed to be more internally complex than anyone expected.
