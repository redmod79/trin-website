    Verses¶

    Question¶

    When Jesus says 'the only true God' (John 17:3) and Paul says 'one God, the Father' (1 Corinthians 8:6), do these passages exclude Jesus from deity? Examine the full chapter context of John 17 and the Shema background of 1 Corinthians 8:6.

    The High Priestly Prayer — 'the only true God' and pre-existent glory¶

    John 17:1 These words spake Jesus, and lifted up his eyes to heaven, and said, Father, the hour is come; glorify thy Son, that thy Son also may glorify thee: John 17:2 As thou hast given him power over all flesh, that he should give eternal life to as many as thou hast given him. John 17:3 And this is life eternal, that they might know thee the only true God, and Jesus Christ, whom thou hast sent. John 17:4 I have glorified thee on the earth: I have finished the work which thou gavest me to do. John 17:5 And now, O Father, glorify thou me with thine own self with the glory which I had with thee before the world was.

    Context: Jesus's prayer to the Father on the night before his crucifixion (the High Priestly Prayer). John 17 is the most sustained prayer of Jesus in the Gospels. The chapter context includes deity claims (v.5 pre-existent glory, v.10 mutual ownership, v.24 pre-creation love).

    Notes: V.3: monos alethinos theos — the crux text. The kai conjunction connects 'the only true God' with 'Jesus Christ whom thou hast sent' as joint objects of life-giving knowledge (both in accusative). V.5: eichon (imperfect active indicative 1st person — continuous past possession) + para soi (with you, dative of personal proximity) — Jesus claims he personally and continuously possessed glory alongside the Father before the world existed. The pre-verbal para seatō (with yourself) is an aorist imperative request for restoration of shared divine presence.

    Isolated crux text — monos alethinos theos + kai construction¶

    John 17:3 And this is life eternal, that they might know thee the only true God, and Jesus Christ, whom thou hast sent.

    Context: Within the High Priestly Prayer. Jesus defines eternal life as knowing the Father AND Jesus Christ — a dual object of saving knowledge. The context of the same prayer includes claims no mere creature could make (v.5, v.10, v.24).

    Paul's Christological reformulation of the Shema — 'one God, the Father... one Lord, Jesus Christ'¶

    1 Corinthians 8:4 As concerning therefore the eating of those things that are offered in sacrifice unto idols, we know that an idol [is] nothing in the world, and that [there is] none other God but one. 1 Corinthians 8:5 For though there be that are called gods, whether in heaven or in earth, (as there be gods many, and lords many,) 1 Corinthians 8:6 But to us [there is but] one God, the Father, of whom [are] all things, and we in him; and one Lord Jesus Christ, by whom [are] all things, and we by him.

    Context: Paul addresses the eating of idol-offered meat. The context is explicitly anti-polytheistic — 'an idol is nothing' (v.4), 'gods many and lords many' (v.5) are the foil. V.6 is Paul's positive confession: one God (theos, the Father) and one Lord (kyrios, Jesus Christ), with creation language distributed between them.

    Mutual ownership — 'All mine are thine, and thine are mine'¶

    John 17:10 And all mine are thine, and thine are mine; and I am glorified in them.

    Context: Within the High Priestly Prayer. Jesus claims mutual possession with the Father — everything the Father has belongs to Jesus and vice versa.

    Notes: Greek: τὰ ἐμὰ πάντα σά ἐστιν καὶ τὰ σὰ ἐμά — 'the things of me all yours are, and the things of yours mine.' Possessive pronouns emos (G1699, mine) and sos (G4674, yours) in neuter plural nominative. This is a reciprocal possession claim — no creature could claim everything belonging to God is also his own. Only a being who shares the divine nature could make this claim.

    Divine unity as the model for believer unity — 'that they may be one, as we are one'¶

    John 17:21 That they all may be one; as thou, Father, [art] in me, and I in thee, that they also may be one in us: that the world may believe that thou hast sent me. John 17:22 And the glory which thou gavest me I have given them; that they may be one, even as we are one: John 17:23 I in them, and thou in me, that they may be made perfect in one; and that the world may know that thou hast sent me, and hast loved them, as thou hast loved me.

    Context: Jesus prays for the unity of all believers, using his own unity with the Father as the standard. The Father-Son unity is the archetype; believer unity is the derivative copy.

    Notes: The unity formula: kathos su pater en emoi kago en soi (as you Father in me and I in you). The mutual indwelling (perichoresis) language parallels John 10:30 (hen esmen, we are one — neuter hen indicating unity of essence/purpose, not numerical identity). The fact that Father-Son unity is the MODEL for believer unity means believers approximate but do not achieve the ontological depth of divine unity.

    Pre-creation love and glory — 'thou lovedst me before the foundation of the world'¶

    John 17:24 Father, I will that they also, whom thou hast given me, be with me where I am; that they may behold my glory, which thou hast given me: for thou lovedst me before the foundation of the world.

    Context: Jesus asserts a personal love-relationship with the Father that existed before creation (pro kataboles kosmou). This is not merely a divine plan — it is a personal, relational claim.

    Notes: pro kataboles kosmou (before the foundation of the world) — this phrase appears also in Eph 1:4 and 1 Pet 1:20. Combined with John 17:5's pre-existent glory, this establishes that the 'only true God' of v.3 had a Son with whom he shared glory and love before any created thing existed.

    The Shema — OT background for 1 Corinthians 8:6¶

    Deuteronomy 6:4 Hear, O Israel: The LORD our God [is] one LORD:

    Context: The foundational monotheistic confession of Israel. The Hebrew reads: shema yisrael YHWH elohenu YHWH echad. The LXX renders: kyrios ho theos hemon kyrios heis estin. Paul's 1 Cor 8:6 distributes heis between theos and kyrios.

Paul's reformulation in 1 Cor 8:6 takes heis theos and heis kyrios from the Shema and distributes them: heis theos = the Father; heis kyrios = Jesus Christ. Every key word of the Shema appears in Paul's confession.

    The Johannine prologue — the Word was God, all things made through him¶

    John 1:1 In the beginning was the Word, and the Word was with God, and the Word was God. John 1:2 The same was in the beginning with God. John 1:3 All things were made by him; and without him was not any thing made that was made.

    Context: The theological prologue of John's Gospel, establishing the pre-existence, deity, and creative agency of the Logos before the narrative begins. This is the same author who records 'the only true God' in 17:3 and 'my Lord and my God' in 20:28.

The Logos 'was' (uncreated existence) while everything else 'became' (created becoming).

    Equal honor — the Son does what the Father does, all must honor the Son as the Father¶

    John 5:17 But Jesus answered them, My Father worketh hitherto, and I work. John 5:18 Therefore the Jews sought the more to kill him, because he not only had broken the sabbath, but said also that God was his Father, making himself equal with God. John 5:19 Then answered Jesus and said unto them, Verily, verily, I say unto you, The Son can do nothing of himself, but what he seeth the Father do: for what things soever he doeth, these also doeth the Son likewise. John 5:20 For the Father loveth the Son, and sheweth him all things that himself doeth: and he will shew him greater works than these, that ye may marvel. John 5:21 For as the Father raiseth up the dead, and quickeneth [them]; even so the Son quickeneth whom he will. John 5:22 For the Father judgeth no man, but hath committed all judgment unto the Son: John 5:23 That all [men] should honour the Son, even as they honour the Father. He that honoureth not the Son honoureth not the Father which hath sent him.

    Context: Jesus's response to persecution for healing on the Sabbath. The Jews understand his 'my Father' language as making himself equal with God (v.18). Jesus then claims divine prerogatives: life-giving (v.21), all judgment (v.22), and equal honor (v.23).

    Notes: V.23 Greek: ἵνα πάντες τιμῶσι τὸν Υἱὸν καθὼς τιμῶσι τὸν Πατέρα — 'that all should honor the Son just as (kathos) they honor the Father.' The kathos (G2531) makes the honor quantitatively and qualitatively identical. This is incompatible with reading 17:3 as excluding the Son from deity — the same Gospel demands equal honor for the Son.

    'I and my Father are one' — the Jews understand this as a deity claim¶

    Perriman's alternative background — 'Have we not all one father? one God created us?'¶

    Malachi 2:10 Have we not all one father? hath not one God created us? why do we deal treacherously every man against his brother, by profaning the covenant of our fathers?

    Context: Malachi's rhetorical question about Israel's common Father and Creator. Perriman argues this is a better OT background for 1 Cor 8:6 than Deut 6:4.

    Notes: Perriman claims Mal 2:10 ('one father... one God created us') is closer to 1 Cor 8:6 ('one God the Father, of whom all things') than Deut 6:4. However: (1) Mal 2:10 lacks the kyrios element — it has 'father' and 'God' but not 'Lord'; (2) 1 Cor 8:6 has BOTH heis theos AND heis kyrios — matching the two terms of the Shema (kyrios ho theos hemon kyrios heis); (3) Mal 2:10 is about Israel's covenant family, not about cosmic monotheism; (4) Paul's context in 1 Cor 8:4-5 is anti-polytheistic ('no God but one,' 'gods many and lords many'), which maps to the Shema's anti-polytheistic function, not to Malachi's covenant faithfulness argument.

    'I am Alpha and Omega... the Almighty' — divine titles applied to Christ¶

    Revelation 1:8 I am Alpha and Omega, the beginning and the ending, saith the Lord, which is, and which was, and which is to come, the Almighty.

    Context: The speaker identifies himself with divine titles: Alpha and Omega, the Almighty (pantokrator). The context (v.7 about Christ's coming, v.9ff about the risen Christ) indicates this is Christ speaking.

    Notes: The title 'the Almighty' (ho pantokrator) is used of God in Rev 4:8, 11:17, 15:3, 16:7, 19:6, 21:22. If Christ is the speaker in 1:8 (as the context suggests), he claims the divine title Pantokrator. Rev 22:13 confirms Jesus uses the Alpha-Omega title.

    'I am Alpha and Omega, the first and the last' — Isaiah 44:6 titles for Jesus¶

    Revelation 22:13 I am Alpha and Omega, the beginning and the end, the first and the last.

    Context: The speaker is identified as Jesus in v.16 ('I Jesus have sent mine angel'). The titles 'first and last' echo Isaiah 44:6 and 48:12 — exclusive YHWH titles.

    Notes: Jesus explicitly identifies himself as 'the first and the last' — the title YHWH claims in Isaiah 44:6 ('beside me there is no God'). This is the strongest evidence that the NT writers included Jesus within YHWH's identity.

    'One flesh' — echad for composite unity¶

    Genesis 2:24 Therefore shall a man leave his father and his mother, and shall cleave unto his wife: and they shall be one flesh.

    Context: The creation narrative. Two persons become 'one' (echad) flesh — the paradigmatic example of composite unity.

    Notes: echad (H259) here describes two persons who become one entity. This demonstrates that echad can express composite unity — multiple persons within a single 'one.' The Shema's YHWH echad (Deut 6:4) uses the same word, permitting (though not requiring) composite unity within the Godhead.

    'One LORD, and his name one' — eschatological echad¶

    Zechariah 14:9 And the LORD shall be king over all the earth: in that day shall there be one LORD, and his name one.

    Context: Eschatological vision. YHWH will be universally acknowledged as the one LORD. Uses echad twice.

    Notes: YHWH echad u-shemo echad — 'YHWH one and his name one.' This eschatological text uses the same echad as the Shema. It envisions the universal recognition of YHWH's oneness — the same outcome Paul envisions in Phil 2:10-11 when every tongue confesses Jesus as kyrios.

    'He that hath seen me hath seen the Father' — mutual indwelling¶

    John 14:9 Jesus saith unto him, Have I been so long time with you, and yet hast thou not known me, Philip? he that hath seen me hath seen the Father; and how sayest thou [then], Shew us the Father? John 14:10 Believest thou not that I am in the Father, and the Father in me? the words that I speak unto you I speak not of myself: but the Father that dwelleth in me, he doeth the works. John 14:11 Believe me that I [am] in the Father, and the Father in me: or else believe me for the very works' sake.

    Context: Jesus's upper room discourse. 'I am in the Father, and the Father in me' — mutual indwelling language.

    Notes: ego en to patri kai ho pater en emoi — 'I in the Father and the Father in me.' This mutual indwelling (perichoresis) language supports the reading that 'the only true God' in 17:3 does not exclude the Son, because the Son is 'in' the Father. EGW citation: 'He declared that He had no existence separate from the Father.'

    'In him dwelleth all the fulness of the Godhead bodily'¶

    Colossians 2:9 For in him dwelleth all the fulness of the Godhead bodily.

    Context: Paul's assertion that the full divine nature (theotetos, G2320) dwells in Christ in bodily form.

    Notes: pan to pleroma tes theotetos somatikos — 'all the fullness of the deity bodily.' theotes (G2320) = 'deity, divine nature' — stronger than theiotēs (G2305, 'divinity' in Rom 1:20). The full divine essence, not merely divine qualities, resides in Christ.

    Context of the prayer — the Father's words given through the Son¶

    John 17:6 I have manifested thy name unto the men which thou gavest me out of the world: thine they were, and thou gavest them me; and they have kept thy word. John 17:7 Now they have known that all things whatsoever thou hast given me are of thee. John 17:8 For I have given unto them the words which thou gavest me; and they have received [them], and have known surely that I came out from thee, and they have believed that thou didst send me.

    Context: Jesus describes his earthly mission — manifesting the Father's name, giving the Father's words. The language of being 'sent' and 'coming out from' the Father.

    Notes: V.8: 'I came out from thee' (exelthon para sou) — Jesus describes his origin as from the Father's personal presence (para + genitive = from beside). This supports the pre-existence claims of v.5 and v.24.

    'That they may be one, as we are' — unity language¶

    John 17:11 And now I am no more in the world, but these are in the world, and I come to thee. Holy Father, keep through thine own name those whom thou hast given me, that they may be one, as we [are].

    Context: Jesus prays for the disciples' unity, modeled on his own unity with the Father.

    Notes: hina osin hen kathos hemeis — 'that they may be one just as we.' The Father-Son unity is again the archetype for derivative believer unity.

    Isaiah 45:23 applied to Jesus — every knee bows to Christ as LORD¶

    Philippians 2:10 That at the name of Jesus every knee should bow, of [things] in heaven, and [things] in earth, and [things] under the earth; Philippians 2:11 And [that] every tongue should confess that Jesus Christ [is] Lord, to the glory of God the Father.

    Context: Application of Isaiah 45:23 (YHWH's exclusive oath) to Jesus.

    Notes: The YHWH-exclusive 'every knee shall bow' oath of Isa 45:23 is applied to Jesus. If kyrios for Jesus derived only from Ps 110:1's adon (Perriman's argument), Paul would not apply a YHWH-oath text to him. This is the decisive counter to Perriman.

    'Whosoever shall call upon the name of the Lord shall be saved' — Joel 2:32 applied to Jesus¶

    Romans 10:13 For whosoever shall call upon the name of the Lord shall be saved.

    Context: Paul quotes Joel 2:32, which reads 'whoever calls on the name of YHWH.' Paul applies it to Jesus (see v.9-12 context: 'if thou shalt confess with thy mouth the Lord Jesus').

    Notes: Joel 2:32: 'whoever calls on the name of YHWH shall be delivered.' Paul applies this to Jesus in the context of confessing 'Jesus is Lord' (v.9). This means Paul identifies Jesus with YHWH — the kyrios who saves is Jesus.

    'Whosoever shall call on the name of the LORD shall be delivered'¶

    Joel 2:32 And it shall come to pass, [that] whosoever shall call on the name of the LORD shall be delivered: for in mount Zion and in Jerusalem shall be deliverance, as the LORD hath said, and in the remnant whom the LORD shall call.

    Context: Joel's prophecy about calling on YHWH's name for salvation. Paul applies this to Jesus in Romans 10:13.

    Notes: The Hebrew has YHWH (the divine name). The LXX renders kyrios. Paul applies this kyrios text to Jesus, confirming that the NT's kyrios for Jesus derives from YHWH, not merely from Psalm 110:1's adon.

    'The Lord GOD, and his Spirit, hath sent me' — three agents in one clause¶

    Isaiah 48:16 Come ye near unto me, hear ye this; I have not spoken in secret from the beginning; from the time that it was, there [am] I: and now the Lord GOD, and his Spirit, hath sent me.

    Context: A mysterious OT passage with three distinct agents: the speaker ('me'), 'the Lord GOD,' and 'his Spirit.'

    Notes: Three distinct agents appear: (1) the speaker who has been present 'from the beginning,' (2) Adonai YHWH, and (3) his Spirit — all in one clause. This is OT evidence for plurality within YHWH, relevant to understanding how 'the only true God' can include the Son.

    'Thy throne, O God, is for ever and ever' — elohim addressed to the king¶

    Psalm 45:6 Thy throne, O God, [is] for ever and ever: the sceptre of thy kingdom [is] a right sceptre. Psalm 45:7 Thou lovest righteousness, and hatest wickedness: therefore God, thy God, hath anointed thee with the oil of gladness above thy fellows.

    Context: A royal psalm. The king is addressed as elohim. Cited in Hebrews 1:8-9 as addressed to the Son.

    Notes: The structure is remarkable: 'God, YOUR God, has anointed you' — the king is called God, yet has a God. Hebrews 1:8 applies this to the Son without qualification, confirming the Son is both called God and has God (the Father).

    'The LORD rained from the LORD' — two YHWH references¶

    Genesis 19:24 Then the LORD rained upon Sodom and upon Gomorrah brimstone and fire from the LORD out of heaven;

    Context: The destruction of Sodom. 'YHWH rained... from YHWH out of heaven' — two distinct YHWH references.

    Notes: YHWH on earth rains fire from YHWH in heaven — suggesting two persons both bearing the name YHWH. This is OT evidence for plurality within YHWH.

    'Isaiah saw his glory and spoke of him' — YHWH's glory attributed to Christ¶

    John 12:41 These things said Esaias, when he saw his glory, and spake of him.

    Context: John attributes Isaiah's vision of YHWH's glory (Isaiah 6) to Christ. 'He saw HIS (Jesus's) glory.'

    Notes: Isaiah 6:1-5 describes Isaiah seeing 'the Lord (Adonai) sitting upon a throne, high and lifted up' — a YHWH vision. John says Isaiah 'saw HIS (autou — referring to Jesus from the preceding context) glory.' John identifies the YHWH of Isaiah 6 with Jesus.

    'The church of God, which he hath purchased with his own blood'¶

    Context: Paul's farewell to the Ephesian elders. 'The church of GOD, which HE purchased with HIS OWN blood' — God's blood, which can only be Christ's blood.

    Notes: The phrase 'the church of God' (tou theou) with 'his own blood' (tou haimatos tou idiou) implies that God purchased the church with his own blood — which is Christ's act on the cross. This implicitly identifies Christ with God.
