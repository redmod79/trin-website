    Web Research Findings¶

    Study Question¶

    Is the Holy Spirit a personal agent who thinks, wills, speaks, and can be sinned against — or an impersonal divine power/force? Examine the Greek grammatical evidence, personal-action verbs attributed to the Spirit, sin-against-the-Spirit passages, and triadic formulas.

    Sources Found¶

    Robert M. Bowman Jr. — "Neuter Pneuma and Neuter Pronouns Mean Not a Person: Bad Arguments against the Personhood of the Holy Spirit #1" (Institute for Religious Research, 2014)¶

    URL: bib.irr.org Key claims: - Grammatical gender is arbitrary relative to personhood: "grammatical gender does not indicate personhood or non-personhood." Greek nouns in all three genders refer to both personal and impersonal referents. - The Greek noun paidion ("child") is neuter and refers to Jesus 11 times in the NT (Matt 2:8, 9, 11, 13 [2x], 14, 20 [2x]; Luke 2:17, 27, 40). "The fact that it is neuter has absolutely nothing to do with whether it refers to a person." - In Matthew 2:13 the masculine pronoun auto is used to refer to the neuter paidion when speaking of Jesus — showing that writers could override grammatical gender for personal referents. - Anti-Trinitarians selectively dismiss gender evidence: the book One God, One Lord appeals to pneuma's neuter gender while dismissing masculine parakletos by claiming gender assignment is "rather arbitrary." Bowman calls this fatally inconsistent. - The neuter pronoun auto in Romans 8:16 simply indicates grammatical agreement with pneuma — "the use of the neuter pronoun auto to refer to the Spirit tells the reader absolutely nothing about whether the Spirit is a person, a force, or an abstraction." - Bowman characterizes the neuter-pronoun objection as "a bad argument because it misunderstands something very basic about the Greek language — something any first-semester student of Greek should know." Greek analysis: Masculine nouns end in -os (e.g., adelphos), feminine in -e (e.g., adelphe), neuter in -ma and -on (e.g., pneuma, paidion). Pronoun gender follows antecedent gender automatically, not metaphysical status. Relevance: Directly addresses the central anti-personhood grammatical argument. The paidion parallel is a strong test case. Status: Lead to verify against Scripture

    Robert M. Bowman Jr. — "The Holy Spirit as the Paraclete in John" (Institute for Religious Research, Part Three)¶

    URL: bib.irr.org Key claims: - John maintains standard grammatical agreement: masculine pronouns (ekeinos, auton) appear when parakletos (masculine) is the antecedent (14:26; 15:26; 16:7, 8, 14); neuter pronouns appear when pneuma (neuter) is the antecedent (14:17a, 26; 15:26). - Citing Daniel Wallace: "the masculine pronouns are used precisely because their antecedent is a personal noun used in a context in which that noun designates someone taking Jesus' place." - The term parakletos appears only in John 14-16 and 1 John 2:1. Jesus as the first Paraclete sends "another Paraclete" (allon parakleton) — the succession motif frames the Spirit as personal. - The Spirit's Paraclete role involves: agency (sent by Father and Son, 14:16; 16:7), presence ("with you forever," 14:16-17), teaching (14:26), testimony (15:26), judgment/conviction (16:8-11), and guidance into truth (16:13). - Lochlan Shelfer argues parakletos derives from Latin advocatus, with courtroom-advocacy usage found in Diogenes Laertius, Dionysius of Halicarnassus, Cassius Dio, and Philo. - Craig Keener and Tricia Gates Brown propose the Spirit functions as broker/patron mediator, paralleling OT intermediary figures like Moses. - Gary Burge: "The Paraclete as an advocate implored and persuaded the opposition concerning the truth; and as a witness the Paraclete brought forward evidence establishing the cases for Christ (and his church)." - "One need not and should not attempt to defend the personhood of the Spirit from grammar alone," but grammar functions within a "complex communication act" involving historical context, narrative structure, semantics, and syntax. Greek analysis: ekeinos (masculine demonstrative) tracks parakletos (masculine) as antecedent, not pneuma (neuter). to pneuma tes aletheias serves as appositional phrase defining ekeinos. Relevance: Critical for the Paraclete passages in John 14-16. The Wallace citation is key for the grammatical debate. Status: Lead to verify against Scripture

    Daniel B. Wallace — Greek Grammar Beyond the Basics (Zondervan, 1996), pp. 331-332¶

    URL: (referenced in multiple sources; book not directly fetchable) Key claims: - In John 16:13, ekeinos (masculine demonstrative) has ho parakletos as its antecedent, not pneuma. The phrase to pneuma tes aletheias is appositional, expanding and defining ekeinos. - "The antecedent of ekeinos, in each case, is parakletos, not pneuma." Therefore the masculine gender of the pronoun follows standard grammatical agreement with the masculine antecedent. - Wallace cautions against building personhood arguments on grammatical gender alone: pneuma being neuter does not disprove personhood, but ekeinos being masculine does not by itself prove it either. Greek analysis: Standard pronoun-antecedent agreement explains the masculine ekeinos without requiring a theological gender override. The grammatical case neither proves nor disproves personhood. Relevance: Wallace is the most-cited NT Greek grammarian on this passage. His position that grammar is neutral (neither proving nor disproving personhood) must be engaged by the analysis. Status: Lead to verify against Scripture

    Ministry Magazine — "'Another Paraclete': The Holy Spirit in John 14-17" (April 2012)¶

    URL: ministrymagazine.org Key claims: - The syntactical antecedent of masculine pronouns like ekeinos is parakletos, not pneuma. Citing Daniel Wallace to this effect. - "The gender of parakletos, as well as that of pneuma, is nothing more than a linguistic accident" — warns against deriving theological conclusions from grammatical gender. - Genuine evidence for personhood lies in functional descriptions: teaching (14:26), testifying and convicting (15:26; 16:8), speaking and hearing (16:13), glorifying and guiding (16:13-14). - Critiques the allos argument: conflating allos ("another of the same kind") with heteros ("another of a different kind") is an error. Cites Joseph Thayer: "allos generally denotes simply distinction of individuals, heteros involves the secondary idea of difference of kind." - The Spirit functions as "the presence of Jesus when Jesus is absent." - In the Farewell Discourses the Spirit is personal; in earlier Gospel narratives the Spirit functions as "mostly a life-giving power." Greek analysis: allos vs heteros distinction analyzed; ekeinos/parakletos antecedent relationship established. Relevance: Important caution against overreliance on grammatical-gender arguments. The allos/heteros discussion is relevant to how "another Paraclete" should be interpreted. Status: Lead to verify against Scripture

    Jiří Moskala — "The Holy Spirit in the Hebrew Scriptures" (JATS 24/2, 2013, pp. 18-58)¶

    URL: atsjats.org Key claims: - The Holy Spirit functioned actively throughout the OT, contrary to assumptions that pneumatic activity was limited before Pentecost. - Genesis 1:2 demonstrates the Spirit's active engagement in creation alongside other divine persons. - Isaiah 63:10 ("they grieved His Holy Spirit") shows the OT portrays the Spirit as possessing personal qualities — specifically the capacity to be grieved by human rebellion. - Challenges the view that OT believers lacked understanding of the Spirit's personal dimensions. - Argues the Spirit's work was "genuine, full in measure" during the OT period. - Addresses the common misunderstanding of "up to that time the Spirit had not been given" (John 7:39). Hebrew analysis: Examines ruach across OT texts, arguing it carries genuine personal attributes rather than functioning merely as impersonal force or divine energy. Relevance: Critical for the OT dimension of this study, especially Genesis 1:2 and Isaiah 63:10. Status: Lead to verify against Scripture

    Ron E. M. Clouzet — "The Personhood of the Holy Spirit and Why It Matters" (JATS 17/1, Spring 2006, pp. 11-32)¶

    URL: digitalcommons.andrews.edu Key claims: - John intentionally uses Greek language freely to present the Holy Spirit as a person. - In John 16:13-14, John uses pneuma (neuter) but employs the masculine demonstrative pronoun ekeinos instead of the expected neuter ekeino. - Standard grammar requires pronoun-antecedent gender agreement; the masculine override is linguistically noteworthy. - The article examines biblical, historical, and theological dimensions of the Spirit's personhood. Greek analysis: Argues John deliberately breaks gender agreement (masculine ekeinos for neuter pneuma) as an indication of personhood. Relevance: Represents the traditional Adventist scholarly position. Directly opposes Wallace's view that ekeinos merely tracks parakletos. Status: Lead to verify against Scripture

    Precept Austin — Commentary on Ephesians 4:30 (Grieving the Holy Spirit)¶

    URL: preceptaustin.org Key claims: - Greek lypeite (λυπέω) from lupe (λύπη) denotes "sadness, grief" — "a pain or grief that can only be experienced between two people who deeply love each other" (Rick Renner). - "Grieve is a love word... He is grieved, because we are the objects of the love of the triune God" (S. Lewis Johnson). - John MacArthur: grieving "the Holy Spirit indicates His personhood, which is seen in the use of personal pronouns" and notes the Spirit possesses "intellect (1 Cor 2:11), feelings (Rom 8:27), and will." - Steven Cole: "You can't grieve a force or a power. You can only grieve a person." - The Greek construction (present imperative + negative particle me) conveys "Stop grieving" or "Do not have the habit of grieving" — ongoing struggle, not isolated incident. - Charles Hodge: "Though He will not finally withdraw from those in whom He dwells, yet when grieved, He withholds the manifestations of His presence." - S.F.D. Salmond acknowledges these are "anthropopathic" (assigning human feelings to deity) but maintains they "have reality behind them." - Cross-references: Isaiah 63:10 ("they rebelled and grieved His Holy Spirit"), Psalm 78:40, 1 Thess 5:19, Acts 7:51, Hebrews 10:29. Greek analysis: lypeite is present active imperative of lupeo; with me = prohibitive present imperative ("stop doing this"). The verb itself requires a personal subject capable of emotional response. Relevance: Key passage for the "sinned against" category. The Greek grammar of lypeite and the anthropopathism question are both important. Status: Lead to verify against Scripture

    K. Erik Thoennes — "Personal Actions of the Holy Spirit" (Crossway, 2012; adapted from Life's Biggest Questions)¶

    URL: crossway.org Key claims: - Catalogs personal actions attributed to the Spirit with verse references: - Comforts (John 14:16, 26; 15:26; 16:7) - Teaches (John 14:26; 1 Cor 2:13) - Speaks (Acts 8:29; 13:2) - Makes decisions (Acts 15:28) - Grieves over sin (Eph 4:30) - Overrules human actions (Acts 16:6-7) - Searches God's deep things (1 Cor 2:10-11) - Distributes spiritual gifts (1 Cor 12:11) - Intercedes in prayer (Rom 8:26-27) - Assures believers (Rom 8:16) - Bears witness to Christ (John 15:26; 16:14) - "An impersonal force could never provide comfort as Jesus did." - These activities "can be performed only if he is a personal agent." Relevance: Comprehensive catalog of personal-action verbs for the Spirit. Each needs biblical verification. Status: Lead to verify against Scripture

    Lectionary Greek — "1 Corinthians 12:1-11" (Greek grammatical analysis)¶

    URL: lectionarygreek.blogspot.com Key claims: - In 1 Cor 12:11, Paul uses bouletai (βούλεται, present middle indicative of boulomai) — "as he wills." Paul "locates the will of God in the Holy Spirit." - The Spirit energei (ἐνεργέω, "energizes") and diairei (διαιρέω, "distributes") the gifts — all three verbs denote conscious agency. - Described as "the strongest statement in the NT that God is Holy Spirit." - The attribution of energizing, distributing, and willing to the Spirit grants characteristics associated with personhood, not mere force. Greek analysis: boulomai conveys deliberate choice/determination; energeo implies active empowerment; diaireo implies purposeful allocation. All three require a volitional subject. Relevance: 1 Cor 12:11 is one of the strongest personhood texts. The bouletai analysis needs verification. Status: Lead to verify against Scripture

    BeliefMap — "Does the Bible Say the Holy Spirit Is a Person?"¶

    URL: beliefmap.org Key claims — PRO-personhood: - The Spirit speaks using first-person language: Acts 13:2, 8:29, 10:19; 1 Tim 4:1; Rev 14:13. "Only persons issue commands." - The Spirit can be lied to (Acts 5:3-4), insulted (Heb 10:29), grieved (Eph 4:30). "Only a person can literally be insulted." - Charles Ryrie on ekeinos: "biblical writers did not follow this normal procedure of grammar... used masculine pronouns... shows they considered the Spirit to be a person." - Matthew 28:19: onoma ("name") used 228 times refers to persons except four instances. The singular "name" for three suggests equivalent ontological status. - Personal properties: mind (Rom 8:27), will (1 Cor 12:11), love (Rom 15:30), initiative (John 16:13).

    Key claims — ANTI-personhood (critical for balance): 1. Pneuma is neuter — impersonal language suggests non-personhood. Counter: neuter gender describes personal agents throughout NT (demons as "spirits" in Matt 8:16; Acts 19:15). 2. Liquid/metaphorical language — Spirit is "poured out" (Isa 44:3; Rom 5:5; Acts 2:17), "fills" (Acts 2:4), can be "baptized in" (1 Cor 12:13). Counter: God and Christ also "dwell in" and "fill" persons without negating their personhood (John 14:23; Col 1:27; Eph 3:17). 3. "Quenching" the Spirit (1 Thess 5:19) — persons cannot be "quenched." Counter: metaphorical, meaning suppressing the Spirit's influence; Spirit also likened to fire (2 Tim 1:6; Acts 2:3). 4. Impersonal representations — dove, fire, wind, oil. Counter: God appears as fire (Exod 3:2-4; Deut 4:24; Heb 12:29), Christ as bread (John 6:35), vine (John 15:1-5), light. 5. Absence from Father-Son formulas — Spirit absent from Paul's standard salutation (Rom 1:7; 1 Cor 1:3). Counter: Trinity doctrine deemed advanced for initial audiences; consistent format for letter authentication. 6. No proper name — unlike YHWH (Father) and Jesus (Son). Counter: YHWH shared by all (2 Cor 3:17); Son also unnamed throughout OT. 7. List context (2 Cor 6:6) — Spirit listed among impersonal virtues ("purity," "kindness"). Counter: one can be "in Christ" (personal); mixed categories in lists. 8. Testimony pattern (1 John 5:8) — "Spirit and water and blood" groups Spirit with impersonal evidence. Counter: contextually all three "testify" — testimony is a personal act.

    Scholars cited: Dan Wallace (Greek Grammar Beyond the Basics, 1997, pp. 331-33), Craig Keener (The Gospel of John, Vol. 2, 2003), J.W. Wenham (Elements of New Testament Greek, 2003), Charles Ryrie. Relevance: The most comprehensive source found for both sides of the debate. The eight anti-personhood arguments each need engagement. Status: Lead to verify against Scripture

    Summary of Scholarly Landscape¶

    The scholarly debate over the Holy Spirit's personhood centers on two categories of evidence: grammatical and functional.

    The grammatical debate is narrower than popularly assumed. Daniel Wallace, the most-cited NT Greek grammarian, argues that masculine pronouns (ekeinos) in John 14-16 follow standard agreement with the masculine antecedent parakletos, not a deliberate gender override for the neuter pneuma. Robert Bowman demonstrates that neuter gender is irrelevant to personhood by pointing to paidion (neuter) referring to Jesus. The Ministry Magazine article calls grammatical gender "nothing more than a linguistic accident." Against this, Ron Clouzet (JATS) argues John deliberately broke gender agreement to signal personhood. Wallace's position — that grammar alone neither proves nor disproves personhood — appears to be the scholarly consensus. The grammatical evidence is therefore inconclusive on its own.

    The functional/semantic evidence is where the stronger case is made. Scholars across traditions (Bowman, Thoennes, Keener, MacArthur, Cole) consistently identify the same cluster of personal-action verbs: the Spirit speaks in the first person (Acts 13:2), wills (bouletai, 1 Cor 12:11), searches and knows (1 Cor 2:10-11), intercedes with a phronema (Rom 8:26-27), grieves (lypeite, Eph 4:30), can be lied to (Acts 5:3-4), and testifies (John 15:26). The triadic formulas (Matt 28:19; 2 Cor 13:14) coordinate the Spirit with the Father and Son under a singular onoma. The anti-personhood side responds with impersonal metaphors (poured out, fire, wind, oil, water) and the absence of the Spirit from Father-Son salutations, but pro-personhood scholars counter that the same impersonal metaphors apply to God and Christ without negating their personhood.

    The Adventist scholarly contribution (Moskala, Clouzet, Davidson, JATS 2019) consistently affirms the Spirit's personhood, with Moskala providing important OT evidence (especially Isaiah 63:10 and Genesis 1:2) and Clouzet defending the grammatical-override reading of John 16:13. The anti-personhood position is primarily represented by non-Trinitarian groups (Unitarians, some Witnesses publications) rather than mainstream biblical scholarship.

    Research Directives for Downstream Agents¶

    1. Wallace vs. Clouzet on ekeinos: Parse John 14:26, 15:26, 16:13 with the Greek parser to determine whether ekeinos grammatically tracks parakletos or pneuma as its antecedent. The grammatical structure of these sentences is the key data point.
    2. Bowman's paidion parallel: Verify that paidion (neuter) is used for Jesus in Matt 2:8, 9, 11, 13, 14, 20; Luke 2:17, 27, 40 — and check whether masculine pronouns appear alongside neuter paidion in any of these verses.
    3. Acts 13:2 first-person speech: Verify the Greek text — does the Spirit use first-person moi ("me") in "Separate me Barnabas and Saul"? Parse the verse for the Spirit as grammatical subject of legō or equivalent.
    4. Acts 5:3-4 lying to the Spirit = lying to God: Verify the Greek — does Peter's statement explicitly equate lying to the Spirit with lying to God? What is the Greek construction?
    5. 1 Cor 12:11 bouletai: Parse the verse — confirm bouletai (present middle indicative of boulomai) has the Spirit as its subject. This is the clearest attribution of personal will.
    6. Romans 8:26-27 phronema: Verify that phronema tou pneumatos ("mind of the Spirit") appears in v. 27 and parse the intercession language in v. 26 (hyperentynchanei — "intercedes").
    7. Ephesians 4:30 lypeite: Parse the Greek — confirm lypeite (present imperative of lupeo, "grieve") and the construction with to pneuma to hagion. Check whether the anthropopathism objection (Salmond) undermines the personhood argument.
    8. Isaiah 63:10 Hebrew: Parse the Hebrew — what verb is used for "grieved" (atsab?) and what does it mean when applied to the Spirit? Is ruach qodsho ("His Holy Spirit") used as a personal agent in context?
    9. Acts 28:25-27 Spirit = YHWH: Verify that Paul attributes Isaiah 6:9-10 (spoken by YHWH in Isaiah's vision) to "the Holy Spirit." This is a direct identification of the Spirit with YHWH.
    10. Matthew 28:19 singular onoma: Parse the Greek — confirm that eis to onoma (singular "name") governs all three persons (Father, Son, Holy Spirit). Check whether onoma is always personal (228 vs. 4 exceptions claimed by BeliefMap).
    11. Anti-personhood metaphors: Examine Acts 2:17-18 ("poured out"), Acts 2:4 ("filled with"), 1 Thess 5:19 ("quench"), and the dove/fire/wind imagery. For each, check whether the same metaphorical language is applied to the Father or Son elsewhere.
    12. 1 Cor 2:10-11 Spirit "searches" and "knows": Parse the Greek — eraunao ("searches") and oiden ("knows") attributed to the Spirit. These are cognitive verbs requiring a personal subject.
    13. 2 Cor 13:14 triadic benediction: Parse the Greek construction — is the Spirit coordinated equally with Father and Son? What is the grammatical structure?
    14. Genesis 1:2 merachephet: Parse the Hebrew participle — what does "moving/hovering" (rachaph) over the waters imply about the Spirit's agency in creation?
