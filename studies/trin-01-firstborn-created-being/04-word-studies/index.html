    Word Studies¶

    Question¶

    What does 'firstborn of all creation' (prototokos pases ktiseos) mean in Colossians 1:15-20, and does it teach that Jesus was the first created being? Also examine 'the beginning of the creation of God' (arche tes ktiseos tou theou) in Revelation 3:14. Investigate the OT usage of 'firstborn' (bekor) as a title of preeminence vs. birth order — Psalm 89:27 (God makes David 'firstborn' = highest king), Exodus 4:22 (Israel = 'my firstborn'), Genesis 41:51-52 with Jeremiah 31:9 (Ephraim called firstborn despite being second-born). Examine the creation paradox: John 1:3 says 'without him was not any thing made that was made' — if Jesus is a created being but ALL things were created through him, the logic is circular. Also examine Hebrews 1:6 (prototokos brought into world), Romans 8:29 (firstborn among many brethren), and the full Colossians 1:15-20 hymn where v.16-17 says 'by him were all things created.' Examine arche (G746) in Revelation 3:14 in its full semantic range: 'beginning' vs. 'ruler/source/origin.'

    G4416 — prototokos¶

    Source: D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/G4416-prototokos.md

    Occurrences: 9

    Compound adjective from G4413 (protos, 'first') + G5088 (tikto, 'to bear'). Used both literally (first child born — Mat 1:25, Luk 2:7) and figuratively (one holding the rank, rights, and preeminence of a firstborn). In the LXX, renders Hebrew bekor (H1060) across both literal and metaphorical rank contexts. When applied to Christ, emphasizes preeminence and sovereign rights: firstborn of all creation (Col 1:15), firstborn from the dead (Col 1:18), firstborn among many brethren (Rom 8:29), firstbegotten into the world (Heb 1:6). The LXX mapping shows 87 co-occurrences with H1060 bekor (PMI 7.7).

    Semantic categories: literal firstborn child, Christological preeminence over creation, firstborn from the dead, preeminent among believers, divine introduction into world, OT firstborn — plague/inheritance

    Key verses: Col 1:15, Col 1:18, Rom 8:29, Heb 1:6, Rev 1:5, Heb 12:23, Heb 11:28, Mat 1:25, Luk 2:7

    Relevance: The CENTRAL word of this study. The semantic range of prototokos determines whether Col 1:15 teaches creation or preeminence. The library entry demonstrates that Christological uses consistently emphasize preeminence and sovereign rights, not creation-sequence membership.

    H1060 — bekor¶

    Source: D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/H1060-bᵉkôwr.md

    Occurrences: 118

    Masculine noun from H1069 (bakar); first-born, hence chief. Applied metaphorically to Israel (Exo 4:22), Ephraim (Jer 31:9, despite being second-born), and David/the Messiah (Psa 89:27), indicating preeminence and special status rather than mere birth order. Six semantic categories: literal firstborn (genealogies), sacred to God (redemption/consecration), divine judgment (Egypt's firstborn), title of preeminence (NOT birth order), firstborn animals, and metaphorical 'chief in class.' LXX maps to prototokos (G4416) 87 times.

    Semantic categories: literal firstborn son, sacred to God — consecration, divine judgment — Egypt, title of preeminence — not birth order, firstborn animals, metaphorical chief/first in class

    Key verses: Psa 89:27, Exo 4:22, Jer 31:9, 1 Chr 5:1-2, Gen 48:14-19, Gen 49:3

    Relevance: The OT foundation for understanding prototokos. The preeminence-title category (Psa 89:27, Exo 4:22, Jer 31:9) is decisive — bekor applied to persons who are demonstrably NOT first-born by natural birth order.

    G746 — arche¶

    Source: D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/G746-arche.md

    Occurrences: 58

    Feminine noun from G756 (archo, 'to rule, begin'). Two semantic poles: (1) temporal/ordinal priority — 'beginning, first'; (2) positional priority — 'rule, authority, principality.' 56 unique verses, 7 semantic categories. Category 7 = 'Christ as the Beginning/Origin' (Col 1:18, Rev 3:14, Rev 1:8, 21:6, 22:13). Hebrew sources include reshith (H7225) and rosh (H7218, 'chief/head/ruler'). The Johannine Alpha-and-Omega formula uses arche as a divine title of eternal completeness. In Revelation, arche appears in 4 contexts: cosmic powers (principalities), creation/temporal origin, Christological title, and Alpha-Omega formula.

    Semantic categories: temporal origin of creation, from the beginning of gospel, beginning of narrative/event, first principles, rulers/principalities, human rulers, Christ as Beginning/Origin, original estate, corner/extremity

    Key verses: Rev 3:14, Rev 1:8, Rev 21:6, Rev 22:13, Col 1:18, John 1:1

    Relevance: ESSENTIAL for Rev 3:14 interpretation. The library entry's Category 7 places Rev 3:14 in the 'Christ as the Beginning/Origin' cluster alongside Col 1:18 and the Alpha-Omega passages. The Hebrew source rosh (H7218, 'chief/head') confirms a 'ruler' sense exists.

    G2937 — ktisis¶

    Source: D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/G2937-ktisis.md

    Occurrences: 19

    Feminine noun from G2936 ktizo; original formation — the act of creating or the thing created. 7 semantic categories: created order/physical creation, creation groaning under futility, new creation in Christ, every creature/universal scope, individual creature before God, institution/ordinance, Christological title. Col 1:15 uses ktisis in the genitive (ktiseos) with pas ('every/all') — 'firstborn of every creature.'

    Semantic categories: physical creation, creation groaning, new creation in Christ, every creature, individual creature, institution/ordinance, Christological title

    Key verses: Col 1:15, Col 1:23, Rev 3:14, Rom 8:19-22, 2 Cor 5:17, Gal 6:15

    Relevance: Defines what 'creation' means in the key phrases. In Rev 3:14, 'the creation of God' uses ktiseos — the created order. In Col 1:15, 'of every creature' uses the same genitive form.

    G2936 — ktizo¶

    Source: D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/G2936-ktizo.md

    Occurrences: 16

    Verb: to fabricate, found, create. 12 unique verses. Overwhelmingly aorist (completed acts). Active voice takes God/Christ as subject. Col 1:16 uses both aorist passive (ektisthe — 'were created') and perfect passive (ektistai — 'have been created and stand created'). The perfect passive emphasizes the enduring result of creation. Hebrew source: H1254 bara (16x, PMI 8.25) — the distinctive Hebrew creation verb.

    Semantic categories: cosmic/material creation, new creation in Christ, corporate unity, purposeful creation of gender/goods, Creator as title

    Key verses: Col 1:16, Rev 4:11, Rev 10:6, Eph 2:10, Eph 3:9

    Relevance: The creation verb in Col 1:16. Christ is the agent of ktizo — all things 'were created' (ektisthe/ektistai) through and for him.

    G3956 — pas¶

    Source: D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/G3956-pas.md

    Occurrences: 1229

    Quantifier of totality — the most frequent adjective in the NT (1,243 occurrences). Meaning determined by syntax: anarthrous singular = 'every' (distributive), articular singular = 'the whole,' anarthrous plural = 'all' (indefinite group), articular plural = 'all' (definite totality). In Col 1:16, ta panta (articular neuter plural) = 'all things' — definite totality, the most comprehensive scope possible.

    Semantic categories: collective plural — all, distributive singular — every, whole — singular with article, indefinite — any, qualitative — all kinds of, total/utter — abstract, substantival — everyone

    Key verses: Col 1:16, Col 1:17, John 1:3, 1 Cor 8:6

    Relevance: Defines the scope of 'ALL things' in the creation paradox. Ta panta in Col 1:16 and John 1:3 is the broadest possible scope — all created things without exception. If Christ is a created thing, he created himself.

    G4409 — proteuo¶

    Source: D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/G4409-prōteúō.md

    Occurrences: 1

    Hapax legomenon. Verb from G4413 (protos); to be first in rank or influence — 'have the preeminence.' Occurs only in Col 1:18. The purpose clause 'that in all things he might have the preeminence' (hina genetai en pasin autos proteuon) is Paul's own summary of the entire hymn's purpose. The scope is universal — 'in all things' (en pasin).

    Semantic categories: Christological supremacy, priority and rank

    Key verses: Col 1:18

    Relevance: Paul's own gloss on the meaning of prototokos in the hymn. If 'firstborn' merely meant 'first created,' Paul would not need to add a purpose clause about 'preeminence' — that would already be implied. The separate proteuo clause shows 'firstborn' carries a preeminence meaning that Paul wants to make explicit.

    G4413 — protos¶

    Source: D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/G4413-protos.md

    Occurrences: 105

    Contracted superlative of pro (G4253, 'before'). 99 unique verses. Two-axis semantic range: (1) ordinal sequence — 'first in a series'; (2) rank/primacy — 'chief, foremost.' Distinguished from prototokos: protos = 'first' (pure ordinal); prototokos = 'firstborn' (ordinal + inheritance rights). Paul had protos available if he meant 'first in sequence' at Col 1:15 but chose prototokos — the word carrying inheritance/preeminence connotations.

    Semantic categories: ordinal position, temporal priority, backward-referring paired set, rank/primacy/chief, first/last paradox, divine designation first-and-last, primacy of importance

    Key verses: Rev 1:11, Rev 1:17, Rev 2:8, Rev 22:13, Acts 26:23

    Relevance: The 'first' semantic domain. The protos vs. prototokos distinction matters: Paul had protos for 'first in sequence' but chose prototokos for its richer connotations of rank and inheritance.

    G5481 — charakter¶

    Source: D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/G5481-charaktēr.md

    Occurrences: 1

    Hapax legomenon. From charassō ('to engrave'). Denotes an exact reproduction — the precise imprint a seal leaves in wax. Applied to Christ as 'the express image of his person' (Heb 1:3). Stronger than eikon (G1504): eikon = derived likeness; charakter = point-for-point exact correspondence.

    Semantic categories: exact representation of divine being

    Key verses: Heb 1:3

    Relevance: Parallel to eikon (G1504) in Col 1:15. The Son as exact representation of the Father's being is incompatible with the Son being a created being — a creature is not an exact imprint of the uncreated God.

    G3439 — monogenes¶

    Source: D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/G3439-monogenḗs.md

    Occurrences: 9

    From monos ('alone') + genos ('kind/class'). 'Only-born' or 'sole of its kind.' Johannine Christological title (John 1:14, 1:18, 3:16, 3:18, 1 John 4:9). Hebrews 11:17 applies it to Isaac — Abraham's 'only-begotten' despite having other sons. The term emphasizes unique, one-of-a-kind relationship. Contrasts with prototokos: monogenes = sole member of a class (no others); prototokos = preeminent among a group (others exist).

    Semantic categories: unique divine sonship, only child of parent, patriarchal — typological context

    Key verses: John 1:14, John 1:18, John 3:16, 1 John 4:9, Heb 11:17

    Relevance: Demonstrates the non-biological usage of 'begotten' language. If monogenes means 'unique in kind' (not 'first offspring'), then the 'born' element in prototokos similarly need not refer to literal creation/birth. The 2 Esdras 6:58 parallel (Israel = both 'firstborn' and 'only begotten') proves these titles can co-exist as status designations.

    G747 — archegos¶

    Source: D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/G747-archēgós.md

    Occurrences: 4

    From G746 (arche) + G71 (ago, 'to lead'). Chief leader, pioneer, originator. Applied to Christ: 'Prince of life' (Acts 3:15), 'Prince and Saviour' (Acts 5:31), 'captain of salvation' (Heb 2:10), 'author of faith' (Heb 12:2). Two semantic categories: pioneer/originator of salvation, and sovereign ruler/prince.

    Semantic categories: pioneer/originator of salvation, sovereign ruler/prince

    Key verses: Acts 3:15, Acts 5:31, Heb 2:10, Heb 12:2

    Relevance: Part of the arche word family applied to Christ. Archegos = 'pioneer-leader who originates'; arche = 'beginning/origin'; archon = 'ruler.' The lexical field shows multiple arche-root words applied to Christ emphasizing origination and supremacy.

    H7069 — qanah¶

    Source: D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/H7069-qanah.md

    Occurrences: 92

    Verb: to erect, create; by extension to procure, purchase, possess. Dominant usage: commercial acquisition (50%). In Proverbs: acquiring wisdom (15%). The theologically critical passage is Pro 8:22 where the KJV translates 'The LORD possessed me' but the LXX uses ektise ('created'). The LXX uses ktizo for qanah in only 4 instances — all where God is subject and the object is cosmic or a person. The Hebrew parsing glosses qanah at Pro 8:22 as 'create' but the KJV tradition follows 'possessed.'

    Semantic categories: commercial acquisition, acquiring wisdom, divine acquisition/possession, creation/origination, ownership/mastery

    Key verses: Pro 8:22, Gen 4:1, Gen 14:19, Gen 14:22, Deu 32:6

    Relevance: The Pro 8:22 debate — 'possessed' vs. 'created' — is background for the Arian/Nicene controversy. If Wisdom was 'created' in Pro 8:22, and Christ is identified with Wisdom, then Christ was created. But Paul's Col 1:15-17 goes far beyond Pro 8's scope: Pro 8 says Wisdom was 'with' God during creation; Col 1:16 says ALL things were created BY Christ and FOR Christ.

    H1069 — bakar¶

    Source: D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/H1069-bakar.md

    Occurrences: 6

    Root verb of bekor (H1060). To burst the womb, bear firstborn; denominatively, to give the birthright. 6 occurrences. Deu 21:16 uses bakar in the sense of 'designating/conferring firstborn status' — showing that firstborn status is legally conferred, not merely biological.

    Semantic categories: bearing firstborn child, conferring firstborn status, firstling animals, bearing new fruit

    Key verses: Deu 21:16, Jer 4:31, Lev 27:26

    Relevance: Provides the verbal root showing 'firstborn' has a legal/conferral dimension — one can 'make firstborn' (levakker) a son.

    G1504 — eikon¶

    Source: D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/G1504-eikon.md

    Occurrences: 23

    Feminine noun from eikō ('to be like, resemble'). A likeness, representation. Col 1:15 — 'the image (eikon) of the invisible God.' Christ as eikon of God = the visible expression of the invisible God. Category 2 in the library: 'not merely resembling God but constituting the full representation of his nature.'

    Semantic categories: imperial coin inscription, Christ as image of God, humanity bearing God's image, shadow vs. true image, image of the beast

    Key verses: Col 1:15, 2 Cor 4:4, Rom 8:29, Heb 10:1

    Relevance: Col 1:15 pairs eikon ('image of the invisible God') with prototokos ('firstborn of every creature'). The 'image' title describes Christ's nature-relation to God; the 'firstborn' title describes his status-relation to creation.

    G758 — archon¶

    Source: D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/G758-archon.md

    Occurrences: 37

    Masculine noun from the present participle of archo (G756). 'A first in rank or power — chief ruler.' 36 unique verses. Rev 1:5 calls Christ 'the prince (archon) of the kings of the earth.' John uses archon for 'ruler' and arche for 'beginning/origin' — they have distinct functions despite sharing the arch- root.

    Semantic categories: Jewish civic/religious rulers, local officials, judicial/magistrate, political rulers, prince/ruler of this world, rulers of this age, positive royal title

    Key verses: Rev 1:5, John 12:31, John 14:30, Acts 7:35

    Relevance: Key to the archon vs. arche distinction in Johannine Christology. John uses archon in Rev 1:5 for 'prince/ruler' but arche in Rev 3:14 — suggesting different nuances. The non-Trinitarian argument uses this to claim arche does NOT mean 'ruler' at Rev 3:14.

    G536 — aparche¶

    Source: D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/G536-aparche.md

    Occurrences: 8

    Feminine noun from apo (G575) + archomai (G756). 'A beginning of sacrifice — firstfruits.' 1 Cor 15:20 — Christ as 'firstfruits of them that slept.' Functionally parallel to prototokos in resurrection contexts. The OT firstfruits principle: the first portion given to God consecrates the whole.

    Semantic categories: Christ's resurrection as firstfruits, Spirit as firstfruits, consecration — dough/root metaphor, early converts, believers as firstfruits, 144,000 as firstfruits

    Key verses: 1 Cor 15:20, 1 Cor 15:23, Rom 8:23

    Relevance: Parallel concept to prototokos in the resurrection domain. If 'firstfruits' in 1 Cor 15:20 means 'the one whose resurrection pioneers and guarantees all others,' then 'firstborn from the dead' in Col 1:18 carries the same force.

    G1096 — ginomai¶

    Source: D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/G1096-ginomai.md

    Occurrences: 702

    Primary verb: to cause to be, become, happen. 631 NT occurrences. The creation verb in John 1:3 (egeneto — aorist: 'came into being'). Critical distinction with eimi (G1510): eimi = absolute being; ginomai = coming-into-being. John 1:1 uses en (eimi, imperfect) for the Word; John 1:3 uses egeneto (ginomai, aorist) for created things. John 8:58 crystallizes this: 'Before Abraham was (genesthai — ginomai), I am (ego eimi — eimi).'

    Semantic categories: narrative transition, happening/occurring, becoming/change of state, being made/coming into existence, emphatic denial, imperatival, existential/Christological identity

    Key verses: John 1:3, John 1:10, John 1:14, John 8:58

    Relevance: The en/egeneto distinction in John 1:1-3 is one of the strongest grammatical arguments separating Christ from creation. Christ 'was' (en, eimi); created things 'came into being' (egeneto, ginomai).

    G5547 — christos¶

    Source: D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/G5547-christos.md

    Occurrences: 569

    From G5548 chriō ('to anoint'). The Anointed One, Greek equivalent of Hebrew mashiyach (H4899). 530 unique verses. The title presupposes a unique divine appointment and role, not createdness.

    Semantic categories: disputed Messianic title, crucified and risen Christ, in Christ — union, Christ as head and authority, Christ's suffering as pattern, the coming Christ

    Key verses: Col 1:15, Col 1:18, Phil 2:6-11

    Relevance: The anointed status of Christ — 'Messiah' = appointed by God to supreme office — aligns with the 'firstborn = preeminence' reading rather than the 'firstborn = first creature' reading.

    G1080 — gennao¶

    Source: D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/G1080-gennáō.md

    Occurrences: 105

    Verb: to procreate, beget (of the father), bear (of the mother). 105 NT occurrences. Four semantic categories: (1) physical generation/birth — the dominant use in Matthew 1 genealogy (41x 'begat'); (2) new birth/spiritual origin — Johannine 'born of God/Spirit' language (John 1:13, 3:3-5, 1 John 5:1); (3) declared sonship/messianic enthronement — Psalm 2:7 'I have begotten thee' applied to Christ in Acts 13:33, Heb 1:5, 5:5; (4) figurative production — Paul 'begetting' Onesimus (Philemon 1:10). The 'begotten' element is one component of proto-tokos (first-BORN/BEGOTTEN), but the compound word's meaning is not simply 'first to be begotten' — the OT usage (bekor H1060) shows the compound carries status/rank connotations beyond the bare etymology.

    Semantic categories: physical generation and birth, new birth and spiritual origin, declared sonship and messianic enthronement, figurative production or outcome

    Key verses: Mat 1:2, Mat 1:16, John 1:13, John 3:3, John 3:5, Acts 13:33, Heb 1:5, Heb 5:5, 1 John 5:1

    Relevance: The 'born/begotten' component of prototokos. The gennao word family covers both physical birth and divine declaration of sonship (Psa 2:7 / Heb 1:5). When Hebrews 1:5 applies 'I have begotten thee' to Christ and v.6 calls him 'firstbegotten' (prototokos), the 'begetting' is messianic enthronement/declaration, not physical creation. The spiritual/declarative uses of gennao (born of God, born again) show that 'begetting' language in the NT often transcends biological origin.
