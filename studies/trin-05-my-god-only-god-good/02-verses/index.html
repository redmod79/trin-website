    Verses¶

    Question¶

    When Jesus says 'Why do you call me good? No one is good except God alone' (Mark 10:18) and 'I ascend to my Father and your Father, to my God and your God' (John 20:17), is he denying his own deity?

    Primary crux text: Rich young ruler and the goodness question¶

    And when he was gone forth into the way, there came one running, and kneeled to him, and asked him, Good Master, what shall I do that I may inherit eternal life? And Jesus said unto him, Why callest thou me good? [there is] none good but one, [that is], God. Thou knowest the commandments, Do not commit adultery, Do not kill, Do not steal, Do not bear false witness, Defraud not, Honour thy father and mother. And he answered and said unto him, Master, all these have I observed from my youth. Then Jesus beholding him loved him, and said unto him, One thing thou lackest: go thy way, sell whatsoever thou hast, and give to the poor, and thou shalt have treasure in heaven: and come, take up the cross, and follow me. And he was sad at that saying, and went away grieved: for he had great possessions.

    Context: Mark 10 records Jesus teaching in Judea. The young man runs to Jesus, kneels, and addresses him as 'Good Master' (Didaskalos agathe). Jesus responds with the goodness question, then lists second-tablet commandments (5-10), omitting all first-tablet commandments (1-4), and climaxes with 'follow me.' Chapter context includes marriage/divorce teaching (vv.1-12), children and the kingdom (vv.13-16), and the rich man episode leading to discussion of the cost of discipleship (vv.23-31).

    Notes: CRITICAL FINDING: The Greek of Mark 10:18 contains 'ei me heis ho Theos' — identical to the phrase in Mark 2:7 ('who can forgive sins ei me heis ho Theos?'). This is the same construction in both verses. In Mark 2, Jesus responds to the 'only God can forgive sins' challenge by demonstrating divine authority (healing the paralytic). Mark 10:18 uses the same phrase, suggesting an intentional literary parallel. The commandment-omission pattern is verified: Jesus lists Do not kill (6th), Do not commit adultery (7th), Do not steal (8th), Do not bear false witness (9th), Defraud not (related to 10th), Honour thy father and mother (5th) — ALL second-tablet commandments. First-tablet commandments (1-4: no other gods, no graven images, no taking name in vain, sabbath) are entirely absent. Then 'follow me' replaces them, placing Jesus in the position of supreme allegiance that the first tablet reserves for God.

    Synoptic parallel: Matthew's divergent phrasing¶

    And, behold, one came and said unto him, Good Master, what good thing shall I do, that I may have eternal life? And he said unto him, Why callest thou me good? [there is] none good but one, [that is], God: but if thou wilt enter into life, keep the commandments.

    Context: Matthew's account of the same episode. The Greek differs significantly: the man asks 'ti agathon poieso' (what good thing shall I do?) and Jesus responds 'Ti me erotas peri tou agathou? heis estin ho agathos' (Why do you ask me concerning the good? One is the Good One). Matthew shifts the 'good' from personal address to abstract concept, possibly clarifying for his audience that the issue is the nature of absolute goodness rather than a personal title.

    Notes: The synoptic divergence is verified. In Mark/Luke, the man calls Jesus 'Good Teacher' and Jesus asks 'Why callest thou me good?' In Matthew, the man asks about 'what good thing' and Jesus responds about the nature of 'the good.' Both versions preserve the key claim: 'none good but one, God' (Mark/Luke) or 'one is the Good One' (Matthew). Matthew adds 'keep the commandments' explicitly.

    Third synoptic parallel: Luke's version confirming the pattern¶

    And a certain ruler asked him, saying, Good Master, what shall I do to inherit eternal life? And Jesus said unto him, Why callest thou me good? none [is] good, save one, [that is], God. Thou knowest the commandments, Do not commit adultery, Do not kill, Do not steal, Do not bear false witness, Honour thy father and thy mother. And he said, All these have I kept from my youth up. Now when Jesus heard these things, he said unto him, Yet lackest thou one thing: sell all that thou hast, and distribute unto the poor, and thou shalt have treasure in heaven: and come, follow me. And when he heard this, he was very sorrowful: for he was very rich.

    Context: Luke identifies the questioner as 'a certain ruler' (archon tis). Luke's Greek parallels Mark closely: 'Didaskalos agathe' and 'oudeis agathos ei me heis ho Theos.' Luke also has 'follow me' as the climax, matching Mark's pattern of replacing the first-tablet commandments with personal allegiance to Jesus.

    Notes: All three synoptic accounts preserve the core structure: (1) address as 'good,' (2) Jesus' response linking goodness to God alone, (3) listing second-tablet commandments, (4) 'follow me' as the final demand. The consistency across three independent accounts confirms these are not accidental features.

    The 'ei me heis ho theos' lexical parallel: forgiveness prerogative¶

    When Jesus saw their faith, he said unto the sick of the palsy, Son, thy sins be forgiven thee. But there were certain of the scribes sitting there, and reasoning in their hearts, Why doth this [man] thus speak blasphemies? who can forgive sins but God only? And immediately when Jesus perceived in his spirit that they so reasoned within themselves, he said unto them, Why reason ye these things in your hearts? Whether is it easier to say to the sick of the palsy, [Thy] sins be forgiven thee; or to say, Arise, and take up thy bed, and walk? But that ye may know that the Son of man hath power on earth to forgive sins, (he saith to the sick of the palsy,) I say unto thee, Arise, and take up thy bed, and go thy way into thine house. And immediately he arose, took up the bed, and went forth before them all; insomuch that they were all amazed, and glorified God, saying, We never saw it on this fashion.

    Context: Mark 2 records Jesus in Capernaum. The scribes' reasoning contains the key phrase: 'tis dynatai aphienai hamartias ei me heis ho Theos?' — who can forgive sins except one, God? Jesus responds by demonstrating divine authority through healing. The pattern is: challenge ('only God can X') followed by Jesus demonstrating that he can X.

    Notes: CRITICAL LEXICAL PARALLEL CONFIRMED: Mark 2:7 contains 'ei me heis ho Theos' — exactly the same phrase as Mark 10:18. Both verses use the identical construction: conditional particle (ei) + negative (me) + numeral 'one' (heis) + article (ho) + Theos. In Mark 2, the 'only God' challenge is followed by Jesus demonstrating the divine prerogative (forgiving sins). This creates an intentional Markan literary pattern: when the claim 'only God can do X' appears, Jesus proceeds to demonstrate that he can do X. In Mark 10, after 'only God is good,' Jesus demonstrates authority over the commandments and demands supreme allegiance ('follow me'), which are divine prerogatives.

    Second crux text: 'My Father and your Father, my God and your God'¶

    Jesus saith unto her, Touch me not; for I am not yet ascended to my Father: but go to my brethren, and say unto them, I ascend unto my Father, and your Father; and [to] my God, and your God.

    Context: John 20 records the resurrection morning. Mary Magdalene encounters the risen Jesus. He tells her 'Me mou haptou' (stop clinging to me) because he has 'not yet ascended' (oupo anabebeka — perfect tense of anabaino G305). Then he delivers the message: 'Anabaino pros ton Patera mou kai Patera hymon kai Theon mou kai Theon hymon.' The deliberate possessive structure is key: 'MY Father and YOUR Father, MY God and YOUR God' — not 'OUR Father and OUR God.' This distinction preserves the unique filial relationship while relating the disciples to God through a different channel.

    Notes: The possessive pronoun structure is theologically significant. Jesus does NOT say 'our Father and our God' — he maintains the distinction between his relationship to the Father and the disciples' relationship. The pattern 'my X and your X' occurs in Ruth 1:16 ('thy people shall be my people, and thy God my God') where it expresses the adoption of a relationship by analogy, not identity. Jesus' use inverts this: he is not adopting the disciples' relationship but granting them a share in his relationship while maintaining its unique character. The verb anabaino (G305) in present tense 'I ascend' indicates the ascension as in-process or imminent, framing the entire exchange in the context of Jesus' return to heavenly glory.

    Thomas's 'My Lord and my God' — deity confession accepted¶

    And after eight days again his disciples were within, and Thomas with them: [then] came Jesus, the doors being shut, and stood in the midst, and said, Peace [be] unto you. Then saith he to Thomas, Reach hither thy finger, and behold my hands; and reach hither thy hand, and thrust [it] into my side: and be not faithless, but believing. And Thomas answered and said unto him, My Lord and my God. Jesus saith unto him, Thomas, because thou hast seen me, thou hast believed: blessed [are] they that have not seen, and [yet] have believed.

    Context: Eleven verses after John 20:17 ('my God and your God'), Thomas declares to Jesus 'Ho Kyrios mou kai ho Theos mou' — 'The Lord of me and the God of me.' The Greek 'eipen auto' (said to him) establishes direct address. Both Kyrios and Theos are articular nominative (T-NSM + N-NSM), functioning as vocative per the nominative-for-vocative pattern (see grammar reference). Jesus accepts this without correction — contrast with Peter (Acts 10:25-26), Paul (Acts 14:14-15), and the angel (Rev 19:10; 22:8-9).

    Notes: The juxtaposition within John 20 is decisive. If 'my God' language in v.17 denies deity, then Thomas's identical 'my God' in v.28 would be blasphemous — yet Jesus accepts it with a blessing. John 20:30-31 then states the Gospel's purpose: 'that ye might believe that Jesus is the Christ, the Son of God.' The chapter moves from 'my God' (spoken BY Jesus about the Father) to 'my God' (spoken TO Jesus by Thomas) and reaches its climax in the purpose statement. The grammar confirms direct address: eipen auto (said to him) + ho Theos mou (nominative-for-vocative). The nominative-for-vocative grammar entry documents this pattern extensively with John 20:28 as the paradigmatic example.

    Jesus' response to Thomas and John's purpose statement¶

    Jesus saith unto him, Thomas, because thou hast seen me, thou hast believed: blessed [are] they that have not seen, and [yet] have believed. And many other signs truly did Jesus in the presence of his disciples, which are not written in this book: But these are written, that ye might believe that Jesus is the Christ, the Son of God; and that believing ye might have life through his name.

    Context: Jesus' response to Thomas is not correction but commendation of belief, followed by a beatitude extending the blessing. John then gives his explicit purpose statement: the entire Gospel was written so readers would believe Jesus is the Christ, the Son of God. Thomas's confession serves as the climactic demonstration of this belief — the last recorded confession before the purpose statement.

    Notes: John 20:28-31 forms an inclusio with John 1:1. The Gospel opens with 'the Word was God' (theos en ho logos) and closes with Thomas's 'my God' (ho theos mou) directed to Jesus. The theological trajectory is: pre-incarnate deity (1:1) → incarnation (1:14) → 'my God and your God' (20:17) → 'my Lord and my God' (20:28) → purpose statement (20:31).

    Father addresses Son as God and applies YHWH-psalm to him¶

    And again, when he bringeth in the firstbegotten into the world, he saith, And let all the angels of God worship him. And of the angels he saith, Who maketh his angels spirits, and his ministers a flame of fire. But unto the Son [he saith], Thy throne, O God, [is] for ever and ever: a sceptre of righteousness [is] the sceptre of thy kingdom. Thou hast loved righteousness, and hated iniquity; therefore God, [even] thy God, hath anointed thee with the oil of gladness above thy fellows. And, Thou, Lord, in the beginning hast laid the foundation of the earth; and the heavens are the works of thine hands: They shall perish; but thou remainest; and they all shall wax old as doth a garment; And as a vesture shalt thou fold them up, and they shall be changed: but thou art the same, and thy years shall not fail.

    Context: Hebrews 1 is a chain of OT quotations applied to the Son. In v.6, the Father commands worship of the Son (proskynesatosan auto pantes angeloi theou). In vv.8-9 (quoting Psalm 45:6-7), the Father addresses the Son as 'ho Theos' — God. In vv.10-12 (quoting Psalm 102:25-27), a psalm addressed to YHWH is applied to the Son. The two-elohim pattern is explicit: in v.8, the Son is called Theos; in v.9, the Son has 'ho Theos sou' (your God) who anointed him. Both 'God' (addressing the Son) and 'God, your God' (the Father) coexist.

    Notes: This passage is structurally parallel to John 20:17. In Hebrews 1:8-9, the Son IS called God while simultaneously having 'God, your God' — the same pattern as John 20:17 where Jesus IS recognized as God (by Thomas in v.28) while saying 'my God' (v.17). The Hebrews passage proves the biblical framework accommodates both: being called God AND having 'my God.' The Psalm 102:25-27 quotation in vv.10-12 is even more striking: this psalm is addressed to YHWH ('of old hast thou laid the foundation of the earth'), yet Hebrews applies it directly to the Son, attributing creation to him.

    Hebrew source: Two-elohim pattern — the addressed king is called God¶

    Thy throne, O God, [is] for ever and ever: the sceptre of thy kingdom [is] a right sceptre. Thou lovest righteousness, and hatest wickedness: therefore God, thy God, hath anointed thee with the oil of gladness above thy fellows.

    Context: Psalm 45 is a royal wedding psalm. The Hebrew of v.6 reads 'kisakha elohiym olam waed' — 'Thy throne, O God, is forever and ever.' The addressed figure is called elohiym (H430). In v.7, the same figure has 'elohiym eloheykha' — 'God, your God' — who anointed him. The two-elohim structure is present in the Hebrew: the addressed king is elohim, and his anointing God is also elohim.

    • כִּסְאֲךָ () — Noun.ms.Abs — + 2ms suffix = 'thy throne'] אֱלֹהִים [אלהים Noun.mp.Abs = 'god(s)' — addressed as 'O God'] עֹולָם [עולם Noun.ms.Abs = 'eternity'] וָעֶד [ו Conj + עד Noun.ms.Abs = 'and ever'] שֵׁבֶט [שׁבט Noun.ms.Cst = 'rod/sceptre'] מִישֹׁר [מישׁור Noun.ms.Abs = 'fairness/righteousness'] שֵׁבֶט [שׁבט Noun.ms.Cst = 'rod/sceptre'] מַלְכוּתֶךָ [מלכות Noun.fs.Abs + 2ms suffix = 'thy kingdom']

    Notes: The Hebrew parsing confirms: kisakha (your throne, Noun.ms.Abs + 2ms suffix) elohiym (Noun.mp.Abs — god(s)). Hebrews 1:8 introduces this quotation with 'pros de ton Huion' (but unto the Son), explicitly applying the two-elohim pattern to the Father-Son relationship. This is the OT foundation for the claim that being called 'God' and having 'my God' / 'your God' are not contradictory.

    YHWH psalm applied to the Son in Hebrews 1:10-12¶

    Of old hast thou laid the foundation of the earth: and the heavens [are] the work of thy hands. They shall perish, but thou shalt endure: yea, all of them shall wax old like a garment; as a vesture shalt thou change them, and they shall be changed: But thou [art] the same, and thy years shall have no end.

    Context: Psalm 102 is a prayer to YHWH. The psalmist addresses YHWH directly. These verses describe YHWH as the creator who endures while creation perishes. Hebrews 1:10-12 quotes these verses verbatim and applies them to the Son (within the 'pros de ton Huion' address that began at Heb 1:8). This is one of three key NT passages that apply YHWH-exclusive OT texts to Jesus (with Phil 2:10-11/Isa 45:23 and Rom 10:13/Joel 2:32).

    • הֵמָּה () — PersPron.3mp — = 'they'] יֹאבֵדוּ [אבד Verb.Qal.Impf.3mp = 'shall perish'] וְאַתָּה [ו Conj + אתה PersPron.2ms = 'but thou'] תַעֲמֹד [עמד Verb.Qal.Impf.2ms = 'shalt endure']

    Notes: The Hebrew of Psalm 102:25 contains 'eli' (H410 + 1st person suffix) — 'my God' — in the same psalm that Hebrews applies to the Son as YHWH. This means Psalm 102 itself contains both 'my God' language (addressed to YHWH) and creation-attributions that the NT transfers to Jesus.

    Glorified Christ's fourfold 'my God' — post-ascension language¶

    Him that overcometh will I make a pillar in the temple of my God, and he shall go no more out: and I will write upon him the name of my God, and the name of the city of my God, [which is] new Jerusalem, which cometh down out of heaven from my God: and [I will write upon him] my new name.

    Context: The glorified, post-ascension Christ speaks from heaven to the church at Philadelphia. He uses 'tou Theou mou' (my God, G2316 + G1473) four times: 'the temple of my God,' 'the name of my God,' 'the city of my God,' 'from my God.' This is NOT humiliation language — this is the exalted Christ speaking from heavenly glory. Yet the SAME speaker in Rev 1:8 claims 'I am Alpha and Omega' (YHWH title), in Rev 1:17-18 claims 'I am the first and the last' (Isa 44:6 YHWH self-designation), and in Rev 22:12-13 stacks Alpha/Omega + First/Last + Beginning/End.

    Notes: The Greek parsing confirms fourfold 'tou Theou mou': (1) tou Theou mou — in the temple of my God; (2) tou Theou mou — the name of my God; (3) tou Theou mou — the city of my God; (4) tou Theou mou — from my God. All four are genitive constructions (Theou = N-GSM, mou = P-1GS). This is decisive evidence that 'my God' language does not negate deity, because the same speaker holds YHWH titles.

    Christ claims Alpha and Omega / Almighty — YHWH titles¶

    I am Alpha and Omega, the beginning and the ending, saith the Lord, which is, and which was, and which is to come, the Almighty.

    Context: Revelation 1:8 attributes 'Alpha and Omega, the beginning and the ending' and 'the Almighty' (ho Pantokrator) to the speaker. The same title appears in Rev 22:13 where Christ is the speaker. The SAME Christ who says 'my God' four times in Rev 3:12 here claims the title 'Almighty.'

    Notes: The juxtaposition of 'my God' (Rev 3:12) with 'the Almighty' (Rev 1:8) in the same book, attributed to the same glorified Christ, demonstrates that 'my God' language is a function of the incarnational relationship, not ontological inferiority.

    Christ claims 'First and Last' — YHWH title from Isaiah 44:6¶

    And when I saw him, I fell at his feet as dead. And he laid his right hand upon me, saying unto me, Fear not; I am the first and the last: [I am] he that liveth, and was dead; and, behold, I am alive for evermore, Amen; and have the keys of hell and of death.

    Context: The risen Christ appears to John and claims 'I am the first and the last' — quoting the YHWH self-designation from Isa 44:6 ('I am the first, and I am the last; and beside me there is no God'). He then adds 'I became dead and behold I am alive' — combining the YHWH title with the incarnational experience of death and resurrection.

    Notes: Isaiah 44:6 uses 'First and Last' as an exclusive YHWH self-designation, paired with 'beside me there is no God.' Christ's claim of this title in Rev 1:17 constitutes a direct deity claim. The addition 'I became dead' (egenomen nekros) integrates the incarnational reality into the YHWH-title claim — he is both the eternal First-and-Last AND the one who died.

    Alpha/Omega + First/Last + Beginning/End stacked — same speaker as Rev 3:12¶

    And, behold, I come quickly; and my reward [is] with me, to give every man according as his work shall be. I am Alpha and Omega, the beginning and the end, the first and the last.

    Context: The final chapter of Revelation. The speaker (Christ, coming with reward) stacks three title-pairs: Alpha/Omega, Beginning/End, First/Last. All three are YHWH-exclusive designations. This is the same speaker who uses 'my God' four times in Rev 3:12.

    Notes: The triple stacking of divine title-pairs by the same Christ who says 'my God' is the most concentrated evidence that 'my God' language does not function as a deity denial. The Revelation data alone resolves the question: the glorified Christ simultaneously holds YHWH titles and says 'my God.'

    YHWH's exclusive 'First and Last' self-designation¶

    Thus saith the LORD the King of Israel, and his redeemer the LORD of hosts; I [am] the first, and I [am] the last; and beside me [there is] no God.

    Context: Isaiah 44:6 is YHWH's self-declaration of exclusive deity. The 'first and last' title is paired with 'beside me there is no God.' When Christ claims 'I am the first and the last' in Rev 1:17 and 22:13, he is claiming this exclusively divine title.

    Notes: The exclusivity clause 'beside me there is no God' makes this a monotheistic declaration. Christ's use of the same title places him within the identity of YHWH, not outside it.

    'My God, my God' cry combined with deity claims in same psalm¶

    My God, my God, why hast thou forsaken me? [why art thou so] far from helping me, [and from] the words of my roaring? O my God, I cry in the daytime, but thou hearest not; and in the night season, and am not silent. But thou [art] holy, [O thou] that inhabitest the praises of Israel. Our fathers trusted in thee: they trusted, and thou didst deliver them. They cried unto thee, and were delivered: they trusted in thee, and were not confounded. But I [am] a worm, and no man; a reproach of men, and despised of the people. All they that see me laugh me to scorn: they shoot out the lip, they shake the head, [saying], He trusted on the LORD [that] he would deliver him: let him deliver him, seeing he delighted in him. But thou [art] he that took me out of the womb: thou didst make me hope [when I was] upon my mother's breasts. I was cast upon thee from the womb: thou [art] my God from my mother's belly.

    Context: Psalm 22 begins with 'Eli, Eli' (H410 + 1st person suffix) — the paradigmatic 'my God' text quoted by Jesus on the cross (Matt 27:46). The psalm combines the most intense 'my God' language with a trajectory toward universal worship (vv.27-28) and dominion (v.28).

    Notes: Hebrew parsing of v.1 confirms 'eli' uses H410 (el) with first-person possessive suffix. The psalm's progression is: forsakenness (v.1) → physical suffering (vv.6-18) → vindication (vv.22-31). The 'my God' cry does not negate the psalmist's trust (v.24: 'he hath NOT despised nor abhorred') or the psalm's trajectory toward universal worship of YHWH (vv.27-28). Hebrews 2:12 quotes Psalm 22:22 and applies it to Jesus.

    Psalm 22 trajectory: from 'my God' to universal worship and dominion¶

    I will declare thy name unto my brethren: in the midst of the congregation will I praise thee. Ye that fear the LORD, praise him; all ye the seed of Jacob, glorify him; and fear him, all ye the seed of Israel. For he hath not despised nor abhorred the affliction of the afflicted; neither hath he hid his face from him; but when he cried unto him, he heard. My praise [shall be] of thee in the great congregation: I will pay my vows before them that fear him. The meek shall eat and be satisfied: they shall praise the LORD that seek him: your heart shall live for ever. All the ends of the world shall remember and turn unto the LORD: and all the kindreds of the nations shall worship before thee. For the kingdom [is] the LORD'S: and he [is] the governor among the nations. All [they that be] fat upon earth shall eat and worship: all they that go down to the dust shall bow before him: and none can keep alive his own soul. A seed shall serve him; it shall be accounted to the Lord for a generation. They shall come, and shall declare his righteousness unto a people that shall be born, that he hath done [this].

    Context: The resolution of Psalm 22. After the 'my God' cry and suffering, the psalm moves to vindication: 'he hath NOT despised nor abhorred' (v.24, triple negation). Then universal worship: 'all the ends of the world shall remember and turn unto the LORD: and all the kindreds of the nations shall worship before thee' (v.27). The 'my God' cry and universal worship coexist in the same psalm.

    Notes: Hebrews 2:12 quotes Psalm 22:22 ('I will declare thy name unto my brethren') and applies it to Jesus. The psalm that contains the paradigmatic 'my God' cry also contains universal worship and dominion language — demonstrating that 'my God' and deity coexist within a single scriptural framework.

    Cross cry: Jesus quotes Psalm 22:1 'My God, my God'¶

    And about the ninth hour Jesus cried with a loud voice, saying, Eli, Eli, lama sabachthani? that is to say, My God, my God, why hast thou forsaken me?

    Context: Jesus on the cross quotes Psalm 22:1. The Aramaic/Hebrew 'Eli, Eli' preserves the H410 (el) + possessive suffix. This is the same 'my God' language as John 20:17, used in the most intense moment of incarnational suffering. The cry is relational — addressed TO God, not away from God.

    Notes: The cross cry connects John 20:17's 'my God' to the incarnational suffering framework. If 'my God' at the cross does not deny deity (and no orthodox interpreter claims it does), then 'my God' in John 20:17 does not deny deity either.

    Peter refuses Cornelius's worship — contrast with Jesus¶

    And as Peter was coming in, Cornelius met him, and fell down at his feet, and worshipped [him]. But Peter took him up, saying, Stand up; I myself also am a man.

    Context: Cornelius prostrates himself before Peter (proskyneo). Peter immediately refuses: 'Stand up; I myself also am a man.' Peter redirects worship away from himself. This is the first of four worship-refusal passages that contrast with Jesus' consistent acceptance of worship.

    Notes: The contrast is stark: a righteous apostle refuses worship with the explanation 'I am a man.' Jesus never makes this disclaimer. When Thomas calls him 'my Lord and my God,' Jesus responds with blessing, not correction.

    Angel refuses John's worship — 'worship God'¶

    And I fell at his feet to worship him. And he said unto me, See [thou do it] not: I am thy fellowservant, and of thy brethren that have the testimony of Jesus: worship God: for the testimony of Jesus is the spirit of prophecy.

    Context: John falls to worship the angel. The angel refuses: 'See thou do it not... worship God.' The angel identifies himself as a 'fellowservant' (syndoulos) and redirects worship to God. This is repeated in Rev 22:8-9.

    Notes: The angel's redirect 'worship God' (to theo proskynesonon) establishes the principle: worship belongs to God alone. When Jesus accepts worship (John 20:28, Matt 14:33, Matt 28:9,17, Heb 1:6), he either IS God or is violating this principle.

    Angel refuses worship a second time — reinforcing the pattern¶

    And I John saw these things, and heard [them]. And when I had heard and seen, I fell down to worship before the feet of the angel which shewed me these things. Then saith he unto me, See [thou do it] not: for I am thy fellowservant, and of thy brethren the prophets, and of them which keep the sayings of this book: worship God.

    Context: Second angelic refusal of worship, using nearly identical language to Rev 19:10. The repetition emphasizes: righteous creatures consistently refuse worship and redirect to God.

    Notes: The double refusal in Revelation creates a clear pattern: even exalted angelic beings refuse worship. Jesus never refuses it. In the same book where angels say 'worship God,' the glorified Christ holds YHWH titles (Rev 1:8, 17; 22:13) and receives worship (Heb 1:6 commands it).

    'None righteous, none that doeth good' — universal depravity background¶

    As it is written, There is none righteous, no, not one: There is none that understandeth, there is none that seeketh after God. They are all gone out of the way, they are together become unprofitable; there is none that doeth good, no, not one.

    Context: Paul quotes Psalm 14:1-3 (and Psalm 53:1-3) to establish universal human depravity. 'There is none that doeth good' (ou poioun chrestoteta — using G5544 chrestotes) establishes the 'none good' verdict as applying to all humanity.

    Notes: This is the OT/NT background for Mark 10:18. If 'none is good' is the universal human verdict, then Jesus' statement 'no one is good except God alone' creates a binary: the God-side (good) and the human-side (not good). Jesus must be on one side or the other. The sinlessness passages (Heb 4:15, 2 Cor 5:21, 1 Pet 2:22, 1 Jn 3:5) place him on God's side of this divide.

    OT source of 'none good' — universal human depravity¶

    The fool hath said in his heart, [There is] no God. They are corrupt, they have done abominable works, [there is] none that doeth good. The LORD looked down from heaven upon the children of men, to see if there were any that did understand, [and] seek God. They are all gone aside, they are [all] together become filthy: [there is] none that doeth good, no, not one.

    Context: David's psalm establishing universal human depravity. The Hebrew of v.1 uses 'ein oseh-towb' (none doing good) — where towb is H2896, the Hebrew equivalent of Greek agathos (G18). The LXX maps H2896 to G18 with the highest PMI score (5.17). Paul quotes this in Romans 3:10-12.

    Notes: Hebrew parsing confirms: 'ein' (H369, negative particle) + 'oseh' (Qal active participle of asah H6213, 'doing') + 'towb' (H2896, 'good'). The towb-agathos LXX connection links this OT depravity passage directly to Jesus' use of agathos in Mark 10:18.

    Kenosis hymn — controlling incarnational framework¶

    Let this mind be in you, which was also in Christ Jesus: Who, being in the form of God, thought it not robbery to be equal with God: But made himself of no reputation, and took upon him the form of a servant, and was made in the likeness of men: And being found in fashion as a man, he humbled himself, and became obedient unto death, even the death of the cross. Wherefore God also hath highly exalted him, and given him a name which is above every name: That at the name of Jesus every knee should bow, of [things] in heaven, and [things] in earth, and [things] under the earth; And [that] every tongue should confess that Jesus Christ [is] Lord, to the glory of God the Father.

    Context: The kenosis hymn provides the controlling framework for all 'lesser' language about Jesus. The structure: pre-incarnation equality with God (morphe theou, G3444 + G2316) → voluntary self-emptying (ekenosen, G2758) → incarnational subordination (morphen doulou, G3444 + G1401) → hyper-exaltation. Philippians 2:10-11 quotes Isaiah 45:23 (a YHWH-exclusive text) and applies it to Jesus.

    Notes: The Greek confirms: 'en morphe Theou hyparchon' (being in the form of God — present participle, indicating ongoing state) + 'ouch harpagmon hegesato to einai isa Theo' (did not consider equality with God something to be grasped/exploited). The pre-incarnation equality is assumed as given; the self-emptying is the voluntary act. 'My God' and 'goodness question' language both fit within the 'morphen doulou' (form of servant) phase — incarnational expressions of the one who is en morphe Theou.

    Prologue: 'The Word was God' — inclusio with John 20:28¶

    In the beginning was the Word, and the Word was with God, and the Word was God.

    Context: John's Gospel opens with the deity of the Word. 'Theos en ho Logos' — the anarthrous predicate nominative (see grammar reference on anarthrous-predicate-nominative) emphasizes the qualitative divine nature of the Word. The Gospel that begins 'the Word was God' ends with Thomas's 'my God' — forming a theological inclusio.

    Notes: The inclusio structure means John 20:17 ('my God') must be read within a Gospel whose controlling thesis is 'the Word was God' (1:1) and whose conclusion is 'my Lord and my God' (20:28). The 'my God' language of 20:17 is not a retraction of the prologue's claims.

    Incarnation: 'The Word became flesh'¶

    And the Word was made flesh, and dwelt among us, (and we beheld his glory, the glory as of the only begotten of the Father,) full of grace and truth.

    Context: The incarnation statement. The Word who 'was God' (1:1) 'became flesh' (1:14). This is the foundational mechanism for 'my God' language: the divine Word took on human nature, and through that human nature relates to the Father as 'my God.'

    Notes: The incarnation is the key that resolves both crux texts. The divine Son who was 'with God' and 'was God' (1:1) became flesh (1:14). The human nature of the incarnate Son genuinely says 'my God' — not as denial of deity but as authentic expression of the incarnational relationship.

    Incarnation mechanism: Christ partook of flesh and blood¶

    Forasmuch then as the children are partakers of flesh and blood, he also himself likewise took part of the same; that through death he might destroy him that had the power of death, that is, the devil; And deliver them who through fear of death were all their lifetime subject to bondage. For verily he took not on [him the nature of] angels; but he took on [him] the seed of Abraham. Wherefore in all things it behoved him to be made like unto [his] brethren, that he might be a merciful and faithful high priest in things [pertaining] to God, to make reconciliation for the sins of the people.

    Context: Hebrews 2 explains the incarnation mechanism. The children are 'partakers' (kekoinoneken — perfect tense, permanent state) of flesh and blood; Christ 'took part' (meteschen — aorist, bounded act) of the same. He was 'made like unto his brethren' in all things — including using 'my God' language, which is what a human high priest relating to God would say.

    Notes: The tense contrast (perfect for human nature, aorist for Christ's assumption of it) highlights the voluntary, deliberate character of the incarnation. The high priest 'in things pertaining to God' (ta pros ton theon) naturally relates to God as 'my God' — this is priestly language, not subordination language.

    Jesus claims equal honor with Father — divine prerogatives¶

    Therefore the Jews sought the more to kill him, because he not only had broken the sabbath, but said also that God was his Father, making himself equal with God. That all [men] should honour the Son, even as they honour the Father. He that honoureth not the Son honoureth not the Father which hath sent him.

    Context: John 5:18 records the Jewish understanding: Jesus' 'my Father' language constituted 'making himself equal with God.' John 5:23 then records Jesus claiming equal honor with the Father. This establishes that 'my Father' / 'my God' language was understood as a deity claim, not a deity denial, by Jesus' contemporaries.

    Notes: The progression from 'my Father' (pateron idion — his own Father, v.18) to 'equal with God' (ison... to theo, v.18) to 'honour the Son even as (kathos) they honour the Father' (v.23) shows escalation, not subordination. Jesus' relational language ('my Father') was heard as an equality claim.

    'I and the Father are one' — stoning attempt confirms deity claim¶

    I and [my] Father are one. The Jews answered him, saying, For a good work we stone thee not; but for blasphemy; and because that thou, being a man, makest thyself God.

    Context: Jesus declares 'I and the Father are one' (ego kai ho pater hen esmen). The Jews attempt stoning 'because that thou, being a man, makest thyself God.' This confirms that first-century trained Torah experts understood Jesus' claims as deity claims. Jesus does not retract the claim.

    Notes: The stoning attempt in John 10:33 parallels the stoning attempt in John 8:59 after 'Before Abraham was, I am.' In both cases, the audience understands a deity claim and Jesus does not correct their understanding.

    Sanhedrin trial: ego eimi + Son of Man produces blasphemy verdict¶

    But he held his peace, and answered nothing. Again the high priest asked him, and said unto him, Art thou the Christ, the Son of the Blessed? And Jesus said, I am: and ye shall see the Son of man sitting on the right hand of power, and coming in the clouds of heaven. Then the high priest rent his clothes, and saith, What need we any further witnesses? Ye have heard the blasphemy: what think ye? And they all condemned him to be guilty of death.

    Context: At the trial, Jesus responds with 'ego eimi' (I am) and the Son of Man claim (Dan 7:13-14). The high priest pronounces it blasphemy — a claim to deity deserving death. Trained Torah experts heard a deity claim in Jesus' words.

    Notes: This is the decisive human-court verdict on Jesus' claims. The same Jesus who said 'Why do you call me good?' (Mark 10:18) now says 'ego eimi' before the Sanhedrin, and the result is a blasphemy verdict. If Mark 10:18 were a deity denial, the Sanhedrin trial makes no sense — they would have cited it as evidence of Jesus' own admission.

    Absolute ego eimi: 'Before Abraham was, I AM'¶

    Jesus said unto them, Verily, verily, I say unto you, Before Abraham was, I am. Then took they up stones to cast at him: but Jesus hid himself, and went out of the temple, going through the midst of them, and so passed by.

    Context: Jesus claims pre-existence using the absolute ego eimi construction — 'I am' without predicate (see grammar reference on absolute-ego-eimi). The stoning attempt shows the audience understood this as a divine self-identification (connecting to LXX rendering of YHWH's 'I am he' in Isaiah 41:4, 43:10, etc.).

    Notes: The absolute ego eimi exceeds any shaliach (agency) framework. No agent claims eternal pre-existence or uses the divine self-identification formula. This claim, combined with worship-acceptance and sin-forgiveness, places Jesus beyond the bounds of any normal agent-sender relationship.

    'This is the true God and eternal life' — applied to Jesus¶

    And we know that the Son of God is come, and hath given us an understanding, that we may know him that is true, and we are in him that is true, [even] in his Son Jesus Christ. This is the true God, and eternal life.

    Context: The nearest antecedent of 'this' (houtos) is 'his Son Jesus Christ.' John applies 'the true God' (ho alethinos theos) to Jesus — the same phrase used in John 17:3 for the Father. The trin-03 study established that alethinos contrasts genuine with counterfeit (1 Thess 1:9), not Father with Son.

    Notes: If Jesus is 'the true God,' then the goodness that belongs to 'God alone' (Mark 10:18) belongs to Jesus. The 'only God is good' statement becomes an implicit deity claim rather than a denial.

    'The church of God, which he purchased with his own blood'¶

    Take heed therefore unto yourselves, and to all the flock, over the which the Holy Ghost hath made you overseers, to feed the church of God, which he hath purchased with his own blood.

    Context: Paul refers to 'the church of God' purchased with 'his own blood.' This attributes blood — and therefore incarnation and death — to God. It demonstrates that the NT framework accommodates divine attributes and incarnational experiences in the same person.

    Notes: This verse illustrates the same dynamic as the crux texts: divine identity ('God') and incarnational experience ('his own blood') coexist. Just as 'God' can have 'blood,' the one who is 'God' can say 'my God.'

    Christ sinless: 'without sin' — God-side of the goodness divide¶

    For we have not an high priest which cannot be touched with the feeling of our infirmities; but was in all points tempted like as [we are, yet] without sin.

    Context: Hebrews affirms Christ's sinlessness: 'without sin' (choris hamartias). If 'none is good' is the universal human verdict (Psalm 14:1-3, Romans 3:10-12), and Christ is 'without sin,' then Christ stands on God's side of the goodness divide — he IS good in the absolute sense that Mark 10:18 reserves for God.

    Notes: Combined with 2 Cor 5:21 ('knew no sin'), 1 Pet 2:22 ('did no sin'), and 1 Jn 3:5 ('in him is no sin'), the sinlessness testimony places Jesus outside the 'none good' category of humanity and within the 'God alone is good' category.

    'Who knew no sin' — sinlessness confirms absolute goodness¶

    For he hath made him [to be] sin for us, who knew no sin; that we might be made the righteousness of God in him.

    Context: Paul affirms Christ 'knew no sin' (me gnonta hamartian). This is one of four sinlessness passages that collectively establish Christ's absolute goodness.

    Notes: If 'none good but God' and Christ 'knew no sin,' then either Christ is an exception to 'none good' (making him more than human) or he is on God's side of the divide.

    Forgiveness prerogative: 'who can forgive sins but God alone?'¶

    When Jesus saw their faith, he said unto the sick of the palsy, Son, thy sins be forgiven thee. But there were certain of the scribes sitting there, and reasoning in their hearts, Why doth this [man] thus speak blasphemies? who can forgive sins but God only?

    Context: Jesus forgives the paralytic's sins. The scribes charge blasphemy using the 'ei me heis ho Theos' formula identical to Mark 10:18.

    Notes: The identical 'ei me heis ho Theos' phrase in Mark 2:7 and 10:18 is the strongest lexical evidence for reading Mark 10:18 as an implicit deity claim.

    Father addresses Son as 'God' while saying 'God, thy God'¶

    But unto the Son [he saith], Thy throne, O God, [is] for ever and ever: a sceptre of righteousness [is] the sceptre of thy kingdom. Thou hast loved righteousness, and hated iniquity; therefore God, [even] thy God, hath anointed thee with the oil of gladness above thy fellows.

    Context: Hebrews 1:8-9 quotes Psalm 45:6-7 and applies it to the Son. The Son is called 'ho Theos' while having 'ho Theos sou' (your God). Two-theos pattern.

    Notes: The Father calls the Son God while being the Son's God. Directly parallel to John 20:17 where Jesus is God (by Thomas's confession in v.28) while saying 'my God.'

    Psalm 102:25-27 (YHWH text) applied to the Son¶

    And, Thou, Lord, in the beginning hast laid the foundation of the earth; and the heavens are the works of thine hands: They shall perish; but thou remainest; and they all shall wax old as doth a garment; And as a vesture shalt thou fold them up, and they shall be changed: but thou art the same, and thy years shall not fail.

    Context: Continuing the address to the Son, the author quotes Psalm 102:25-27, a YHWH psalm, applying creation credit to the Son.

    Notes: YHWH-text transfer: a psalm addressed to YHWH is applied to the Son, crediting him with creation.

    Barnabas 'a good man' — human relative use of agathos¶

    For he was a good man, and full of the Holy Ghost and of faith: and much people was added unto the Lord.

    Context: Barnabas called 'aner agathos' — human relative goodness, contrasting with the absolute divine sense in Mark 10:18.

    Notes: Establishes that agathos can describe humans in a relative sense. Mark 10:18 uses it in the absolute sense applicable only to God.

    Jesus uses 'good man' — human relative goodness acknowledged¶

    A good man out of the good treasure of the heart bringeth forth good things: and an evil man out of the evil treasure bringeth forth evil things.

    Context: Jesus himself uses 'agathos anthropos' for humans who bring forth good. He does not deny all human goodness — he distinguishes relative from absolute.

    Notes: Jesus' own use of agathos for humans confirms that Mark 10:18 concerns absolute divine goodness, not the existence of any human goodness at all.

    Joseph of Arimathaea 'a good man' — third human use of agathos¶

    And, behold, [there was] a man named Joseph, a counseller; [and he was] a good man, and a just:

    Context: Luke describes Joseph as 'aner agathos kai dikaios.' Third NT example of human relative goodness alongside Acts 11:24 and Matt 12:35.

    Notes: Three human uses of agathos establish the relative sense; Mark 10:18 specifies the absolute, supreme sense applicable only to God.

    Post-resurrection: 'All authority given unto me in heaven and earth'¶

    And Jesus came and spake unto them, saying, All power is given unto me in heaven and in earth.

    Context: Post-resurrection Jesus holds 'pasa exousia' — all authority. The one who said 'my God' now holds all authority.

    Notes: Post-resurrection authority removal supports reading 'my God' language as incarnational rather than ontological.

    'The only true God' — trin-03 established this does not exclude Jesus¶

    And this is life eternal, that they might know thee the only true God, and Jesus Christ, whom thou hast sent.

    Context: Jesus calls the Father 'the only true God.' The trin-03 study established monos functions doxologically against false gods. 1 John 5:20 applies 'the true God' to Jesus.

    Notes: If 'only true God' does not exclude Jesus from deity (trin-03), then 'no one good but God alone' need not exclude him either.

    Pre-existent glory — same prayer contains deity claims¶

    And now, O Father, glorify thou me with thine own self with the glory which I had with thee before the world was.

    Context: Same prayer as John 17:3. Jesus claims pre-existent glory 'before the world was.' Only deity possesses glory before creation.

    Notes: 'Only true God' (v.3) and 'glory before the world' (v.5) coexist in one prayer, paralleling the John 20:17/20:28 juxtaposition.

    Shema distributed between Father (theos) and Son (kyrios)¶

    But to us [there is but] one God, the Father, of whom [are] all things, and we in him; and one Lord Jesus Christ, by whom [are] all things, and we by him.

    Context: Paul distributes the Shema between Father (heis theos) and Son (heis kyrios). Jesus is placed within the Shema as kyrios.

    Notes: Paul includes Jesus within the Shema structure. If Jesus is within the 'one God' framework, he is within the 'God alone is good' of Mark 10:18.

    Jesus affirms the Shema — monotheistic framework¶

    And Jesus answered him, The first of all the commandments [is], Hear, O Israel; The Lord our God is one Lord:

    Context: Jesus affirms the Shema as the greatest commandment. He operates within strict monotheism.

    Notes: Jesus' affirmation of the Shema means Mark 10:18 must be read within the same monotheistic framework. The question is whether he is inside or outside 'one God.'

    'In him dwelleth all the fulness of the Godhead bodily'¶

    For in him dwelleth all the fulness of the Godhead bodily.

    Context: All the fullness of deity (theotes G2320) dwells in Christ bodily. Not merely divine qualities but the divine essence itself.

    Notes: If all deity's fullness dwells in Christ, the goodness belonging to God alone (Mark 10:18) dwells in Christ.

    'In him is no sin' — ontological sinlessness¶

    And ye know that he was manifested to take away our sins; and in him is no sin.

    Context: Ontological sinlessness: 'hamartia en auto ouk estin' — sin in him is not. Combined with Heb 4:15, 2 Cor 5:21, 1 Pet 2:22.

    Notes: Four sinlessness passages place Christ outside the 'none good' category of fallen humanity and on God's side of the goodness divide.

    'No man hath ascended... but he that came down from heaven'¶

    And no man hath ascended up to heaven, but he that came down from heaven, [even] the Son of man which is in heaven.

    Context: Jesus claims heavenly origin. The one who says 'my God' (John 20:17) also claims to have come down from heaven — exceeding any shaliach framework.

    Notes: The descent-ascent pattern frames John 20:17 within a pre-existence claim. 'My God' comes from one who originated in heaven.

    'What if ye shall see the Son of man ascend up where he was before?'¶

    [What] and if ye shall see the Son of man ascend up where he was before?

    Context: Pre-existence implied: ascend to 'where he was before.' John 20:17's ascension is a return, not a first visit.

    Notes: Establishes that John 20:17's ascension is return to pre-incarnate glory. 'My God' accompanies a return to heavenly origin.

    He that descended is the same that ascended¶

    Wherefore he saith, When he ascended up on high, he led captivity captive, and gave gifts unto men. (Now that he ascended, what is it but that he also descended first into the lower parts of the earth? He that descended is the same also that ascended up far above all heavens, that he might fill all things.)

    Context: The descender and ascender are the same person. Incarnational descent and ascension-glorification belong together.

    Notes: 'My God' language (ascension context, John 20:17) reflects incarnational descent, not ontological inferiority.

    LXX precedent: nominative-as-vocative in address to God¶

    Stir up thyself, and awake to my judgment, [even] unto my cause, my God and my Lord.

    Context: Hebrew: 'Elohai wa-Adonai' (my God and my Lord) as direct address to YHWH. Harris cites this as LXX precedent for nominative-as-vocative in John 20:28.

    Notes: Thomas's 'ho Kyrios mou kai ho Theos mou' (John 20:28) uses the same title-pair as Psalm 35:23 directed to YHWH. Grammar reference confirms this nominative-for-vocative pattern.

    YHWH-exclusive: 'Every knee shall bow, every tongue shall swear'¶

    I have sworn by myself, the word is gone out of my mouth [in] righteousness, and shall not return, That unto me every knee shall bow, every tongue shall swear.

    Context: YHWH's exclusive claim. Paul quotes this in Phil 2:10-11 and applies it to Jesus.

    Notes: YHWH-text transfer from Isa 45:23 to Phil 2:10-11 demonstrates the NT identifies Jesus within YHWH's identity.

    Joel 2:32 'call on the name of the Lord' applied to Jesus¶

    For whosoever shall call upon the name of the Lord shall be saved.

    Context: Paul quotes Joel 2:32 (calling on YHWH's name) and applies it to Jesus (per context of Rom 10:9-12). Third YHWH-text transfer.

    Notes: Combined with Heb 1:10-12/Psalm 102 and Phil 2:10-11/Isa 45:23, this establishes consistent YHWH-identification of Jesus.

    God 'alone' has immortality — parallel exclusivity formula¶

    Which in his times he shall shew, [who is] the blessed and only Potentate, the King of kings, and Lord of lords; Who only hath immortality, dwelling in the light which no man can approach unto; whom no man hath seen, nor can see: to whom [be] honour and power everlasting. Amen.

    Context: God the 'only Potentate' who 'alone has immortality.' Exclusivity formula parallels Mark 10:18. Yet Christ claims eternal life (Rev 1:18).

    Notes: The trin-03 finding applies: monos formulas are doxological, not intra-Trinitarian exclusions.

    Father gave Son life in himself — self-existent life¶

    For as the Father hath life in himself; so hath he given to the Son to have life in himself;

    Context: Self-existent life — a divine attribute — is possessed by the Son. Incarnational 'given' language coexists with divine attribute.

    Notes: Same pattern as crux texts: relational/incarnational language ('given') coexists with divine attribute claims ('life in himself').

    'My Father is greater than I' — incarnational subordination¶

    Ye have heard how I said unto you, I go away, and come [again] unto you. If ye loved me, ye would rejoice, because I said, I go unto the Father: for my Father is greater than I.

    Context: Father is 'greater' (meizon G3187) — positional/functional, not kreitton (nature-level superiority). Trin-02 finding.

    Notes: Meizon/kreitton distinction from trin-02: positional subordination within incarnation, not ontological inferiority. Parallels 'my God' in John 20:17.

    First commandment: worship God alone — the omitted commandments¶

    Thou shalt have no other gods before me. Thou shalt not make unto thee any graven image, or any likeness [of any thing] that [is] in heaven above, or that [is] in the earth beneath, or that [is] in the water under the earth: Thou shalt not bow down thyself to them, nor serve them: for I the LORD thy God [am] a jealous God, visiting the iniquity of the fathers upon the children unto the third and fourth [generation] of them that hate me; And shewing mercy unto thousands of them that love me, and keep my commandments.

    Context: First-tablet commandments (1-4) concern the God-human relationship. Jesus omits ALL of these in Mark 10:19 and replaces them with 'follow me.'

    Notes: The commandment-omission pattern: Mark 10:19 lists only commandments 5-10. All first-tablet commandments are absent. 'Follow me' replaces them.

    The Shema: 'the LORD our God is one LORD'¶

    Hear, O Israel: The LORD our God [is] one LORD: And thou shalt love the LORD thy God with all thine heart, and with all thy soul, and with all thy might.

    Context: Israel's monotheistic confession. Jesus affirms it (Mark 12:29). Paul distributes it between Father and Son (1 Cor 8:6).

    Notes: The Shema provides the monotheistic context for Mark 10:18. Jesus affirms it while claiming divine prerogatives.

    'Christ... who is over all, God blessed for ever'¶

    Whose [are] the fathers, and of whom as concerning the flesh Christ [came], who is over all, God blessed for ever. Amen.

    Context: Paul calls Christ 'the one who is over all, God blessed forever' — direct application of theos with doxological benediction.

    Notes: Doxological 'blessed forever, Amen' is characteristically reserved for God. Paul applies it to Christ.

    'The great God and our Saviour Jesus Christ' — Granville Sharp¶

    Looking for that blessed hope, and the glorious appearing of the great God and our Saviour Jesus Christ;

    Context: Granville Sharp Rule: single article governs both 'God' and 'Savior,' identifying them as the same person — Jesus is 'the great God.'

    Notes: Another direct application of theos to Jesus. Combined with John 1:1, 20:28, Heb 1:8, Rom 9:5, 1 John 5:20.

    'He that hath seen me hath seen the Father'¶

    Jesus saith unto him, Have I been so long time with you, and yet hast thou not known me, Philip? he that hath seen me hath seen the Father; and how sayest thou [then], Shew us the Father?

    Context: Seeing Jesus IS seeing the Father — not mere representation but identity-revelation.

    Notes: If seeing Jesus reveals the Father, then God's goodness (Mark 10:18) is visible in Jesus.

    Image of the invisible God, firstborn, by him all things created¶

    Who is the image of the invisible God, the firstborn of every creature: For by him were all things created, that are in heaven, and that are in earth, visible and invisible, whether [they be] thrones, or dominions, or principalities, or powers: all things were created by him, and for him: And he is before all things, and by him all things consist.

    Context: Christ as image of God, creator of all things. Trin-01 established prototokos as preeminence title. Creation claim parallels Heb 1:10-12 and Isa 44:24.

    Notes: If Christ created all things, he cannot be a created thing. If YHWH created alone (Isa 44:24), and Christ created all, then Christ is within YHWH's identity.
