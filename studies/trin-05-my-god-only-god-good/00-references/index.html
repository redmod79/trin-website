    Reference Gathering: "My God" and "Only God Good" — Deity Denial or Deity Claim?¶

    Question¶

    When Jesus says "Why do you call me good? No one is good except God alone" (Mark 10:18) and "I ascend to my Father and your Father, to my God and your God" (John 20:17), is he denying his own deity?

    Study Plan Context¶

    This is Study 5 (trin-05) of a 10-study Trinity series. Studies 1–4 are complete: - trin-01 — "Firstborn of all creation" / "beginning of the creation of God" - trin-02 — Subordination passages (John 14:28, 5:19, Mark 13:32, 1 Cor 15:28) - trin-03 — "The only true God" (John 17:3) / "One God, the Father" (1 Cor 8:6) - trin-04 — Ransom sacrifice, whether God can die, Christ's death-sufficiency

    No formal Integrate list exists for a trin-series plan file, but all four prior studies are directly relevant and should be consulted.

    Prior Studies¶

    From Trinity Series (trin-01 through trin-04)¶

    trin-01-firstborn-created-being: - Question: Does "firstborn of all creation" (Col 1:15) or "beginning of the creation of God" (Rev 3:14) teach Jesus was the first created being? - Key finding: Prototokos (G4416) functions as a preeminence title, not a birth-order indicator. OT bekor (H1060) is applied to David (Psa 89:27), Israel (Exo 4:22), Ephraim (Jer 31:9) — all non-firstborn recipients of the title. - Key finding: The hoti causal clause (Col 1:16) grounds the firstborn title in creative agency — "He is firstborn BECAUSE by him were all things created" — making the partitive genitive reading incoherent. - Key finding: The creation paradox — if Christ made ALL things (John 1:3, Col 1:16, Heb 1:2, 1 Cor 8:6), he cannot be a made thing. Isaiah 44:24 says YHWH created alone (lebaddi). - Key finding: Arche (G746) in Rev 3:14 functions within Revelation's Alpha-Omega framework as a divine title of eternal completeness, not createdness. Rev 22:13 stacks arche/telos with protos/eschatos (YHWH's self-designation, Isa 44:6). - Key finding: Hebrews 1:6-12 calls the Son prototokos (v.6), theos (v.8), and kyrios who created heavens/earth (vv.10-12) within one chapter. - Relevance to trin-05: Establishes that titles appearing to diminish Jesus (firstborn, beginning) are actually preeminence/origination claims. The same principle may apply to "Why do you call me good?" — the question may assert rather than deny deity.

    trin-02-subordination-passages: - Question: Do John 14:28, John 5:19, Mark 13:32, 1 Cor 15:28 describe Jesus' eternal nature or voluntary mission? - Key finding: Meizon (G3187) vs. kreitton (G2909) distinction — meizon for positional rank (same-nature comparison), kreitton for qualitative/nature-level superiority. John 14:28 uses meizon, not kreitton. - Key finding: John 5:19-30 pericope escalates from dependence (v.19) to divine prerogatives: equal honor (v.23), all judgment (v.22), self-existent life (v.26). Subordination language FRAMES equality claims. - Key finding: Phil 2:5-11 kenosis hymn provides controlling framework: pre-incarnation equality → voluntary self-emptying → incarnational subordination → hyper-exaltation. No pre-incarnation text describes the Son as subordinate. - Key finding: Post-resurrection evidence removes limitations: Matt 28:18 (all authority), Rev 1:8 (Alpha and Omega), Rev 22:1,3 (shared throne), Rev 5:12-13 (identical worship). - Key finding: Oida (G1492) word study found zero causative/declarative instances — Mark 13:32 ignorance is genuine but temporary/incarnational. - Relevance to trin-05: The incarnational/kenotic framework established here applies directly to both crux texts — "my God" language and the goodness question may both reflect incarnational posture rather than ontological inferiority.

    trin-03-only-true-god: - Question: Do John 17:3 ("only true God") and 1 Cor 8:6 ("one God, the Father") exclude Jesus from deity? - Key finding: Monos (G3441) functions doxologically — praising divine uniqueness against false gods, not cataloguing who is excluded from deity. If monos excluded the Son, Christ could not be immortal (Rev 1:18) or King of kings (Rev 19:16). - Key finding: Alethinos (G228) consistently contrasts genuine with counterfeit. 1 Thess 1:9 explicitly identifies the contrast: true God vs. idols. 1 John 5:20 applies "the true God" to Jesus. - Key finding: John 17:5 (pre-existent glory), 17:10 (mutual ownership), 17:24 (pre-creation love) — the same prayer makes claims only deity could make, making exclusionary reading self-defeating. - Key finding: Paul's 1 Cor 8:6 distributes the Shema's heis between theos (Father) and kyrios (Jesus Christ), placing Jesus within the Shema. The ek/dia preposition distinction collapses because Paul uses both of the Father in Rom 11:36. - Key finding: Three NT passages apply YHWH-exclusive OT texts to Jesus: Isa 45:23 → Phil 2:10-11, Joel 2:32 → Rom 10:13, Psa 102:25 → Heb 1:10. - Relevance to trin-05: DIRECTLY relevant — the monos+theos analysis applies to "No one is good except God alone." If "only true God" does not exclude Jesus, "no one good except God alone" need not either. The contrastive pattern (genuine God vs. false gods) may parallel the goodness statement (God's goodness vs. human goodness).

    trin-04-ransom-sacrifice: - Question: Does the ransom require exact equivalence? Can God die? Did Jesus truly die? - Key finding: Christ genuinely died through the incarnation mechanism — the divine Son assumed mortal humanity while divine nature remained intact. Rev 1:18 captures both: "I am the Living One, and I became dead." - Key finding: 1 Tim 6:16 (God alone has immortality) + Acts 20:28 (God's own blood) resolved via hypostatic union — the divine person owns the human experience. - Key finding: Koinoneo/metecho tense contrast (Heb 2:14) — perfect (permanent mortal state) vs. aorist (bounded voluntary entry) — incarnation as deliberate divine act. - Key finding: Christ claims exousia (John 10:18) over both dying and rising — no mere human possesses this. - Relevance to trin-05: The incarnation mechanism explains how the divine Son can genuinely use "my God" language — the human nature relates to the Father as God, just as the human nature could genuinely die.

    From Semantic Search (additional)¶

    jesus-never-said-i-am-god: (score: 0.524) - Question: Why is there no sentence on Jesus' lips of the form "I am God"? - Key finding: Jesus claimed deity through an "indirect-with-load matrix" — absolute ego eimi sayings, divine-prerogative actions, acceptance of worship, YHWH-title reception, post-resurrection YHWH-formula speech. - Key finding: The Sanhedrin's verdict (Mark 14:64) proves the indirect claim was successful — trained Torah-experts heard a deity claim worth Lev-24:16 execution. - Key finding: Mark 10:18 directly analyzed — "Jesus is testing the rich young ruler's address. The rejoinder is neither 'I am not good' nor 'I am not God' — it is a Socratic probe inviting the man to follow his own address through to its conclusion." - Key finding: Mark 2:5-7 — scribes ask "who can forgive sins but God only?" and Jesus demonstrates he possesses that divine prerogative. The "God only" formula in Mark 2:7 parallels Mark 10:18's "no one good but God." - Key finding: Worship-acceptance pattern — angels refuse worship (Rev 19:10; 22:8-9), apostles refuse (Acts 10:25-26; 14:11-15), only Jesus accepts. - Relevance to trin-05: Provides the comprehensive framework for Jesus' indirect deity claims, including the specific analysis of Mark 10:18 as Socratic probe.

    cry-of-dereliction: (score: 0.342) - Question: What does "My God, my God, why hast thou forsaken me?" reveal about Christ's suffering? - Key finding: The address "My God" (Eli/Eloi) uses el (H410) with first-person possessive — even in forsakenness, the relational claim persists. "This is a cry OF relationship, not FROM it." - Key finding: Psalm 22 moves from forsakenness (v.1) to vindication (vv.22-31). Psa 22:24 triple negation: God did NOT despise, abhor, or hide his face. - Key finding: The seven cross statements trace Father → My God → Father arc. The forsakenness was bounded, temporal, not permanent relational severance. - Key finding: 1 Pet 2:23 imperfect tense paredidou — "kept on entrusting" throughout the passion. Trust maintained even during forsakenness. - Relevance to trin-05: The "my God" language at the cross (Matt 27:46) parallels John 20:17's "my God." In both cases, the "my God" address reflects the incarnational relationship — the human nature relating to the Father — not deity denial.

    trin-03-only-true-god: (score: 0.532 on first search) - Already covered above. Top result across semantic searches.

    greatest-commandment-shema: (score: 0.431) - Question: What did Jesus say about the greatest commandment in Mark 12? - Relevance: Mark 12:29 has Jesus affirming the Shema ("the Lord our God is one Lord"), which establishes that Jesus himself upheld monotheism — making the question whether his "only God good" statement aligns with this.

    Web Research Findings¶

    The web research agent (Phase 0) produced comprehensive scholarly findings in 00-web-research.md. Key findings organized by relevance:

    Mark 10:18 — Scholarly Consensus¶

    • Hampton/Gundry: The phrase "except God alone" (ei me heis ho theos) appears in both Mark 2:7 and Mark 10:18, both beginning with tis ("why"). Gundry: "Jesus proceeds to answer the present question for proof of his divine possession of goodness." This is a deliberate Markan literary device linking the goodness question to the sin-forgiveness prerogative.
    • Gathercole: Jesus cites only commandments 5-10 (neighbor obligations), omitting 1-4 (God obligations), then replaces the first tablet with "follow me" — implying Jesus takes God's position in the man's priorities.
    • Bratcher & Nida (UBS Handbook): Agathos as used by Jesus refers to moral attributes and "here, with reference to God, is the equivalent of 'perfect.'" Confirms the heis ho theos formula and its Mark 2:7 parallel.
    • Lyons (Apologetics Press): Distinguishes relative vs. absolute usage of agathos — humans can be called "good" (Acts 11:24), but Jesus uses the word in the supreme, absolute sense applicable only to God.
    • Calvin dissent: Calvin rejects the "implicit deity claim" reading, arguing Jesus' point concerns doctrinal authority ("thou falsely callest me a good Master, unless thou acknowledgest that I have come from God"). Significant because it shows disagreement even among Trinitarian scholars.
    • Christ's Words Project: Proposes "capable" as an alternative rendering of agathos. Notes the verbless Greek construction and the synoptic divergence with Matt 19:17.
    • BibleHub Commentators (Poole, Gill, Bengel, Cambridge, Pulpit): Broad consensus that Jesus does not deny deity but challenges the ruler to think through the implications of calling him "good."

    John 20:17 — Scholarly Landscape¶

    • Thomas's confession (John 20:28) just 11 verses after John 20:17 — "my Lord and my God" — creates critical juxtaposition. Jesus accepts the confession without correction. Grammatical debate: nominative ho Theos mou may function as vocative (Harris cites Psalm 35:23 LXX precedent). The eipen auto ("said to him") constitutes direct address.
    • Hebrews 1:8-9 / Psalm 45:6-7: The Father addresses the Son as "God" while simultaneously referring to "God, your God" — demonstrating that "my God" language is compatible with deity within the biblical framework itself.
    • Hebrews 1:10-12 / Psalm 102:25-27: The NT applies a YHWH psalm to the Son, showing YHWH-texts transfer to Jesus.
    • Revelation 3:12: The glorified Christ uses "my God" four times in one verse — post-resurrection/post-ascension — suggesting this language reflects the permanent incarnational relationship rather than ontological subordination.
    • Shaliach (agency) concept: In Jewish law, the agent is "as the man himself" (Mishnah Berakhot 5:5). Some argue this explains Jesus' divine prerogatives without requiring ontological deity; others argue Jesus exceeds any normal agent.
    • DavarLogos article: Psalm 22 enables Mark to present Jesus as both divine and human. Psalm 22:1 ("my God, my God") is the paradigmatic "my God" text on Jesus' lips.

Parse the synoptic divergence between Matt 19:16-17 and Mark 10:17-18 4. Examine John 20:17 vs. 20:28 tension — parse the Greek, verify nominative-as-vocative 5. Check Hebrews 1:8-9 / Psalm 45:6-7 two-elohim pattern 6. Verify Revelation 3:12 — glorified Christ's fourfold "my God" 7. Examine Psalm 22:1,10 "my God" language in its full context 8. Check Romans 3:10-12 / Psalm 14:1-3 "none good" OT background 9. Verify agathos usage scope — human uses (Acts 11:24, Matt 12:35)

    Summary for Scoping Agent¶

    • 6 prior studies found with directly relevant findings (trin-01 through trin-04, jesus-never-said-i-am-god, cry-of-dereliction)
    • 12+ external corpus claims identified for biblical verification (EGW, Bohr, Clarke, web scholars)
    • Key leads:
    • The Mark 2:7 / Mark 10:18 lexical parallel (ei me heis ho theos) is the single strongest piece of evidence that Jesus' "only God good" statement is an implicit deity claim — it links the goodness question to the sin-forgiveness episode where Jesus demonstrates divine prerogative. This MUST be verified in the Greek text.
    • The John 20:17 / 20:28 juxtaposition — "my God" language followed 11 verses later by Thomas's "my Lord and my God" accepted without correction — creates a decisive test. If Jesus uses "my God" for the Father and then accepts "my God" from Thomas, the "my God" language cannot exclude deity.
    • Revelation 3:12 — the GLORIFIED, post-ascension Christ uses "my God" four times. If "my God" language denies deity, the glorified Christ denies his own deity after the resurrection — contradicting Rev 1:8, 17; 22:13 where the same Christ claims YHWH titles. This is a decisive data point the research agent must investigate.
    • The incarnational/kenotic framework from trin-02 and trin-04 provides the interpretive grid: "my God" language reflects the human nature's relationship to the Father, just as Mark 13:32's ignorance reflects incarnational self-limitation.
    • The commandment-omission pattern in Mark 10:19 (Gathercole's argument) — if Jesus replaces the first tablet (God-obligations) with "follow me," this is an implicit claim to occupy God's position in the man's priorities.
