    Question¶

    When Jesus says 'Why do you call me good? No one is good except God alone' (Mark 10:18) and 'I ascend to my Father and your Father, to my God and your God' (John 20:17), is he denying his own deity?

    Prior Research Summary¶

    Six prior studies found with directly relevant findings: trin-01 (firstborn/created being — titles that appear to diminish Jesus are actually preeminence claims), trin-02 (subordination — incarnational/kenotic framework explains 'lesser' language), trin-03 (only true God — monos/heis theos does NOT exclude Jesus; Shema distribution in 1 Cor 8:6), trin-04 (ransom — incarnation mechanism explains how divine Son genuinely uses 'my God'), jesus-never-said-i-am-god (Mark 10:18 as Socratic probe; Mark 2:7 parallel), cry-of-dereliction (Psalm 22 'my God' cry reflects incarnational relationship). Web research found scholarly consensus reading Mark 10:18 as implicit deity claim (Hampton/Gundry, Gathercole, Bratcher/Nida, Lyons, BibleHub commentators); Calvin dissents (doctrinal authority reading). EGW reads Mark 10:18 as refusing casual flattery while asserting equality with Father.

    Primary Verses (33 — full analysis required)¶

    Mark 10 — Rich Young Ruler Pericope: All three synoptic accounts of the goodness question - Mark 10:17-22 — Primary crux text — rich young ruler encounter with 'Why do you call me good?' question and follow-up commandment list + 'follow me' - Matt 19:16-17 — Synoptic parallel — Matthew's version differs ('why askest thou me concerning that which is good?'), indicating editorial shaping - Luke 18:18-23 — Third synoptic parallel — Luke's version for comparative analysis

    John 20 — Resurrection Deity Matrix: The chapter spanning 'my God' (v.17) to 'my Lord and my God' (v.28) - John 20:17 — Second crux text — 'I ascend to my Father and your Father, to my God and your God'

    Hebrews 1 — Father Calls Son God: Two-elohim pattern and YHWH-text transfer - Heb 1:8-9 — Father addresses Son as 'God' (theos) while saying 'God, thy God hath anointed thee' — two-elohim pattern from Psalm 45 - Heb 1:10-12 — Psalm 102:25-27 (YHWH text) applied to the Son — creation attributed to Christ - Heb 1:6 — Father commands worship of the Son — 'let all the angels of God worship him'

    Psalm 45 and Psalm 102 — OT Sources: Hebrew source texts for Hebrews 1 quotations - Psalm 45:6-7 — Hebrew source of Heb 1:8-9 — elohim used for both the one addressed and the anointing God - Psalm 102:25-27 — Hebrew source of Heb 1:10-12 — YHWH psalm applied to the Son

    Revelation — Glorified Christ's 'My God' + YHWH Titles: Post-ascension 'my God' language coexisting with YHWH claims - Rev 3:12 — Glorified post-ascension Christ uses 'my God' four times — permanent incarnational language, not ontological subordination - Rev 1:8 — Christ claims Alpha and Omega / Almighty — YHWH titles in same book where he says 'my God' - Rev 1:17-18 — First and Last (YHWH title from Isa 44:6) + 'I am the Living One, and I became dead' - Rev 22:12-13 — Alpha/Omega + First/Last + Beginning/End stacked — same speaker who uses 'my God' in Rev 3:12

    Psalm 22 — My God Cry + Deity Claims: Paradigmatic 'my God' text combining forsakenness with universal worship - Psalm 22:1-10 — Paradigmatic 'my God' text on Jesus' lips (Matt 27:46) — combines 'my God' cry with deity claims in same psalm

    Worship Pattern: Worship-refusal by creatures vs. worship-acceptance by Jesus - Acts 10:25-26 — Peter refuses Cornelius's worship — contrast with Jesus accepting Thomas's worship - Rev 19:10 — Angel refuses John's worship — 'worship God' — contrast with Jesus accepting worship - Rev 22:8-9 — Angel refuses worship a second time — reinforces that only God may be worshipped - John 20:28 — Thomas's confession 'my Lord and my God' — 11 verses after John 20:17, Jesus accepts without correction - Heb 1:6 — Father commands worship of the Son — 'let all the angels of God worship him'

    None Good / Depravity Background: OT universal depravity + sinlessness passages - Rom 3:10-12 — Paul quotes Psalm 14:1-3 'there is none righteous, no not one; none that doeth good' — OT background for 'none good' claim - Psalm 14:1-3 — OT source of 'none good' — universal human depravity, establishing the God-side vs. human-side divide

    Incarnation/Kenosis Framework: NT basis for two-natures understanding - Phil 2:5-11 — Kenosis hymn — pre-incarnation equality → voluntary self-emptying → incarnational subordination → exaltation. Controls the framework - John 1:14 — Incarnation — 'the Word became flesh' provides the framework for 'my God' language

    Focus Areas¶

    1. Mark 10:18 — Socratic Probe or Deity Denial?: The research agent should parse Mark 10:17-22 with greek_parser.py and retrieve the full chapter context. The critical question is whether Jesus' response is a denial or a probe. READ $WORD_STUDIES/G1520-heis.md for the numeral 'one' and $WORD_STUDIES/G2316-theos.md for theos. Parse the Greek of all three synoptic accounts (Mark 10:17-22, Matt 19:16-17, Luke 18:18-23) to verify the synoptic divergence — Matthew reportedly has 'why askest thou me concerning that which is good?' rather than 'why callest thou me good?' Verify whether this represents editorial softening or a different tradition. Check the logical structure: if only God is good, and Jesus IS good (which he never denies), then the question becomes an implicit identity claim. Run cross_testament_parallels_v2.py --hybrid-ot and --hybrid-nt on Mark 10:18. Verses: Mark 10:17-22, Matt 19:16-17, Luke 18:18-23, Mark 2:5-7 Strong's: G18, G1520, G2316, G2962

    2. The Mark 2:7 / Mark 10:18 Lexical Parallel (ei me heis ho theos): This is the single strongest piece of evidence from the web research (Hampton/Gundry). The research agent must parse Mark 2:5-12 with greek_parser.py to extract the exact Greek of 'who can forgive sins but God alone?' and compare it word-for-word with Mark 10:18's 'no one is good except God alone.' If the phrases share the same construction (tis + ei me + heis + ho theos), this is a significant Markan literary device. In Mark 2, Jesus responds to the 'only God' challenge by demonstrating his divine authority to forgive sins. The question is whether Mark 10:18 functions analogously — Jesus' subsequent actions (commanding obedience + 'follow me') demonstrate his divine authority to define eternal life requirements. Run greek_parser.py --verse on both MRK 2:7 and MRK 10:18 to verify the lexical link. Verses: Mark 2:5-12, Mark 10:17-22 Strong's: G1520, G2316, G5101, G863

    3. The Commandment-Omission Pattern in Mark 10:19: The research agent should retrieve Mark 10:19 text and compare each cited commandment against the Decalogue (Exo 20:3-17). Verify that commandments 1-4 (no other gods, no graven images, not take name in vain, remember sabbath) are ALL absent from Jesus' list, while commandments 5-10 (honor parents, do not kill, do not commit adultery, do not steal, do not bear false witness, do not covet) are present. Then examine the climax in Mark 10:21 — 'come, take up the cross, and follow me.' If 'follow me' functionally replaces the first tablet (supreme allegiance to God), this is a structural deity claim. Compare with Matt 22:37-40 where Jesus separates the two great commandments (love God / love neighbor) — the Mark 10 pattern mirrors this division. Run greek_parser.py on MRK 10:19 and MRK 10:21. Verses: Mark 10:19, Mark 10:21, Exo 20:3-17, Deut 6:4-5 Strong's: G18, G190, G1785

    4. John 20:17 — 'My God' in Post-Resurrection Context: The research agent should parse John 20:17 with greek_parser.py and retrieve the full John 20 chapter. Key observation: Jesus says 'MY Father and YOUR Father, MY God and YOUR God' — NOT 'our Father and our God.' This deliberate distinction suggests Jesus relates to the Father differently than the disciples do. The 'my God' reflects the incarnational human-nature relationship, while the possessive distinction preserves the unique Son-Father relationship. READ $WORD_STUDIES/G2316-theos.md for theos usage patterns. Run cross_testament_parallels_v2.py --hybrid-ot and --hybrid-nt on JHN 20:17. Check the broader John 20 narrative: the chapter OPENS with resurrection and CLOSES with Thomas's 'my Lord and my God' — the entire chapter frames 'my God' language within a resurrection-deity matrix. Verses: John 20:17, John 20:28-31, John 1:1, John 1:14 Strong's: G305, G2316, G3962, G2962

    5. John 20:28 — Thomas's 'My Lord and My God' and Jesus' Acceptance: The research agent should parse John 20:28 with greek_parser.py to verify the Greek text ho Kyrios mou kai ho Theos mou and the eipen auto ('said to him') that constitutes direct address. READ $WORD_STUDIES/G2962-kyrios.md for kyrios. The grammatical debate centers on whether the nominative case (ho Theos mou) can function as vocative. Harris cites Psalm 35:23 LXX as precedent for nominative-as-vocative in direct address to God — verify this by checking the Greek of Psalm 35:23 (LXX numbering). The critical contrast is with worship-refusal: Peter refuses Cornelius's prostration (Acts 10:25-26), Paul and Barnabas refuse worship at Lystra (Acts 14:11-15), the angel refuses John's worship TWICE (Rev 19:10; 22:8-9). Jesus does none of this — he accepts Thomas's confession and responds with blessing. READ $WORD_STUDIES/G4352-proskyneo.md for worship patterns. Run cross_testament_parallels_v2.py --hybrid-nt on JHN 20:28. Verses: John 20:26-29, Acts 10:25-26, Acts 14:11-15, Rev 19:10, Rev 22:8-9 Strong's: G2316, G2962, G4352, G4314

    6. Hebrews 1:8-12 / Psalm 45:6-7 / Psalm 102:25-27 — The Two-Elohim Pattern and YHWH-Text Transfer: The research agent should parse Psalm 45:6-7 with hebrew_parser.py and Hebrews 1:8-9 with greek_parser.py. In Psalm 45:6, the addressed king is called elohim (God) — 'Thy throne, O God, is for ever and ever.' In 45:7, the same figure has 'God, thy God' who anointed him. READ $WORD_STUDIES/H430-elohiym.md for elohim patterns and $WORD_STUDIES/H410-el.md for el. The author of Hebrews explicitly applies this to the Father-Son relationship (Heb 1:8-9). Then Heb 1:10-12 quotes Psalm 102:25-27, a psalm addressed to YHWH ('Of old hast thou laid the foundation of the earth'), and applies it to the Son. Parse Psalm 102:25-27 with hebrew_parser.py to verify the YHWH address. This is one of the three NT passages (with Phil 2:10-11/Isa 45:23 and Rom 10:13/Joel 2:32) that apply YHWH-exclusive OT texts to Jesus. Run cross_testament_parallels_v2.py on HEB 1:8 and HEB 1:10. Verses: Heb 1:8-9, Heb 1:10-12, Psalm 45:6-7, Psalm 102:25-27 Strong's: H430, H410, G2316, G2962

    7. Revelation 3:12 — The Glorified Christ's Fourfold 'My God': The research agent should retrieve the full text of Rev 3:12 and parse it with greek_parser.py. The glorified Christ says 'my God' (tou theou mou) four times: 'a pillar in the temple of my God...the name of my God...the city of my God...the new Jerusalem, which cometh down out of heaven from my God.' This is not pre-resurrection humiliation language — this is the exalted, glorified Christ speaking from heaven. Yet the same speaker in Rev 1:8 claims 'I am Alpha and Omega' (YHWH title), in Rev 1:17-18 claims 'I am the first and the last' (Isa 44:6 YHWH self-designation), and in Rev 22:12-13 stacks Alpha/Omega + First/Last + Beginning/End. READ $WORD_STUDIES/G746-arche.md for arche as divine title. The only coherent resolution is that 'my God' reflects the permanent incarnational relationship — the divine Son who became human forever maintains the human-nature's relationship to the Father, while simultaneously possessing the divine attributes and titles. Run cross_testament_parallels_v2.py on REV 3:12. Verses: Rev 3:12, Rev 1:8, Rev 1:17-18, Rev 22:12-13, Isa 44:6 Strong's: G2316, G746, G4413

    8. Psalm 22 — 'My God' Combined with Deity Claims in the Same Psalm: The research agent should retrieve the full text of Psalm 22 from KJV and parse Psalm 22:1 with hebrew_parser.py. The psalm begins with 'My God, my God' (eli, eli — using H410 el with first-person possessive suffix). READ $WORD_STUDIES/H410-el.md for el. Note the progression: v.1 forsakenness → v.22 'I will declare thy name unto my brethren' (quoted in Heb 2:12) → v.24 triple negation (God did NOT despise, abhor, or hide) → vv.27-28 'all the ends of the world shall remember and turn unto the LORD; all the kindreds of the nations shall worship before thee. For the kingdom is the LORD's.' The 'my God' cry occurs within a psalm that attributes universal worship and dominion to YHWH — and Hebrews applies the psalm's language to Jesus. This demonstrates that 'my God' and deity coexist. Run cross_testament_parallels_v2.py on PSA 22:1. Verses: Psalm 22:1-10, Psalm 22:22-31, Matt 27:46, Heb 2:12 Strong's: H410, H430, G2316

    9. The Agathos (G18) Word Study — Relative vs. Absolute Goodness: The research agent should run search_strongs.py --lookup G18 and search_strongs.py --verses G18 to get all occurrences. READ $WORD_STUDIES/G2570-kalos.md for the near-synonym kalos to understand the distinction. Key human uses of agathos: Acts 11:24 (Barnabas 'a good man'), Matt 12:35 ('a good man out of the good treasure'), Matt 25:21 ('good and faithful servant'), Luke 23:50 (Joseph of Arimathaea). In these cases, agathos means morally upright in a relative sense. But in Mark 10:18, Jesus specifies 'no one is good except God alone' — elevating the word to its absolute, supreme sense. The question is NOT whether humans can be called good (they can, relatively), but whether anyone is good in the way God is good (absolutely). Lyons (Apologetics Press) makes this distinction explicit. Run search_strongs.py --verses G18 to find all instances. Also check G19 agathosyne (goodness) for the noun form.
